How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what's causing the problem: weak signal, slow speeds, interference, or too many devices. Here are the most effective steps, roughly in order of impact:

- Move your router to a better location:
  - Place it centrally in your home.
  - Keep it elevated (on a shelf rather than the floor).
  - Avoid closets, cabinets, and behind large furniture.
  - Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, and other electronics.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band:
  - 5 GHz (or 6 GHz if available) offers higher speeds but shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz offers better range but lower speeds and more interference.
  - Use 5/6 GHz for nearby devices and 2.4 GHz for distant devices.

- Update your equipment:
  - Install router firmware updates.
  - If your router is several years old, consider upgrading to a Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E model.

- Optimize wireless channels:
  - In crowded areas, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Use a Wi‑Fi analyzer app to find less congested channels.
  - For 2.4 GHz, channels 1, 6, and 11 are usually best.

- Improve security:
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 encryption.
  - Set a strong password.
  - Remove unknown devices from your network.

- Reduce congestion:
  - Disconnect devices you don't use.
  - Use wired Ethernet for gaming PCs, desktop computers, TVs, or other bandwidth-heavy devices.

- Add coverage if needed:
  - For larger homes, use a mesh Wi‑Fi system.
  - A mesh system is usually better than a traditional Wi‑Fi extender for maintaining speed and reliability.

- Check your internet connection:
  - Connect a device directly to the router via Ethernet and run a speed test.
  - If wired speeds are also slow, the issue may be with your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.

- Adjust router settings:
  - Enable Quality of Service (QoS) if available to prioritize important traffic such as video calls or gaming.
  - Ensure the router is using modern standards (Wi‑Fi 5, 6, or newer).
