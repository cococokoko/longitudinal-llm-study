Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cooking comes down to understanding a few key variables: the cut, seasoning, heat, and doneness.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, forgiving to cook.
- New York strip: Good balance of tenderness and beef flavor.
- Filet mignon: Extremely tender but leaner.
- Sirloin: More affordable, flavorful, slightly less tender.

2. Start with quality meat
- Look for good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- Thicker steaks (1–1.5 inches / 2.5–4 cm) are generally easier to cook evenly.

3. Season properly
- Salt generously at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before it goes in the pan.
- Add freshly ground black pepper just before cooking.
- Keep additional seasonings simple until you've mastered the basics.

4. Bring the steak close to room temperature
- About 20–30 minutes on the counter can help promote more even cooking.

5. Use high heat
- Cast-iron pans are excellent for creating a crust.
- Preheat the pan thoroughly before adding oil.
- Use a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.

6. Learn temperature targets
Use an instant-read thermometer for consistency:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well-done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

7. Try the classic pan-sear method
- Pat the steak dry.
- Season well.
- Heat pan until very hot.
- Add oil.
- Sear 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
- During the final minute, add butter, garlic, and herbs (optional), and baste with a spoon.
- Remove when a few degrees below your target temperature.

8. Rest the steak
- Let it rest 5–10 minutes before slicing.
- This helps retain juices.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain to maximize tenderness.

10. Practice deliberately
If your work schedule at the hospital means you're often cooking after long shifts, keep notes on:
- Steak thickness
- Cooking method
- Internal temperature
- Final result

After a few attempts, you'll quickly identify what produces your preferred texture and doneness.

A simple goal for beginners is a 1-inch ribeye cooked in a cast-iron skillet to 130–135°F (54–57°C) internal temperature for a medium-rare finish. It's one of the most reliable ways to build steak-cooking skills.
