# Different Views on Discipleship

Discipleship—the process of following, learning from, and being formed by a teacher or tradition—has been understood in remarkably different ways across traditions and contexts.

---

## Christian Perspectives

### Traditional/Catholic View
- Discipleship as **lifelong formation** in virtue and doctrine
- Structured through sacraments, spiritual direction, and community
- Imitation of Christ as the central model

### Protestant Evangelical View
- Emphasis on **personal relationship with Jesus**
- Bible study and accountability as core practices
- Often focused on **multiplication** — disciples making disciples

### Mainline Protestant View
- Discipleship connected to **social justice and service**
- Formation through worship and community engagement
- Less emphasis on explicit evangelism

### Anabaptist/Radical Reformation View
- Discipleship as **costly following** — separation from worldly values
- Strong community accountability
- Pacifism and simplicity as discipleship markers

---

## Other Religious Traditions

### Jewish (Talmidim)
- Student sits under a rabbi to **absorb their way of life**
- Learning Torah through relationship and practice

### Buddhist
- Following the **Dharma and Sangha**
- Gradual path of practice and mentorship

### Sufi Islam
- Murshid (master) guides the murid (seeker)
- Spiritual transformation through devotion

---

## Key Theological Debates

| Question | Views |
|----------|-------|
| **Who disciples?** | Clergy vs. every believer |
| **What is the goal?** | Knowledge, character, mission, or union with God |
| **How long does it take?** | Program vs. lifelong process |
| **Is it individual or communal?** | Personal faith vs. community formation |
| **Role of suffering** | Optional vs. essential to discipleship |

---

## Contemporary Tensions

- **Consumerism** — treating discipleship as a program to complete
- **Individualism** vs. communal accountability
- **Depth vs. breadth** — forming few deeply or reaching many broadly
- **Cultural relevance** vs. countercultural distinctiveness
