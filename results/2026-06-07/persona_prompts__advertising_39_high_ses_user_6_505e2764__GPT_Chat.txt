── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

How to get started with meditation and mindfulness?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given your role, the biggest challenge is usually not understanding meditation intellectually—it's creating a practice that fits into a demanding schedule and a high-performance environment.

A practical way to get started:

1. Understand the distinction

- Meditation is a formal practice: setting aside time to train attention.
- Mindfulness is the skill of noticing what's happening in the present moment without immediately reacting.
- Meditation develops mindfulness, but mindfulness can also be practiced throughout the day.

2. Start much smaller than you think

Many busy professionals fail because they start with 20–30 minutes daily.

Instead:
- Start with 5 minutes per day.
- Do it at the same time each day (e.g., before opening email or after waking up).
- Focus on consistency, not duration.

A consistent 5-minute practice is more valuable than an occasional 30-minute session.

3. Use a simple breath meditation

For 5 minutes:

- Sit comfortably.
- Set a timer.
- Bring attention to the sensation of breathing.
- When your mind wanders (it will), notice that it wandered.
- Gently return attention to the breath.

The "return" is the practice. The goal is not to stop thinking.

4. Apply mindfulness during the workday

You can build mindfulness without adding much time.

Examples:
- Before a client meeting, take three conscious breaths.
- During conversations, notice when you're preparing your response instead of listening.
- Between meetings, spend 30 seconds observing your surroundings rather than checking your phone.
- While walking, occasionally pay attention to physical sensations rather than planning the next task.

5. Use transitions strategically

Partners often move rapidly between contexts.

A useful practice:
- Before entering a meeting, ask: "What deserves my attention right now?"
- After a meeting, ask: "What outcome from that meeting actually matters?"

These brief pauses can reduce cognitive carryover from one activity to the next.

6. Expect common obstacles

"I can't stop thinking."
- Normal. Meditation is attention training, not thought elimination.

"I don't have time."
- Five minutes is sufficient to start.

"It feels unproductive."
- Many executives initially experience meditation as inefficient because it doesn't generate immediate output. The benefits tend to appear as improved attention, emotional regulation, listening, and decision quality over time.

7. Consider guided resources

Widely used options include:
- Waking Up (Sam Harris)
- Headspace
- Calm
- Ten Percent Happier

Guided meditation is often easier than practicing alone at the beginning.

8. Track one outcome

Rather than asking "Am I meditating enough?", track:
- Focus during important work
- Quality of listening
- Stress reactivity
- Sleep quality
- Ability to recover after difficult interactions

These are often more meaningful indicators than meditation minutes.

A simple starter plan:

Week 1:
- 5 minutes of breath meditation each morning.

Week 2:
- Continue 5 minutes.
- Add three mindful breaths before major meetings.

Week 3:
- Increase to 8–10 minutes if it feels sustainable.

Week 4:
- Keep the formal practice.
- Add one mindful walk or commute each week.

For many senior leaders, the most valuable result is not becoming calmer all the time. It's developing the ability to notice when attention, emotions, or assumptions are pulling them off course and regaining choice more quickly.
