# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose a hospital patient care aide's **weekly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **x** = regular hours worked
- **y** = overtime hours worked

The earnings function is:

$$E(x, y) = 15x + 22y + 0.5xy$$

*(The 0.5xy term reflects a small bonus when both regular and overtime hours are high)*

---

## Finding the Partial Derivatives

### ∂E/∂x (rate of change with respect to regular hours)

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial x} = 15 + 0.5y$$

*Treat y as a constant, differentiate with respect to x*

### ∂E/∂y (rate of change with respect to overtime hours)

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial y} = 22 + 0.5x$$

*Treat x as a constant, differentiate with respect to y*

---

## Numerical Evaluation

Suppose you worked **x = 36 regular hours** and **y = 8 overtime hours**:

| Partial Derivative | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| ∂E/∂x | 15 + 0.5(8) | **$19/hour** |
| ∂E/∂y | 22 + 0.5(36) | **$40/hour** |

---

## Interpretation

- **∂E/∂x = 19** → Each additional regular hour adds **$19** to earnings (holding overtime fixed at 8)
- **∂E/∂y = 40** → Each additional overtime hour adds **$40** to earnings (holding regular hours fixed at 36)

> The key idea: a partial derivative measures the effect of **one variable at a time**, while holding all others **constant**.
