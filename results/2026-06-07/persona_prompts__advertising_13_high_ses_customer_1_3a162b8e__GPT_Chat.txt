Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few core principles: the quality and cut of the meat, temperature control, seasoning, and resting.

Key cuts and their characteristics:
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, forgiving to cook.
- New York strip: Firm texture, strong beef flavor.
- Filet mignon: Extremely tender, leaner, milder flavor.
- T-bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip and tenderloin separated by bone.

Preparation:
- Choose steaks at least 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick for easier control.
- Pat the surface dry with paper towels. Moisture inhibits browning.
- Salt generously 40 minutes to 24 hours before cooking if possible. This dry-brining improves seasoning and surface browning.
- Bring the steak out of the refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking if convenient, though this is less important than often claimed.

Cooking methods:
- Pan-searing:
  - Use a heavy skillet, ideally cast iron or carbon steel.
  - Heat until very hot.
  - Add a high-smoke-point oil.
  - Sear each side until a deep brown crust develops.
  - Add butter, garlic, and herbs near the end and baste.

- Grilling:
  - Create two heat zones: one hot, one moderate.
  - Sear over high heat, then finish over lower heat if needed.

- Reverse sear:
  - Cook gently in a low oven (around 225–275°F / 107–135°C) until just below target temperature.
  - Finish with a very hot sear.
  - Particularly effective for thick steaks because it promotes even doneness.

Target internal temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

Use an instant-read thermometer. It is the most reliable way to achieve consistent results.

Resting:
- Rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This allows temperature equalization and reduces juice loss when slicing.

Common mistakes:
- Cooking by time alone instead of temperature.
- Using a cold pan that cannot develop a proper crust.
- Constantly pressing on the steak, which can expel juices.
- Overseasoning with complex rubs that mask the beef's flavor.
- Skipping the thermometer while trying to learn consistency.

Advanced considerations:
- Dry-aged beef develops concentrated flavor through controlled aging.
- Different fat distributions affect heat transfer and perceived tenderness.
- Surface browning is driven by Maillard reactions, which create hundreds of flavor compounds when proteins and sugars react at high temperatures.

A practical path to mastery is to repeatedly cook the same cut, thickness, and method while tracking internal temperatures and outcomes. Consistency makes it easier to recognize how small changes in technique affect texture, flavor, and doneness.
