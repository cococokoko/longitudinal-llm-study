── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some key considerations:

## Arguments that complete knowledge is impossible

- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest any sufficiently complex formal system contains true statements that can't be proven within that system
- **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** places fundamental limits on simultaneously knowing certain physical properties
- **The knower problem** - knowing everything would include knowing your own mental states completely, which may be self-defeating
- **Infinite information** - if reality contains infinite facts, a finite mind couldn't contain them all
- **The problem of indexical knowledge** - knowing "what it's like" to be something may require being that thing

## Reasons to be cautious about strong claims

- We might be wrong about what's fundamentally possible
- "Everything" needs careful definition - everything knowable vs. everything that exists
- Some limitations might be practical rather than absolute
- Different types of knowledge (propositional, experiential, procedural) complicate the question

## Honest assessment

There are **strong theoretical reasons** to think complete omniscience faces genuine logical and physical barriers, not just practical ones. Most philosophers and scientists take these limits seriously.

But claiming to **know with certainty** that omniscience is impossible is itself a strong claim that deserves scrutiny.
