Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive often requires a different approach than generic wellness advice. The challenge is usually not a lack of resilience, but sustained exposure to uncertainty, high-stakes decisions, constant communication, and responsibility for outcomes that affect many people.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Separate pressure from anxiety.
  - Pressure comes from real demands and deadlines.
  - Anxiety often comes from uncertainty, anticipation, or mentally rehearsing worst-case scenarios.
  - Ask: "What specifically requires action today?" This can help distinguish actionable problems from mental projections.

- Reduce decision fatigue.
  - Standardize routine decisions where possible.
  - Reserve your highest-energy hours for the most consequential decisions.
  - Delegate decisions that do not require your unique judgment.

- Create structured thinking time.
  - Many executives spend their calendars reacting to inputs.
  - Consider protecting recurring blocks for strategic thinking, reflection, or planning.
  - Anxiety often increases when there is no dedicated time to process complex issues.

- Manage information intake.
  - Continuous exposure to messages, dashboards, news, and internal updates can keep your nervous system in a state of constant activation.
  - Design specific times for email, messaging platforms, and status reviews rather than remaining continuously available.

- Identify recurring stress triggers.
  - Examples:
    - Board meetings
    - Organizational change
    - Performance concerns
    - Public visibility
    - Conflict with peers
  - Once identified, prepare specific response plans rather than treating all stress as one problem.

- Maintain recovery practices that are non-negotiable.
  - Sleep is usually the highest-leverage intervention.
  - Regular exercise can significantly reduce anxiety symptoms.
  - Time with family, friends, or activities unrelated to work helps prevent your identity from becoming fully tied to company outcomes.

- Build a confidential support system.
  - Executive coaches, therapists, trusted peers, or mentors can provide perspective.
  - Senior leaders are often expected to project certainty, which can make it harder to process concerns openly.

- Watch for signs that stress is becoming chronic.
  - Persistent irritability
  - Sleep disruption
  - Difficulty concentrating
  - Increased alcohol or substance use
  - Loss of motivation
  - Physical symptoms such as headaches, digestive issues, or elevated blood pressure

If your stress or anxiety is significantly affecting sleep, health, relationships, or decision-making, speaking with a licensed mental health professional can be very valuable. Many high-performing leaders find that targeted therapy or executive-focused counseling improves both well-being and effectiveness.
