Stress is the body’s natural response to perceived challenges or threats. When a person encounters a stressful situation, the brain activates the sympathetic nervous system and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. This leads to the release of hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol, which increase heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose levels, helping the body respond quickly.

In the short term, stress can be beneficial. It may improve alertness, focus, and reaction time, enabling individuals to cope with demanding situations. However, when stress becomes chronic, it can have significant effects on both physical and mental health.

Prolonged elevation of stress hormones can contribute to cardiovascular disease, weakened immune function, sleep disturbances, digestive problems, and metabolic disorders. Chronic stress is also associated with muscle tension, headaches, and increased inflammation throughout the body.

The mind is similarly affected. Persistent stress can impair concentration, memory, and decision-making. It may increase the risk of anxiety, depression, irritability, and emotional exhaustion. Over time, chronic stress can reduce overall well-being and quality of life.

Managing stress through regular exercise, adequate sleep, social support, relaxation techniques, and healthy coping strategies can help protect both physical and mental health. Understanding the effects of stress is important for maintaining long-term resilience and wellness.
