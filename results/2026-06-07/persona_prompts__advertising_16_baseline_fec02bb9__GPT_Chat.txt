For immediate stress or anxiety:
- Slow your breathing. Try inhaling for 4 seconds, exhaling for 6 seconds, and repeating for a few minutes.
- Ground yourself in the present. Notice:
  - 5 things you can see
  - 4 things you can feel
  - 3 things you can hear
  - 2 things you can smell
  - 1 thing you can taste
- Reduce stimulation if possible by stepping away from overwhelming environments, news, or social media for a short period.
- Engage in physical movement, such as a brief walk, stretching, or light exercise.

For ongoing stress management:
- Maintain a regular sleep schedule.
- Exercise consistently, even if it's just 15–30 minutes most days.
- Eat regular, balanced meals and stay hydrated.
- Set realistic priorities and break large tasks into smaller steps.
- Build time for activities you enjoy and for social connection.
- Practice mindfulness, meditation, journaling, or relaxation exercises.

Helpful ways to think about anxiety:
- Anxiety often involves predicting threats or worst-case scenarios. It can help to ask:
  - What evidence supports this worry?
  - What evidence does not support it?
  - What is the most likely outcome?
  - If the difficult outcome happened, how would I cope?
- Try to distinguish between problems you can act on now and worries that are currently outside your control.

When to seek additional support:
- If stress or anxiety is persistent, feels overwhelming, interferes with work, school, relationships, sleep, or daily functioning, consider speaking with a mental health professional.
- Therapies such as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) have strong evidence for helping anxiety.
