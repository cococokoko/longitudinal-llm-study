Running a marathon is as much about consistent training and recovery as it is about race-day effort. Since you work long hours in a warehouse and are already on your feet a lot, managing fatigue will be especially important.

Some key tips:

- Build mileage gradually
  - Increase weekly running volume slowly (often no more than about 10% per week).
  - Aim for a structured training plan that lasts 12–20 weeks, depending on your experience.

- Prioritize long runs
  - Do one long run each week to prepare your body for marathon distance.
  - Gradually extend the long run over time rather than making large jumps.

- Don't run hard every day
  - Most training runs should feel comfortable and conversational.
  - Include occasional speed work or tempo runs, but keep easy days easy.

- Account for your physically demanding job
  - Time on your feet at work adds to your overall fatigue.
  - If you're exhausted after a particularly demanding shift, it may be smarter to shorten, move, or skip a workout rather than force it.

- Focus on recovery
  - Sleep is one of the biggest performance enhancers.
  - Stay hydrated throughout the day, especially during long shifts and training days.
  - Consider light stretching or mobility work if your legs feel stiff from work.

- Practice race-day nutrition
  - Use training runs to test gels, sports drinks, and hydration strategies.
  - Never try new nutrition products for the first time on race day.

- Strength training helps
  - Even 1–2 short sessions per week can improve durability.
  - Focus on core, glutes, hamstrings, calves, and overall leg strength.

- Invest in proper shoes
  - Get fitted for running shoes that match your gait and comfort needs.
  - Replace them periodically as they wear out.

- Learn pacing
  - Starting too fast is one of the most common marathon mistakes.
  - Practice running at your intended marathon pace during training.

- Taper before the race
  - Reduce training volume in the final few weeks while maintaining some intensity.
  - Arrive at the starting line rested rather than exhausted.

If this is your first marathon, a common goal is simply to finish comfortably rather than chase a specific time. Consistency over several months is usually more important than any single workout.
