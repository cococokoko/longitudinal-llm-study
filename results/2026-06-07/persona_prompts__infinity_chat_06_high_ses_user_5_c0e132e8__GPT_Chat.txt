── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Many successful executives spend years optimizing for goals without regularly asking whether those goals are actually theirs. The difficulty is that "what do I want?" is often obscured by incentives, expectations, habits, and identity.

A useful distinction is:

- What do I want because it is rewarded?
- What do I want because it is admired?
- What do I want because I genuinely value it?

Some approaches that can help:

- Examine moments of envy.
  - Envy is often a signal of desire.
  - When you find yourself thinking about someone else's life, achievement, freedom, or influence, ask: "What exactly am I envious of?"
  - It is often not the whole package. It may be autonomy, creativity, recognition, time, or impact.

- Follow your attention.
  - Notice what topics, problems, and possibilities you return to when nobody requires you to.
  - Attention is frequently a more reliable indicator than stated goals.

- Distinguish goals from experiences.
  - Many people can name what they want to achieve but not how they want to live.
  - Ask:
    - What kind of days do I want?
    - What kinds of conversations do I want?
    - What problems do I want to spend my time solving?

- Run the "already successful" thought experiment.
  - Imagine you have already achieved your current professional ambitions.
  - You have the title, wealth, reputation, and security.
  - What would you do next?
  - The answers often reveal desires that are currently hidden behind ambition.

- Look for recurring regrets.
  - Regret is information.
  - Not just mistakes, but opportunities repeatedly deferred.
  - Patterns matter more than isolated incidents.

- Separate wanting from obligation.
  - Write two lists:
    - Things I genuinely want.
    - Things I feel I should want.
  - The exercise is often surprisingly clarifying.

- Pay attention to energy rather than performance.
  - Executives are often good at many things.
  - Being good at something does not mean you want to keep doing it.
  - Ask: "What activities leave me with more energy than they consume?"

A deeper question is whether you're trying to identify a fixed desire or make a choice among competing desires.

Most people do not discover a single authentic answer hidden inside themselves. They observe their reactions, make commitments, and gradually learn what matters through action.

One question I find especially revealing for people in senior leadership is:

- If nobody would ever know about the choice, and it would have no effect on status, compensation, or reputation, what would I choose?

The gap between that answer and your current trajectory often contains important information.

What prompted the question for you? Are you trying to make a specific decision, or are you feeling uncertain about the broader direction of your life and career?
