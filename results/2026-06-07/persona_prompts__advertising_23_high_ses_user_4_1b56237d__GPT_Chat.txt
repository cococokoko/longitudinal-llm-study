── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have one advantage: you're accustomed to learning complex skills through systems, iteration, and evidence. Cooking can be approached the same way.

The fastest path is usually not learning recipes first; it's learning a small set of techniques and meal templates that can be repeated.

A practical approach:

- Learn 10–15 foundational meals rather than hundreds of recipes.
- Master a few cooking methods:
  - Sheet-pan roasting
  - Stir-frying
  - Sautéing
  - Grilling or broiling
  - Pressure cooking or slow cooking
  - Basic grain preparation (rice, quinoa, farro)
- Build meals from a template:
  - Protein
  - Vegetables
  - Whole-grain or legume carbohydrate
  - Flavor source (herbs, spices, sauce)

Examples:
- Salmon + roasted broccoli + quinoa
- Chicken stir-fry + mixed vegetables + brown rice
- Lentils + roasted vegetables + yogurt sauce
- Tofu + bok choy + soba noodles

Focus on efficiency:

- Batch-cook proteins once or twice weekly.
- Wash and chop vegetables in advance.
- Keep frozen vegetables available.
- Use appliances that reduce active cooking time:
  - Rice cooker
  - Instant Pot
  - Air fryer
  - Sheet pans

Develop a minimal pantry:

- Olive oil
- Vinegar (balsamic, rice, or red wine)
- Garlic
- Onions
- Lemons
- Soy sauce or tamari
- Dijon mustard
- Canned beans
- Whole grains
- Basic spices (black pepper, paprika, cumin, chili powder)

A useful learning strategy:

- Cook the same meal several times before moving on.
- Change one variable at a time:
  - Different protein
  - Different vegetable
  - Different seasoning
- This builds intuition much faster than constantly trying new recipes.

If you like evidence-based learning, treat cooking as deliberate practice:

- Choose one technique each week.
- Make 3–4 meals using that technique.
- Keep brief notes:
  - Cooking time
  - What worked
  - What you'd change

Resources that teach technique rather than recipes:

- J. Kenji López-Alt's books and videos
- America's Test Kitchen
- Serious Eats
- Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat (Samin Nosrat)

For someone with a demanding academic schedule, a realistic goal is:
- 5–10 healthy meals you can prepare in under 30 minutes,
- 2–3 meals that can be batch-cooked for the week,
- enough technique knowledge to improvise from ingredients on hand.

That level of competence provides most of the health, cost, and convenience benefits of cooking without requiring a major time investment.
