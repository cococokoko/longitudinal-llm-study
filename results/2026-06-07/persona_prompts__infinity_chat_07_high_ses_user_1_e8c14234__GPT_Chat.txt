── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any meaningful sense, it is impossible for a human being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- Physical limits:
  - A human brain has finite storage, finite processing speed, and a finite lifespan.
  - The amount of information in the universe appears vastly greater than what any individual could acquire and retain.

- Epistemic limits:
  - Knowledge is not just information; it requires justification, understanding, and often the ability to apply what is known.
  - Many facts are inaccessible to any given observer because of limitations of location, time, and measurement.

- Scientific limits:
  - Some phenomena may be fundamentally unknowable with complete precision. For example, quantum mechanics places limits on simultaneous measurements of certain properties.
  - There may be truths that cannot be derived from a given formal system (as suggested by Gödel's incompleteness theorems in mathematics).

- The moving target problem:
  - New information is constantly being generated. Even if someone somehow mastered all current human knowledge, the body of knowledge would continue to expand.
  - Events occurring elsewhere in the universe create new facts continuously.

- Logical considerations:
  - If "everything" includes every true proposition about the future, there are deep philosophical questions about whether such knowledge is even coherent or available.

For a neurosurgeon, there is an additional practical observation: the more expertise one gains, the more visible the boundaries of one's knowledge become. Deep specialization often increases awareness of complexity rather than creating a sense of completeness.

A more interesting question may be whether it is possible to know "enough." In medicine, expertise is usually not the accumulation of all knowledge but the development of reliable judgment under conditions of incomplete knowledge.
