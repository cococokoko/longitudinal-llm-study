# Tip: Create Structured but Brief Regular Check-ins

**One-on-one meetings** with each team member (even 15-20 minutes biweekly) can be remarkably effective because they:

- Surface problems **before they escalate**
- Give trainees/staff dedicated time to raise concerns without competing for your attention
- Let you track progress on individual projects more accurately than group meetings
- Build trust and psychological safety

## Practical suggestion for a research setting:

Have team members **come prepared with a brief agenda** - for example:
- What's going well
- What's blocked or frustrating
- What they need from you specifically

This respects your time while making the meeting genuinely useful rather than a status report you could have received by email.

---

**Why this matters particularly for physician-scientists:** Your time is likely split between clinic, lab, grants, and administration. Without structured touchpoints, team members may hesitate to interrupt you, leading to **silent problems** - stalled experiments, interpersonal conflicts, or trainees who are struggling but not asking for help.

The small time investment typically **saves time overall** by reducing miscommunication and keeping projects on track.
