# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Sundqvist has crafted something genuinely rare — a film that treats scientific uncertainty as dramatic tension rather than obstacle.

Cate Blanchett plays Elena Voss, a molecular epidemiologist whose landmark paper on prion transmission begins unraveling the same week her clinical trial enters its final phase. The film resists the obvious villain arc. Nobody committed fraud. The data was just *wrong*, in that quiet, devastating way data sometimes is.

What makes *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* extraordinary is its patience with process. A 14-minute sequence showing Voss and her postdoc (a revelatory performance from Ke Huy Quan) re-running Western blots at 2am is somehow the most suspenseful scene of the year. Sundqvist understands that the real horror isn't being wrong — it's the specific moment you *know* you're wrong and haven't yet told anyone.

The film stumbles slightly in its third act, leaning on a funding-board confrontation that feels borrowed from lesser films about institutional pressure.

But the final image — Voss submitting a correction letter, alone, expression unreadable — lands with quiet devastation.

A film about the dignity of rigor. Long overdue.

*Rated PG-13. 127 minutes.*
