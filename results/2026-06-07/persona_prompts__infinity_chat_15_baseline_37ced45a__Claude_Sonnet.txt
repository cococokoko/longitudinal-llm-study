── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Power asymmetries**
- Wealthy states have greater leverage in negotiations, institutions, and coercive capacity
- Economic dependence creates political vulnerability for poorer states
- This can produce genuine cooperation or exploitation depending on circumstances

**Competing theoretical interpretations**
- *Liberal institutionalists* emphasize how trade interdependence reduces conflict incentives
- *Dependency theorists* argue global structures systematically disadvantage developing nations
- *Realists* see economic power as simply another instrument of state competition
- Each captures something real while missing other dynamics

## Specific Tensions Worth Examining

- **Trade negotiations**: Wealthy states often shape WTO rules favorably while demanding openness from others
- **Debt relationships**: IMF/World Bank conditionality genuinely constrains sovereignty
- **Climate negotiations**: Historical emitters vs. current developing economies creates real legitimacy conflicts
- **Migration pressures**: Economic inequality drives movement that creates political friction

## Where Honest Uncertainty Exists

- Whether inequality *causes* conflict or whether other factors explain both
- Whether current institutions primarily help or harm developing nations
- How much agency poorer states actually exercise vs. structural constraints

## What's Often Oversimplified

The relationship isn't simply "rich exploit poor" - there are genuine mutual dependencies, successful development stories, and cases where poorer states exercise significant leverage.
