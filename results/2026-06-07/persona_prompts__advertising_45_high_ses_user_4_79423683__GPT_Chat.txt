── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many skills that transfer well to DIY: breaking complex problems into components, following procedures, troubleshooting, and learning from reference materials. The main challenge is developing practical experience, tool familiarity, and judgment about safety.

A structured approach works best:

- Start with a home-systems overview
  - Learn the basics of:
    - Electrical systems
    - Plumbing
    - HVAC
    - Drywall and painting
    - Carpentry
    - Roofing and exterior maintenance
  - This gives context before attempting repairs.

- Use high-quality learning resources
  - Books:
    - Home Improvement 1-2-3 (The Home Depot)
    - Black & Decker Complete Guide series (Electrical, Plumbing, Remodeling, etc.)
    - Reader's Digest Complete Do-It-Yourself Manual
  - Video channels:
    - This Old House
    - See Jane Drill
    - Everyday Home Repairs
    - Home RenoVision DIY
  - Manufacturer installation manuals are often more accurate than general tutorials.

- Build skills in a deliberate sequence
  - Beginner:
    - Hanging shelves
    - Caulking
    - Replacing faucets and showerheads
    - Patching drywall holes
    - Painting
    - Replacing door hardware
  - Intermediate:
    - Installing light fixtures
    - Replacing outlets and switches
    - Minor trim carpentry
    - Toilet replacement
    - Ceiling fan installation
  - Advanced:
    - New electrical circuits
    - Major plumbing alterations
    - Structural modifications
    - Roofing
    - Large-scale renovations

- Learn tools systematically
  - Start with:
    - Tape measure
    - Drill/driver
    - Stud finder
    - Level
    - Utility knife
    - Adjustable wrench
    - Pliers
    - Socket set
    - Multimeter
  - Learn what each tool is for before buying specialized equipment.

- Create a "practice environment"
  - Buy scrap lumber and drywall.
  - Practice:
    - Drilling
    - Screwing
    - Cutting
    - Drywall patching
    - Painting
  - This reduces the pressure of learning on your actual home.

- Treat projects like experiments
  - Before starting:
    - Define the problem.
    - Review multiple sources.
    - Identify failure modes.
    - Estimate cost and time.
    - Develop a contingency plan.
  - This mirrors scientific methodology and often accelerates learning.

- Understand safety boundaries
  - Learn:
    - Electrical lockout and circuit verification
    - Ladder safety
    - PPE use
    - Water shutoff procedures
    - Gas shutoff procedures
  - Many DIY failures are actually safety failures rather than technical failures.

- Know when to hire professionals
  - Consider outsourcing:
    - Structural work
    - Service-panel electrical work
    - Gas line work
    - Complex HVAC repairs
    - Projects requiring permits if you're unfamiliar with local code requirements
  - Even if you hire out these jobs, understanding the systems helps you supervise and evaluate the work.

- Learn from your own home
  - Create a maintenance log.
  - Document:
    - Appliance ages
    - Filter replacement schedules
    - Paint colors
    - Shutoff locations
    - Previous repairs
  - Routine maintenance teaches home systems with lower risk than major repairs.

A particularly effective path for a busy professional is to spend 20–30 hours on foundational learning, then complete one small project every month. After a year, most homeowners become competent at handling the majority of routine repairs and improvements without professional assistance.
