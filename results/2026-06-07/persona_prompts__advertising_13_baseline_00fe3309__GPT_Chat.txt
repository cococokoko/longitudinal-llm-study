── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding a few key principles: choosing the right cut, controlling temperature, seasoning properly, and knowing when the steak is done.

1. Choose the Right Cut
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, and forgiving.
- New York Strip: Firm texture with a good balance of flavor and tenderness.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender but less flavorful due to lower fat content.
- Sirloin: Leaner and often more affordable.
- Flank or Skirt Steak: Best cooked quickly over high heat and sliced against the grain.

2. Start with Quality Meat
- Look for good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- Thicker steaks (1–1.5 inches / 2.5–4 cm) are easier to cook evenly.
- If possible, choose USDA Choice or Prime (or equivalent grading in your region).

3. Bring the Steak to Room Temperature (Optional)
- Many cooks let steak sit out for 20–30 minutes before cooking.
- More important than room temperature is ensuring the surface is dry.

4. Season Generously
- Salt the steak well.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.
- For deeper flavor, salt the steak 1–24 hours ahead and refrigerate uncovered (dry brining).

5. Get the Pan or Grill Hot
- Use a cast-iron skillet, stainless steel pan, or a hot grill.
- High heat helps create a flavorful crust through the Maillard reaction.
- Pat the steak dry before cooking.

6. Cooking Method (Pan-Seared Example)
- Heat a heavy pan until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, grapeseed).
- Place steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.
- Sear for 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs near the end if desired, and baste the steak.

7. Use a Thermometer
Internal temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well-done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

For most premium steaks, medium-rare is often preferred.

8. Rest Before Slicing
- Rest the steak for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps juices redistribute throughout the meat.

9. Slice Correctly
- For cuts with noticeable muscle fibers (flank, skirt, sirloin), slice against the grain.
- This shortens muscle fibers and makes the steak easier to chew.

Common Mistakes
- Cooking cold, wet steak without drying the surface.
- Using insufficient heat and failing to develop a crust.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak before a crust forms.
- Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.
- Relying only on cooking time rather than internal temperature.

Practice Exercise
Cook the same cut (such as ribeye or strip steak) three times:
- First to medium-rare.
- Second to medium.
- Third using a reverse-sear method (slow oven, then hot sear).

Comparing the results will teach you more about steak cookery than memorizing cooking times.
