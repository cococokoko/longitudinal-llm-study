── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at an established law firm, a strong social media personal brand should reinforce three things simultaneously:

- Your professional credibility
- Your distinctive expertise and perspective
- Trustworthiness and professionalism consistent with legal ethics

A useful framework is to position yourself at the intersection of:
- What you are known for
- What clients need to understand
- What colleagues and referral sources respect

Key steps

1. Define your positioning

Avoid trying to be "a lawyer who posts online." Instead, become known for a specific area.

Examples:
- Corporate attorney explaining complex transactions in plain language
- Employment attorney analyzing workplace legal developments
- Litigation attorney discussing risk management and dispute strategy
- Private wealth attorney educating business owners on succession planning

Ask:
- What matters do I want more of?
- What clients do I want to attract?
- What topics can I discuss consistently for years?

2. Choose a primary audience

Your content should primarily target one group:
- Prospective clients
- Existing clients
- Referral sources
- Executives and business owners
- In-house counsel
- Other lawyers

A common mistake is trying to speak to everyone.

3. Focus on education, not self-promotion

The most effective legal brands teach.

Content ideas:
- Recent legal developments and practical implications
- Common mistakes clients make
- Risk management guidance
- Frequently asked questions
- Industry trends
- Case law analysis (without giving legal advice)
- Lessons learned from years of practice

A simple format:
- What happened?
- Why does it matter?
- What should businesses or individuals consider?

4. Showcase judgment, not just knowledge

Many lawyers demonstrate technical expertise. Fewer demonstrate judgment.

Share:
- Strategic considerations
- Decision-making frameworks
- Business implications of legal issues
- Leadership lessons from practice
- Professional observations from years in the field

This is often what differentiates senior attorneys from junior practitioners.

5. Maintain ethical and professional boundaries

Always:
- Avoid specific legal advice
- Protect client confidentiality
- Include appropriate disclaimers when necessary
- Follow firm policies
- Follow applicable bar advertising and solicitation rules

6. Build visibility through consistency

A practical schedule:
- 2–3 substantive posts per week
- 1 commentary post on a legal or industry development
- 1 professional insight or leadership post
- Meaningful engagement with others in your network

Consistency generally matters more than volume.

7. Use LinkedIn as the foundation

For most senior attorneys, LinkedIn is typically the highest-value platform because:
- Decision-makers are present
- Professional content performs well
- Referral sources are active
- Media and speaking opportunities often originate there

Other platforms can be useful depending on your practice area, but LinkedIn is usually the strongest starting point.

8. Develop signature content themes

Choose 3–5 recurring pillars.

Example for a corporate attorney:
- M&A insights
- Corporate governance
- Executive decision-making
- Business risk management
- Leadership and professional development

This creates a recognizable brand rather than a collection of unrelated posts.

9. Leverage your existing reputation

You likely already have:
- Significant client experience
- Speaking engagements
- Articles
- CLE presentations
- Industry involvement

Repurpose these assets into shorter social content instead of creating everything from scratch.

10. Measure the right outcomes

Focus less on follower count and more on:
- Quality of connections
- Referral inquiries
- Speaking invitations
- Media requests
- Client conversations
- Opportunities generated

For a senior attorney, a highly respected audience of 2,000 relevant professionals can be more valuable than 100,000 general followers.
