Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The answer depends on what is meant by "equal pay for equal work."

If it means that two people doing the same job, with the same productivity, qualifications, experience, responsibilities, and performance should be paid the same regardless of characteristics such as sex or race, many economists and policymakers argue that this can improve efficiency as well as fairness. Discrimination can cause talent to be misallocated and reduce economic output.

Arguments against imposing a strict legal version of equal pay usually focus on practical and economic concerns:

- Measuring "equal work" is difficult.
  - Jobs that appear identical may differ in productivity, experience, reliability, working hours, risk, location, or other factors.
  - Employers may need flexibility to reward performance and scarce skills.

- Compliance costs.
  - Firms may need extensive record-keeping, audits, and legal reviews.
  - Smaller businesses can face a disproportionate administrative burden.

- Reduced flexibility in wage-setting.
  - Employers often use pay differences to attract workers with specific skills or to fill hard-to-staff positions.
  - Strict rules can make it harder to respond to changing market conditions.

- Increased legal disputes.
  - Determining whether two jobs are truly equivalent can lead to costly litigation.

- Potential effects on hiring.
  - If employers fear legal challenges over pay decisions, they may become more cautious in hiring or promotion decisions.

On the other hand, supporters argue that:

- It reduces discrimination.
- It can increase labor-force participation.
- It may improve morale and retention.
- It can lead to a more efficient use of talent.

So the economic debate is usually not about whether discrimination is desirable. Rather, it is about whether the benefits of enforcing equal-pay rules outweigh the costs and how precisely such rules can be designed and implemented. Most modern economies try to strike a balance: prohibiting unjustified pay discrimination while still allowing wage differences based on legitimate factors such as performance, experience, skills, or working conditions.
