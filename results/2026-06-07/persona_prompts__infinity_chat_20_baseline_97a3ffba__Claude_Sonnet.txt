── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth thinking through carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## Actually, the premise deserves scrutiny

**Arguments people make against mandating equal pay:**
- Measurement problems - defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult
- Compliance costs and administrative burden
- May reduce hiring of protected groups if employers fear litigation
- Wages reflect negotiation, experience, and other factors beyond job title
- Market competition theoretically should already punish discriminatory employers

## But strong counterarguments exist:

**The case FOR equal pay requirements:**
- Markets demonstrably haven't eliminated pay gaps after decades
- Information asymmetries prevent workers from knowing they're underpaid
- Discrimination persists even controlling for measurable factors
- Power imbalances in wage negotiation favor employers
- Historical exclusion created compounding disadvantages
- Evidence from some countries suggests mandates reduce gaps without major economic disruption

## What the evidence actually suggests:
- Pay transparency laws show measurable positive effects
- Some equal pay legislation has reduced gaps
- Economic costs appear modest in practice

## My honest assessment:
The framing "not worth imposing" assumes market outcomes are inherently more legitimate than policy corrections, which is itself a contestable value judgment rather than an economic fact.
