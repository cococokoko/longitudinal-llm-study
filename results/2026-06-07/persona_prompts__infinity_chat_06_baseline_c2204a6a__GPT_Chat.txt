── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experience.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice envy and admiration.
  - When you feel jealous of someone, ask: "What specifically do I wish I had?" Often envy points toward desires that haven't been fully acknowledged.
  - Similarly, notice who you admire and why.

- Pay attention to energy.
  - Which activities leave you feeling engaged, curious, or satisfied afterward?
  - Which ones consistently drain you, even when you're good at them?
  - What you want is not always what you're best at.

- Distinguish desires from expectations.
  - Ask:
    - "If nobody judged me, what would I choose?"
    - "If I couldn't post about it or get recognition for it, would I still want it?"
  - Sometimes we pursue what we think we should want rather than what we actually want.

- Look at recurring themes.
  - Desires often repeat over years in different forms.
  - Review old interests, daydreams, journal entries, or goals. What keeps coming back?

- Experiment instead of overthinking.
  - Many people try to think their way into certainty.
  - Often it's more effective to run small experiments:
    - Take a class.
    - Volunteer.
    - Try a side project.
    - Talk to people living the life you're considering.
  - Action provides information that reflection alone can't.

- Separate means from ends.
  - For example:
    - You may think you want a particular job, but what you really want is autonomy.
    - You may think you want money, but what you really want is security.
    - You may think you want recognition, but what you really want is belonging.
  - Keep asking, "What would that give me?" until you reach the deeper desire.

- Accept that wants can be unclear and change.
  - You don't need a perfectly defined life purpose to move forward.
  - Sometimes the next meaningful step becomes visible before the long-term destination does.

A useful question is: "When have I felt most alive, absorbed, or proud of how I spent my time?" The answers often reveal more than asking, "What do I want?" in the abstract.
