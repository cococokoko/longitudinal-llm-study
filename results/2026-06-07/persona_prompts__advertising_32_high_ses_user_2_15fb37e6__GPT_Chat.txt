── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality is usually most effective when approached in this order: control pollution sources, improve ventilation, and clean the air.

Key strategies:

1. Eliminate or reduce pollution sources
- Do not allow smoking or vaping indoors.
- Minimize use of products that emit volatile organic compounds (VOCs), such as some paints, solvents, air fresheners, and cleaning products.
- Choose low-VOC paints, finishes, furniture, and building materials when possible.
- Store chemicals, fuels, and pesticides outside living or working areas.
- Ensure combustion appliances (gas stoves, furnaces, fireplaces, water heaters) are properly installed, vented, and maintained.
- Address mold and moisture problems promptly.

2. Control moisture and humidity
- Keep indoor relative humidity generally between 30% and 50%.
- Repair leaks in roofs, plumbing, and windows.
- Use exhaust fans in bathrooms and kitchens.
- Dehumidify damp basements or other moisture-prone areas.
- Dry water-damaged materials quickly to prevent mold growth.

3. Increase ventilation
- Open windows and doors when outdoor air quality and weather permit.
- Use mechanical ventilation systems that bring in outdoor air.
- Run kitchen range hoods vented to the outside while cooking.
- Use bathroom exhaust fans during and after showers.
- Verify that HVAC systems are bringing in appropriate amounts of fresh air.

4. Improve air filtration
- Use high-quality HVAC filters and replace them according to manufacturer recommendations.
- For many systems, MERV 13 filters (or the highest rating the system can handle) provide substantial improvement in particle removal.
- Consider portable HEPA air purifiers in occupied rooms, especially bedrooms, conference rooms, or areas with allergy sufferers.
- Ensure air purifiers are appropriately sized for the space.

5. Reduce dust and allergens
- Vacuum regularly using a vacuum with a HEPA filter.
- Dust surfaces with damp cloths rather than dry dusting.
- Wash bedding frequently in hot water.
- Reduce clutter that collects dust.
- Consider removing or cleaning carpets and rugs if they harbor allergens.

6. Address specific contaminants
- Test for radon in areas where it is a known risk.
- Install carbon monoxide detectors.
- If concerned about lead, asbestos, or other hazardous materials in older buildings, seek professional assessment before renovation or disturbance.

7. Maintain HVAC systems
- Schedule regular inspection and maintenance.
- Clean drain pans and address standing water.
- Ensure ducts are in good condition and not introducing contaminants.
- Verify proper airflow throughout the building.

8. Monitor air quality
- Consider indoor air quality monitors that track particulate matter (PM2.5), carbon dioxide (CO2), humidity, and sometimes VOCs.
- Elevated CO2 levels can indicate insufficient ventilation in occupied spaces.

For most homes and offices, the highest-impact combination is:
- Source control (especially smoking, moisture, and chemical emissions),
- Adequate ventilation,
- MERV 13 HVAC filtration or portable HEPA air cleaners,
- Humidity control.

If your question is focused on a law office or commercial workplace, there are additional building-management considerations that may be relevant.
