As a tenured professor, you have several advantages that many creators do not: deep subject expertise, credibility, access to current research, and years of experience explaining complex ideas.

To build a successful YouTube channel, focus on the intersection of:
- What you know exceptionally well
- What a broad audience is curious about
- What you can consistently create

A practical approach:

1. Define a clear channel concept
- Avoid "everything in my field."
- Choose a specific promise for viewers.
- Examples:
  - "Explaining the science behind daily life"
  - "History myths and what the evidence actually shows"
  - "Economics explained through current events"
  - "How researchers think and solve problems"

2. Make content accessible
- Most viewers are not specialists.
- Replace jargon with examples, stories, and analogies.
- Aim to explain concepts to an educated non-expert audience.

3. Start with high-demand topics
- Answer questions people are already searching for.
- Address misconceptions.
- Explain current events related to your expertise.
- Use tools such as YouTube Search suggestions, Google Trends, and audience comments.

4. Prioritize titles and thumbnails
- Excellent content can fail if nobody clicks.
- Focus on curiosity and relevance.
- Example:
  - Instead of: "Lecture 3: Cognitive Biases"
  - Try: "Why Smart People Make Predictable Mistakes"

5. Develop a repeatable format
Examples:
- 10-minute concept explainers
- Myth vs. evidence
- Research paper breakdowns
- Expert reactions to news stories
- Answers to audience questions

6. Publish consistently
- One strong video per week is often better than ambitious but irregular schedules.
- Consistency builds audience expectations.

7. Learn YouTube analytics
Watch:
- Click-through rate (CTR)
- Average view duration
- Audience retention
- Returning viewers

These metrics often reveal more than subscriber count.

8. Adapt academic communication for YouTube
Academic incentives reward completeness and nuance.
YouTube rewards clarity, engagement, and structure.
You can maintain intellectual rigor while being concise.

A useful structure:
- Hook (first 15–30 seconds)
- The question/problem
- Main explanation
- Evidence/examples
- Conclusion and takeaway

9. Build trust rather than chase trends
- Cite evidence when relevant.
- Acknowledge uncertainty.
- Avoid sensationalism.
- Your credibility can become a major differentiator.

10. Think long-term
Many educational channels grow slowly at first.
A library of 50–100 strong videos often performs far better than a handful of highly polished uploads.
