The early Han emperors, influenced by Huang-Lao Daoist philosophy, adopted relatively laissez-faire economic policies, reducing taxes and allowing agricultural production to recover after the devastation of the Qin Dynasty and subsequent civil wars. Under Emperor Wu (r. 141–87 BCE), however, the state assumed a far more interventionist role, nationalizing the lucrative salt and iron industries, implementing price stabilization policies through the ever-normal granary system, and introducing a system of government monopolies that generated substantial imperial revenue. While the Han economy remained fundamentally agrarian, the dynasty's sophisticated blend of state management and market activity established institutional and infrastructural foundations that would influence Chinese economic organization for centuries to come.
