Different views on discipleship.

“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and helping others do the same. Here are some major perspectives:

- Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus Christ.
  - Discipleship often involves Bible study, prayer, accountability, evangelism, and spiritual growth.
  - Small groups, mentoring relationships, and one-on-one discipleship are common.

- Reformed (Calvinist) view
  - Focuses on discipleship as lifelong sanctification under God's grace.
  - Strong emphasis on Scripture, doctrine, and participation in the church community.
  - Discipleship is often tied to covenant community and spiritual formation through ordinary means such as preaching and sacraments.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as participation in the life of Christ through faith, the sacraments, prayer, and service.
  - The Church plays a central role in forming disciples.
  - Emphasizes both personal holiness and works of charity.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Understands discipleship as a process of theosis (becoming united with God by grace).
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation through prayer, worship, fasting, sacraments, and ascetic practices.
  - Strong emphasis on continuity with historic Christian tradition.

- Wesleyan / Methodist view
  - Highlights personal holiness and social holiness.
  - Discipleship involves spiritual disciplines, accountability, and service to others.
  - Often stresses ongoing growth in love for God and neighbor.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in the believer's life.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, evangelism, and personal transformation.
  - Often encourages active participation in ministry.

- Anabaptist view
  - Stresses obedience to Jesus' teachings and visible Christian living.
  - Often emphasizes community, simplicity, peace-making, and discipleship as a practical way of life.
  - Following Christ is demonstrated through daily conduct.

- Liberation theology perspective
  - Focuses on discipleship as solidarity with the poor and marginalized.
  - Following Jesus involves working for justice and addressing social and economic oppression.
  - Common in parts of Latin America and other contexts concerned with social justice.

- Radical discipleship movements
  - Emphasize costly obedience, sacrifice, and a countercultural lifestyle.
  - Often draw on passages such as Jesus' call to "take up your cross and follow me."
  - May stress intentional community and simplicity.

Common themes across many traditions include:
- Following Jesus as Lord.
- Learning and applying his teachings.
- Spiritual growth and transformation.
- Participation in a faith community.
- Service to others.
- Helping others become disciples.

Outside Christianity, the term "discipleship" can also refer more generally to the relationship between a teacher and followers, though the Christian usage is by far the most common today.
