Best skin routine for dry skin.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For dry skin, the goal is to support the skin barrier, reduce water loss, and keep the skin comfortably hydrated. A simple, consistent routine is usually more effective than a complicated one.

Morning:
1. Gentle cleanser (or just rinse with water if your skin isn't oily)
   - Look for a fragrance-free, non-foaming cleanser.
   - Examples: CeraVe Hydrating Cleanser, La Roche-Posay Toleriane Hydrating Gentle Cleanser.

2. Hydrating serum (optional)
   - Ingredients like hyaluronic acid, glycerin, or panthenol can help attract moisture.

3. Moisturizer
   - Choose a cream rather than a lightweight lotion if your skin is very dry.
   - Look for ceramides, glycerin, squalane, and shea butter.

4. Sunscreen (daily)
   - SPF 30 or higher.
   - Dry skin often does well with moisturizing sunscreen formulations.

Evening:
1. Gentle cleanser
   - Remove sunscreen and makeup without stripping the skin.

2. Treatment (optional)
   - If you're targeting aging, uneven tone, or acne, consider a retinoid, but start slowly (1–2 nights per week) since retinoids can increase dryness.

3. Moisturizer
   - Apply generously while skin is still slightly damp.

4. Occlusive layer (if very dry)
   - A thin layer of petrolatum (e.g., Vaseline) or a healing ointment over moisturizer can significantly reduce overnight moisture loss.

Key ingredients that tend to work well for dry skin:
- Ceramides (support skin barrier)
- Glycerin (humectant)
- Hyaluronic acid (humectant)
- Squalane (emollient)
- Urea (hydrating, especially for very dry skin)
- Petrolatum (excellent at preventing water loss)

Things to avoid if dryness is a concern:
- Harsh scrubs
- Over-cleansing
- Alcohol-heavy toners
- Strong exfoliating acids used too frequently
- Very hot showers or face washing

A practical, low-complexity routine:
- AM: Gentle cleanser → moisturizer → SPF 30+
- PM: Gentle cleanser → moisturizer → thin layer of petrolatum if needed

If your dryness is accompanied by significant redness, itching, flaking, or irritation that doesn't improve with moisturization, it may be worth consulting a dermatologist to evaluate conditions such as eczema, rosacea, or seborrheic dermatitis.
