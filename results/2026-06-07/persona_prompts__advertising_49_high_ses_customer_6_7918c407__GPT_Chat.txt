How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to placement, coverage, interference, and equipment quality. A practical approach:

- Place the router centrally
  - Put it near the middle of the home rather than at one end.
  - Keep it elevated (on a shelf or table).
  - Avoid cabinets, closets, and behind TVs.

- Reduce interference
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, baby monitors, and large metal objects.
  - If possible, use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band for devices that need higher speeds and lower interference.

- Optimize router settings
  - Update the router firmware.
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 security.
  - Check whether the router can automatically select the best channel, or manually choose a less congested channel.

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system
  - For larger homes, multiple floors, or homes with thick walls, a mesh system often provides much better coverage than a single router.
  - Place mesh nodes where they still receive a strong signal from the main unit.

- Upgrade older equipment
  - If your router is more than 4–5 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router may significantly improve performance.
  - Ensure devices also support newer Wi‑Fi standards where possible.

- Use wired connections strategically
  - Connect bandwidth-heavy devices (gaming PCs, streaming boxes, workstations) via Ethernet if feasible.
  - Wired backhaul between mesh nodes can improve performance substantially.

- Check internet service versus Wi‑Fi performance
  - Run a speed test while connected directly to the modem/router via Ethernet.
  - If wired speeds are slow, the issue may be with the internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.

- Measure and diagnose
  - Use a Wi‑Fi analyzer app to identify weak-signal areas and channel congestion.
  - Test speeds in different rooms to determine whether the issue is coverage, interference, or ISP performance.
