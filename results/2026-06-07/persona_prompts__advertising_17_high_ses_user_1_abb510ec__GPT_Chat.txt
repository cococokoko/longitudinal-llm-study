── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home energy efficiency is usually most effective when you prioritize measures with the highest return on investment first.

Start with an energy audit
- Hire a professional home energy auditor or use your utility company's program if available.
- An audit can identify air leaks, insulation deficiencies, inefficient equipment, and major energy losses.

Air sealing
- Seal gaps around doors, windows, plumbing penetrations, electrical outlets, attic hatches, and ductwork.
- Weatherstrip exterior doors.
- Air sealing is often one of the most cost-effective upgrades.

Insulation
- Ensure adequate attic insulation; this is frequently the highest-impact insulation project.
- Insulate walls, crawl spaces, basements, and rim joists where appropriate.
- Proper insulation reduces both heating and cooling costs.

Heating and cooling (HVAC)
- Replace aging furnaces, boilers, or air conditioners with high-efficiency models.
- Consider a heat pump if suitable for your climate.
- Change filters regularly and maintain equipment annually.
- Seal and insulate ducts if you have forced-air systems.

Smart temperature control
- Install a programmable or smart thermostat.
- Reduce heating and cooling when the home is unoccupied or during sleeping hours.

Windows and doors
- If replacement is needed, choose high-performance windows with appropriate glazing for your climate.
- Before replacing windows, air sealing and insulation often provide greater savings per dollar spent.

Water heating
- Lower the water heater temperature if safe and practical.
- Insulate hot-water pipes.
- Consider a heat-pump water heater when replacement is due.
- Install low-flow showerheads and fixtures.

Lighting
- Replace incandescent or halogen bulbs with LEDs.
- Use occupancy sensors or smart controls in less frequently used areas.

Appliances and electronics
- Choose ENERGY STAR–rated appliances when replacing old units.
- Use advanced power strips to reduce standby power consumption.
- Unplug rarely used devices.

Laundry and kitchen habits
- Wash clothes with cold water when appropriate.
- Run full loads in dishwashers and washing machines.
- Air-dry clothes when practical.
- Keep refrigerator coils clean and maintain proper temperatures.

Solar energy
- If your roof, climate, and local incentives are favorable, rooftop solar can significantly reduce electricity costs.
- Battery storage may provide resilience and improve solar utilization, depending on local utility rates.

Landscaping
- Plant shade trees strategically to reduce summer cooling loads.
- Use exterior shading such as awnings, shutters, or solar screens.

Monitor usage
- Review utility bills regularly.
- Consider a whole-home energy monitor to identify high-consumption devices and patterns.

For many homes, the highest-value sequence is:
1. Energy audit
2. Air sealing
3. Attic insulation
4. HVAC optimization or heat pump upgrades
5. Water-heating improvements
6. Lighting and appliance upgrades
7. Solar installation
