You can stay very fit without ever setting foot in a gym. The key is to cover four areas: cardiovascular fitness, strength, mobility, and consistency.

A simple framework:

• Cardiovascular fitness (3–5 times per week)
  - Brisk walking
  - Running or jogging
  - Cycling
  - Hiking
  - Swimming (if accessible)
  - Jump rope

Aim for 20–60 minutes depending on intensity. Even daily walks contribute substantially to health and longevity.

• Strength training (2–4 times per week)
  - Push-ups
  - Squats
  - Lunges
  - Step-ups on stairs
  - Planks
  - Glute bridges
  - Pull-ups (if you have access to a bar)

As these become easier, increase repetitions, add pauses, slow the movement, use a backpack with books for resistance, or progress to more challenging variations.

Example full-body routine:
  - Push-ups: 3 sets
  - Squats: 3 sets
  - Lunges: 3 sets
  - Plank: 3 rounds
  - Pull-ups or rows (if available): 3 sets

• Mobility and flexibility (most days)
  - Stretch major muscle groups
  - Yoga routines
  - Mobility exercises for hips, shoulders, and spine

Even 10 minutes daily can improve movement quality and reduce stiffness.

• Daily activity
  - Take stairs instead of elevators
  - Walk during phone calls
  - Use active transportation when possible
  - Break up long periods of sitting

For many academics and knowledge workers, increasing daily movement outside formal exercise can have a surprisingly large impact.

A practical weekly schedule:
• Monday: Strength
• Tuesday: Brisk walk or run
• Wednesday: Strength
• Thursday: Walk, cycle, or mobility work
• Friday: Strength
• Saturday: Longer outdoor activity (hike, bike ride, run)
• Sunday: Easy walk and stretching

If your primary goal is general health, maintaining muscle mass and achieving the recommended amount of weekly aerobic activity are more important than having access to specialized equipment. A well-designed bodyweight program plus regular walking or running can provide excellent fitness outcomes.
