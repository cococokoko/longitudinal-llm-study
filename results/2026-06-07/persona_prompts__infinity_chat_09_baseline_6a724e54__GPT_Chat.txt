Is there life on other planets?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
We don't currently know whether life exists on other planets, but many scientists think it's plausible.

Here’s why:

- The universe is enormous. There are hundreds of billions of stars in our galaxy alone, and likely hundreds of billions of galaxies.
- Many stars have planets. Since the 1990s, astronomers have confirmed thousands of exoplanets (planets orbiting other stars).
- Some of those planets appear to be in a "habitable zone," where temperatures could allow liquid water to exist on the surface.
- On Earth, life arose relatively early in the planet's history, suggesting that life might emerge under the right conditions.

At the same time:
- We have not found definitive evidence of life beyond Earth.
- We do not yet know how common the conditions required for life are.
- We do not know how likely it is for life to originate even when conditions seem favorable.

Scientists are searching in several ways:
- Looking for microbial life on Mars.
- Studying oceans beneath the icy surfaces of moons such as Europa and Enceladus.
- Analyzing the atmospheres of exoplanets for possible biosignatures, such as combinations of gases that might be produced by living organisms.
- Searching for signals from technological civilizations (SETI).

The current scientific consensus is that extraterrestrial life is possible and perhaps even likely somewhere in the universe, but there is no confirmed discovery yet. As of today, Earth is the only place where life is known to exist.
