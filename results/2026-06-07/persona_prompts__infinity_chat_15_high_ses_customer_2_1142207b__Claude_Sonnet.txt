## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately influence international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO voting structures)
- Economic leverage translates directly into diplomatic leverage
- Debt relationships create dependency that constrains sovereign decision-making

**Competing theoretical frameworks**
- *Dependency theory* argues inequality is systematically reproduced through trade structures
- *Liberal institutionalism* suggests institutions can partially offset power asymmetries
- *Realist perspectives* treat economic inequality as simply another power resource
- Each captures something real while having genuine limitations

## Concrete Manifestations

- Sanctions effectiveness depends heavily on economic asymmetry
- Foreign aid creates complex obligation relationships
- Trade agreement negotiations reflect bargaining power disparities
- Climate negotiations are substantially shaped by development status disputes

## Honest Tensions Worth Noting

- Economic interdependence sometimes *reduces* conflict, sometimes creates new friction points
- Rising powers (China, India) complicate simple North-South frameworks
- Domestic inequality within nations increasingly affects international positioning
- Whether institutions genuinely moderate inequality or legitimize it remains genuinely contested

## Practical Relevance

For legal work specifically, this matters for understanding treaty interpretation, investment arbitration dynamics, and why certain international legal frameworks face legitimacy challenges from developing nations.
