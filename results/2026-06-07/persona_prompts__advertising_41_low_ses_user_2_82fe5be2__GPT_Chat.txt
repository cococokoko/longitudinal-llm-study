  - Payment history is the most important credit-scoring factor.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders if childcare and work responsibilities make it easy to lose track of due dates.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Review your reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute any inaccurate late payments, balances, or accounts that don't belong to you.
  - Correcting errors can sometimes improve a score fairly quickly.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If your income and credit history support it, a higher limit can lower your utilization ratio.
  - Ask whether the request will involve a hard credit inquiry before proceeding.

- Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts
  - New applications can temporarily lower your score and add hard inquiries.
  - Only apply for credit if you genuinely need it.

- Bring past-due accounts current
  - If any accounts are delinquent, catching up can help prevent further damage.
  - Some lenders may offer hardship programs or payment arrangements.

- Become an authorized user (if appropriate)
  - If a trusted family member has a long history of on-time payments and low balances, being added as an authorized user may help, depending on the issuer and scoring model.
