── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on your finances, your relationship, and what both of you want the ownership arrangement to be.

A useful way to think about it is: money and ownership should generally align.

Some common options:

- She buys the house herself.
  - She pays the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and major ownership costs.
  - You contribute to household expenses if you move in.
  - This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage if the house is solely hers.

- She owns the house, and you pay a form of rent.
  - You contribute a mutually agreed monthly amount for living there.
  - You do not gain ownership simply by paying toward living costs.
  - This can be fair if your contribution is roughly comparable to what you'd pay for housing elsewhere and both parties are comfortable with it.

- You contribute to ownership.
  - If you are helping with the down payment or mortgage as an investment, your ownership interest should be documented.
  - That could mean being on the title, having a formal co-ownership agreement, or another legally recognized arrangement.
  - Avoid informal situations where you put substantial money into a house but have no clear legal rights.

- Wait until marriage to combine ownership.
  - Many couples keep pre-marital assets separate until they marry and then decide whether and how to merge finances.

A few practical principles:

- Don't contribute a large down payment without written agreements about ownership.
- Don't assume marriage later will automatically make everything fair or simple.
- Discuss expectations now: Is the house her investment? A future family home? Something you will eventually co-own?
- If significant money is involved, a consultation with a real-estate attorney or family-law attorney is often worth the cost.

One question that would help narrow this down: when you say "pay for the house," do you mean contributing to the down payment, helping with the monthly mortgage, splitting all housing expenses, or buying the house together?
