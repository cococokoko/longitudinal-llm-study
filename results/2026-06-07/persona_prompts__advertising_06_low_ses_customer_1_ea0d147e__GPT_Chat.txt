If your goal is mastering digital photography, a structured approach works well, especially if your work schedule changes from week to week.

A practical learning path:

1. Learn the camera fundamentals
- Exposure triangle:
  - Aperture (depth of field)
  - Shutter speed (motion blur/freezing action)
  - ISO (sensor sensitivity/noise)
- Focus modes and autofocus settings
- Shooting modes (Manual, Aperture Priority, Shutter Priority)

2. Practice composition
- Rule of thirds
- Leading lines
- Framing
- Symmetry and patterns
- Using negative space

3. Understand light
- Golden hour and blue hour
- Hard vs. soft light
- Backlighting
- Indoor lighting techniques
- Using reflectors or simple light sources

4. Shoot intentionally
- Portraits
- Street photography
- Landscapes
- Food photography
- Action photography

5. Learn editing
- Adobe Lightroom, Capture One, or free alternatives such as Darktable
- Exposure adjustments
- Color correction
- Cropping and composition refinement
- Basic retouching

6. Build a portfolio
- Select your best images
- Organize by category
- Track progress over time
- Share work for feedback

A schedule-friendly practice plan:
- 15–20 minutes: study one concept
- 20–30 minutes: take photos focused on that concept
- On days off: review and edit images

Recommended resources:
- YouTube: Simon d'Entremont, Nigel Danson, Adorama, B&H Photo
- Books:
  - Understanding Exposure by Bryan Peterson
  - The Photographer's Eye by Michael Freeman
- Practice communities:
  - Flickr
  - 500px
  - Photography subreddits and forums

A strong beginner milestone is being able to consistently control exposure manually and explain why you chose specific camera settings for a shot.
