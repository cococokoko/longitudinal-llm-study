Improving productivity while working from home.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For attorneys working remotely, productivity tends to depend less on working longer hours and more on reducing context-switching, protecting deep-work time, and maintaining clear communication with clients and colleagues.

A few approaches that are particularly effective in legal practice:

- Structure the day around cognitive intensity
  - Reserve your highest-focus hours for substantive legal work such as drafting briefs, analyzing case law, preparing arguments, or reviewing complex agreements.
  - Schedule administrative tasks, email, billing entries, and routine calls during lower-energy periods.

- Create uninterrupted work blocks
  - Block 60–120 minute periods on your calendar for concentrated work.
  - Silence nonessential notifications during these periods.
  - Consider having support staff or colleagues route only urgent matters through alternative channels.

- Manage email deliberately
  - Avoid continuous inbox monitoring.
  - Process email at designated intervals unless your practice area requires immediate responsiveness.
  - Use folders, rules, and templates for recurring communications.

- Maintain a dedicated workspace
  - A separate office, even if modest, creates psychological and practical boundaries between work and personal life.
  - Ensure reliable videoconferencing equipment, lighting, and document security.

- Use matter-based task management
  - Organize tasks by client or matter rather than maintaining a single undifferentiated to-do list.
  - Prioritize according to deadlines, strategic importance, and client commitments.

- Strengthen communication rhythms
  - Establish predictable check-ins with associates, paralegals, and administrative staff.
  - Clarify expectations regarding response times, escalation procedures, and project status reporting.

- Protect confidentiality and security
  - Use secure networks, VPNs, and firm-approved systems.
  - Be mindful of conversations that may be overheard in a home environment.
  - Follow document-handling protocols as rigorously as in the office.

- Reduce meeting overload
  - Decline or shorten meetings that do not require your participation.
  - Encourage concise agendas and clear action items.
  - Consider whether a brief email or phone call can achieve the same objective.

- Track time contemporaneously
  - Recording billable work as it occurs improves accuracy and reduces end-of-day reconstruction.
  - It also provides visibility into where time is being spent and where inefficiencies arise.

- Preserve professional and personal boundaries
  - Define a clear start and end to the workday whenever possible.
  - Remote work can blur boundaries and lead to longer hours without corresponding gains in productivity.

A useful framework is to identify the three most important outcomes you need to advance each day—such as completing a motion draft, preparing for a deposition, or finalizing a transaction document—and ensure those receive attention before lower-value tasks consume the day.
