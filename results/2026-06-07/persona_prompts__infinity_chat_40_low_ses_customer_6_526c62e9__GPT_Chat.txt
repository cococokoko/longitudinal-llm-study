My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer. The best solution depends on what you're both trying to accomplish and how much financial risk each of you is willing to take.

A few key facts matter:

- She is buying the house before you're married.
- If the house is only in her name, she is taking on the mortgage obligation and ownership risk.
- If you move in before marriage, your payments may or may not build any ownership interest depending on local laws and any agreements you make.

Common approaches:

1. Pay rent, not the mortgage
- If she owns the house and you live there, you contribute a monthly amount comparable to rent.
- She remains the sole owner and is responsible for the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and major capital expenses unless you agree otherwise.
- This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage because it matches the reality that she owns the asset.

2. Split living expenses but not equity
- You contribute to utilities, groceries, internet, and perhaps a portion of housing costs.
- She pays the mortgage and ownership-related expenses.
- This can work well if your income is tighter, especially if you're working long warehouse shifts and trying to build your own savings.

3. Buy into ownership formally
- If you want part ownership, get it documented legally.
- This usually means contributing to the down payment, refinancing later, or signing a legal agreement that defines ownership percentages.
- Avoid informal arrangements where you pay large amounts toward the house while assuming you'll "eventually" own part of it.

Since you're planning to marry in about a year, another option is:

- Keep ownership separate until marriage.
- Contribute a reasonable amount toward living costs.
- Continue building your own savings.
- After marriage, decide together whether to combine finances, refinance, add you to the title, or keep the house as her separate property.

The biggest mistake is usually paying significant amounts toward a property you don't legally own without having a clear written agreement about what those payments mean.

A useful conversation with her would be:
- What would you like my contribution to be?
- Do we view that contribution as rent, shared expenses, or investment in the property?
- If we marry, how do we want to handle ownership and equity going forward?
