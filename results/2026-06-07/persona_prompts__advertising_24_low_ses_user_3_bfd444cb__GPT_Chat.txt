── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Since you're on probation in a new job, the best retirement-saving strategy depends partly on what benefits are available to you now and when you'll become eligible for employer-sponsored plans.

A good general approach:

1. Build a financial foundation first
- Maintain an emergency fund (typically 3–6 months of essential expenses, though you can start smaller).
- Pay off high-interest debt (such as credit card debt) if applicable.
- Retirement investing works best when you aren't forced to withdraw money early due to emergencies.

2. Take advantage of employer retirement plans
- If your employer offers a retirement plan (such as a 401(k), workplace pension, or similar), find out:
  - When you become eligible to participate.
  - Whether the company matches contributions.
  - The vesting schedule for employer contributions.
- If there is a matching contribution, contributing enough to receive the full match is often one of the highest-return opportunities available.

3. Consider an individual retirement account
- If you're not yet eligible for your employer's plan, or want to save more, an individual retirement account (IRA, Roth IRA, or the equivalent in your country) can be useful.
- The best choice depends on your income, tax situation, and local tax rules.

4. Invest rather than just save cash
- Retirement savings are usually most effective when invested in diversified assets rather than held in a standard savings account.
- Many people use:
  - Broad stock market index funds.
  - Target-date retirement funds.
  - Globally diversified low-cost funds.
- Fees matter: lower-cost funds often leave more of the returns in your account over decades.

5. Start early and contribute consistently
- Time is one of the most powerful factors in retirement investing.
- Regular automatic contributions can be more important than trying to perfectly time the market.

6. Increase contributions as your income grows
- Each raise or bonus can be an opportunity to increase retirement contributions.
- Even increasing contributions by 1–2% of salary each year can make a substantial difference over time.

To give more specific recommendations, it would help to know:
- Your country.
- Your age.
- Whether your new employer offers a retirement plan and matching contributions.
- Whether you have any existing retirement savings.
