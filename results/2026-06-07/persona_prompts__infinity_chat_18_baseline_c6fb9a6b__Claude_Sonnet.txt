# Review: *The Last Frequency* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Okonkwo's debut feature is a quiet devastation. Set in a dying radio station in rural Montana, the film follows two strangers — a retired sound engineer and a teenage runaway — who spend one winter night trying to broadcast a signal that no one may be listening to.

What could have been a quirky indie cliché becomes something genuinely aching. The performances are extraordinary. You believe every silence between these two characters as much as every word.

Okonkwo shoots on grainy 16mm, and the crackling audio design mirrors the story's central question: **does it matter if you're heard, as long as you speak?**

The third act stumbles slightly — a subplot involving the station's foreclosure feels rushed — but the final ten minutes are among the most quietly devastating I've seen this year.

*The Last Frequency* won't be for everyone. It's slow, it's small, and it trusts you to meet it halfway. But for those willing to tune in, it rewards you completely.
