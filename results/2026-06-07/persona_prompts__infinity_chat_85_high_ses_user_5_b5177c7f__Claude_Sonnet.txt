── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here's a powerful rhetorical question:

**"If we required teenagers to sign liability waivers before entering a casino, why do we hand them a device with algorithmically engineered addiction in their pocket and call it connection?"**

This works because it:
- Creates a **sharp contrast** that feels immediately unfair
- Implies **corporate responsibility** without stating it directly
- Uses a concrete comparison most readers instantly understand
- Puts the burden of justification on those who *defend* the status quo

A few alternative options depending on your tone:

- **More emotional:** "Would you hand your child a product if its own designers refused to let their children use it?"
- **More data-driven:** "When did 'move fast and break things' become an acceptable approach to the developing minds of an entire generation?"
- **More direct:** "At what point does engagement become exploitation?"

The **casino comparison** is probably strongest for an executive audience because it speaks the language of **risk management and liability** while still landing emotionally.
