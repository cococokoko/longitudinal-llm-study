**Question:**
While birds like cardinals and flamingos get their vibrant colors from pigments in the food they eat, bluebirds (and all other blue-feathered birds) do not actually possess a single drop of blue pigment. How, then, do they appear blue to the human eye?

**Answer:**
Through a phenomenon called "structural coloration." The microscopic, sponge-like structure of the keratin in their feathers acts like a prism. It absorbs longer wavelengths of light (like red and yellow) and scatters the shorter blue wavelengths back to the viewer. Because the color is a trick of the light rather than a physical dye, if you were to grind up a bluebird's feather, the resulting powder would actually be a dull brown or gray!
