If your home Wi‑Fi is slow, unreliable, or has dead spots, these steps usually help:

- Move the router to a central, open location.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band.
  - 5 GHz: Faster speeds, shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz: Longer range, slower speeds.
  - If available, Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E can improve performance on newer devices.

- Reduce interference.
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and Bluetooth-heavy areas.
  - In apartment buildings, changing Wi‑Fi channels can help avoid congestion.

- Update router firmware.
  - Manufacturers regularly release updates that improve performance and security.

- Check your internet plan.
  - Run a speed test near the router. If speeds are much lower than what you're paying for, contact your ISP or investigate modem/router issues.

- Use Ethernet where possible.
  - Devices that need reliable performance (gaming PCs, work computers, streaming boxes) benefit from wired connections.

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system.
  - For larger homes or multiple floors, mesh systems often provide better coverage than a single router.

- Restart networking equipment occasionally.
  - Rebooting the modem and router can resolve temporary issues.

- Upgrade older hardware.
  - If your router is more than 4–5 years old, a newer model may provide significantly better coverage and speed.
