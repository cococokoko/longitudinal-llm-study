Improving mental health is often about building a combination of habits, support systems, and coping skills that work for your situation. What helps can vary from person to person, but some evidence-based approaches include:

- Prioritize sleep
  - Aim for a consistent sleep schedule.
  - Reduce screen use before bed if possible.
  - Seek medical advice if you have persistent sleep problems.

- Stay physically active
  - Regular exercise can reduce symptoms of stress, anxiety, and depression.
  - Even short walks or light activity can be beneficial.

- Strengthen social connections
  - Spend time with supportive friends, family, or community groups.
  - Regular contact, even brief conversations, can help reduce isolation.

- Manage stress intentionally
  - Practices such as mindfulness, meditation, journaling, or breathing exercises can help.
  - Identify recurring stressors and consider what changes are within your control.

- Take care of your physical health
  - Eat regularly and try to maintain a balanced diet.
  - Stay hydrated.
  - Address ongoing medical concerns with a healthcare professional.

- Limit substances that can affect mood
  - Alcohol, recreational drugs, and excessive caffeine can worsen mental health symptoms for some people.

- Set realistic goals
  - Break large tasks into smaller steps.
  - Recognize progress rather than expecting perfection.

- Engage in meaningful activities
  - Hobbies, volunteering, creative projects, learning, or spiritual practices can increase a sense of purpose and satisfaction.

- Challenge unhelpful thinking patterns
  - Notice self-critical or catastrophic thoughts.
  - Ask whether there is evidence for and against those thoughts.
  - Consider alternative, more balanced perspectives.

- Consider professional support
  - Therapy can be helpful for many concerns, including stress, anxiety, depression, relationship difficulties, and life transitions.
