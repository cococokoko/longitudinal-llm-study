Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding a few key principles: choosing the right cut, controlling temperature, seasoning properly, and resting the meat after cooking.

1. Choose the Right Cut
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, and forgiving.
- New York Strip: Firm texture with a good balance of tenderness and flavor.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender but less flavorful than fattier cuts.
- T-Bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip steak and tenderloin in one cut.
- Sirloin: Leaner and often more affordable.

2. Start with Quality Meat
- Look for good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- Thicker steaks (1–1.5 inches / 2.5–4 cm) are easier to cook accurately.
- If possible, choose USDA Choice or Prime grades (or equivalent in your country).

3. Bring to Room Temperature (Optional but Helpful)
- Remove the steak from the refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking.
- Pat it dry thoroughly with paper towels.

4. Season Simply
- Generously season with kosher salt.
- Add freshly ground black pepper just before cooking or after searing.
- Garlic powder or herbs can be added, but simplicity often works best.

5. Use High Heat for Searing
For pan-searing:
- Use a heavy skillet, preferably cast iron.
- Heat the pan until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, grapeseed).
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.

For grilling:
- Preheat the grill thoroughly.
- Create direct and indirect heat zones if possible.

6. Learn Internal Temperatures
Use an instant-read thermometer for accuracy.

- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Remove the steak about 5°F (3°C) before the target temperature because it will continue cooking while resting.

7. Baste for Extra Flavor
During the last few minutes:
- Add butter, crushed garlic, and herbs such as thyme or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest the Steak
- Rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps the juices redistribute throughout the meat.

9. Slice Correctly
- Cut against the grain to maximize tenderness.
- For thick steaks, slice and serve immediately after resting.

A Reliable Pan-Seared Medium-Rare Method
- 1.25-inch (3 cm) ribeye or strip steak.
- Salt generously 40 minutes before cooking or just before cooking.
- Heat cast-iron skillet until very hot.
- Add 1 tablespoon high-smoke-point oil.
- Sear 2–4 minutes per side.
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs.
- Baste for 1–2 minutes.
- Cook until the center reaches about 130°F (54°C).
- Rest 5–10 minutes.
- Serve.

Common Mistakes
- Cooking cold, wet steak.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Flipping too frequently before a crust forms.
- Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.
- Relying solely on cooking time instead of temperature.

The single biggest upgrade for most home cooks is using an instant-read thermometer. It removes guesswork and makes steak doneness highly repeatable.
