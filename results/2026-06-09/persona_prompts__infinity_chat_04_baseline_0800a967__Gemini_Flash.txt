### Thesis Idea 1: Gorz, AI, and the "Non-Class of Non-Workers" in the Age of Platform Capitalism
* **The Core Concept:** In *Farewell to the Working Class*, Gorz identified the "non-class of non-workers"—those permanently marginalized from the traditional, secure industrial proletariat by automation. Today, we see this realized not as total leisure, but as the rise of the "precariat," gig workers, and those whose data is harvested to train AI.
* **The Intervention:** Traditional Marxism often struggles to theorize the revolutionary potential of the unorganized, fragmented gig economy. Gorz’s framework allows us to see this "non-class" not as a failure of class consciousness, but as the vanguard of a new form of resistance that rejects the work ethic entirely.
* **How to frame it:** Put Gorz in conversation with **Italian Autonomism (Post-Operaismo)**—specifically Franco "Bifo" Berardi or Maurizio Lazzarato—and contemporary theorists of platform capitalism (like Nick Srnicek). 
* **Potential Title:** *The Digital Non-Class: Re-reading Gorz’s "Farewell to the Working Class" in the Age of Algorithmic Labor.*
* **Key Question:** How does Gorz’s critique of the "work society" help us theorize resistance when the workplace is no longer a physical factory, but a decentralized digital platform?

### Thesis Idea 2: Degrowth Marxism: Gorz vs. the "Eco-Modernists"
* **The Core Concept:** There is a fierce debate within contemporary Marxism between "Eco-Modernists" / "Left Accelerationists" (who argue that post-capitalism requires *more* technological development and production, just planned democratically) and "Degrowth Marxists" (like Kohei Saito, author of *Slow Down*). 
* **The Intervention:** Gorz is the bridge between these two camps. He was a Marxist who believed in high-tech automation (to reduce labor time) but was also a fierce advocate for ecological limits and "conviviality" (influenced by Ivan Illich). You can use Gorz to critique both the techno-optimism of Left Accelerationism (e.g., Bastani's *Fully Automated Luxury Communism*) and the potential primitivism of extreme degrowth.
* **How to frame it:** Use Gorz’s distinction between **"autonomous spheres"** (activities done for their own sake) and **"heteronomous spheres"** (activities dictated by necessity/machinery) to argue for a "Gorzian Eco-Socialism" that balances high-tech automation with ecological contraction.
* **Potential Title:** *Beyond Luxury Communism: André Gorz and the Synthesis of Degrowth and Automation.*
* **Key Question:** How does Gorz’s distinction between "open" and "locking" technologies help us design a socialist ecology that avoids both ecological collapse and techno-authoritarianism?

### Thesis Idea 3: The Existentialist Marxist: Gorz, Sartre, and the Phenomenology of Alienation
* **The Core Concept:** Before Gorz was an ecologist, he was a close disciple of Jean-Paul Sartre (his early book *The Traitor* is a masterpiece of existentialist autobiography). Many Marxist scholars ignore Gorz’s existentialist roots, treating his ecological phase as a complete break.
* **The Intervention:** You can argue that Gorz’s entire ecological and anti-work philosophy is actually a translation of Sartrean existentialism into materialist politics. For Gorz, the fight against capitalism is not just about redistributing surplus value; it is about reclaiming **existential time**—the freedom to define one's own existence.
* **How to frame it:** Conduct a close reading of Gorz’s early existentialist works (*The Traitor*, *La Morale de l'histoire*) alongside his late works (*Critique of Economic Reason*). Connect this to the "humanist" vs. "anti-humanist" debates in Marxism (Sartre vs. Althusser), positioning Gorz as the ultimate theorist of "existential Marxism."
* **Potential Title:** *The Time of Freedom: Existentialist Phenomenology as the Foundation of Gorz’s Ecological Marxism.*
* **Key Question:** How does viewing Gorz’s critique of capitalism through a Sartrean lens redefine the Marxist concept of "alienation" as a theft of existential time rather than just a theft of labor?

### Thesis Idea 4: "Non-Reformist Reforms" in the Neoliberal Ruins
* **The Core Concept:** Gorz coined the term **"non-reformist reforms"** (or structural reforms) in his 1964 book *Strategy for Labor*. These are reforms that do not seek to stabilize the capitalist system, but rather to undermine it and build counter-power (e.g., Universal Basic Income, radical reductions in the workweek).
* **The Intervention:** Today, the Left is caught in a deadlock between a toothless social-democratic reformism (e.g., Scandinavian-style welfare state nostalgia) and an impotent revolutionary purism. Gorz’s "non-reformist reforms" offer a rigorous tactical manual for the contemporary Left.
* **How to frame it:** Apply Gorz’s strategic framework to contemporary policy proposals like the **Green New Deal**, **Universal Basic Income (UBI)**, or the **Four-Day Workweek**. Critique how these proposals are currently co-opted by neoliberalism, and use Gorz to show how they can be weaponized for anti-capitalist transition.
* **Potential Title:** *Tactics for the Transition: Weaponizing Gorzian "Non-Reformist Reforms" Against Neoliberal Hegemony.*
* **Key Question:** How can a reform like UBI be prevented from becoming a "neoliberal cushion" and instead be used as a Gorzian tool to de-commodify labor?
