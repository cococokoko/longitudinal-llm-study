**1. The Jurisprudence of "Non-Work": Gorz, UBI, and the Legal Deconstruction of the Wage Relation**
*   *The Idea:* Gorz famously argued for the liberation *from* work, rather than liberation *through* work (as traditional Marxists argued), eventually advocating for a Universal Basic Income (UBI). 
*   *The Angle:* Analyze the legal and institutional architecture required to transition from a society based on the "wage-labor contract" to one based on a "social dividend." You could critique how current labor laws are entirely predicated on the commodification of time, and use Gorz to theorize a new legal framework for the "right to time" or the "right to autonomy."

**2. Enclosing the Immaterial: Gorz’s Cognitive Capitalism vs. Modern Intellectual Property**
*   *The Idea:* In his later work (*The Immaterial*), Gorz explored how capitalism attempts to commodify knowledge, human faculties, and social relations. 
*   *The Angle:* Read Gorz’s critique of immaterial labor against the modern expansion of Intellectual Property (IP) law, data privacy, and AI. You could argue that modern IP law and corporate data harvesting are the primary legal mechanisms of alienation in the 21st century, acting as the new "enclosure of the commons" that Gorz warned about. 

**3. Eco-Capitalism vs. Eco-Socialism: A Gorzian Critique of Modern Environmental Regulation (ESG)**
*   *The Idea:* Gorz was one of the founding thinkers of political ecology, arguing that ecological crises cannot be solved within a capitalist framework of infinite growth.
*   *The Angle:* Apply Gorz’s political ecology to critique contemporary, market-based environmental solutions (like carbon trading, ESG compliance, and corporate sustainability laws). A thesis could argue that these modern regulatory frameworks represent the exact "eco-fascism" or "eco-capitalism" Gorz predicted—managing the crisis to save capitalism rather than restructuring society to save the environment.

**4. The "Farewell to the Working Class" in the Gig Economy**
*   *The Idea:* In *Farewell to the Working Class*, Gorz argued that the traditional proletariat was no longer the revolutionary subject because work had become too fragmented and alienated.
*   *The Angle:* Re-examine this text through the lens of the modern "precariat" and the gig economy (Uber, TaskRabbit, etc.). You could explore how the legal misclassification of workers (independent contractors vs. employees) has accelerated the fragmentation Gorz described, and ask who the "revolutionary subject" is when the workplace itself has been legally and physically dissolved.

**5. Existential Marxism and the "Right to Disconnect"**
*   *The Idea:* Gorz was heavily influenced by Sartre and existentialism, focusing on human autonomy and the alienation of the self in the modern workplace.
*   *The Angle:* Look at recent international labor movements and laws regarding the "right to disconnect" (e.g., in France and Australia). You could frame this not just as a labor rights issue, but as a deeply Gorzian existential struggle to reclaim the "lived time" of the individual from the totalizing reach of digital capitalism.
