Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In Christianity, **discipleship** generally refers to the process of learning from, following, and becoming more like Jesus Christ. The word derives from the Greek *mathētēs*, meaning "learner" or "apprentice." 

While almost all Christian traditions agree on the ultimate goal of discipleship—Christlikeness—they differ significantly in their theological emphasis and practical methods. Here is a breakdown of the different views on discipleship:

### 1. Denominational and Theological Views

**The Evangelical/Protestant View: Word and Mission**
*   **Emphasis:** Personal relationship with Jesus, biblical literacy, and evangelism.
*   **Approach:** Discipleship is often seen as a process of studying Scripture, praying, and sharing the gospel with others. It is heavily tied to the "Great Commission" (Matthew 28), meaning a mature disciple is one who makes other disciples.
*   **Key Elements:** Bible studies, quiet times (personal devotion), and apologetics.

**The Catholic and Eastern Orthodox View: Sacramental and Liturgical**
*   **Emphasis:** Spiritual formation through the Church, the sacraments, and tradition.
*   **Approach:** Discipleship is not just an individual pursuit but a communal participation in the life of the Church. It involves partaking in the Eucharist, confession, observing the liturgical calendar, and learning from the lives of the Saints or Church Fathers.
*   **Key Elements:** Catechism, liturgy, sacraments, and spiritual direction (often with a priest or spiritual guide).

**The Anabaptist View: Radical Obedience and Community**
*   **Emphasis:** Daily obedience to the teachings of Jesus, particularly the Sermon on the Mount.
*   **Approach:** Anabaptists (e.g., Mennonites, Amish) view discipleship primarily as *orthopraxy* (right living) rather than just *orthodoxy* (right belief). It emphasizes non-violence, simplicity, mutual aid, and living in stark contrast to the secular world.
*   **Key Elements:** Peacemaking, communal living, and ethical rigor.

**The Pentecostal/Charismatic View: Spirit-Empowered Living**
*   **Emphasis:** The active presence and power of the Holy Spirit.
*   **Approach:** Discipleship involves learning to hear the voice of God, being led by the Spirit, and operating in spiritual gifts (such as healing, prophecy, or speaking in tongues). 
*   **Key Elements:** Prayer, worship, spiritual warfare, and experiential encounters with God.

**The Progressive/Liberation Theology View: Social Justice**
*   **Emphasis:** Systemic change, caring for the marginalized, and bringing about the Kingdom of God on earth.
*   **Approach:** Discipleship is viewed through the lens of Jesus’ ministry to the poor and oppressed. To be a disciple is to actively fight against poverty, racism, inequality, and injustice.
*   **Key Elements:** Activism, community service, and challenging oppressive power structures.

---

### 2. Methodological Views (How Discipleship is Done)

Beyond theology, Christians differ on the *methods* used to make disciples:

*   **The Programmatic (Classroom) Model:** Discipleship is viewed as the transfer of information. It happens in Sunday school classes, seminars, or through completing a specific curriculum. The belief is that right knowledge leads to right living.
*   **The Mentorship (One-on-One) Model:** Discipleship is viewed as a micro-relationship. An older, more mature believer takes a younger believer under their wing to guide them, hold them accountable, and answer their questions.
*   **The Small Group (Cell) Model:** Discipleship happens in a circle of 8–12 people who meet regularly in homes. It emphasizes mutual accountability, shared life, and group discussion.
*   **The "Life-on-Life" (Apprenticeship) Model:** Moving away from formal programs, this view treats discipleship like a trade apprenticeship. It happens organically by doing life together—running errands, eating meals, and serving together—allowing the "apprentice" to observe and imitate the mentor's life.

### Summary
Today, many church leaders advocate for a **holistic view** of discipleship that blends these traditions. A holistic approach suggests that true discipleship requires the head (Evangelical biblical knowledge), the heart (Charismatic spiritual experience and Catholic spiritual formation), and the hands (Anabaptist obedience and Progressive social action).
