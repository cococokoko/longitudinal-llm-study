Treating a marathon like a major client engagement is the best way to guarantee success, especially given the demands on your bandwidth as a partner. You already have the discipline, the drive, and the ability to execute long-term strategies. The challenge for you won't be motivation; it will be resource allocation (time) and risk mitigation (injury). 

Here is your executive summary for marathon training, tailored to a high-travel, high-stress lifestyle:

### 1. The Strategy & Roadmap (Training Plan)
*   **Adopt the 80/20 Rule:** 80% of your runs should be at a conversational, easy pace (Zone 2 heart rate), and 20% should be speed or tempo work. High-achievers often make the mistake of running every run too hard. Zone 2 builds your aerobic base and prevents burnout.
*   **Prioritize the Long Run:** If a crazy week of pitches and travel means you have to drop a mid-week run, drop it. The weekend long run is your non-negotiable milestone. 
*   **Build in Buffer:** Choose a 16- to 18-week plan (like Hal Higdon or Hansons), but mentally map it out over 20 weeks. You *will* get sick, or a deal will blow up, or you’ll be stuck on a red-eye. Having buffer weeks prevents panic when you miss a few days.

### 2. Resource Allocation (Time & Travel Management)
*   **Win the Morning:** The only time you can truly control is 5:00 AM to 7:00 AM. If you leave your run for the evening, client escalations or partner dinners will inevitably cannibalize it.
*   **The "Hotel Treadmill" Contingency:** When traveling, map out running routes near your hotel on Strava in advance. If the weather or location is bad, embrace the treadmill. A 60-minute treadmill run with a podcast is better than a zero.
*   **Outsource the Thinking:** Hire a running coach or use a dynamic AI app like Runna or V.O2. You make enough decisions during the day; you want to wake up, look at your watch, and just do what it tells you.

### 3. CapEx & Tech (Gear)
*   **Data Tracking:** Invest in a high-end Garmin or Coros watch. Track your KPIs: resting heart rate, HRV (Heart Rate Variability), and weekly mileage. If your HRV tanks, it means work stress and training stress are compounding—take a rest day.
*   **Shoe Rotation:** Buy a comfortable, highly cushioned shoe for easy/long runs (e.g., Hoka Clifton, Brooks Ghost) and a carbon-plated "super shoe" for race day and key workouts (e.g., Nike Alphafly, Saucony Endorphin Pro). 

### 4. Supply Chain (Nutrition & Recovery)
*   **Practice Your Fueling:** Your gut needs training just like your legs. On long runs, practice taking a carbohydrate gel (like Maurten or Gu) every 30–45 minutes. Never try a new gel or breakfast on race day.
*   **Sleep is the Ultimate ROI:** You cannot out-train a lack of sleep. Training breaks your muscles down; sleep is when the actual fitness adaptations occur. Protect your sleep hygiene ruthlessly.

### 5. Go-Live (Race Day Execution)
*   **Stick to the Pacing Strategy:** The biggest risk on race day is going out too fast because of the adrenaline and crowd. Treat the first 10 miles as a warm-up, the next 10 miles as the actual run, and the final 10K (6.2 miles) as a race of pure mental grit.
*   **Negative Splits:** Aim to run the second half of the marathon slightly faster than the first half. It requires immense discipline early on, but it yields the best results.

You know how to grind through a 14-hour workday; the mental fortitude required for miles 20-26 will feel surprisingly familiar. Trust the process, protect your time, and enjoy the fact that running is one of the few metrics in life where input perfectly equals output.
