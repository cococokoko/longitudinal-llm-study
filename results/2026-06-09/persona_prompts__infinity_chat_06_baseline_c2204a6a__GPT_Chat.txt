── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experience.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice what energizes you.
  - After activities, conversations, projects, or experiences, ask:
    - Did this leave me feeling more alive or more drained?
    - Would I want to do this again?
  - Energy is not the same as ease. Sometimes difficult things are energizing.

- Pay attention to recurring desires.
  - What do you keep returning to in your thoughts?
  - What interests survive for months or years, even after you've tried to ignore them?

- Separate "want" from "should."
  - Ask:
    - If nobody judged me, what would I choose?
    - If there were no status attached, would I still want this?
    - Am I pursuing this because I genuinely care about it, or because it seems like the correct thing to want?

- Look at your envy carefully.
  - Envy can be uncomfortable, but it often points toward something you value.
  - When you envy someone, ask: What exactly do they have that I wish I had? Freedom? Recognition? Creativity? Stability? Connection?

- Experiment instead of overthinking.
  - Many people try to think their way to certainty before acting.
  - Often you learn what you want by trying small versions of things and observing your reaction.

- Imagine multiple futures.
  - Picture yourself five years from now in several different lives.
  - Which version creates relief, excitement, curiosity, or a sense of expansion?
  - Which version feels constricting?

- Identify your values.
  - Make a list of things that matter deeply to you, such as:
    - Autonomy
    - Security
    - Love
    - Achievement
    - Creativity
    - Service
    - Adventure
    - Learning
  - Sometimes conflict comes not from not knowing what you want, but from wanting two values that pull in different directions.

It's also worth knowing that many people don't have one clear, stable answer to "What do I want?" Wants can change as you gain experience. Clarity often emerges from a cycle of acting, reflecting, and adjusting rather than from a single moment of insight.
