# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Camille Okafor's quietly devastating film follows a computational biologist who discovers a fundamental error buried in her most celebrated paper — one that has since shaped a decade of drug development.

What could have been a procedural thriller instead becomes something rarer: a meditation on **how science actually feels from the inside**. The lab meetings are unglamorous and accurate. The grant anxiety is almost unbearably recognizable. And the protagonist's internal debate — correct the record and watch a field unravel, or stay silent — never gets the easy resolution audiences might expect.

The performance by Léa Seydoux is restrained and extraordinary. She conveys the specific exhaustion of someone who is simultaneously a mentor, a manager, a spouse, and a hypothesis.

The film's greatest achievement is refusing to make the error a matter of fraud or ambition. It was just **a mistake**. Made tired, made fast, made human.

A few scenes in the third act lean too heavily on courtroom-style confrontation, which feels imported from a lesser film.

But *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* understands something most science films don't: that the hardest part of the work is rarely the experiment.

**It's what you do with what you find.**
