── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you are likely looking for a precise, mathematically rigorous definition rather than a high-school-level explanation of a straight line on a Cartesian plane. 

In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** is historically used in two distinct ways depending on the subfield or the era of the literature. Below are the two standard interpretations, along with concrete examples for each.

---

### Interpretation 1: The Path Graph ($P_n$)
In modern graph theory, "linear graph" is most commonly used as a synonym for a **path graph** (often denoted as $P_n$). This is a tree with no branches, where two vertices have degree 1 (the endpoints) and all other vertices have degree 2.

#### Formal Example: $P_4$ (Path Graph of order 4)
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a simple undirected graph.

*   **Vertex Set:** $V = \{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}$
*   **Edge Set:** $E = \{\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \{v_3, v_4\}\}$

```text
(v1) ——— (v2) ——— (v3) ——— (v4)
```

**Properties:**
*   **Adjacency Matrix ($A$):**
    $$A = \begin{pmatrix} 
    0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 
    1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 
    0 & 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 
    0 & 0 & 1 & 0 
    \end{pmatrix}$$
*   **Spectrum:** The eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix of $P_n$ are given by $\lambda_k = 2 \cos\left(\frac{k\pi}{n+1}\right)$ for $k = 1, \dots, n$. For $P_4$, the spectrum is $\{\pm \frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}, \pm \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}\}$ (the golden ratio and its conjugate).
*   **Chromatic Number:** $\chi(P_4) = 2$ (it is bipartite).

---

### Interpretation 2: The Line Graph ($L(G)$)
In structural and algebraic graph theory, "linear graph" is sometimes used interchangeably with **line graph** (or representative graph). Given a base graph $G$, its line graph $L(G)$ represents the adjacencies between edges of $G$.

#### Formal Example: $L(K_4)$ (Line Graph of the Complete Graph on 4 vertices)
Let the base graph be the complete graph $G = K_4$.
*   $V(G) = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$
*   $E(G) = \{e_1, e_2, e_3, e_4, e_5, e_6\}$, where:
    *   $e_1 = \{1,2\}, e_2 = \{1,3\}, e_3 = \{1,4\}$
    *   $e_4 = \{2,3\}, e_5 = \{2,4\}, e_6 = \{3,4\}$

The "linear graph" (line graph) $L(K_4)$ is constructed by mapping these edges to vertices:
*   **Vertex Set of $L(K_4)$:** $V' = \{e_1, e_2, e_3, e_4, e_5, e_6\}$
*   **Edge Set of $L(K_4)$:** $E' = \{\{e_i, e_j\} \mid e_i \cap e_j \neq \emptyset\}$ (i.e., the edges share a vertex in $K_4$).

```text
       (e1)-------(e2)
      /  |  \   /  |  \
    /    |    X    |    \
  /      |  /   \  |      \
(e4)---(e5)-------(e3)---(e6)
  \      |         |      /
    \    |         |    /
      \  |         |  /
       (  \_______/  )  <-- (e4 is connected to e6, etc.)
```
*(Note: $L(K_4)$ is isomorphic to the Octahedral graph, $K_6$ minus a perfect matching, or the cocktail party graph $CP(3)$).*

**Properties of $L(K_4)$:**
*   It is a 4-regular graph on 6 vertices.
*   **Whitney's Isomorphism Theorem:** Since $K_4$ is connected and not isomorphic to $K_3$, $L(K_4)$ uniquely determines $K_4$.

### Historical Note (Classical Physics & Network Theory)
If your background touches on electrical engineering or classical network thermodynamics (e.g., the work of Paul Penfield or MacFarlane), "linear graph" was historically used simply to distinguish 1-dimensional topological complexes (graphs) from higher-dimensional simplicial complexes. In that context, *any* standard graph $G = (V,E)$ is a "linear graph."
