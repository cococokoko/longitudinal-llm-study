As a neurosurgeon, you are accustomed to navigating high-stakes situations with precision, careful planning, and risk mitigation. Approaching this real estate situation requires that same analytical mindset. Because you are likely in a high-income bracket (and carry the liability and asset-protection needs that come with your profession), how you structure this purchase now can have significant legal and financial implications later.

Since you are not married yet, the law views you as two separate individuals. Commingling assets before marriage without a clear legal framework can lead to messy complications. 

Here are the most practical, fair, and legally sound solutions, depending on what you both want:

### 1. The "Cleanest" Solution: She Buys, You Pay "Rent"
If she is qualifying for the mortgage and putting down the deposit on her own, let her buy the house in her name. 
* **How it works:** You do not pay for the house directly. Instead, if you live there, you pay her a fair market "rent" and perhaps cover half of the utilities and groceries. 
* **Why it’s fair:** She builds equity in an asset she purchased, and you are paying for your living expenses just as you would anywhere else. 
* **Post-Marriage:** Once you marry, you can revisit the arrangement. You can be added to the deed, or you can use your (likely higher) income to pay off the mortgage faster, at which point the house becomes a marital asset.

### 2. The "Joint Investment" Solution: Tenants in Common
If you want to contribute to the down payment or mortgage right now, do not do it on a handshake. 
* **How it works:** You buy the house together as "Tenants in Common." This allows you to own unequal shares of the property (e.g., she owns 70%, you own 30%) based on exactly what each of you contributes. 
* **Why it’s fair:** It protects your capital. If the relationship were to unexpectedly end, your financial contribution is legally recognized and protected. You will need a real estate attorney to draft a cohabitation property agreement.

### 3. The "Future Planning" Solution: The Prenup
Given your career trajectory, a prenuptial agreement is highly recommended—not because you don't trust her, but because it is standard financial "preventative care" for physicians. 
* **How it works:** She buys the house now. As part of your wedding planning over the next year, you both sit down with attorneys to draft a prenup. The prenup will explicitly state what happens to the house (e.g., it transitions from her separate property to marital property, and how your future income will be used to pay for it).
* **Why it’s fair:** It forces a transparent conversation about finances, debt, and expectations before you tie the knot.

### The Best Next Step
Have a transparent conversation with her. Ask her: *"I'm so excited for our future together. Since we aren't married yet, how were you envisioning we handle the household finances and equity in the short term?"*

**The golden rule right now:** Do not pay for a mortgage or a down payment on a house where your name is not on the deed. Until the marriage certificate is signed, keep the equity tied directly to whoever is providing the capital.
