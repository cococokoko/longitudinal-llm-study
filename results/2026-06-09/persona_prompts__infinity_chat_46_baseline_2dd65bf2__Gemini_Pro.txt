── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, a "linear graph" is most commonly referred to as a **Path Graph** (denoted as $P_n$, where *n* is the number of vertices). 

In a path graph, all vertices are arranged in a single line, connected one after the other, with no branches and no loops (cycles).

Here is a clear example of a **Path Graph with 4 vertices ($P_4$)**:

### 1. Visual Representation
```text
(A) —— (B) —— (C) —— (D)
```

### 2. Mathematical Definition
*   **Vertices (Nodes):** $V = \{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges (Connections):** $E = \{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$

### 3. Real-World Example
Imagine a single train line with four consecutive stops. 
*   **A** is the starting station.
*   To get to station **C**, the train must first travel through station **B**.
*   **D** is the end of the line. 
There are no intersecting train lines and no circular tracks.

### Key Properties of this Graph:
*   **Degrees:** The endpoints (A and D) have a degree of 1 (they are only connected to one other node). The internal nodes (B and C) have a degree of 2 (they connect to the node before and after them).
*   **Acyclic:** There are no cycles; you cannot start at A, travel along the edges without backtracking, and end up back at A.
