── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of the most common—and potentially minefield-prone—financial situations couples face. Because you are not yet married, and she is the sole buyer, you must navigate both the **legal** and **emotional** aspects of this decision. 

Here is a breakdown of what is fair, what is risky, and the best solutions depending on your financial goals.

---

### The Golden Rule of Pre-Marital Real Estate
**If your name is not on the deed, you should not be paying the mortgage.** 

In the eyes of the law, if you pay the mortgage on a house you do not own, you are essentially giving her a tax-free gift or paying rent. If you break up in 10 months, she keeps the house and 100% of the equity, and you walk away with nothing. 

Therefore, the "fairness" of your contribution depends entirely on how you structure your living arrangements now, and how you plan to merge finances later.

---

### Option 1: The "Fair Rent" Model (Best for Pre-Marriage)
Until you are married, treat the living situation as a landlord-tenant relationship, but with a romantic discount. 

*   **How it works:** You do not pay the mortgage. Instead, you pay her a set amount of "rent" and split utilities 50/50. 
*   **What is a fair rent amount?** 
    *   **Market Rate:** Look up what it would cost to rent a room in a similar house in your area, and pay that (or slightly less).
    *   **Proportional to Income:** If she makes $100k and you make $50k, you pay 1/3 of the household running costs.
    *   **Half the Mortgage Interest/Taxes:** You pay half of the "non-equity" costs (interest, property tax, insurance), while she pays 100% of the principal (the part that actually buys the house).
*   **Why this is fair:** She gets help with the monthly carrying costs, and you get a place to live that is likely cheaper than renting your own apartment. If you break up, you have no claim to the house, but you also didn't waste money buying her an asset.

### Option 2: The "Future Co-Owner" Model (Best if you want to buy in now)
If you have the cash and want to be an equal partner in this house from day one, you need to be on the paperwork.

*   **How it works:** You contribute to the down payment and/or the monthly mortgage, but **your name must be added to the deed** (title) of the house. 
*   **The Legal Catch:** If you do this before marriage, you **must** hire a real estate attorney to draft a **Cohabitation Agreement** (or a "Tenancy in Common" agreement). This document states exactly what happens to the house if you break up (e.g., "The house will be sold, and Partner A gets 60% of the equity, Partner B gets 40%").
*   **Why this is fair:** Your financial investment is legally protected. 

### Option 3: The "Wait and See" Model (Easiest/Safest)
She buys the house entirely on her own. She pays the down payment and the mortgage. You move in, but you do not pay "rent." Instead, you cover other household expenses.

*   **How it works:** She pays the mortgage. You pay for all the groceries, the internet, the utilities, and dinners out. 
*   **Why this is fair:** It keeps your money completely separate from her real estate asset, preventing any legal gray areas, while still ensuring you are contributing to your shared life.

---

### What happens when you get married?
Once you get married, the dynamic changes. You have two main options:

1.  **Refinance and Add Your Name:** Once married, you can refinance the mortgage to put both of your names on the loan and the deed. At this point, the house becomes "ours," and you both pay for it out of joint funds.
2.  **Keep it Separate:** If she wants to keep the house as her sole non-marital property, you continue with the "Rent" or "Shared Expenses" model, but you both agree that your joint savings will go toward other goals (like retirement, travel, or a future second home). *Note: State laws vary on whether a pre-marital home remains sole property after marriage if marital funds are used to pay the mortgage.*

### Summary: The Best Solution
For the next year (before you are married), **Option 1 (The "Fair Rent" Model)** is the best and safest solution. 

1.  **Do not pay her mortgage directly.**
2.  **Do pay a reasonable, agreed-upon "rent"** or cover the utilities and groceries to pull your weight.
3.  **Save the money** you would have spent on a mortgage in a high-yield savings account. Once you are married, you can use that saved money to pay down the principal of the house, renovate it, or use it as a joint nest egg. 

**The Conversation to Have:**
Sit down with her and say: *"I am so excited for you to buy this house, and I want to support you. Since we aren't married yet, I want to make sure we protect both of our finances. I’d love to pay a fair share of 'rent' or cover the utilities/groceries so I'm contributing, but keep the mortgage in your name until we get married. Once we are married, we can talk about adding my name to the deed and merging our housing payments. How do you feel about that?"*
