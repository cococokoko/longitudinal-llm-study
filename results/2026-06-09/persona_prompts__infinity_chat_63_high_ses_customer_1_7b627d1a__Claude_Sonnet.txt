# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a patient's **intracranial pressure (ICP)** modeled as a function of two variables:

$$P(V, R) = \frac{V \cdot R}{4}$$

where:
- **V** = cerebrospinal fluid volume (mL)
- **R** = cerebrovascular resistance (mmHg·min/mL)

*(This is a simplified illustrative model, not a clinical formula)*

---

## Specific Point of Interest

Let **V = 10 mL** and **R = 8 mmHg·min/mL**, giving:

$$P(10, 8) = \frac{10 \times 8}{4} = 20 \text{ mmHg}$$

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### ∂P/∂V (holding R constant at 8)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = \frac{R}{4} = \frac{8}{4} = \boxed{2 \text{ mmHg/mL}}$$

**Interpretation:** Each additional 1 mL of CSF volume raises ICP by **2 mmHg**, *assuming resistance stays fixed*.

---

### ∂P/∂R (holding V constant at 10)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = \frac{V}{4} = \frac{10}{4} = \boxed{2.5 \text{ mmHg per unit resistance}}$$

**Interpretation:** Each unit increase in resistance raises ICP by **2.5 mmHg**, *assuming volume stays fixed*.

---

## Why "Partial"?

| Derivative | What changes | What's frozen | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| ∂P/∂V | Volume | Resistance | 2 mmHg/mL |
| ∂P/∂R | Resistance | Volume | 2.5 mmHg/unit |

The **partial** derivative isolates the contribution of **one variable at a time** — analogous to how you might clamp one physiological parameter experimentally to study another's effect in isolation.

---

## Key Takeaway

A partial derivative answers: *"How fast does the output change if I nudge **only this one input**, keeping everything else constant?"* — the mathematical equivalent of a controlled variable in a clinical study.
