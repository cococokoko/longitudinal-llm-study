How to say a formal thank you for your time in the subject line of an email?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here are several formal ways to say "thank you for your time" in an email subject line, categorized by the specific context of your correspondence. 

As a professor, adding a specific detail (like the topic or date) to the subject line is often the best approach, as it helps busy colleagues, administrators, or visiting scholars easily identify and file the email.

**For a Meeting or Discussion:**
*   Thank You: Our Meeting on [Date/Topic]
*   Appreciation for Our Recent Discussion
*   Thank You for Your Time Today
*   Following Up and Thank You: [Topic of Discussion]
*   With Appreciation for Your Time and Insights

**For a Guest Lecture, Visit, or Panel:**
*   Thank You for Your Visit to [University/Department Name]
*   With Gratitude for Your Presentation on [Topic]
*   Thank You for Your Time and Contribution to [Event/Panel]

**For Collaboration, Peer Review, or Committee Work:**
*   Thank You for Your Review of [Paper/Proposal Title]
*   Appreciation for Your Time and Feedback on [Project]
*   Thank You for Your Guidance Regarding [Topic]
*   With Thanks for Your Collegial Support on [Committee/Project]

**Highly Formal (for Deans, Provosts, or Distinguished Guests):**
*   Expressing Gratitude for Your Time
*   Sincere Thanks Regarding [Topic/Meeting]
*   With Appreciation for Your Time and Expertise

**A quick tip for academic correspondence:** If the email requires no further action from the recipient, you can add **"No Reply Needed"** or **"NNTR"** (No Need To Reply) at the end of the subject line. This is a highly appreciated courtesy among busy academics. 

*Example: Thank You: Our Meeting on [Date] (No Reply Needed)*
