What is the difference between analysis and design? Can one begin to design without analysis? Why? Be concise.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Analysis and design are related but distinct activities:

- Analysis focuses on understanding the problem: goals, requirements, constraints, stakeholders, and current conditions. It answers, “What needs to be achieved?”
- Design focuses on creating a solution: architecture, components, processes, interfaces, and implementation approaches. It answers, “How will it be achieved?”

Can design begin without analysis?

- Yes, preliminary or exploratory design can begin with limited analysis, especially in innovation, prototyping, or when requirements are uncertain.
- However, effective design generally requires some level of analysis. Without understanding the problem, design decisions are likely to be misaligned with user needs, business objectives, or constraints.
- In practice, analysis and design are often iterative: early analysis informs design, and design work reveals new information that leads to further analysis.

In short: analysis defines the problem; design defines the solution. Some design can start before analysis is complete, but meaningful design depends on at least a basic understanding of the problem.
