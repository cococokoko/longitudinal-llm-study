Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The answer depends on what is meant by "equal pay for equal work."

If it means that people doing substantially the same job for the same employer, with similar qualifications, performance, and responsibilities, should receive the same pay, many economists and policymakers argue that this can improve fairness and reduce discrimination with relatively small economic costs.

Arguments against imposing it too rigidly include:

- Difficulty of defining "equal work"
  - Two employees with the same job title may differ in productivity, experience, reliability, sales performance, or specialized skills.
  - A strict rule may prevent employers from rewarding these differences.

- Reduced flexibility in labor markets
  - Employers sometimes pay different wages to attract scarce talent, retain valuable employees, or respond to local labor shortages.
  - Uniform pay rules can make hiring and retention harder.

- Administrative and compliance costs
  - Firms may need extensive documentation, audits, and legal review to justify pay differences.
  - These costs can be especially burdensome for smaller businesses.

- Potential effects on incentives
  - If pay cannot reflect differences in performance or contribution, some workers may have weaker incentives to improve productivity.

- Risk of unintended consequences
  - Employers may redesign job titles or responsibilities to fit regulations.
  - Some firms may become more cautious about hiring if they fear litigation over pay decisions.

On the other hand, arguments for equal-pay requirements include:

- Reducing discrimination based on sex, race, or other irrelevant characteristics.
- Increasing perceived fairness and workplace morale.
- Improving labor-market efficiency by ensuring compensation reflects work rather than bias.
- Strengthening trust in institutions and employers.

As a result, most modern economies do not choose between "no equal-pay rules" and "perfectly equal pay." Instead, they typically adopt a middle approach: employers are generally prohibited from paying workers differently for substantially equal work because of protected characteristics, while still allowing pay differences based on factors such as experience, performance, qualifications, location, or market conditions.
