Here is an incredible, highly relevant, and very affordable STEM fair project tailored to a major issue in Vietnam: 

### Project Title: **"From Waste to Pure: Using Rice Husks to Filter Polluted Water"**

### **The Vietnam Connection (The Problem)**
Vietnam faces two major environmental challenges:
1. **Water Pollution:** Rapid industrialization and agricultural runoff have severely polluted many rivers and canals, making access to clean water difficult in some rural areas.
2. **Agricultural Waste:** As one of the world's largest rice exporters, Vietnam produces millions of tons of rice husks every year. Often, these are burned, contributing to air pollution.

### **The Solution (The Project)**
This project turns agricultural waste into a life-saving technology. You will create **Biochar** (charcoal made from plant matter) out of rice husks and use it to build a low-cost, highly effective water filtration system. 

### **Why it’s an "Incredible" STEM Project:**
* **Affordable:** The materials are practically free. You just need empty plastic bottles, sand, gravel, and rice husks.
* **Visually Striking:** You can do a live demonstration where muddy, dirty water goes into the top of your filter and comes out crystal clear at the bottom.
* **Real-World Impact:** It demonstrates a circular economy—solving a water crisis by using a waste product.

---

### **Materials Needed (Under $10)**
* 3-4 large clear plastic water bottles (cut the bottoms off)
* Rice husks (can be obtained for free or very cheap from local farmers or markets)
* Sand and small gravel/pebbles
* Coffee filters or cotton balls
* "Dirty" water (mix tap water with dirt, a little cooking oil, and food coloring)
* *Optional but recommended:* A cheap water testing kit (pH and TDS meter) to get hard data.

### **The Experiment (The Scientific Method)**
To make this a true STEM project, you need to test a variable. 
* **Independent Variable:** The type of filter material used. 
* **Dependent Variable:** The clarity and purity of the filtered water.

**Set up 3 different filters to compare:**
1. **Filter A (Control):** Only sand and gravel.
2. **Filter B:** Sand, gravel, and *raw* (unburned) rice husks.
3. **Filter C:** Sand, gravel, and *Rice Husk Biochar* (rice husks that have been burned without oxygen to create carbon).

### **How to Make Rice Husk Biochar (Requires Adult Supervision)**
Place the rice husks in a metal tin (like an old cookie tin with a few small holes poked in the lid). Place the tin over a fire or grill for about 30-45 minutes. The lack of oxygen inside the tin stops the husks from turning to ash; instead, they turn into pure black carbon. Carbon is incredibly porous and traps toxins, heavy metals, and dyes!

### **How to Build the Filters**
1. Turn the cut plastic bottles upside down (cap pointing down).
2. Poke a small hole in the cap.
3. Layer 1 (Bottom): Put a coffee filter or cotton ball in the neck of the bottle.
4. Layer 2: Add 2 inches of gravel.
5. Layer 3: Add 2 inches of sand.
6. Layer 4: Add 3 inches of your test material (Sand for Filter A, Raw Husks for Filter B, Biochar for Filter C).
7. Layer 5 (Top): Add another inch of gravel to hold everything down.

### **Testing and Data Collection**
Pour exactly 2 cups of your "dirty water" into each filter. 
* **Measure Time:** How long does it take for the water to pass through?
* **Measure Clarity:** Put the filtered water in clear glasses side-by-side. Which is clearest? (Filter C should win by a landslide).
* **Measure Quality:** Use your water testing kit to measure the pH and Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) before and after filtration.

### **How to Wow the Judges at the Fair**
* **The Display:** Create a tri-fold board with photos of the Mekong Delta, rice farming, and water pollution. 
* **The Live Demo:** Have a pitcher of really gross-looking muddy water on your table. When judges walk by, pour it through your Biochar filter so they can watch it drip out clear in real-time.
* **The Pitch:** Explain that this technology could be scaled up in rural Vietnamese villages to provide cheap, sustainable drinking water while reducing the air pollution caused by burning rice waste in open fields.
