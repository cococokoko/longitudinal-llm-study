── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work in a warehouse position that requires long hours.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working long hours in a demanding warehouse job requires immense physical and mental stamina. Interestingly, the traits that make someone able to endure such grueling work—like a high pain tolerance, a relentless work ethic, and the ability to "just get through it" without complaining—can sometimes be connected to how they learned to survive and seek approval in childhood.

When a child does not receive sufficient love, emotional warmth, or validation from their mother (often referred to as maternal deprivation or emotional neglect), they develop specific coping mechanisms. These mechanisms carry over into adulthood, affecting their relationships, self-worth, and work habits.

Here are the key signs that a person may not have been loved or emotionally supported enough by their mother:

### 1. The "Over-Achiever" or "Workhorse" Mentality
*   **How it looks:** You feel like your worth is entirely tied to what you *do* rather than who you *are*. You might take on the hardest shifts, never call in sick, and push your body to its absolute limits.
*   **The childhood root:** If a mother’s love was conditional—only given when you got good grades, did chores perfectly, or didn't cause trouble—you learn that you must "earn" the right to exist and be loved. In adulthood, this translates into overworking to prove your value.

### 2. Extreme Self-Reliance (Hyper-Independence)
*   **How it looks:** You refuse to ask for help, even when you are physically struggling or overwhelmed. You might think, *"If I want it done right, I have to do it myself,"* or *"No one is going to help me anyway."*
*   **The childhood root:** When a primary caregiver is emotionally unavailable or unreliable, a child learns early on that they cannot depend on others for support. They shut down their vulnerability and decide to rely solely on themselves to survive.

### 3. Difficulty Setting Boundaries (People-Pleasing)
*   **How it looks:** You find it incredibly hard to say "no." If a manager asks you to stay late or take on extra physical labor, you agree even if your body is screaming for rest. You fear that saying no will make people dislike you or think you are lazy.
*   **The childhood root:** Children of emotionally neglectful mothers often learn to suppress their own needs to keep the peace or to keep their mother happy. They become highly attuned to other people's moods and prioritize others' comfort over their own safety and well-being.

### 4. A Harsh "Inner Critic" and Low Self-Esteem
*   **How it looks:** You are incredibly hard on yourself. If you make a minor mistake at work, you might beat yourself up for hours or days, feeling deeply ashamed rather than just accepting it as a normal human error.
*   **The childhood root:** A mother’s voice often becomes a child’s inner voice. If she was critical, cold, or absent, the child internalizes the belief that they are fundamentally flawed, incompetent, or "not enough."

### 5. Emotional Numbness or "Toughing It Out"
*   **How it looks:** You are disconnected from your physical and emotional pain. You might ignore injuries, exhaustion, or sadness, pushing them down to keep moving forward. You might find it hard to identify what you are feeling at any given moment.
*   **The childhood root:** If a mother ignores or invalidates a child's tears or pain (e.g., saying "stop crying" or "it's not a big deal"), the child learns to disconnect from their emotions as a survival strategy. 

### 6. Difficulty Trusting Others and Fear of Rejection
*   **How it looks:** In relationships (both romantic and platonic), you might keep people at a distance. You might constantly wait for the "other shoe to drop," expecting people to eventually abandon you, betray you, or realize they don't actually like you.
*   **The childhood root:** The mother-child bond is the blueprint for all future relationships. If that bond was insecure, unstable, or cold, it creates a core belief that people are unsafe and that love is fragile and temporary.

### How This Relates to Your Current Situation
Working long hours in a warehouse is physically taxing. If you recognize these signs in yourself, you might be using the grueling nature of your job to:
*   **Distract yourself** from painful internal feelings or loneliness.
*   **Validate your worth** through physical suffering and hard labor.
*   **Avoid intimacy** or social situations because work gives you a "valid" excuse to be unavailable.

### Moving Forward
Recognizing these signs is not about blaming your mother, but about understanding **why** you operate the way you do. 
*   **Practice self-compassion:** Try to treat yourself with the kindness you deserved as a child. 
*   **Listen to your body:** If you are exhausted, allow yourself to rest without feeling guilty. Rest is a biological necessity, not a reward you have to earn.
*   **Consider therapy:** If these patterns are causing you distress, talking to a therapist (especially one specializing in childhood trauma or emotional neglect) can help you rebuild your self-worth from the inside out.
