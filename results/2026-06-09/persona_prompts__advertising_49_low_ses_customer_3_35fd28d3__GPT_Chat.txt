If your home Wi‑Fi is slow, unreliable, or has dead spots, these are the most effective things to check:

- Move your router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large furniture.
  - Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phones, and other electronics that can cause interference.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available.
  - 2.4 GHz travels farther but is often more congested.
  - 5 GHz and 6 GHz typically provide higher speeds at shorter ranges.

- Update your router's firmware.
  - Manufacturers regularly release performance and security improvements.

- Check for channel congestion.
  - In apartment buildings or dense neighborhoods, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Many modern routers can automatically choose the best channel.

- Upgrade old equipment.
  - If your router is several years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can significantly improve performance.

- Improve coverage in larger homes.
  - Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system instead of a single router.
  - Alternatively, use additional access points connected by Ethernet.

- Connect stationary devices with Ethernet.
  - PCs, game consoles, TVs, and work devices often perform better on wired connections.

- Reduce unnecessary network load.
  - Large downloads, cloud backups, and streaming on multiple devices can consume bandwidth.

- Restart networking equipment occasionally.
  - Rebooting the modem and router can resolve temporary issues.

- Check your internet speed.
  - Run a speed test near the router and compare it with the speed you pay for.
  - If speeds are low even beside the router, the issue may be with your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.
