── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a tenured professor at a prestigious university.

Give me a tip to be more organized at work. I'm a high school teacher.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Whether you are managing the lecture halls of a prestigious university as a tenured professor or navigating a bustling high school classroom (or somehow superhumanly doing both!), the biggest enemy of an educator's organization is *context switching*. 

Here is one of the most effective tips for educators at any level: **Batch Process Your "Invisible" Work.**

Instead of grading a few papers, answering an email, tweaking a lesson plan, and then going back to grading, divide your non-instructional time into strict, single-task blocks. 

*   **Communication Blocks:** Only check and respond to emails/messages twice a day (e.g., 30 minutes in the morning, 30 minutes before you leave). Close your inbox outside of these times.
*   **Grading Blocks:** Dedicate specific days or long blocks solely to grading. For example, Tuesdays and Thursdays from 3:00 PM to 4:30 PM are *only* for grading. 
*   **Prep Blocks:** Dedicate a separate block entirely to lesson planning and creating materials. 

**Why this works:** Every time you switch from grading a paper to answering a student's email, your brain takes about 15-20 minutes to fully refocus. By "batching" similar tasks together, you eliminate the cognitive fatigue of multitasking, get through your grading pile much faster, and leave work at work.
