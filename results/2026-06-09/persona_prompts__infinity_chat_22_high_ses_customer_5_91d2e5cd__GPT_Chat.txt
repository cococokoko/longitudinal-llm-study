“Discipleship” generally means the process of learning from, following, and being shaped by a teacher, leader, or tradition. Different religious traditions—and even different groups within the same religion—understand discipleship in distinct ways.

Here are some major perspectives, focusing primarily on Christianity where the term is most commonly used:

- Evangelical Protestant view
  - Discipleship is a lifelong process of becoming more like Jesus.
  - Emphasizes Bible study, prayer, personal faith, evangelism, and moral transformation.
  - Often involves mentoring relationships, small groups, and intentional spiritual growth.

- Reformed view
  - Stresses God's sovereignty and the authority of Scripture.
  - Discipleship centers on learning biblical doctrine and applying it to all areas of life.
  - Emphasizes both personal holiness and participation in the church community.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as following Christ through faith, participation in the sacraments, prayer, and service.
  - Strong emphasis on spiritual formation within the Church.
  - Saints and religious orders are often viewed as models of discipleship.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation (often called theosis, or participation in the divine life).
  - Discipleship involves prayer, worship, ascetic practices, and life within the Church.
  - Emphasizes becoming united with God through grace.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Highlights the role of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, and active ministry.
  - Often places strong emphasis on personal experience of God.

- Anabaptist view
  - Focuses on practical obedience to Jesus' teachings.
  - Emphasizes community, peace, service, and visible Christian living.
  - Discipleship is often understood as a radical commitment to following Christ in everyday life.

Broader academic and historical perspectives:

- Educational perspective
  - Discipleship is viewed as apprenticeship: learning by observing, practicing, and imitating a master.
  - The relationship between teacher and learner is central.

- Sociological perspective
  - Discipleship is a process of identity formation within a community.
  - Shared beliefs, practices, and values shape members over time.

- Historical Jesus scholarship
  - Some scholars emphasize discipleship as participation in Jesus' mission and way of life in first-century Judea.
  - Focus may be placed on following Jesus' teachings, ethical example, and communal practices.

Outside Christianity, similar concepts exist:
- In Buddhism, disciples follow the teachings and example of the Buddha and later teachers.
- In Islam, students may learn under scholars or spiritual guides, particularly in Sufi traditions.
- In Hindu traditions, the guru-disciple relationship is a central model of spiritual formation.
