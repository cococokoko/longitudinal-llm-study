── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, your professional life is defined by high cognitive load, unpredictable schedules, and a need for efficiency. To succeed in the kitchen, you should approach cooking not as an art or a chore, but as a **system design problem**. 

By applying the principles of clinical triage, laboratory optimization, and protocol-driven execution, you can prepare highly nutritious meals in minimal time.

Here is a framework designed specifically for your analytical, time-constrained lifestyle.

---

### 1. The "Modular" Meal System (Deconstruct the Plate)
Do not try to cook complex, single-recipe dishes (like a lasagna or a complex curry) on weeknights. Instead, treat your meals as an assembly of pre-optimized modules. 

Every healthy meal is simply a combination of three macronutrient/micronutrient blocks:
$$\text{Meal} = \text{Clean Protein} + \text{Complex Carbohydrate} + \text{Fibrous Vegetable} + \text{Flavor Vehicle}$$

If you have these modules prepped in your fridge, dinner is just a 2-minute assembly and reheat process.

### 2. Apply "Batching" (The Sunday Lab Prep)
In a lab, you wouldn't run a PCR reaction for one sample at a time; you run a 96-well plate. Apply this to your food. Dedicate **90 minutes on Sunday** to batch-process your base ingredients.

*   **Proteins (The Rate-Limiting Step):** 
    *   Bake 4-5 chicken breasts, roast a side of salmon, or boil a dozen eggs. 
    *   *Time-saver:* Buy rotisserie chickens, remove the skin, and shred the meat immediately.
*   **Carbohydrates (The Substrate):** 
    *   Cook a massive batch of quinoa, brown rice, or farro in a rice cooker (completely hands-off).
    *   Sweet potatoes: Prick them with a fork and microwave them for 5–7 minutes when ready to eat (no pre-baking required).
*   **Vegetables (The Micronutrients):**
    *   **Roast Sheet:** Chop broccoli, Brussels sprouts, and bell peppers. Toss in olive oil, salt, and pepper, and roast at 400°F (200°C) for 20 minutes. 
    *   **The Raw Shortcut:** Buy pre-washed baby spinach, arugula, and coleslaw mixes. No chopping required.

### 3. Leverage High-Yield Kitchen Hardware
Invest in tools that reduce active "standing-at-the-stove" time. Your goal is to maximize **passive cooking time**.

*   **The Air Fryer:** This is the ultimate physician-scientist tool. It is essentially a high-powered convection oven that requires zero preheating. You can cook a salmon fillet or chicken breast from raw to perfect in 10–12 minutes, or roast vegetables in 8 minutes.
*   **The Instant Pot (Pressure Cooker):** Excellent for hands-off cooking of tough meats, stews, or grains. You can throw frozen chicken breasts, salsa, and black beans into it, press a button, and walk away to read a journal article. 
*   **The High-Speed Blender (e.g., Vitamix):** For 5-minute nutrient-dense breakfasts. 
    *   *Protocol:* Whey/plant protein + spinach + frozen berries + chia seeds + almond milk.

### 4. Optimize Your "Reagent" Pantry (Flavor Vehicles)
Healthy food tastes bland without acids, fats, and salt. To avoid spending time making sauces, keep high-quality, low-calorie, pre-made flavor enhancers on hand:
*   **Acids/Vinegars:** Balsamic glaze, apple cider vinegar, fresh lemons/limes.
*   **Healthy Fats:** Extra virgin olive oil, avocado oil, tahini, pesto.
*   **Umami/Spice:** Low-sodium soy sauce (or liquid aminos), Sriracha, Dijon mustard, salsa, and kimchi.
*   **Dry Spices:** Garlic powder, smoked paprika, cumin, and Italian seasoning. (These add zero calories but massive flavor instantly).

### 5. The "Under 15-Minute" Emergency Protocols
When you come home late after a grueling day of clinic or grant writing, you need "no-cook" or "micro-cook" options to prevent ordering takeout.

*   **Protocol A: The Mediterranean Bowl (Zero Cooking)**
    *   *Base:* Pre-washed arugula.
    *   *Protein:* Canned tuna (in olive oil) or canned chickpeas (rinsed).
    *   *Additions:* Halved cherry tomatoes, kalamata olives, crumbled feta cheese.
    *   *Dressing:* Squeeze of lemon juice, splash of olive oil, dried oregano.
*   **Protocol B: The 10-Minute Stir-Fry**
    *   *Base:* Microwaveable pouch of brown rice (90 seconds).
    *   *Protein:* Pre-cooked chicken strips or firm tofu (cubed).
    *   *Vegetables:* Frozen stir-fry vegetable mix (microwaved or quickly pan-seared).
    *   *Sauce:* 1 tbsp soy sauce + 1 tsp sriracha + 1 tsp sesame oil.
*   **Protocol C: The Elevated Scramble**
    *   Whisk 3 eggs (or 1 whole egg + 3 egg whites for higher protein/lower fat).
    *   Throw a handful of baby spinach and cherry tomatoes into a pan, let them wilt for 1 minute, pour in the eggs, and scramble. 
    *   Serve with half an avocado.

### 6. Cognitive Offloading: Outsource the Logistics
As a PI, you know the value of delegation. If your budget allows, outsource the lowest-value tasks (grocery shopping and chopping).
*   **Grocery Delivery:** Use Instacart or Amazon Fresh. Set up a "recurring cart" with your weekly staples (eggs, chicken, spinach, berries, avocados, oats). Never spend an hour walking down grocery aisles.
*   **Pre-Prepped Ingredients:** Buy pre-chopped onions, butternut squash, and washed greens. The extra cost is a highly efficient trade-off for your limited time.

### Summary for the Physician-Scientist:
1.  **Standardize the inputs:** Keep the same 10–12 staple ingredients in rotation.
2.  **Batch the processing:** Cook once, eat 4 times.
3.  **Automate the execution:** Use the air fryer and microwave to eliminate active cooking time.
4.  **Analyze and iterate:** Treat your weekly meals like a protocol. If a meal took too long or tasted poor, identify the bottleneck and adjust the variable next week.
