── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney, your career requires you to be highly analytical, objective, and adept at identifying patterns and root causes. When turning that same analytical lens inward toward your own childhood, it can be a profound and sometimes painful process. 

In psychology, a lack of adequate love, warmth, or emotional attunement from a primary caregiver (often the mother) is referred to as "childhood emotional neglect" or an "attachment wound." Because you are a high-achieving professional, the signs might not look like failure or dysfunction; instead, they often manifest as highly adaptive survival mechanisms.

Here are common signs in adulthood that indicate you may not have received the emotional nourishment you needed from your mother as a child:

### 1. The "Overachiever" Dynamic (Performance as Worth)
Many children who do not feel inherently loved learn that they can "earn" attention or approval through success. As a senior attorney, you may have built a highly successful career, but you might feel that your worth is entirely tied to your output, your billable hours, or your victories. You may suffer from severe Imposter Syndrome or feel that if you stop achieving, you will become unlovable or invisible.

### 2. Hyper-Independence
If your mother was emotionally unavailable, unreliable, or dismissive, you likely learned early on that you could only rely on yourself. In adulthood, this looks like an extreme reluctance to ask for help, difficulty delegating (even when you are overwhelmed), and a deep-seated belief that depending on others is dangerous or a sign of weakness.

### 3. A Harsh Inner Critic
Children internalize the voice of their primary caregivers. If your mother was highly critical, emotionally cold, or absent, you may have developed a relentless inner critic. You might hold yourself to impossible standards of perfectionism and speak to yourself in a way you would never speak to a colleague or friend. 

### 4. Difficulty Identifying and Expressing Emotions
If your emotions were ignored, mocked, or punished when you were a child, you may have learned to suppress them. You might find it easy to intellectualize situations (a great skill in law, but difficult in personal life) but struggle to answer the simple question: *"What am I feeling right now?"* You might also feel a chronic, lingering sense of emptiness or numbness.

### 5. Attachment and Relationship Struggles
The relationship with a mother sets the template for future intimacy. A lack of maternal love often results in insecure attachment styles:
*   **Anxious Attachment:** A constant fear of abandonment, overthinking texts/conversations, and people-pleasing to keep partners around.
*   **Avoidant Attachment:** Pushing people away when they get too close, feeling suffocated by intimacy, or choosing emotionally unavailable partners because it feels "familiar."

### 6. Difficulty Setting Boundaries (or Rigid Boundaries)
You may struggle with people-pleasing, fearing that saying "no" will result in a withdrawal of love or cause conflict. Conversely, you might have incredibly rigid boundaries, keeping everyone at a distance to protect yourself from being hurt or disappointed again.

### 7. Discomfort with Nurturing or Affection
If you didn't receive warmth, physical affection, or soothing as a child, receiving it as an adult can feel incredibly uncomfortable. You might feel awkward when someone tries to comfort you, or you might be suspicious of people who are "too nice," waiting for the other shoe to drop.

### 8. A Deep Sense of Being "Fundamentally Flawed"
Children are egocentric by nature. If a mother does not love a child adequately, the child does not think, *"My mother has psychological limitations."* Instead, the child thinks, *"There is something wrong with me that makes me unlovable."* This can carry into adulthood as a pervasive, hidden feeling of being fundamentally broken or different from everyone else.

**A Note on Moving Forward:**
Recognizing these patterns is the first step in dismantling them. High-achieving professionals often carry these burdens silently because their external success masks their internal distress. If these signs resonate with you, you might find it incredibly beneficial to work with a therapist who specializes in attachment theory, childhood emotional neglect (CEN), or trauma. You have spent your life advocating for others; it is entirely appropriate to seek out someone to help advocate for you.
