── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common situation for modern couples, but it can be tricky because it mixes romance with legal and financial realities. Because you are not married yet, the house will legally be considered her pre-marital asset. 

The "best solution" depends on whether you will be living with her before the wedding, and how you plan to handle finances once you are married. Here is a breakdown of the fairest ways to handle this, split into two phases: before marriage and after marriage.

### Phase 1: Before Marriage (The Next Year)
Until you are legally married, you should protect yourself financially while still being a supportive partner. 

*   **If you are living together:** It is absolutely fair for you to contribute to living expenses, but you should frame it as **paying rent**, not "paying her mortgage." You would have to pay rent to live anywhere else. Agree on a fair monthly amount (often half the mortgage, or a percentage based on your respective incomes) and split utilities and groceries. 
*   **Do not pay for capital improvements:** Before you are married, you should *not* pay for major renovations, a new roof, or the down payment. If you break up, you will have no legal right to get that money back. Maintenance and upgrades are the responsibility of the homeowner (her).
*   **If you are NOT living together:** You should not be paying for her house at all. Keep your finances separate until you move in together or get married.

### Phase 2: After Marriage
Once you are married, the dynamic changes. You need to sit down *now* and discuss what will happen to the house after you tie the knot. You generally have two options:

*   **Option A: It becomes a shared asset.** After you get married, she can add your name to the deed (and potentially refinance the mortgage to include you). At this point, you both own the house, and it is entirely fair for you to contribute equally to the mortgage, repairs, and renovations. 
*   **Option B: It remains her separate property.** Some couples prefer to keep pre-marital assets separate. If she wants the house to remain solely in her name, you should continue treating your contribution as "rent." However, you will need to agree on how you will build your own wealth, since she will be building equity in a house and you will not. 

### The Best Solution: A Cohabitation Agreement
The absolute best way to handle this is to have an open, honest conversation right now and put it in writing. A **cohabitation agreement** (drawn up by a lawyer) is highly recommended. It acts like a pre-nup for unmarried couples living together. 

It should outline:
1. How much "rent" and utilities you will pay.
2. That your rent does *not* entitle you to equity in the home if you break up.
3. What the plan is once you get married (e.g., "Upon marriage, Partner A will be added to the deed").

**How to start the conversation:**
Say something like: *"I'm so proud of you for buying this house, and I can't wait to build our future together. Since we aren't married yet, I want to make sure we figure out a financial arrangement for the next year that feels fair to both of us, and talk about how we'll handle the house once we're married."*
