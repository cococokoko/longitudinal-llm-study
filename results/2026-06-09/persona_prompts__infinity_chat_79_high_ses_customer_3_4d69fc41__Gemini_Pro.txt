### **Project Title:** 
**"From Waste to Clean Water: Synthesizing Low-Cost Bio-Adsorbents from Rice Husks to Remediate Industrial Textile Dyes"**

### **The Vietnam Connection:**
Vietnam faces two intersecting realities:
1. **Agricultural Abundance:** Vietnam is one of the world's largest exporters of rice, producing millions of tons of rice husk waste annually. Often, this waste is burned, contributing to Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City's severe PM2.5 air pollution.
2. **Water Pollution:** Vietnam's booming textile and manufacturing industries frequently discharge untreated or poorly treated wastewater (containing synthetic dyes and heavy metals) into river systems, threatening local ecosystems and agriculture.

### **The Core Experiment:**
Students will convert raw rice husks into a bio-adsorbent (biochar) to filter out synthetic dyes from water, modeling a localized, circular-economy solution to industrial pollution.

**Materials Needed (Highly Affordable):**
*   Rice husks (virtually free/easily sourced)
*   Methylene blue or standard food coloring (to simulate textile effluent)
*   A heat source (an oven or a safe outdoor fire pit to carbonize the husks)
*   Basic glassware or clear plastic bottles
*   A spectrophotometer (if available in your university lab) or a smartphone camera with a colorimetry app (for a low-cost, accessible alternative).

### **The Methodology (Tiered for Rigor):**
1. **Preparation:** Students wash, dry, and thermally treat (pyrolyze) the rice husks to create biochar. They can test different carbonization temperatures or chemical activations (e.g., soaking in a mild citric acid solution) to see how it affects porosity.
2. **The Assay:** Create a standardized "polluted" water sample using the dye. 
3. **Filtration & Kinetics:** Introduce the rice husk biochar to the polluted water. 
4. **Data Collection:** Measure the concentration of the dye over time. If using a smartphone, students can use the RGB values of the water's photos to create a standard calibration curve, applying Beer-Lambert’s Law to calculate the concentration of the remaining dye.

### **Why this stands out for a STEM Fair:**
* **Interdisciplinary:** It combines environmental science, chemistry (adsorption kinetics), and technology (using smartphone colorimetry for data analysis).
* **Mathematical Modeling:** Instead of just showing that the water gets cleaner, students can plot adsorption isotherms (Langmuir or Freundlich models) and calculate the maximum adsorption capacity of the husks. This level of data analysis will highly impress STEM fair judges.
* **Scalability:** It demonstrates a real-world, decentralized solution. Farmers could theoretically use their own agricultural waste to filter local water sources before using them for irrigation.

### **How You Can Guide Them:**
As a professor, you can guide the students in structuring their presentation like a formal research poster. You can teach them about independent/dependent variables, the importance of control groups (e.g., comparing the biochar to commercial activated carbon), and statistical significance (calculating standard deviation across multiple trials). 

This project is incredibly cheap to execute, but the *science* behind it is rich, publishable in student journals, and directly addresses a critical sustainable development goal in Vietnam.
