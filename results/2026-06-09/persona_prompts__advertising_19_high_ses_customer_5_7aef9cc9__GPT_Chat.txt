How can I improve my mental health?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving mental health is usually less about finding a single solution and more about building a set of habits, supports, and perspectives that work together over time.

A practical framework:

- Prioritize sleep
  - Aim for a consistent sleep schedule.
  - Reduce alcohol and heavy meals close to bedtime.
  - Keep screens and stimulating work out of the last hour before sleep when possible.
  - Poor sleep can significantly affect mood, resilience, concentration, and stress tolerance.

- Exercise regularly
  - Even 20–30 minutes of walking most days can help.
  - Strength training and aerobic exercise are both associated with better mental well-being.
  - Consistency matters more than intensity.

- Strengthen relationships
  - Invest in a few meaningful connections rather than maximizing the number of contacts.
  - Schedule time with friends, family, mentors, or peers.
  - Social support is one of the strongest predictors of long-term mental health.

- Manage stress deliberately
  - Identify the specific sources of stress rather than treating stress as one large problem.
  - Create boundaries around work, especially if you have a high-responsibility role.
  - Consider practices such as mindfulness, meditation, journaling, or reflective walks.

- Pay attention to your thinking patterns
  - Notice recurring self-criticism, catastrophizing, or all-or-nothing thinking.
  - Ask whether the evidence supports your conclusions.
  - Cognitive behavioral techniques can be very effective for many people.

- Create recovery time
  - High performers often schedule meetings and deadlines but not recovery.
  - Include time for hobbies, learning, nature, creativity, or simply unstructured downtime.
  - Mental health tends to improve when effort and recovery are both intentional.

- Maintain physical health
  - Eat regularly and nutritiously.
  - Address chronic pain, medical conditions, or substance use issues.
  - Physical and mental health are closely connected.

- Clarify purpose and values
  - Many people experience stress when their daily activities drift away from what matters most to them.
  - Periodically review:
    - What is important to me?
    - Where am I spending my time?
    - Are those aligned?

- Consider professional support
  - Therapy is not only for crises; it can help with stress management, relationships, decision-making, and personal growth.
  - If symptoms are persistent or severe, a licensed mental health professional can provide tailored guidance.

For executives and senior leaders specifically:
- Protect uninterrupted thinking time.
- Be cautious about being "always available."
- Build a trusted circle where you can speak candidly.
- Delegate decisions that do not require your direct involvement.
- Monitor whether chronic pressure is becoming chronic exhaustion.
