── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Thinking about retirement this early is a fantastic financial move. 

Because you are on probation, your strategy should balance **building long-term wealth** with **protecting yourself in the short term** (since job security is slightly lower during a probationary period). 

Here is a step-by-step guide to the best ways to save for retirement in your specific situation:

### 1. Prioritize an Emergency Fund First
Before locking your money away in retirement accounts (which often have penalties for early withdrawal), make sure you have a safety net. Because you are on probation, if the job doesn't work out, you will need cash to pay rent and buy groceries while you look for a new one.
*   **The Goal:** Save 3 to 6 months of essential living expenses.
*   **Where to put it:** A High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA). These accounts currently pay around 4% to 5% interest, and you can access the money at any time without penalty.

### 2. Check Your Employer’s 401(k) Rules
Many employers offer a 401(k) or 403(b) retirement plan, but the rules vary widely regarding new hires.
*   **Check Eligibility:** Some companies let you contribute on day one; others make you wait until your probation is over (usually 90 days to 6 months). If you have to wait, set a calendar reminder for the exact date you become eligible.
*   **Get the Match:** If your employer offers a "match" (e.g., they match 100% of your contributions up to 5% of your salary), **contribute exactly that amount as soon as you are allowed.** This is literally free money. 
*   **Check the Vesting Schedule:** "Vesting" means how long you have to stay at the company to keep the employer's matched money. If you leave or are let go during probation, you will keep your own contributions, but you might lose the employer match if you aren't fully vested.

### 3. Open an Individual Retirement Account (IRA)
If you cannot use your employer's 401(k) yet, or if you want to save more than the employer match, open an IRA. This account is tied to *you*, not your employer, so it doesn't matter if you are on probation or change jobs.
*   **Roth IRA:** You contribute money that has already been taxed. The money grows tax-free, and you pay zero taxes when you withdraw it in retirement. (This is usually the best option for young professionals or those in lower tax brackets).
*   **Traditional IRA:** You contribute pre-tax money (which lowers your tax bill this year), but you pay taxes on it when you withdraw it in retirement.
*   **Where to open one:** Fidelity, Vanguard, or Charles Schwab are excellent, low-cost options. The maximum you can contribute to an IRA in 2024 is $7,000.

### 4. Actually *Invest* the Money
A common mistake beginners make is putting money into a 401(k) or IRA and leaving it in cash. You have to choose investments for that money to grow.
*   **The easiest option:** Choose a **Target Date Index Fund** (e.g., "Target Retirement 2060"). You pick the year closest to when you plan to retire, and the fund automatically manages the mix of stocks and bonds for you. It is the ultimate "set it and forget it" strategy.

### Summary Action Plan for Your Probationary Period:
1.  Funnel extra cash into a High-Yield Savings Account until you have a 3-month emergency fund.
2.  Read your employee handbook to find out when you are eligible for the 401(k) and if there is a company match.
3.  If eligible now, contribute enough to get the match. 
4.  If not eligible yet, open a Roth IRA and set up an automatic monthly transfer of $50, $100, or whatever you can comfortably afford.
