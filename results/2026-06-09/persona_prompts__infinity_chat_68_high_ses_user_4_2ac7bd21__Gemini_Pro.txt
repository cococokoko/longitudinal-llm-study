── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

Give me a tip for managing a team of coworkers.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, your most scarce resource is time. Because you are constantly pulled between clinical duties, grant writing, and research, your lab can easily suffer from the **"PI Bottleneck"**—where progress halts because your team is waiting on your approval, edits, or input. 

Here is the most effective tip for your specific role: **Implement a "Tiered Autonomy and Mentorship" structure.**

Here is how to execute it:

**1. Establish "Zones of Autonomy"**
Explicitly define what decisions your team can make without you, and what requires your direct approval. 
*   *Green Zone (No approval needed):* Troubleshooting standard assays, ordering routine supplies under a certain budget, scheduling clinical research follow-ups.
*   *Yellow Zone (Decide and inform):* Modifying a minor protocol step, submitting an abstract to a local conference. They make the call, but send you a quick update.
*   *Red Zone (PI approval mandatory):* Changing a primary endpoint, spending over a specific dollar amount, submitting a manuscript, or altering an IRB protocol.

**2. Create a Tiered Mentorship Model**
You cannot be the primary day-to-day mentor for every undergraduate, medical student, or new tech. Pair junior members with senior members (postdocs, senior clinical research coordinators, or experienced PhD students). The rule should be: *“Ask your direct mentor first. If you both can’t solve it, bring it to me.”* This empowers your senior staff to develop leadership skills while protecting your time.

**3. Use Structured, Asynchronous Updates**
Because your clinical schedule can be unpredictable, rely on asynchronous communication. Have your team members (or sub-team leads) send a structured "Friday Update" email with three bullet points:
*   What I accomplished this week.
*   What I plan to do next week.
*   *Where I am stuck and need your input.*

**Why this works for you:** It keeps the research moving while you are on service or in the clinic, prevents you from micromanaging, and trains your senior team members to become independent investigators themselves.
