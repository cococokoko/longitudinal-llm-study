Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
First, thank you for your work as a patient care aide! Hospitals couldn't function without the hands-on care you provide. 

In graph theory, a "linear graph" is more commonly called a **Path Graph**. It is a graph where all the points (called **vertices** or **nodes**) are connected in a single, straight sequence by lines (called **edges**), with no branches, loops, or intersections. 

Here is an example tailored to your work at the hospital:

### The Hospital Hallway Example
Imagine you are doing your rounds to check patient vitals in a specific hallway. There are four patient rooms at the end of the hall: Room 101, Room 102, Room 103, and Room 104, in that exact order.

If we turn this into a graph:
*   **The Vertices (Nodes):** Each patient room is a dot on the graph. (Node A = 101, Node B = 102, Node C = 103, Node D = 104).
*   **The Edges (Lines):** The physical hallway connecting one room to the very next room is the line between the dots.

**The Graph looks like this:**
(Room 101) —— (Room 102) —— (Room 103) —— (Room 104)

### Why is this a Path (Linear) Graph?
1.  **No branching:** You walk from 101 to 102 to 103 to 104. There are no side-hallways in this specific model.
2.  **Degrees of connection:** In a linear graph, the "endpoints" (Room 101 and Room 104) only connect to *one* other room. The "middle points" (Room 102 and Room 103) connect to exactly *two* rooms (the one before it, and the one after it). 
3.  **No cycles:** You don't walk in a circle. To get back to Room 101 from Room 104, you have to retrace your exact steps. 

Another quick example from your shift could be a **linear workflow**: 
*(Clock In)* —— *(Receive Handoff)* —— *(Check Vitals)* —— *(Chart Data)*. 
Each task is a node, and the transition to the next task is the edge, forming a perfect linear graph!
