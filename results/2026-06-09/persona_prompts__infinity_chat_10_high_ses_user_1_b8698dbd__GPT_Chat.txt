── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is that there is no strong, consistent correlation between intelligence and compassion in humans.

A few distinctions matter:

- Intelligence is usually measured through cognitive abilities such as reasoning, problem-solving, learning, and abstract thinking.
- Compassion refers to concern for others' suffering combined with a motivation to help.
- Empathy is often divided into:
  - Cognitive empathy: understanding another person's perspective or mental state.
  - Affective empathy: sharing or resonating with another person's emotions.

What the research generally shows:

- IQ and compassion:
  - Studies do not find a robust positive correlation between IQ and compassionate behavior.
  - Highly intelligent individuals can be very compassionate, indifferent, or even exploitative; intelligence alone does not predict moral concern.

- IQ and cognitive empathy:
  - There is some evidence that higher cognitive ability is associated with better perspective-taking and theory of mind in many contexts.
  - However, this relationship is modest and not universal.

- IQ and affective empathy/compassion:
  - Findings are weaker and often inconsistent.
  - Being better at understanding others does not necessarily mean caring more about them.

- Emotional intelligence:
  - Measures of emotional intelligence sometimes correlate more strongly with empathy and prosocial behavior than traditional IQ measures do.
  - Even here, correlations tend to be moderate rather than large.

Neuroscientifically, the systems involved are only partially overlapping.

- General intelligence is often associated with frontoparietal networks involved in executive function and abstract reasoning.
- Compassion and affective empathy involve networks including medial prefrontal cortex, anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex, temporoparietal junction, and limbic structures.
- Damage or dysfunction can dissociate these capacities. One can retain high intellectual ability while showing markedly reduced compassion, and vice versa.

An interesting observation from moral psychology is that intelligence may amplify a person's existing goals rather than determine them. A highly intelligent person may become exceptionally altruistic or exceptionally self-interested depending on personality, values, incentives, and socialization.

If one were forced to summarize the literature in a single sentence: intelligence is somewhat related to the ability to understand other people, but it is only weakly related—if at all—to the degree of compassion one feels or expresses toward them.
