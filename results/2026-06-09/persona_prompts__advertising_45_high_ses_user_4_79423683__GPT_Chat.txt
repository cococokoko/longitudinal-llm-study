── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have a skill set that transfers well to DIY: breaking complex systems into components, following procedures, troubleshooting, and learning from reference material. The main challenge is acquiring practical experience and developing an intuition for how buildings and tools behave.

A structured approach works well:

1. Learn the fundamentals of how a house works
- Start with the major systems:
  - Electrical
  - Plumbing
  - HVAC
  - Structural/framing
  - Roofing and exterior envelope
  - Drywall, paint, and finishes
- Understanding these systems is often more important than learning isolated repairs.

Good resources:
- "The Complete Do-It-Yourself Manual" (Family Handyman)
- "Renovation" by Michael Litchfield
- This Old House (website and YouTube)
- Fine Homebuilding
- Family Handyman
- Home Repair Tutor (especially for bathrooms and tile)

2. Build tool literacy
Learn:
- How to use a drill/driver
- Circular saw
- Oscillating multi-tool
- Stud finder
- Level
- Basic plumbing tools
- Electrical tester/multimeter

You do not need a large workshop initially. A basic toolkit and cordless drill can accomplish a surprising number of projects.

3. Progress through projects of increasing complexity
Begin with low-risk projects:
- Caulking
- Painting
- Replacing fixtures
- Installing shelves
- Replacing door hardware
- Weatherstripping
- Basic drywall patching

Then:
- Replacing faucets
- Installing ceiling fans
- Building simple storage
- Minor trim carpentry
- Toilet replacement

Later:
- Tile work
- Cabinet installation
- Flooring
- Moderate electrical and plumbing projects

4. Learn from project-specific videos
For each project:
- Watch 3–5 different videos.
- Compare techniques.
- Read the manufacturer's instructions.
- Understand failure modes before starting.

This resembles reviewing multiple papers before designing an experiment.

5. Develop a safety framework
Know when to stop and call a professional.

High-risk areas include:
- Service-panel electrical work
- Gas lines
- Structural modifications
- Roof work at height
- Major water-intrusion issues
- Foundation problems

Many experienced DIYers can perform these tasks, but the consequences of mistakes are much larger.

6. Use your own home as a laboratory
When something breaks:
- Diagnose it yourself first.
- Research the issue.
- Decide whether it is within your competence.
- Even if you hire someone, watch and ask questions.

This accelerates learning substantially.

7. Learn building science
Many DIY mistakes stem from misunderstanding moisture, ventilation, insulation, and heat transfer.

Resources:
- Green Building Advisor
- Building Science Corporation
- Fine Homebuilding articles on moisture management

For a scientifically minded person, building science is often one of the most intellectually satisfying aspects of home improvement.

8. Consider hands-on instruction
Options:
- Community college trade courses
- Adult education programs
- Habitat for Humanity volunteer builds
- Maker spaces
- Woodworking classes

Hands-on repetition often teaches more in a weekend than dozens of videos.

9. Create a "house systems notebook"
Keep:
- Photos of wiring and plumbing
- Paint colors
- Appliance model numbers
- Repair records
- Measurements
- Maintenance schedules

Treat it like a lab notebook for your building.

10. Accept that DIY proficiency is experiential
Reading and videos create understanding; competence comes from repeatedly measuring, cutting, fastening, leveling, troubleshooting, and fixing mistakes.

A practical progression for the next year could be:
- Month 1–2: learn tools, painting, caulking, drywall patching
- Month 3–4: replace faucets, toilet components, light fixtures
- Month 5–6: build shelving and simple carpentry projects
- Month 7–9: flooring or tile projects
- Month 10–12: larger room refreshes involving multiple trades
