How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to optimizing placement, reducing interference, and ensuring the equipment matches your needs. A practical approach:

1. Optimize router placement
- Place the router in a central location.
- Keep it elevated (on a shelf rather than the floor).
- Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large furniture.
- Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, and other electronics.

2. Use the right Wi‑Fi band
- 5 GHz: Faster speeds, shorter range.
- 2.4 GHz: Longer range, lower speeds, more interference.
- 6 GHz (Wi‑Fi 6E/7 devices): Fastest and least congested, but shortest range.

3. Update equipment and software
- Install the latest router firmware.
- Consider upgrading older routers (e.g., Wi‑Fi 5) to Wi‑Fi 6, 6E, or 7 if many devices are connected.

4. Improve channel selection
- In dense neighborhoods or apartment buildings, neighboring networks can cause congestion.
- Many modern routers automatically select channels, but manually choosing a less crowded channel can help.

5. Strengthen coverage
- For larger homes, consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system rather than a single router.
- If possible, connect mesh nodes via Ethernet backhaul for better performance.

6. Use wired connections where practical
- Connect stationary devices (TVs, gaming consoles, desktop PCs) via Ethernet.
- This reduces Wi‑Fi congestion and improves reliability.

7. Check your internet plan
- Sometimes the issue is the broadband connection rather than Wi‑Fi.
- Run a speed test next to the router and compare it with tests from distant rooms.

8. Manage device load
- Disconnect unused devices.
- Enable Quality of Service (QoS) if your router supports it to prioritize video calls, conferencing, or streaming.

9. Review security settings
- Use WPA3 (or WPA2 if WPA3 is unavailable).
- Avoid outdated security protocols that can reduce performance and security.

10. Measure before and after
- Use a Wi‑Fi analyzer app or your router’s diagnostics to identify weak-signal areas and verify improvements.
