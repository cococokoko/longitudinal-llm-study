Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few key variables: the cut, temperature control, seasoning, and resting.

Core principles

- Start with a good cut:
  - Ribeye: rich marbling, forgiving, flavorful.
  - Strip steak (New York strip): balanced tenderness and beef flavor.
  - Tenderloin/Filet mignon: very tender, milder flavor.
  - Sirloin: leaner, economical, benefits from careful cooking.

- Thickness matters:
  - Steaks 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) thick are generally easier to cook precisely than thin steaks.

Preparation

- Dry the surface thoroughly with paper towels. A dry surface promotes browning.
- Salt generously:
  - Either immediately before cooking or 1–24 hours in advance (dry brining).
  - Dry brining improves seasoning and surface drying.
- Bring the steak out of the refrigerator 20–60 minutes before cooking if desired, though this is less important than many people assume.

Heat and browning

- The flavorful crust comes from the Maillard reaction, which occurs at high temperatures.
- Use a heavy pan (cast iron is a classic choice) or a very hot grill.
- Avoid overcrowding the cooking surface.

Temperature targets

Internal temperatures (approximate):
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

For many premium cuts, medium-rare is often considered an ideal balance of tenderness and flavor.

Use an instant-read thermometer whenever possible. It removes much of the guesswork.

Pan-searing method

1. Preheat the pan until very hot.
2. Add a high-smoke-point oil.
3. Place the steak in the pan and leave it undisturbed initially.
4. Flip every 30–60 seconds or use a traditional single-flip approach; both can work well.
5. In the final minutes, add butter, garlic, and herbs (thyme or rosemary) and baste.
6. Remove the steak a few degrees below your target temperature to account for carryover cooking.

Reverse sear technique

Especially useful for thick steaks.

1. Cook the steak in a low oven (around 225–275°F / 107–135°C) until nearly at the desired internal temperature.
2. Finish with a very hot sear in a pan or on a grill.
3. This method often produces a more even interior with an excellent crust.

Resting

- Rest the steak for about 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps temperature equalize and can improve the eating experience.

Common mistakes

- Cooking by time alone instead of temperature.
- Using a wet steak surface.
- Constantly moving the steak before a crust forms.
- Using insufficient heat for searing.
- Skipping seasoning.
- Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.

If you want to develop expert-level steak skills, keep notes on:
- Cut and thickness
- Cooking method
- Pan or grill temperature
- Final internal temperature
- Resting time
- Your assessment of texture and flavor

This systematic approach—similar to how one might refine any technical skill through measurement and feedback—rapidly improves consistency and results.
