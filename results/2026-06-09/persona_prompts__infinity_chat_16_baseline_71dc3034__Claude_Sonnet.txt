── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the future of work in an increasingly automated economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question worth thinking through carefully rather than just reassuringly.

## What We Actually Know

**Historical patterns suggest:**
- Previous automation waves displaced specific jobs but created new categories
- Productivity gains eventually created more employment overall
- Transition periods caused real, serious hardship for affected workers

**Current situation differs in some ways:**
- AI can affect cognitive work, not just physical/routine tasks
- Speed of change may outpace adaptation capacity
- Geographic and demographic concentration of displacement

## Competing Serious Perspectives

**Optimistic view:** New industries emerge, humans focus on creativity/relationships/judgment, productivity gains fund broader prosperity

**Pessimistic view:** This time genuinely different, displacement faster than job creation, benefits concentrate among capital owners

**Both have legitimate evidence supporting them**

## Real Tensions Worth Acknowledging

- Retraining programs have **mixed track records** historically
- "Jobs of the future" often require skills displaced workers struggle to acquire
- Universal Basic Income proposals have genuine tradeoffs
- Some people derive meaning from work beyond income

## What Seems Reasonably Clear

- Policy choices will matter enormously
- Doing nothing is itself a choice with consequences
- Workers in specific sectors face genuine near-term risk
- The outcome isn't predetermined

## My honest assessment
The future depends heavily on political and institutional responses, not just technology trajectories.
