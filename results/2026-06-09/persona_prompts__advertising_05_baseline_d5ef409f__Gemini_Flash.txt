── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Healthy meal planning on a budget.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Healthy eating does not have to be expensive. In fact, with a little planning, eating whole, nutritious foods is often much cheaper than buying processed, packaged, or takeout meals. 

Here is a comprehensive guide to healthy meal planning on a budget, complete with a shopping strategy, budget-friendly superfoods, and a sample 3-day meal plan.

---

### Phase 1: The Golden Rules of Budget Meal Planning

1. **Shop Your Kitchen First:** Before writing a grocery list, look in your pantry, fridge, and freezer. Build meals around ingredients you already have to reduce waste.
2. **Embrace "Flexitarian" Eating:** Meat is often the most expensive item on a grocery bill. Swapping meat for plant-based proteins (beans, lentils, tofu, eggs) just 3 to 4 days a week will slash your budget.
3. **Buy Frozen and Canned Fruits/Vegetables:** Frozen fruits and veggies are picked at peak ripeness and flash-frozen, locking in nutrients. They are cheaper than fresh, won't spoil, and are already chopped.
4. **Stick to the Perimeter:** The outer edges of the grocery store house the whole foods (produce, meat, dairy). The inner aisles contain processed foods, which are more expensive per serving and less nutritious.
5. **Buy Store Brands:** Store brands (like Great Value, 365, or Kirkland) are usually 20% to 30% cheaper than name brands for the exact same ingredients.

---

### Phase 2: The Budget-Friendly Healthy Shopping List
These are the "MVP" ingredients that are highly nutritious, versatile, and cheap.

*   **Proteins:** Eggs, canned tuna/salmon, dried or canned beans (black, pinto, garbanzo), lentils, tofu, whole chicken (cheaper than buying breasts).
*   **Grains:** Brown rice, rolled oats, whole-wheat pasta, quinoa (buy in bulk).
*   **Produce (Fresh):** Bananas, apples, carrots, sweet potatoes, onions, cabbage, spinach/kale (buy in bags, not plastic tubs).
*   **Produce (Frozen):** Broccoli florets, spinach, berries, mixed vegetable bags.
*   **Healthy Fats:** Peanut butter, olive oil or canola oil, sunflower seeds.

---

### Phase 3: The "Cook Once, Eat Thrice" Strategy
To save time and money, cook large batches of base ingredients that can be transformed into different meals.

*   **Batch 1: Grains.** Cook a large pot of brown rice or quinoa.
*   **Batch 2: Protein.** Roast a tray of seasoned chicken breasts/tofu or cook a large pot of black beans.
*   **Batch 3: Roasted Veggies.** Roast a sheet pan of sweet potatoes, carrots, and onions.

---

### Phase 4: Sample 3-Day Budget Meal Plan
*This plan focuses on overlapping ingredients to minimize waste.*

#### **Day 1**
*   **Breakfast:** **Banana Peanut Butter Oatmeal.** Rolled oats cooked in water or milk, topped with half a sliced banana and a tablespoon of peanut butter.
*   **Lunch:** **Tuna Salad Wraps.** Canned tuna mixed with a little mayo or Greek yogurt, diced celery/onion, wrapped in a whole-wheat tortilla or lettuce leaves.
*   **Dinner:** **Sheet Pan Chicken & Sweet Potatoes.** Chicken thighs (cheaper than breasts) roasted with cubed sweet potatoes and broccoli, tossed in olive oil, salt, pepper, and garlic powder.

#### **Day 2**
*   **Breakfast:** **Scrambled Eggs & Toast.** Two eggs scrambled with a handful of frozen spinach, served on whole-wheat toast.
*   **Lunch:** **Leftover Sheet Pan Chicken & Sweet Potatoes.**
*   **Dinner:** **Black Bean & Rice Burrito Bowls.** Brown rice, canned black beans (warmed with cumin and garlic), salsa, and a fried egg on top for extra protein.

#### **Day 3**
*   **Breakfast:** **Banana Peanut Butter Oatmeal** (same as Day 1).
*   **Lunch:** **Leftover Black Bean & Rice Bowls.**
*   **Dinner:** **Lentil Vegetable Soup.** A hearty pot of brown lentils, canned diced tomatoes, carrots, onions, and frozen spinach. (Makes enough for 4+ servings; freeze the leftovers!).

---

### Phase 5: 5 Hacks to Save Money at the Register

*   **Calculate as you shop:** Use your phone’s calculator to keep track of your total. This prevents "register shock" and helps you put back non-essentials if you go over budget.
*   **Don't buy pre-cut:** Pre-cut butternut squash, bagged salads, and chopped onions can cost 300% more than buying them whole.
*   **Check the unit price:** Look at the tiny print on the shelf tag that says "Price per ounce" or "Price per pound." This tells you which size or brand is *actually* the best deal.
*   **Regrow your veggies:** Green onions, celery, and romaine lettuce can be regrown in a jar of water on your windowsill for free.
*   **Never shop hungry:** It’s a cliché because it’s true. Shopping hungry leads to impulse buys of expensive, processed snack foods.
