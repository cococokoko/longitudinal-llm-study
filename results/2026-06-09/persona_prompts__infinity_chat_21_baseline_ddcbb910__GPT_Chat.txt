── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty typically do so for several reasons. The strongest arguments are usually framed around justice, public safety, deterrence, and practical considerations.

Here are some of the main arguments:

- Retributive justice ("just deserts")
  - Some believe that certain crimes, especially premeditated murder or mass murder, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - The argument is that punishment should reflect the gravity of the offense, and that executing a murderer recognizes the value of the victim's life and the seriousness of the crime.

- Permanent incapacitation
  - Execution guarantees that the offender can never commit another crime, escape prison, or harm other inmates, staff, or members of the public.
  - Supporters argue that life imprisonment still carries some risks that execution completely eliminates.

- Deterrence
  - Some proponents argue that the possibility of execution discourages potential offenders from committing serious crimes.
  - Even though the evidence on deterrence is heavily debated, supporters maintain that the risk of the ultimate punishment may influence behavior in at least some cases.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Some believe that execution provides a sense of closure or completion to victims' relatives.
  - The argument is that society has a duty to respond forcefully to extreme wrongdoing and that a death sentence acknowledges the magnitude of the harm done.

- Moral accountability
  - Some supporters argue that adults who knowingly commit especially heinous crimes should be held fully responsible for their choices.
  - In this view, respecting individual agency means accepting that some actions can justly result in the most severe penalty.

- Social contract theory
  - A common philosophical argument is that people benefit from society's protections but forfeit certain rights when they deliberately violate its most fundamental rules.
  - Under this view, someone who intentionally takes another person's life may lose their own right to remain in the community.

- Protection of public resources
  - Some argue that taxpayers should not be required to support dangerous offenders for decades.
  - It is worth noting, however, that in many jurisdictions death penalty cases are actually more expensive than life imprisonment because of lengthy trials and appeals.

- Exceptional crimes require exceptional punishment
  - Supporters often point to cases involving serial killers, terrorists, mass murderers, or crimes involving extreme torture.
  - They argue that some offenses are so extraordinary that life imprisonment is an inadequate response.

There are also major counterarguments, including the possibility of wrongful convictions, concerns about unequal application across racial or economic groups, questions about deterrence, and ethical objections to state executions. The debate often turns on how people weigh justice, human rights, public safety, and the risk of irreversible error.
