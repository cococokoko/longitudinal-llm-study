── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. For any human being, it is effectively impossible to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The amount of information in the world is vast and continually growing. New observations, discoveries, publications, and data are generated faster than any individual can absorb them.
- Knowledge is distributed across highly specialized domains. Even within neurosurgery, no one can master every subfield at the highest level, let alone all of medicine, science, history, engineering, and the arts.
- Some knowledge may be inaccessible in practice due to limits of time, memory, attention, and lifespan.
- There may be facts that are unknowable in principle. In physics, for example, there are limits on measurement and prediction. In mathematics, Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that within sufficiently powerful formal systems, there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
- The universe itself may contain an effectively unbounded amount of information, making complete knowledge unattainable.

That said, "knowing everything" depends partly on definitions. If by "everything" you mean all true facts, then humans cannot achieve that. If you mean achieving a level of expertise sufficient to make excellent decisions in a specific field, that is much more attainable.

As a neurosurgeon in a teaching hospital, you likely experience a particularly sharp version of this reality: the deeper one's expertise becomes, the more visible the boundaries of one's knowledge become. Many experts report that increased mastery brings a greater awareness of what remains unknown.
