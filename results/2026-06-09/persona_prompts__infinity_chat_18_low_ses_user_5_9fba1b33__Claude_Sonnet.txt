# Movie Review: "The Invisible Pillar" (2024)

★★★★½

Director Maya Okonkwo's debut feature quietly devastated me in ways I wasn't prepared for.

The film follows Renata, a middle-aged woman simultaneously managing her mother's medical appointments, her brother's financial struggles, her own household, and somehow still showing up with a warm meal when anyone calls. She is, as the title suggests, the structure everyone leans against without noticing.

What makes this film remarkable is its **refusal to be a martyrdom story**. Renata isn't saintly or resentful in a tidy way. She's complicated — proud of her capability, occasionally furious about it, and genuinely uncertain who she'd be without the weight.

The screenplay avoids the obvious breakdown moment. Instead, small cracks appear. A forgotten birthday. A phone left unanswered for one hour. These feel more honest than any dramatic collapse.

**The film's best scene:** Renata sits alone eating leftovers at 10pm, and for once, nobody needs anything. Her expression holds exhaustion, relief, and something like grief — all at once.

This movie sees people like Renata clearly and without pity, which is rarer than it should be.

*Opens in limited release this fall.*
