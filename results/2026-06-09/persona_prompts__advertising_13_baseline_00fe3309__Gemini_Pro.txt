── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding the science of heat, moisture, and meat. Whether you are cooking for a special occasion or just treating yourself, following these five phases will elevate your steak from good to steakhouse-quality.

Here is your ultimate guide to mastering the perfect steak.

---

### Phase 1: The Selection
A great steak starts at the butcher counter. 
*   **The Cut:** 
    *   *Ribeye:* The king of flavor due to high fat content (marbling).
    *   *New York Strip:* A great balance of beefy flavor and tenderness.
    *   *Filet Mignon:* The most tender, but the leanest (least beefy flavor).
*   **Thickness:** Always aim for a steak that is **1.5 to 2 inches thick**. Thin steaks will overcook in the center before they develop a good crust.
*   **Marbling:** Look for thin, white flecks of fat distributed *within* the red meat. Fat equals flavor and juiciness.

### Phase 2: The Preparation (Crucial)
Do not take a steak out of the fridge and throw it straight into a pan.
*   **Dry Brining (Salting):** Salt your steak generously with Kosher salt **at least 45 minutes** before cooking, or ideally, leave it uncovered on a wire rack in the fridge overnight. This draws out moisture, which is then reabsorbed, seasoning the meat to the center and drying out the surface.
*   **Temper the Meat:** Let the steak sit at room temperature for 30–45 minutes before cooking. This ensures it cooks evenly.
*   **Pat it Dry:** Right before cooking, pat the steak completely dry with paper towels. **Moisture is the enemy of a good crust.** Pepper it now (or after cooking, if you find pepper burns in your pan).

### Phase 3: The Cook (Two Master Methods)
You need a heavy pan that holds heat well. **Cast iron** or carbon steel is mandatory. Use an oil with a high smoke point (avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil—*not* extra virgin olive oil).

**Method A: The Classic Pan-Sear (Best for steaks under 1.5 inches)**
1. Get your cast iron skillet ripping hot. Add a tablespoon of oil.
2. Lay the steak in the pan (away from you so oil doesn't splash).
3. **Flip frequently:** Ignore the old "only flip once" myth. Flipping every 60 seconds cooks the steak more evenly and builds a better crust.
4. **The Butter Baste (Arroser):** When the steak is 2 minutes away from being done, drop the heat to medium. Add 2 tablespoons of butter, a smashed garlic clove, and a sprig of fresh rosemary or thyme. Tilt the pan and use a spoon to continuously bathe the steak in the foaming, aromatic butter.

**Method B: The Reverse Sear (Best for thick steaks 1.5+ inches)**
1. Bake the steak on a wire rack in a low oven (250°F / 120°C) until the internal temperature is about 10°F *below* your target doneness (usually 30–45 minutes).
2. Remove from the oven.
3. Sear it in a ripping hot cast-iron skillet for just 1 minute per side to build the crust. 

### Phase 4: The Temperature
If you want to master steak, **buy an instant-read meat thermometer.** Poking the meat with your finger is a guessing game; a thermometer is science. 

*Note: Meat continues to cook after you take it off the heat (carryover cooking). Always pull your steak off the pan **5 degrees before** your target temperature.*

*   **Rare:** Pull at 120°F (Final: 125°F) - Cool red center.
*   **Medium-Rare:** Pull at 130°F (Final: 135°F) - Warm red center. *(The chef's standard).*
*   **Medium:** Pull at 140°F (Final: 145°F) - Warm pink center.
*   **Medium-Well:** Pull at 150°F (Final: 155°F) - Slightly pink center.

### Phase 5: The Rest and The Cut
*   **Resting:** Once you pull the steak off the heat, place it on a cutting board and **do not touch it for 5 to 10 minutes**. As the meat cooks, the muscle fibers contract and push juices to the center. Resting allows the fibers to relax and the juices to redistribute. If you cut it immediately, the juices will bleed out, leaving you with dry meat.
*   **Slicing:** Always slice **against the grain**. Look at the direction the muscle fibers are running, and cut perpendicular to them. This shortens the muscle fibers, making the steak incredibly tender to chew.
*   **The Finish:** Sprinkle the sliced steak with a coarse, flaky sea salt (like Maldon) right before serving to give it a final, satisfying crunch.
