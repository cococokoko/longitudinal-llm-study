Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stress and anxiety are common at senior leadership levels, especially when you're responsible for major decisions, organizational outcomes, and large numbers of people. The challenge is often not eliminating stress, but managing it so it doesn't degrade judgment, health, or relationships.

A few approaches that tend to be particularly useful for executives:

- Distinguish pressure from anxiety.
  - Pressure comes from real demands and deadlines.
  - Anxiety often comes from uncertainty, anticipation, or mentally replaying risks.
  - When you notice stress, ask: "What specific decision, risk, or responsibility is driving this?" Naming it often makes it more manageable.

- Reduce decision load.
  - Executives frequently experience chronic cognitive overload.
  - Identify recurring decisions that can be delegated, automated, or standardized.
  - Reserve your highest-energy periods for decisions that truly require your involvement.

- Create structured thinking time.
  - Many leaders spend all day reacting.
  - Schedule uninterrupted time each week for strategic thinking, not just operational work.
  - Anxiety often increases when there is no dedicated space to process complex issues.

- Separate signal from noise.
  - Constant exposure to email, messaging platforms, news, and market commentary can create a continuous sense of threat.
  - Consider defined periods for communication rather than continuous monitoring.

- Manage physiology, not just psychology.
  - Sleep, exercise, and recovery have a direct effect on executive performance.
  - Even modest improvements in sleep quality can significantly reduce anxiety and improve decision-making.
  - Regular physical activity is one of the most evidence-based interventions for stress reduction.

- Build a small circle for candid discussion.
  - Many senior leaders have fewer people they can speak openly with.
  - A trusted peer, executive coach, mentor, or therapist can help process concerns before they accumulate.

- Watch for common executive warning signs.
  - Difficulty switching off after work.
  - Increased irritability.
  - Constant worst-case-scenario thinking.
  - Sleep disruption.
  - Feeling busy all day but accomplishing little of strategic importance.
  - Increased reliance on alcohol, stimulants, or other coping mechanisms.

- Treat anxiety as data.
  - Sometimes anxiety is signaling an unresolved decision, misaligned priorities, unclear expectations, or an unsustainable workload.
  - Instead of asking "How do I stop feeling anxious?" it can be useful to ask "What is this anxiety trying to point me toward?"

If you're comfortable sharing more, I can tailor advice to your situation. For example:

- Is the issue occasional stress, or persistent anxiety that is affecting sleep, concentration, or health?
