── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you want to know what happens at the very end of a movie. 

Usually, you can just skip to the last minute of the video, or ask a friend who has seen it to tell you the ending. That is a **shortcut**.

But imagine a special, magical movie where **there are no shortcuts**. 

If you try to skip ahead, the screen just goes blurry. The only way to find out what happens at the end is to sit down and watch every single second of the movie, from the very beginning to the very end. 

**Computational irreducibility** is just a big, fancy science word for that kind of movie. 

It means that for some things in nature—like the weather, or how a pile of leaves swirls in the wind—there is no math trick or supercomputer that can guess the future. The only way to find out what is going to happen is to wait and watch it happen in real life. You have to do all the steps, and you cannot skip ahead!
