# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose your **hourly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **x** = number of hours worked per week
- **y** = hourly wage rate ($/hour)

The total weekly pay function is:

$$f(x, y) = x \cdot y + 0.5x^2 \cdot 0.1$$

But let's use a cleaner, realistic example:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^2$$

---

## Finding Partial Derivatives

### ∂f/∂x (rate of change with respect to hours, holding wage constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

### ∂f/∂y (rate of change with respect to wage, holding hours constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 2y$$

---

## Numerical Evaluation

Say you work **x = 36 hours/week** and your wage is **y = $18/hour**:

| Partial Derivative | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x | 6(36) + 2(18) | **252** |
| ∂f/∂y | 2(36) + 2(18) | **108** |

---

## Interpretation

- **∂f/∂x = 252** → If you pick up **one extra hour** (holding wage fixed at $18), your output value increases by **252 units**
- **∂f/∂y = 108** → If your **wage increases by $1** (holding hours fixed at 36), your output value increases by **108 units**

> Each partial derivative isolates the effect of **one variable at a time** — exactly like asking *"what happens to my paycheck if only my hours change, but nothing else does?"*
