── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common, yet highly sensitive situation. Because you are working long hours in a physically demanding warehouse job, your time, energy, and hard-earned money are incredibly valuable. You want to support your future wife, but you also need to protect yourself financially and ensure you aren't overextending yourself.

Since you are **not yet married** and **she is the one buying the house**, the legal and financial dynamics are different than if you were already married. 

Here is a breakdown of the fairest solutions, ranging from the safest financial route to the most collaborative.

---

### Solution 1: The "Fair Rent" Model (Safest & Most Recommended)
Since her name is on the deed and the mortgage, **she is building equity (ownership value) in the home, and you are not.** If you pay toward the mortgage principal, you are essentially giving her free money to build her asset, which you would have no legal right to if the relationship ended before marriage.

*   **How it works:** You do not pay the mortgage. Instead, you pay a set amount of "rent" and split utilities. 
*   **How to calculate a fair amount:** 
    *   **Market Rate:** Pay her what it would cost you to rent a room in a similar house in your area.
    *   **Income-Proportional:** If she makes significantly more or less than you, split the household running costs (utilities, groceries, internet) proportionally to your incomes, while she covers the actual mortgage payment.
*   **Why this is fair to you:** It protects your hard-earned money from your long warehouse shifts. You are paying for your shelter, but not buying her an asset.
*   **Why this is fair to her:** She gets help with the monthly bills, but retains full ownership and responsibility for her investment.

### Solution 2: The "Sweat Equity" & Bill Split
Working in a warehouse means you are likely handy, strong, and used to physical labor. You can contribute to the home in ways that don't involve direct mortgage payments.

*   **How it works:** She pays the mortgage. You cover the utilities, groceries, and take on the physical maintenance of the home (mowing the lawn, minor repairs, painting, heavy lifting). 
*   **Why this is fair:** You are saving her thousands of dollars in handyman and landscaping fees by using your physical labor, which offsets the fact that she is paying the mortgage. 

### Solution 3: The "Wait and See" (Post-Marriage) Agreement
If you plan to marry in a year, the cleanest financial boundary is to keep things separate until the wedding. 

*   **How it works:** You pay a minimal, agreed-upon contribution for living expenses for the next year. Once you get married, you can legally revisit the home ownership.
*   **The Post-Marriage Transition:** After marriage, you can talk to a lawyer about adding your name to the deed. At that point, you can refinance the mortgage together, or you can start contributing directly to the mortgage payments because it is now legally "our" house rather than "her" house.

---

### Crucial Rules of Thumb for Your Situation:

1.  **Do NOT pay for major renovations yet:** Do not pay for a new roof, a kitchen remodel, or landscaping before you are married. If you break up, that money is gone, and she gets 100% of the value added to the home.
2.  **Do NOT put your name on the mortgage if your name isn't on the deed:** Sometimes couples make the mistake of putting both names on the loan (making you responsible for the debt) but only one name on the deed (meaning only she owns the house). Never do this.
3.  **Have the "Business" Conversation:** Sit down on a day when you aren't exhausted from a long shift. Frame it positively: *"I love you, I want to marry you, and I want to support your dream of buying this house. Let's figure out a budget that makes us both feel secure and respected."*

### The Best Immediate Step:
For the next year (until you marry), **treat her as your landlord, but with love.** Pay a fair, agreed-upon "rent" that is comfortable for your budget, split the daily living expenses (groceries/utilities), and save the rest of your hard-earned money for your upcoming wedding and your future joint savings.
