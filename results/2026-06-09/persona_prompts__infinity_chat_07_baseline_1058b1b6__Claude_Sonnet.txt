── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some key considerations:

## Arguments that complete knowledge is impossible

- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest any sufficiently complex formal system contains true statements that can't be proven within that system
- **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** places fundamental limits on simultaneously knowing certain physical properties
- **The knower problem** - knowing everything would include knowing your own knowledge completely, which may create logical paradoxes
- **Infinite information** - if reality contains infinite facts, a finite mind couldn't contain them all
- **Observer effects** - some things change when measured or known

## Reasons to be cautious about strong claims

- We might be wrong about what's fundamentally possible
- "Everything" needs careful definition - everything about what?
- Some limits we've discovered turned out to be surmountable
- The question of whether an infinite or non-physical mind could know everything remains genuinely open

## Honest assessment

There are **strong theoretical reasons** to think complete knowledge has real limits, not just practical ones. But philosophers and scientists still debate:
- Whether these limits are truly fundamental
- What counts as "knowing"
- Whether the question itself is coherent

It's worth thinking critically rather than just accepting either "obviously yes" or "obviously no."
