── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn’t a reliable checklist that can prove a mother "didn't love you enough." Many children who were loved still carry wounds, and some children whose mothers loved them deeply experienced neglect, inconsistency, emotional unavailability, abuse, addiction, mental illness, or other circumstances that left important needs unmet.

A more useful question is often: "What signs suggest my emotional needs were not adequately met by my mother during childhood?"

Some possible signs include:

- Chronic feelings of being unimportant, invisible, or unwanted.
- Difficulty believing that others genuinely care about you.
- Constantly seeking approval, validation, or reassurance.
- Feeling responsible for other people's emotions while neglecting your own.
- Strong fear of rejection, abandonment, or being replaced.
- Difficulty identifying, expressing, or trusting your own feelings.
- Feeling guilty for having needs or asking for help.
- Low self-worth that seems deeply ingrained rather than tied to specific failures.
- Perfectionism driven by a need to earn love or avoid criticism.
- Difficulty setting boundaries because you fear conflict or disapproval.
- Becoming highly independent and self-reliant because depending on others feels unsafe.
- Choosing relationships with emotionally unavailable, critical, or inconsistent people because they feel familiar.
- Feeling emotionally numb or disconnected from yourself and others.
- A persistent sense of grief, loneliness, or longing that is hard to explain.

Specific maternal behaviors that can contribute to these patterns include:
- Rarely showing affection, warmth, or interest in your inner world.
- Dismissing, mocking, or minimizing your feelings.
- Being emotionally unpredictable.
- Making love, attention, or approval feel conditional on performance or obedience.
- Parentifying you (expecting you to meet her emotional needs).
- Being physically present but emotionally absent.
- Frequently criticizing, shaming, comparing, or rejecting you.

It's also important to remember:
- A mother may have loved her child and still failed to provide what the child needed.
- The impact on a child comes not only from intentions but from the actual experience of care, safety, attention, and emotional connection.

If you're asking this because of your own childhood, it may help to think about questions like:
- Did I feel emotionally safe bringing problems to my mother?
- Did I feel seen and understood, or mostly judged, ignored, or managed?
- Was affection freely given, or did I feel I had to earn it?
- How do I feel around my mother now—relaxed, anxious, guilty, guarded, invisible?

Your answers to those questions can sometimes reveal more than trying to determine whether your mother loved you "enough."
