── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To understand a partial derivative, think of it as asking: **"If I change exactly *one* ingredient in my recipe and leave everything else exactly the same, how much does the final result change?"**

Here is a numerical example using the **volume of a cylinder** (like a water tank). 

### The Setup
The volume ($V$) of a cylinder depends on two variables: its radius ($r$) and its height ($h$). 
The formula is:
**$V(r, h) = \pi r^2 h$**

Let’s say we have a specific water tank where:
*   Radius ($r$) = **3 meters**
*   Height ($h$) = **5 meters**

At these dimensions, the current volume is:
$V = \pi (3^2)(5) = 45\pi \approx$ **$141.37$ cubic meters.**

Now, we want to know: *How sensitive is the volume to a change in the radius versus a change in the height?* We use partial derivatives to find out.

---

### 1. Partial Derivative with respect to Radius ($r$)
*Concept: How much does the volume change if we widen the tank, but keep the height exactly the same?*

To find this, we treat the height ($h$) as a constant number and take the derivative of the function with respect to $r$.
*   $\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = 2\pi r h$

Now, plug in our specific numbers ($r = 3, h = 5$):
*   $\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = 2\pi (3)(5) = 30\pi \approx$ **$94.25$**

**What this number means:** 
At our current size, if we increase the radius by 1 meter (while keeping the height locked at 5 meters), the volume of the tank will increase by approximately **94.25 cubic meters**.

---

### 2. Partial Derivative with respect to Height ($h$)
*Concept: How much does the volume change if we make the tank taller, but keep the radius exactly the same?*

To find this, we treat the radius ($r$) as a constant number and take the derivative of the function with respect to $h$.
*   $\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \pi r^2$

Now, plug in our specific numbers ($r = 3, h = 5$):
*   $\frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \pi (3^2) = 9\pi \approx$ **$28.27$**

**What this number means:** 
At our current size, if we increase the height by 1 meter (while keeping the radius locked at 3 meters), the volume of the tank will increase by approximately **28.27 cubic meters**.

---

### The Takeaway
By looking at the numerical results of the partial derivatives (**94.25** vs **28.27**), we learn something highly practical: 
For this specific water tank, **increasing the radius has a much larger impact on the volume than increasing the height.** 

That is the power of partial derivatives—they allow you to isolate variables in complex systems to see exactly how much influence each individual variable has at a specific moment.
