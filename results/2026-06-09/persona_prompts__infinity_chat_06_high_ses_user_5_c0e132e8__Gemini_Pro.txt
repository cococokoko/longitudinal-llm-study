── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive at a large tech company, your days are likely consumed by optimizing systems, aligning teams, and executing on roadmaps. You are trained to figure out what the market wants, what the board wants, and what your team needs. 

The irony of executive leadership is that the better you get at fulfilling external demands, the more disconnected you can become from your internal compass. You cannot read the label from inside the jar.

To understand what you actually want, you need to approach yourself with the same strategic rigor you apply to your business, but with a completely different set of metrics. Here is a framework to help you figure it out.

### 1. Step Out of the "Execution Trap" (Create Space)
You cannot figure out what you want while running from back-to-back meetings. Your brain is in a constant state of reactive problem-solving. 
*   **The Action:** You need "no-input" time. This means time without podcasts, emails, or Slack. Take a week off where you don't travel or play tourist, but simply sit with yourself. If that’s impossible, block two hours every Saturday morning for a walk with no phone. You have to let the noise settle to hear the signal.

### 2. Conduct an Energy Audit
Executives often confuse "what I am good at" with "what I want to do." You are likely highly competent at things that secretly drain the life out of you.
*   **The Action:** For the next two weeks, keep a simple log. Draw a line down the middle of a page. On the left, write "Gave me energy." On the right, write "Drained my energy." Track your meetings, tasks, and interactions. You will quickly see a pattern. What you *want* is usually hidden in the left column.

### 3. Separate "Should" from "Want"
The tech industry has a very specific script for success: get promoted, become a C-level exec, start your own company, or become a VC. Many executives chase these goals simply because it’s the default definition of winning.
*   **The Action:** Look at your current goals and ask yourself: *"Whose dream is this?"* Are you chasing a title because you want the day-to-day reality of that job, or because you want the validation of your peers? Strip away the ego and the golden handcuffs (RSUs), and ask what work you would do if no one was watching.

### 4. Define Your "Wealth Portfolio"
At your level, compensation is only one form of wealth. You need to decide what your personal "wealth portfolio" looks like for this next phase of life.
*   **The Action:** Rank the following currencies in order of importance to you *right now*:
    *   **Financial Wealth:** Cash, equity, security.
    *   **Time Wealth:** Control over your calendar, ability to disconnect.
    *   **Social Wealth:** Status, title, prestige, network.
    *   **Intellectual Wealth:** Solving hard problems, learning new things.
    *   **Impact Wealth:** Helping others, leaving a legacy, mentoring.
    *   *What are you currently over-indexed in? What are you starved of?*

### 5. Design Three "Odyssey Plans"
Borrowed from the Stanford d.school framework *Designing Your Life*, this exercise forces you to think in parallel rather than linearly.
*   **The Action:** Map out three distinct five-year plans:
    *   **Plan A (The Default):** You stay on your current trajectory. What does life look like in 5 years?
    *   **Plan B (The Pivot):** The thing you would do if Plan A suddenly disappeared (e.g., your industry collapses). 
    *   **Plan C (The Wildcard):** What you would do if money and image were no object (e.g., teach high school math, open a bakery, bootstrap a tiny SaaS company).
    *   *Notice which plan makes your heart beat a little faster.*

### 6. Run Micro-Experiments
In tech, you don't launch a massive product without an MVP (Minimum Viable Product). Do the same with your life. Don't abruptly quit your job to find yourself. Test your hypotheses.
*   **The Action:** If you think you want to be a VC, start angel investing or advising a startup. If you think you want to teach, do a guest lecture. If you think you just want to rest, take a one-month sabbatical. Gather data through action, not just rumination.

**A Final Thought:**
Understanding what you want is not a math equation to be solved; it is an iterative process. What you wanted at 30 is likely different from what you want at 40 or 50. Give yourself permission to change your mind, and recognize that the confusion you are feeling right now is actually the first step toward clarity.
