I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a very long line of dominoes set up in a special pattern.

You ask: "If I knock over the first domino, what will the 1,000th domino be doing at the end?"

For some things, you can use a shortcut. It's like knowing that if you count by twos, you can quickly figure out what number you'll reach without saying every number.

But computational irreducibility means there is no shortcut.

The only way to know what happens to the 1,000th domino is to watch:
- Domino 1 fall,
- then domino 2,
- then domino 3,
- and so on,
- all the way to domino 1,000.

You can't skip ahead and calculate the answer much faster.

So, computational irreducibility is the idea that some processes are like stories you have to read page by page. Even if you're very smart and have a very fast computer, the quickest way to know the ending is often just to let the process happen step by step.

A one-sentence version:

"Some things are so complicated that the fastest way to know what they'll do is to watch them do it."
