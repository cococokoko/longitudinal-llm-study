When discussing "discipleship"—a concept rooted in religious traditions (particularly Christianity) but highly applicable to modern leadership and human capital development—there are several distinct frameworks. 

As a technology executive, you can think of these different views as varying operational models for scaling culture, transferring knowledge, and developing leaders. Just as a tech company might debate the merits of centralized vs. decentralized architecture, or Waterfall vs. Agile methodologies, theologians and organizational leaders debate the best models for discipleship.

Here are the four primary views on discipleship, translated through the lens of leadership and organizational scaling:

### 1. The Programmatic View (The "Structured Curriculum" Model)
* **The Concept:** Discipleship is viewed as a systematic, educational process. It relies on classes, seminars, structured curricula, and clear milestones. 
* **The Tech Equivalent:** Traditional corporate training, onboarding boot camps, or a "Waterfall" approach to development.
* **Pros:** It is highly scalable, ensures quality control, and guarantees that a baseline of core information is distributed to everyone.
* **Cons:** It can become purely intellectual. Just as watching a video on coding doesn't make someone a great software engineer, completing a discipleship class doesn't necessarily result in deep personal transformation or behavioral change.

### 2. The Relational View (The "Life-on-Life" Model)
* **The Concept:** This view argues that discipleship cannot happen in a classroom; it must happen in the context of a deep, interpersonal relationship. It is highly organic, focusing on shared experiences, vulnerability, and informal mentoring over time.
* **The Tech Equivalent:** Executive coaching, deep 1-on-1 mentorship, or the "Agile" approach to personal development (iterative, responsive to immediate needs).
* **Pros:** It yields incredibly high-quality results. The transformation is deep, customized, and holistic.
* **Cons:** It is notoriously difficult to scale. A leader only has the bandwidth to deeply mentor a handful of people at a time. 

### 3. The Missional / Multiplication View (The "Network Effect" Model)
* **The Concept:** In this view, discipleship is only successful if it reproduces. The goal isn't just to create a follower, but to create a leader who can create other leaders. It focuses heavily on outward mission and decentralized growth.
* **The Tech Equivalent:** Viral growth loops, open-source communities, or decentralized networks. 
* **Pros:** Exponential scalability. It empowers people at the edges of the organization rather than relying on a centralized bottleneck of "experts."
* **Cons:** It requires a massive release of control. When you empower the edges to replicate the culture, you risk brand dilution or theological/cultural drift if the core DNA isn't incredibly strong.

### 4. The Apprenticeship View (The "Action-Oriented" Model)
* **The Concept:** Discipleship is viewed as a transfer of practical skills and lifestyle through proximity and practice. It follows a strict progression: *I do, you watch. I do, you help. You do, I help. You do, I watch.*
* **The Tech Equivalent:** Pair programming, shadowing, or hands-on incubator models.
* **Pros:** It bridges the gap between theory and practice. It ensures that the "disciple" is actually capable of executing the work and living out the values.
* **Cons:** It is highly resource-intensive and requires the "master" to slow down their own output to bring the apprentice up to speed.

### The Executive Takeaway
In both spiritual communities and large tech organizations, the most effective leaders rarely rely on just one of these models. They build a **hybrid ecosystem**:
* They use the **Programmatic** model to scale baseline knowledge efficiently.
* They use the **Apprenticeship** model to train specific, high-value skills.
* They use the **Relational** model to develop their top-tier executive team.
* They use the **Multiplication** model as their ultimate metric of success—ensuring that the culture and mission outlast their direct involvement. 

Whether you are looking at this from a personal, spiritual perspective or applying it to how you develop the next generation of VPs and Directors in your company, the core question of discipleship remains the same: *What is the most effective way to transfer our core DNA into the lives of others so that they can do the same?*
