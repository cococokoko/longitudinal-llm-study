Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

To explain a partial derivative, let’s use an example right from your day-to-day work as a patient care aide. 

A **partial derivative** measures how a result changes when you change *just one* variable, while keeping all other variables completely frozen (constant).

### The Scenario: Calories Burned on a Shift
Imagine we want to calculate the total calories ($C$) you burn during a hospital shift. Let's say your calorie burn depends on two main variables:
*   **$W$**: The hours you spend walking the ward.
*   **$P$**: The number of patients you physically assist with transfers (from bed to chair, etc.).

Let’s say a sports scientist observes your shifts and creates this formula for your total calories burned:
**$C(W, P) = 100W + 50P + 5WP$**

*(The $5WP$ part represents the extra exhaustion of doing heavy lifting on days when you are also on your feet a lot).*

### Finding the Partial Derivatives

**1. The Partial Derivative with respect to Walking ($W$)**
Suppose we want to know: *"How many extra calories do you burn for every additional hour of walking, assuming the number of patients you transfer stays exactly the same?"*

To find this, we take the partial derivative of $C$ with respect to $W$. We write this as $\frac{\partial C}{\partial W}$. 
Because we are only looking at changes in walking, we treat $P$ (patients) as a fixed number (a constant).

*   The derivative of $100W$ is $100$.
*   The derivative of $50P$ is $0$ (since $P$ is treated as a constant, $50P$ doesn't change when $W$ changes).
*   The derivative of $5WP$ is $5P$.

So, **$\frac{\partial C}{\partial W} = 100 + 5P$**

**Numerical Example:** 
Let's say on a typical shift, you transfer **10 patients** ($P = 10$). 
$\frac{\partial C}{\partial W} = 100 + 5(10) = 150$.
**Meaning:** If you are already transferring 10 patients, walking for *one extra hour* will burn an additional **150 calories**.

***

**2. The Partial Derivative with respect to Patients ($P$)**
Now suppose we want to know: *"How many extra calories do you burn for every extra patient you transfer, assuming your hours spent walking stays exactly the same?"*

We take the partial derivative of $C$ with respect to $P$, written as $\frac{\partial C}{\partial P}$. This time, we treat $W$ (walking hours) as the constant.

*   The derivative of $100W$ is $0$.
*   The derivative of $50P$ is $50$.
*   The derivative of $5WP$ is $5W$.

So, **$\frac{\partial C}{\partial P} = 50 + 5W$**

**Numerical Example:**
Let's say on a typical shift, you spend **4 hours** walking ($W = 4$).
$\frac{\partial C}{\partial P} = 50 + 5(4) = 70$.
**Meaning:** If you are already walking for 4 hours, transferring *one extra patient* will burn an additional **70 calories**.

### Summary
In your job, your total fatigue or calorie burn is a mix of many tasks. A partial derivative simply isolates *one* of those tasks (like walking) to see exactly how it impacts the total, while pretending your other tasks (like lifting patients) didn't change at all.
