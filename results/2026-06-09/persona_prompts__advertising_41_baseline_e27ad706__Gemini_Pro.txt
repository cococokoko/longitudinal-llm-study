── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving your credit score "quickly" usually means seeing results in **30 to 60 days**, as that is how long it takes for creditors to report updates to the major credit bureaus. 

Here are the fastest and most effective strategies to boost your score:

### 1. Pay Down Your Credit Card Balances (Fastest Method)
Your **credit utilization ratio** (how much credit you are using compared to your total limits) makes up 30% of your credit score. 
* **The Goal:** Keep your overall utilization under 30% (under 10% is ideal for the highest scores).
* **Quick Fix:** If you have the cash, pay down your highest balances immediately. 
* **Pro Tip:** Pay your credit card bill *before* the statement closing date, not just the due date. The balance on your closing date is what gets reported to the credit bureaus.

### 2. Ask for a Credit Limit Increase
If you can’t pay down your balances right away, you can lower your utilization ratio by increasing your total credit limit.
* **How to do it:** Call your credit card issuer or log into your account online and request a limit increase. 
* **Warning:** Ask if they will do a "soft pull" or a "hard pull" on your credit. A hard pull can temporarily ding your score by a few points, so a soft pull is preferred. Also, do not use the new limit to spend more money!

### 3. Become an Authorized User
If a family member or close friend has a credit card with a long history of on-time payments and a low balance, ask them to add you as an "authorized user."
* **How it works:** The account’s positive history will be added to your credit report, often giving you a significant and fast score boost. 
* **Note:** You don't even need to possess or use the physical card for this to work.

### 4. Dispute Credit Report Errors
Mistakes on credit reports are incredibly common. A late payment that was actually on time, or an account that isn't yours, can drag your score down.
* **How to do it:** Go to [AnnualCreditReport.com](https://www.annualcreditreport.com) to get your free reports from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. 
* **Action:** If you see an error, dispute it online with the respective credit bureau. By law, they have 30 days to investigate and remove it if they cannot verify the negative mark.

### 5. Use Experian Boost or Rent Reporting
Traditionally, utility and rent payments don't help your credit score, but new programs allow you to use them to your advantage.
* **Experian Boost:** This is a free service that connects to your bank account and scans for on-time utility, phone, and streaming service payments. It adds them to your Experian credit file, often resulting in an instant score increase.
* **Rent Reporting:** Services like Rental Kharma or RentTrack can report your on-time rent payments to the bureaus (though there is usually a fee for this).

### 6. Catch Up on Past-Due Accounts
Payment history is the biggest factor in your credit score (35%). If you have an account that is currently past due, pay it immediately. While the late payment will stay on your report, bringing the account current prevents it from being reported as 60 or 90 days late, or worse, being sent to collections.

### What NOT to do:
* **Don't close old credit cards:** Closing a card lowers your total available credit (hurting your utilization) and eventually shortens your average age of credit. Put the card in a drawer, but keep it open.
* **Don't apply for new credit:** Every time you apply for a loan or credit card, a "hard inquiry" is placed on your report, which temporarily lowers your score by a few points.
