How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually involves a mix of better placement, reducing interference, and upgrading equipment if needed.

Try these steps:

- Place your router centrally
  - Put it near the center of your home rather than at one end.
  - Keep it elevated (on a shelf or table).
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large objects.

- Reduce interference
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and Bluetooth-heavy devices.
  - Avoid placing it next to metal objects or large appliances.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band
  - 2.4 GHz: longer range, lower speeds, more interference.
  - 5 GHz: faster speeds, shorter range.
  - 6 GHz (Wi‑Fi 6E/7): fastest and least congested, but shorter range and requires compatible devices.

- Optimize your Wi‑Fi channel
  - In crowded areas, neighboring networks can cause congestion.
  - Many routers can automatically select the best channel.
  - A Wi‑Fi analyzer app can help identify less crowded channels.

- Update firmware
  - Install router firmware updates to improve performance, stability, and security.

- Improve security
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 encryption.
  - Remove unknown devices from your network.

- Upgrade your router
  - If your router is several years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 7 model may significantly improve performance.

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system
  - For larger homes or areas with dead spots, mesh systems often provide better coverage than a single router.

- Use wired connections when possible
  - Connect stationary devices (gaming consoles, desktops, smart TVs) via Ethernet to reduce Wi‑Fi congestion.

- Check your internet plan
  - Sometimes the bottleneck is the internet connection itself rather than Wi‑Fi.
  - Compare speeds near the router versus speeds promised by your ISP.
