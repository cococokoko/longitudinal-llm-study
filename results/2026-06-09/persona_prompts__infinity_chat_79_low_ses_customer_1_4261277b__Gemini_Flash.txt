### Project Title: *The Clay Pot Filter: Upcycling Local Waste to Clean Vietnam’s Water*

#### **The Real-World Vietnam Connection**
Many communities in Vietnam—from the Mekong Delta to rural areas outside major cities—struggle with access to clean drinking water due to heavy metal contamination (like arsenic), agricultural runoff, and industrial waste. 

#### **The STEM Concept**
You will build and test a low-cost, gravity-driven water filtration system using **terracotta clay** mixed with different local organic waste materials (like **rice husks** or **coffee grounds**, both of which are incredibly abundant and cheap/free in Vietnam). 

When clay is mixed with rice husks or coffee grounds and fired (or baked), the organic material burns away, leaving behind microscopic pores. These pores act as a natural filter that traps bacteria and sediment. 

---

### **Why this fits a busy, unpredictable schedule:**
*   **No living subjects:** Unlike projects with plants or bacteria cultures, this doesn't require daily watering or precise feeding times. If you get called into a shift unexpectedly, your project can sit on your counter for days without any issue.
*   **Batch testing:** You can do the physical building and testing in 1 or 2 focused blocks of time on your days off.
*   **Low cost:** The materials are either free waste products or very cheap household items.

---

### **Materials Needed (Under $15–$20 total):**
1.  **Terracotta Clay:** A small block of air-dry clay or natural sculpting clay (available at craft stores for a few dollars).
2.  **Organic Waste:** 
    *   Rice husks (you can get these free/cheap from Asian grocery stores or pet stores).
    *   Used coffee grounds (free from your workplace or a local cafe).
3.  **Dirty Water:** Mix tap water with dirt, a bit of vegetable oil, and food coloring to simulate polluted river water.
4.  **Plastic Bottles:** Cut in half to act as the funnels/stands for your filters.
5.  **A cheap TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) meter:** (Optional, but highly recommended. You can find these on Amazon for $6–$8 to get precise, scientific data).

---

### **The Experiment (Step-by-Step):**

1.  **Create the Filters (Do this on a day off):**
    *   Divide your clay into three equal portions.
    *   **Filter A (Control):** Pure clay.
    *   **Filter B:** Clay mixed with 20% dried coffee grounds.
    *   **Filter C:** Clay mixed with 20% crushed rice husks.
    *   Shape them into small, hollow cups or bowls. 
    *   Let them dry completely (this takes 2–3 days of just sitting on a shelf—zero effort from you). *Note: If using air-dry clay, they will harden on their own. If using natural clay, you can bake them in a home oven at its highest setting to burn off the organic material.*

2.  **The Testing Phase (Takes about 30 minutes):**
    *   Place each clay cup inside the top half of a cut plastic bottle (acting as a funnel).
    *   Pour your "polluted water" into each clay cup.
    *   Measure how long it takes for the water to drip through (filtration rate).
    *   Compare the clarity of the water that drips out.
    *   Use your TDS meter (or a simple pH strip) to measure the purity of the filtered water compared to the dirty water.

*   **It’s culturally relevant:** Using rice husks (Vietnam is one of the world's largest exporters of rice) and coffee grounds (famous Vietnamese coffee culture) shows incredible thought and connection to the region.
