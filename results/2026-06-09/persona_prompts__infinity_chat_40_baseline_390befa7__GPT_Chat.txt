── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer. The best solution depends on how you want to balance finances, risk, and your future plans.

A key point: if your girlfriend is buying the house solely in her name, she is taking on the ownership risk and building the equity. If you contribute to the mortgage without any ownership interest, you may effectively be helping her build wealth while not gaining any legal stake yourself.

Common approaches include:

- Pay rent, not the mortgage.
  - Treat her as the homeowner and yourself as a tenant.
  - Agree on a reasonable monthly amount that contributes to housing costs.
  - Usually this amount is less than half the mortgage if she alone owns the asset and receives the equity growth.
  - This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.

- Split household expenses but not equity-building costs.
  - You pay part of utilities, groceries, internet, maintenance, etc.
  - She pays the mortgage and property taxes.
  - This avoids ambiguity about ownership.

- Buy into the property.
  - If you want to contribute to the mortgage as an owner, you could obtain a legal ownership interest.
  - This requires legal documentation and often refinancing, depending on the lender and location.
  - Get professional legal advice before doing this.

- Wait until marriage and then reevaluate.
  - Once married, many couples combine finances to varying degrees.
  - The treatment of the house after marriage depends on local laws, prenups, and how expenses are handled.

What I would generally avoid:
- Paying large amounts toward the down payment, mortgage principal, renovations, or major capital improvements without a clear written agreement about ownership and reimbursement.
- Assuming that marriage plans automatically protect your contributions. In many places, they do not.

A practical conversation might sound like:
- "Since you'll own the house, I'd like us to agree on a fair housing contribution from me that reflects that I'm living there but not building equity. Then after we're married, we can revisit how we want to handle ownership and finances."

A few questions would help narrow this down:
- Will the house be entirely in her name?
- Will you move in immediately?
- Are you planning to combine finances after marriage?
- Are you helping with the down payment?
- What country or state/province are you in? Property laws matter a lot.
