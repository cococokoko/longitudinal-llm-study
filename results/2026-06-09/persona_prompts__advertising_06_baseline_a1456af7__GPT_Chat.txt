── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering digital photography.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering digital photography involves understanding both the technical and creative aspects of making images. A structured approach can help you improve steadily.

Key areas to learn:

1. Understand Exposure
- Aperture: Controls how much light enters the lens and affects depth of field.
  - Wide aperture (f/1.8, f/2.8): Blurred background.
  - Narrow aperture (f/8, f/11): More of the scene in focus.
- Shutter Speed: Controls how long light reaches the sensor.
  - Fast (1/1000s): Freezes motion.
  - Slow (1s or longer): Creates motion blur.
- ISO: Controls sensor sensitivity.
  - Low ISO (100–200): Cleaner images.
  - High ISO (1600+): More noise but useful in low light.

2. Learn Composition
- Rule of thirds.
- Leading lines.
- Framing subjects naturally.
- Symmetry and patterns.
- Negative space.
- Balance and visual weight.

3. Master Focus
- Single-point autofocus for precise subjects.
- Continuous autofocus for moving subjects.
- Understand focus modes and focus area settings on your camera.

4. Understand Light
- Golden hour (shortly after sunrise and before sunset).
- Blue hour (twilight).
- Soft light on cloudy days.
- Direction of light:
  - Front lighting.
  - Side lighting.
  - Backlighting.

5. Shoot in RAW
- Captures more image data.
- Greater flexibility in editing exposure, color, and shadows.

6. Learn Post-Processing
Popular software:
- Adobe Lightroom
- Adobe Photoshop
- Capture One
- Darktable (free)

Basic editing skills:
- Exposure correction.
- White balance adjustment.
- Contrast and color.
- Cropping and straightening.
- Noise reduction.
- Sharpening.

7. Know Your Equipment
- Learn every setting on your camera.
- Understand different lenses:
  - Wide-angle (landscapes, architecture).
  - Standard (general use).
  - Telephoto (sports, wildlife).
  - Macro (close-up photography).

8. Practice Specific Genres
- Portrait photography.
- Landscape photography.
- Street photography.
- Wildlife photography.
- Sports photography.
- Product photography.

9. Analyze Great Photographs
- Study work from respected photographers.
- Ask:
  - Where is the light coming from?
  - Why does the composition work?
  - What emotions does the image create?

10. Develop a Consistent Practice Routine
- Take photos regularly.
- Review your images critically.
- Identify recurring mistakes.
- Create projects around specific themes or techniques.

Suggested learning path:
- Week 1–2: Exposure triangle.
- Week 3–4: Composition fundamentals.
- Week 5–6: Light and focus techniques.
- Week 7–8: RAW editing and workflow.
- Ongoing: Genre specialization and portfolio building.

A useful principle is: focus first on light, then composition, then camera settings. Strong light and composition usually have a greater impact on a photograph than technical perfection alone.
