There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time. If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, it would attract capital until the edge diminished.

A more useful way to think about day trading is that profitability comes from a combination of:
- A genuine market edge
- Risk management
- Discipline and execution
- Costs (commissions, spreads, slippage)
- Market conditions

Historically, some of the most durable day-trading approaches have included:

- Trend-following momentum
  - Buy assets showing strong upward momentum and short those showing strong downward momentum.
  - Common in stocks, futures, and currencies.
  - Strengths: Can capture large moves.
  - Weaknesses: Frequent false signals and losing streaks.

- Opening range breakout
  - Trade breakouts above or below the first portion of the trading session (for example, the first 15–30 minutes).
  - Widely used by professional traders.
  - Works best in strongly trending markets.

- Mean reversion
  - Bet that unusually stretched price moves will revert toward an average.
  - Common among quantitative traders.
  - Often has a high win rate but occasional large losses.

- Market making and statistical arbitrage
  - Historically among the most profitable institutional strategies.
  - Require technology, capital, and infrastructure beyond most retail traders.

For an individual trader, one of the more practical approaches is:
- Trade highly liquid instruments.
- Focus on one setup (such as opening range breakouts or momentum pullbacks).
- Risk a small fixed percentage of capital per trade (many traders use 0.25%–1%).
- Track every trade and evaluate results over hundreds of trades.
- Avoid overtrading.

If preserving and growing family resources is a priority, long-term diversified investing has historically been a more reliable wealth-building approach than active day trading.
