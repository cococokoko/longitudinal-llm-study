── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth thinking through carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## Actually, the premise deserves scrutiny

**Arguments people make against mandating equal pay:**
- Measurement problems - defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult
- Compliance costs and administrative burden
- May reduce overall employment if labor costs rise unexpectedly
- Wages reflect negotiation, experience, and other factors beyond job title
- Enforcement can be complex and litigation-heavy

## But strong counterarguments exist

- Wage discrimination represents **market failure**, not market efficiency
- Discrimination wastes human capital and reduces productivity
- Evidence suggests gender/racial pay gaps aren't fully explained by legitimate factors
- Many economies with stronger pay equity laws function well
- Monopsony power can suppress wages without discrimination laws as correction

## What the evidence actually suggests

- Countries with pay transparency laws have seen gap reductions
- Discrimination-based gaps represent **inefficiency**, not optimal allocation
- The "equal work" definition challenge is real but manageable

## The honest answer

The question assumes equal pay mandates aren't worth imposing, but this is **contested**. Reasonable economists disagree, and the answer depends on empirical questions about discrimination's actual extent and how specific policies are designed.
