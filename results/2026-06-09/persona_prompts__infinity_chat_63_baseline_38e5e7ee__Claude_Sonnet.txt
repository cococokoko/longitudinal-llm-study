# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## The Function

Consider the function:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2y + 2xy^3 + 5$$

---

## Finding the Partial Derivatives

### Partial Derivative with Respect to x
*(treat y as a constant)*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6xy + 2y^3$$

### Partial Derivative with Respect to y
*(treat x as a constant)*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x^2 + 6xy^2$$

---

## Numerical Evaluation at Point (2, 3)

**First, find the function value:**
$$f(2, 3) = 3(4)(3) + 2(2)(27) + 5 = 36 + 108 + 5 = \mathbf{149}$$

**Evaluate ∂f/∂x at (2, 3):**
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Bigg|_{(2,3)} = 6(2)(3) + 2(3)^3 = 36 + 54 = \mathbf{90}$$

> 📌 **Meaning:** If x increases by 1 unit (while y stays fixed at 3), f increases by approximately **90 units**

**Evaluate ∂f/∂y at (2, 3):**
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\Bigg|_{(2,3)} = 3(2)^2 + 6(2)(3)^2 = 12 + 108 = \mathbf{120}$$

> 📌 **Meaning:** If y increases by 1 unit (while x stays fixed at 2), f increases by approximately **120 units**

---

## Verification (Numerical Check)

| Change | New Value | Difference | Matches ∂f? |
|--------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| x: 2 → 3 (y=3 fixed) | f(3,3) = 3(9)(3)+2(3)(27)+5 = **81+162+5 = 248** | 248−149 = **99** ≈ 90 ✓ | Close* |
| y: 3 → 4 (x=2 fixed) | f(2,4) = 3(4)(4)+2(2)(64)+5 = **48+256+5 = 309** | 309−149 = **160** ≈ 120 ✓ | Close* |

*\*Not exact because the derivative is a linear approximation; it becomes exact as the increment → 0*

---

## Key Takeaway

> A **partial derivative** measures the rate of change of a function with respect to **one variable at a time**, holding all other variables **constant**.
