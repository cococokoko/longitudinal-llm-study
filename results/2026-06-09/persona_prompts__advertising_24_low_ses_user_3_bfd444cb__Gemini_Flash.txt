── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Starting a new role is an exciting time, and thinking about retirement savings right now is a fantastic move. 

Being on probation introduces a few unique constraints (like waiting periods for benefits), but it shouldn't stop you from saving. Here is a step-by-step guide on the best ways to save for retirement, tailored for someone in your position.

---

### Phase 1: What to Do *During* Your Probationary Period

While you are waiting for your full benefits to kick in, you can still take active steps to save.

#### 1. Build Your "Runway" (Emergency Fund)
Before putting money into accounts you can't touch until age 59½, make sure you have **3 to 6 months of living expenses** in a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA). 
* **Why now?** Starting a new job comes with a risk of it not working out (or you realizing you hate it). Having cash on hand ensures you won't have to raid retirement accounts (and pay penalties) if things go sideways during probation.

#### 2. Open a Roth IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
If you cannot yet access your employer’s 401(k) or pension, a Roth IRA is the absolute best place to start.
* **How it works:** You contribute after-tax money. The money grows tax-free, and you pay **zero taxes** when you withdraw it in retirement.
* **The Probation Advantage:** With a Roth IRA, **you can withdraw your contributions (but not the earnings) at any time, penalty-free.** This acts as a secondary emergency backup while you transition into your new job.
* **2024 Limit:** You can contribute up to $7,000 per year ($8,000 if you are 50 or older), provided you earn at least that much.
* **Where to open one:** Fidelity, Vanguard, or Charles Schwab (all have $0 fees to open).

#### 3. Find Out the Rules of Your Employer’s Plan
Don't wait until day 90 to look at your benefits package. Ask HR or log into your benefits portal now to find out:
* **When do I qualify?** (e.g., Day 1, 30 days, 90 days, or 1 year?)
* **Is there a company match?** (e.g., "We match 100% up to 4% of your salary.")
* **What is the vesting schedule?** "Vesting" is how long you must work there to keep the employer's matching money. If you leave before you are vested, you lose the match (but you always keep your own contributions).

---

### Phase 2: What to Do *After* You Pass Probation

Once you are officially off probation and eligible for company benefits, follow this classic financial order of operations:

#### Step 1: Get the "Free Money" (The 401(k) Match)
If your employer offers a 401(k), 403(b), or TSP match, **contribute exactly enough to get the maximum match.** 
* *Example:* If they match up to 4%, contribute 4%. This is an instant 100% return on your investment. Never pass this up.

#### Step 2: Max Out Your Roth IRA
Once you’ve secured the employer match, put your next savings dollars into your Roth IRA (up to the $7,000 limit). IRAs generally have lower fees and much better investment choices than employer-sponsored 401(k)s.

#### Step 3: Go Back to the 401(k)
If you still have money you want to save for retirement after getting the match and maxing out your Roth IRA, go back to your employer's 401(k) and increase your contribution percentage. (The 2024 limit for 401(k) contributions is $23,000).

---

### Phase 3: How to Actually Invest the Money

Opening the account is only half the battle; you must actually invest the money inside the account. If you don't, it will just sit there as cash and won't grow. 

If you want a "set-it-and-forget-it" approach, use one of these two options:

1. **Target Date Funds (TDFs):** You choose the fund with the year closest to when you plan to retire (e.g., "Target Date 2060"). The fund automatically starts aggressive (mostly stocks) and becomes safer (more bonds) as you get closer to retirement.
2. **Broad Market Index Funds:** If you want to be a bit more hands-on, invest in low-cost index funds that track the entire stock market (like an S&P 500 index fund or a Total Stock Market index fund). 

### Summary Checklist for Your First Week:
1. **Open a High-Yield Savings Account** (if you don't have one) and make sure you have a safety net.
2. **Open a Roth IRA** at Fidelity or Vanguard and set up an automatic monthly contribution (even if it's just $50/month to start).
3. **Mark your calendar** for the exact date your probation ends so you can sign up for your employer's 401(k) match the very first day you are eligible.
