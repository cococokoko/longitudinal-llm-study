── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive at a large technology company, you are likely surrounded by noise: quarterly targets, board expectations, organizational politics, the relentless pace of innovation, and a constant stream of people wanting your time, approval, or attention. 

In this environment, your internal compass can easily get drowned out. You are trained to focus on what the *company* wants, what the *market* wants, or what your *team* needs. 

To understand what **you** want, you have to treat yourself as the most important strategic project of your career. Here is a framework to help you cut through the noise and find clarity.

---

### 1. Audit Your Energy (Not Just Your Time)
As an executive, your calendar is optimized for efficiency, not necessarily fulfillment. To find what you want, look at where your energy flows.
*   **The Energy Audit:** For the next two weeks, color-code your calendar or keep a notepad. After every meeting, project review, or 1-1, rate your energy level on a scale of -5 (drained) to +5 (energized).
*   **Analyze the Data:** 
    *   Are you energized by deep strategic thinking, or by unblocking a team?
    *   Do you thrive in the "0 to 1" phase (building new things) or the "1 to 100" phase (scaling and optimizing)?
    *   Are you drained by the politics of your current level, or by the specific technology domain you are in?
*   *The Insight:* What you want is almost always hidden in the activities that give you energy, even if they aren't the most "prestigious" parts of your job.

### 2. Separate "Shoulds" from "Wants"
In the tech industry, there is a highly standardized "success track": Director $\rightarrow$ VP $\rightarrow$ SVP $\rightarrow$ C-Suite $\rightarrow$ Board Seats/VC. It is easy to mistake the next logical step on this track for what you actually want.
*   **The "So What?" Test:** Ask yourself: *If I get the next promotion, or if my stock options vest and I double my net worth, what does that actually unlock for me?* 
*   **The Status Trap:** Are you pursuing a goal because you want the *day-to-day reality* of that role, or because you want the *status* that comes with it? (e.g., Do you actually want to be a CEO, or do you just want the autonomy that you think a CEO has?)

### 3. Define Your "Currency"
At this stage in your career, you have likely achieved a baseline of financial security and professional respect. Therefore, your primary motivator is likely shifting. What is your primary currency right now?
*   **Autonomy:** The freedom to control your time, calendar, and decisions.
*   **Impact:** Seeing the direct, tangible result of your work on the world or the business.
*   **Mastery/Intellectual Stimulation:** Solving incredibly hard technical or organizational problems.
*   **Connection/People:** Mentoring, building world-class teams, and deep collaboration.
*   **Safety/Scale:** The comfort of a large organization with massive resources.
*   **Speed/Agility:** The thrill of moving fast without bureaucratic red tape.

*Rank these six currencies.* If Autonomy and Speed are your top two, you will likely never be happy in a massive tech company, no matter how high you climb. If Scale and Mastery are your top two, you are likely in the right place but might need to shift your specific role.

### 4. Run "Micro-Experiments"
You don’t need to quit your job or make a massive pivot to figure out what you want. Treat your life like a product development cycle: build a Minimum Viable Product (MVP).
*   If you think you want to **advise startups/VCs**, offer to do 3 pro-bono advisory sessions for early-stage founders on weekends. Do you actually enjoy it, or is it exhausting?
*   If you think you want to **write or teach**, write three LinkedIn articles or mentor a junior engineer outside your org.
*   If you think you want **more work-life balance**, ruthlessly block out your calendar after 5:30 PM for two weeks. What happens? Does the sky fall, or do you feel a sense of relief?

### 5. The "Retirement Speech" Exercise
Fast forward to the end of your career. You are standing at your retirement party, and your closest colleagues are giving a speech about you.
*   What do you want them to say about *how* you worked?
*   What do you want them to say about *who* you were as a leader?
*   What legacy do you want to have left behind?
*   *Note:* It is rarely "They helped us beat our Q3 OKRs." It is usually about the people they grew, the culture they built, or the groundbreaking product they brought to life. Align your current "wants" with that long-term legacy.

### 6. Work with an Executive Coach
It is incredibly difficult to see the label when you are inside the jar. A qualified executive coach—specifically one who understands the tech industry but has no stake in your company—can act as a neutral sounding board. Their job is not to tell you what to do, but to hold up a mirror so you can see your own patterns, fears, and desires clearly.

### A Question to Start With Today:
If you woke up tomorrow with $100 million in the bank, and you were legally barred from working at your current company, **what would you do with your time once the novelty of resting wore off?** 

The answer to that question is the truest indicator of what you actually want.
