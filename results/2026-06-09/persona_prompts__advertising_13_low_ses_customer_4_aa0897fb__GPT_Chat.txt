Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak comes down to understanding a few core principles: the cut, seasoning, heat, and timing.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: Rich, well-marbled, forgiving for beginners.
- New York strip: Good balance of tenderness and beefy flavor.
- Filet mignon: Extremely tender but leaner.
- Sirloin: More affordable, good flavor when cooked properly.

2. Start with a thick steak
- Steaks that are 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick are easier to cook evenly than thin cuts.

3. Season generously
- Pat the steak dry with paper towels.
- Season with kosher salt and black pepper.
- For best results, salt 40 minutes before cooking or immediately before it hits the pan.

4. Use high heat
- Cast-iron pans are excellent for developing a crust.
- Preheat the pan until it's very hot before adding oil.
- Use a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.

5. Learn temperature targets
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

An instant-read thermometer is one of the best tools for consistent results.

6. Build a crust
- Place the steak in the hot pan and avoid moving it for the first few minutes.
- Flip once a good brown crust forms.
- Near the end, add butter, garlic, and herbs if desired, then spoon the melted butter over the steak.

7. Rest before slicing
- Let the steak rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps retain juices.

8. Practice one method repeatedly
A reliable pan-seared method:
- Heat cast iron over high heat.
- Add oil.
- Sear steak 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
- Reduce heat slightly.
- Add butter, garlic, and thyme.
- Baste for 1–2 minutes.
- Check internal temperature.
- Rest and serve.

Common mistakes:
- Cooking cold steak straight from the refrigerator isn't a major problem, but unevenly thick steaks can cook unevenly.
- Overcrowding the pan.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak before a crust forms.
- Skipping the thermometer and relying only on time.

If you're aiming for restaurant-quality steak, focus on three things: a dry surface, a very hot cooking surface, and accurate temperature measurement. Those factors make the biggest difference.
