── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. If by "everything" you mean all facts, all knowledge, and complete understanding of reality, then it is impossible for a human being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- Time is limited. New information is constantly being created, discovered, and recorded faster than any one person could learn it.
- Human memory is limited. Even exceptionally knowledgeable people cannot store or perfectly recall an unlimited amount of information.
- Knowledge changes. Scientific understanding, historical discoveries, technologies, and cultural information evolve over time.
- Some things may be fundamentally unknowable. There are open questions in science, mathematics, and philosophy that currently have no known answers, and some may never be fully resolved.

That doesn't mean learning is futile. People usually develop deep knowledge in some areas and broader knowledge in others. Knowing "everything" is impossible, but knowing enough to understand the world, solve problems, and pursue your interests is very achievable.

Given that you work full-time with an unpredictable schedule, it may help to think of learning as a long-term accumulation rather than a race to master everything. Even 10–20 minutes of focused learning on a regular basis can add up significantly over months and years.
