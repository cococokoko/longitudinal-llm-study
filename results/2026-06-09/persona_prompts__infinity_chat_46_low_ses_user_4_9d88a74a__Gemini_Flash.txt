Thank you for the work you do! Hospital staff are the backbone of healthcare, and your role as a patient care aide is incredibly important to patient well-being. 

In graph theory, a **"linear graph"** is most commonly referred to as a **Path Graph** (usually denoted as $P_n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices). 

It is a graph where the vertices (nodes) are connected in a single, straight line with no branches, loops, or cycles. 

Here is a simple example, using a scenario you might see every day at the hospital: **the chain of communication for a patient's care.**

### The Hospital Communication Path ($P_4$)

Imagine a patient needs a specific adjustment to their care plan. The information flows in a direct, linear line from one person to the next:

```text
[Patient] ─── [You (Aide)] ─── [Nurse] ─── [Doctor]
   (A)            (B)            (C)          (D)
```

In graph theory terms, this is a **Path Graph with 4 vertices ($P_4$)**:

*   **Vertices (Nodes):** The people involved.
    *   $A$ = Patient
    *   $B$ = Patient Care Aide (You)
    *   $C$ = Nurse
    *   $D$ = Doctor
*   **Edges (Connections):** The direct lines of communication.
    *   Edge 1: Between Patient ($A$) and Aide ($B$) — *The patient tells you they are uncomfortable.*
    *   Edge 2: Between Aide ($B$) and Nurse ($C$) — *You report this to the supervising nurse.*
    *   Edge 3: Between Nurse ($C$) and Doctor ($D$) — *The nurse consults the doctor for a new order.*

### Why this is a "Linear" (Path) Graph:
1.  **No Cycles:** The information doesn't loop back. It goes from start to finish.
2.  **Degree of Vertices:** 
    *   The endpoints (Patient and Doctor) are connected to only **one** other person (degree of 1).
    *   The internal nodes (You and the Nurse) act as bridges, connected to exactly **two** people (degree of 2).
3.  **Single Path:** There is exactly one way to get from the Patient ($A$) to the Doctor ($D$).
