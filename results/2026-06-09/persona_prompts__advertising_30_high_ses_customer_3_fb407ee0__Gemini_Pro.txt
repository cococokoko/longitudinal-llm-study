What's the best way to start learning coding from scratch?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given your background in academia, you already possess the two most critical skills for programming: rigorous logical thinking and the ability to research complex problems. Learning to code is simply a matter of learning a new syntax to express the analytical skills you use every day.

Here is a structured, highly effective approach to learning how to code from scratch, tailored to someone with your level of expertise:

### 1. Start with Python (or R)
For a professor, **Python** is almost certainly the best starting point. It has a clean, highly readable syntax and is the lingua franca of modern academic research, data science, and automation. 
* *Alternative:* If your primary goal is heavy statistical analysis and you work frequently with datasets, **R** is also an excellent choice, though Python is more versatile.

### 2. Anchor Your Learning to a Specific Project
Because your time is valuable, avoid learning coding in a vacuum. The pedagogy of computer science heavily favors project-based learning. Pick a small, low-stakes problem related to your current work. For example:
* Automating the formatting of a bibliography.
* Scraping data from a website or digital archive for a paper.
* Running a basic sentiment analysis on a text corpus.
* Creating a data visualization for an upcoming lecture or publication.

### 3. Use High-Quality, Structured Resources
Skip the gamified mobile apps. Given your academic background, you will likely appreciate resources that explain the "why" alongside the "how."
* **For practical, immediate utility:** Read *Automate the Boring Stuff with Python* by Al Sweigart (available free online). It bypasses heavy computer science theory and teaches you how to write scripts to do useful tasks immediately.
* **For a rigorous academic foundation:** Harvard’s **CS50** (available free on edX) is widely considered the best introductory computer science course in the world. It is demanding but brilliant.
* **For data-focused learning:** The **Coursera** specialization *Python for Everybody* by Dr. Charles Severance (University of Michigan) is an excellent, gently paced introduction.

### 4. Embrace the "Jupyter Notebook" Environment
When you are ready to start typing code, I highly recommend using **Jupyter Notebooks** (often installed via a package called Anaconda). Jupyter is ubiquitous in academia. It allows you to write code, run it in small chunks, and take formatted notes in the same document. It feels very much like an interactive research journal.

### 5. Leverage AI as a Tutor, Not a Crutch
Large Language Models (like ChatGPT) are incredible tools for learning to code. You can use them as a personalized teaching assistant. If you get an error message, paste it into the AI and ask, *"Explain why I am getting this error and how to fix it, but don't just give me the code—explain the underlying concept."* 

### A Final Thought on the Learning Curve
In the beginning, coding can feel frustrating because computers are entirely literal; a misplaced comma will break a program. Treat these errors not as failures, but as standard feedback in the debugging process. Your experience in navigating the peer-review process and tackling long-term research questions has already given you the exact kind of resilience required to master this!
