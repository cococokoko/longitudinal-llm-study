My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't a single "best" solution, because it depends on your finances, your relationship, and how much legal and financial risk each of you is comfortable taking. The key is to separate the emotional question ("we're building a future together") from the ownership question ("who owns the asset and who bears the risk?").

A few common approaches:

- She buys the house on her own, and you pay rent.
  - If the house is solely hers, this is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.
  - You contribute a mutually agreed monthly amount similar to rent or toward shared living expenses.
  - She retains the equity because she is taking the ownership risk and receiving the ownership benefit.

- She buys the house, and you contribute only to shared expenses.
  - For example, she pays the mortgage, taxes, insurance, and major repairs.
  - You split utilities, groceries, and other household costs.
  - This avoids ambiguity about whether your payments create an ownership interest.

- You contribute toward the house and receive a documented ownership interest.
  - If you're helping with the down payment or mortgage and expect to build equity, that should be documented legally.
  - This can be done through co-ownership agreements or other formal arrangements, depending on your jurisdiction.
  - Do not rely on informal understandings.

- Wait until marriage and then decide how to handle ownership.
  - Some couples prefer to keep the premarital house as the purchasing partner's separate property.
  - Others refinance, retitle, or otherwise restructure ownership after marriage.

Questions that may help clarify what's fair:
- Is your name going to be on the deed?
- Are you contributing to the down payment?
- Are you helping qualify for the mortgage?
- If you paid toward the house for a year and the relationship ended, would you expect any of that money back?
- If the house appreciated significantly, would you expect to share in the gain?

As a general principle, if you have no ownership stake, paying a reasonable amount to live there is usually not unfair—people pay for housing whether through rent or ownership. What often creates problems is paying substantial amounts toward a partner's asset while both people have different assumptions about whether those payments create equity.

Given that marriage is being discussed within a year, it may be worth having a very explicit conversation now and, if significant money is involved, consulting a real-estate or family-law attorney in your area before making contributions. A few hundred dollars of legal advice can prevent major misunderstandings later.
