If you're balancing childcare and work, the most effective credit-score improvements are usually the ones that can be automated and require the least ongoing attention.

Some of the fastest ways to improve a credit score include:

- Bring any past-due accounts current.
  - Payment history is a major factor in most credit scoring models.
  - If you're behind on any accounts, catching up can help over time.

- Lower your credit card balances.
  - Credit utilization (how much of your available credit you're using) has a significant impact.
  - If possible, aim to keep balances below 30% of each card's limit, and lower is often better.

- Make all future payments on time.
  - Set up automatic minimum payments to avoid missed due dates.
  - Even one missed payment can hurt your score.

- Check your credit reports for errors.
  - Review your reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute inaccurate late payments, balances, or accounts that don't belong to you.

- Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts in a short period.
  - Each application may create a hard inquiry, which can temporarily lower your score.

- Ask for a credit limit increase.
  - If approved without a hard credit inquiry, a higher limit can reduce your utilization ratio.
  - Don't increase spending just because the limit is higher.

- Become an authorized user (if appropriate).
  - If a trusted family member has a long history of on-time payments and low balances, being added as an authorized user may help in some scoring models.

- Consider tools that report recurring payments.
  - Some services can add eligible rent, utility, or subscription payment history to your credit profile.

If you're looking for the biggest short-term impact, paying down credit card balances and eliminating any current delinquencies are often the most effective steps.
