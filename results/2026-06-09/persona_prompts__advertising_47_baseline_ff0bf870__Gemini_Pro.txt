── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon (26.2 miles / 42.195 km) is an incredible physical and mental achievement. Whether you are aiming to just finish or trying to hit a specific time, preparation is everything. 

Here is a comprehensive list of tips broken down by the different phases of your marathon journey:

### 1. Training Phase
*   **Follow a Plan:** Don’t just wing it. Find a training plan that fits your experience level (e.g., Hal Higdon, Nike Run Club, or a local running group). Most plans are 16–20 weeks long.
*   **Prioritize the Long Run:** The weekly long run is the most important part of your training. It teaches your body to burn fat for fuel and builds mental toughness. 
*   **Run Slow to Run Fast:** About 80% of your runs should be at an easy, conversational pace. This builds your aerobic base without overtaxing your body.
*   **Respect Rest Days:** Rest is when your muscles rebuild and get stronger. Skipping rest days leads to burnout and injury.
*   **Cross-Train and Strength Train:** Incorporate core work, light weightlifting, cycling, or swimming 1–2 times a week to prevent injuries and build overall strength.

### 2. Gear and Nutrition
*   **The Golden Rule: Nothing New on Race Day.** Do not wear new shoes, new socks, or a new shirt on race day. Do not eat a new breakfast or try a new energy gel. 
*   **Get Fitted for Shoes:** Go to a specialty running store to get your gait analyzed. Buy two pairs of the same shoe if possible, and rotate them during training. Retire shoes after 300–500 miles.
*   **Practice Your Fueling:** Your body cannot store enough glycogen to run 26.2 miles. You must take in calories (energy gels, chews, or real food) every 30–45 minutes during runs over 90 minutes. Practice this during your long runs to train your stomach.
*   **Lube Up:** Chafing is the enemy. Use products like BodyGlide or Vaseline on your thighs, underarms, sports bra lines, and nipples. 

### 3. The Weeks Before (The Taper)
*   **Trust the Taper:** 2 to 3 weeks before the race, your training plan will drastically reduce your mileage. You will feel sluggish, anxious, and like you are losing fitness (often called "taper tantrums"). Trust the process; your body is healing and storing energy. **Do not try to cram in extra miles.**
*   **Carb-Load Correctly:** Don't just eat a massive bowl of pasta the night before (that will just make you bloated). Start increasing your carbohydrate intake 3 to 4 days before the race.
*   **Focus on the "Night-Before-the-Night-Before":** You likely won't sleep well the night before the race due to nerves. The sleep you get *two* nights before the race is actually the most important.

### 4. Race Day Strategy
*   **Start Slow:** The biggest mistake runners make is going out too fast because of the adrenaline and cheering crowds. Run the first 3–4 miles slightly *slower* than your goal pace. 
*   **Run the Tangents:** Marathons are measured along the shortest possible route. Try to run in straight lines and take the inside of curves when the course allows, so you don't end up running 26.8 miles.
*   **Break It Up:** Thinking about 26.2 miles is overwhelming. Break the race into chunks. A common strategy is the 10/10/10K method: Run the first 10 miles with your head (smart and slow), the next 10 miles with your legs (steady pace), and the final 10K (6.2 miles) with your heart (give it whatever you have left).
*   **Prepare for "The Wall":** Around mile 18–20, your body will likely run low on glycogen, and everything will hurt. Have a mental strategy ready. Dedicate specific miles to loved ones, have a mantra ("strong and steady"), or just focus on getting to the next aid station.

### 5. Post-Race Recovery
*   **Keep Moving:** When you cross the finish line, do not sit down immediately. Keep walking for 10–15 minutes to let your heart rate come down and prevent your legs from seizing up.
*   **Refuel Quickly:** Eat a mix of carbs and protein within 30 minutes of finishing, even if you don't have an appetite. 
*   **Take Time Off:** Take at least a full week off from running after a marathon. Do light walking, cycling, or yoga to promote blood flow, but give your body the deep rest it has earned.
