── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** can refer to two different things depending on the context:

1. **A Path Graph ($P_n$):** A graph where the vertices are connected in a single, straight line (no branches, no cycles). This is the most common informal meaning.
2. **A Line Graph ($L(G)$):** A specific mathematical operation where the edges of one graph become the vertices of a new graph.

Here are examples of both.

---

### Example 1: A Path Graph (The informal "linear" graph)
A path graph of length $n$ (denoted as $P_n$) consists of a sequence of vertices connected by edges in a single line. 

Let's look at **$P_4$** (a path graph with 4 vertices):

* **Vertices (Nodes):** $\{A, B, C, D\}$
* **Edges (Connections):** $\{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$

**Visual Representation:**
```text
A —————— B —————— C —————— D
```
**Properties of this graph:**
* It is **connected** (you can get from any vertex to any other vertex).
* It has **no cycles** (you cannot start at a vertex and loop back to it without retracing your steps).
* The "end" vertices ($A$ and $D$) have a degree of 1 (only one connection).
* The "middle" vertices ($B$ and $C$) have a degree of 2.

---

### Example 2: A Line Graph (The formal mathematical definition)
In formal graph theory, a **Line Graph** of a graph $G$ (written as $L(G)$) is a graph where:
* Each vertex in $L(G)$ represents an edge in $G$.
* Two vertices in $L(G)$ are connected if their corresponding edges in $G$ share a common vertex.

**Let's construct one:**

1. Start with a simple star graph **$G$**:
```text
      Y
     / \
    X   Z
```
* Vertices of $G$: $\{X, Y, Z\}$
* Edges of $G$: $e_1 = (X, Y)$ and $e_2 = (Y, Z)$

2. Now, create the Line Graph **$L(G)$**:
* The vertices of $L(G)$ are the edges of $G$: $\{e_1, e_2\}$
* Since $e_1$ and $e_2$ both share the vertex $Y$ in the original graph, we connect them with an edge in the new graph.

**Visual Representation of $L(G)$:**
```text
e1 —————— e2
```
