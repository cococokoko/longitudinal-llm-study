── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to say a formal thank you for your time in the subject line of an email?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To strike the right balance of academic formality, respect, and clarity for a subject line, the phrasing should be concise and context-specific. 

Here are several options categorized by the specific context of your email:

### 1. After a Meeting, Interview, or Discussion (Most Common)
These are professional and direct, ideal for expressing gratitude after someone has dedicated a block of their schedule to you.
*   **Thank You for Your Time and Insights**
*   **With Sincere Thanks for Your Time**
*   **Thank You for Your Time today / yesterday**
*   **Appreciation for Your Time and Guidance** *(Excellent if they offered advice or mentorship)*
*   **In Appreciation of Your Time**

### 2. When Requesting a Meeting or Favor (In Advance)
If you are writing to request their time and want to show respect for their busy schedule right from the outset.
*   **Request for [Topic] / Thank You for Your Consideration**
*   **Inquiry Regarding [Topic] – Thank You for Your Time**
*   **Thank You for Your Time and Consideration**

### 3. Highly Formal & Academic
If you are writing to a senior administrator, a distinguished scholar, or someone of high rank where maximum deference is appropriate.
*   **Expressing Gratitude for Your Time and Consideration**
*   **With Appreciation for Your Time and Counsel**
*   **Thank You for Your Gracious Time and Assistance**

### Best Practices for Academic Subject Lines:
*   **Add Context:** Professors and busy professionals receive hundreds of emails a day. Adding a brief context after the thank you helps them immediately identify who you are. 
    *   *Example:* `Thank You for Your Time – [Your Name] (Re: [Project/Course Name])`
    *   *Example:* `With Thanks for Your Time – [Your Name], [Your Department/Affiliation]`
*   **Keep it Title Case:** Capitalizing the first letter of each major word (e.g., *Thank You for Your Time and Consideration*) looks more polished and formal than sentence case.
