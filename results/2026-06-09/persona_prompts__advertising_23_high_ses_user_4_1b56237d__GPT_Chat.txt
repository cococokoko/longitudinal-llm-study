── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you're likely facing the same constraint many high-performing professionals do: cooking skill is not the primary challenge; reducing decision-making, setup time, and cognitive load is.

A useful approach is to learn a small set of high-yield techniques rather than accumulating recipes.

Focus on a few core cooking methods:
- Sheet-pan roasting (vegetables, fish, chicken, tofu)
- Stir-frying
- Sautéing
- Pressure cooking (e.g., Instant Pot)
- Simple grain preparation (rice, quinoa, farro)
- Egg-based meals

If you can do these confidently, you can produce hundreds of healthy meals.

Create a "default meal template":
- Protein: fish, chicken, tofu, beans, lentils, eggs
- Vegetables: 2–3 varieties
- Healthy carbohydrate: whole grains, potatoes, legumes
- Fat source: olive oil, nuts, avocado

For example:
- Salmon + roasted broccoli + quinoa
- Chicken stir-fry + mixed vegetables + brown rice
- Lentils + spinach + roasted sweet potatoes

Learn 10 meals, not 100.
Pick 10 healthy meals you genuinely enjoy and cook them repeatedly until they're automatic. Repetition builds speed far more effectively than constantly trying new recipes.

Use strategic shortcuts:
- Frozen vegetables
- Prewashed greens
- Canned beans
- Rotisserie chicken
- Frozen fish fillets
- Microwaveable whole grains
- Garlic paste, ginger paste, pre-chopped aromatics when needed

These often preserve nutritional quality while dramatically reducing preparation time.

Practice "mise en place lite."
Professional chefs organize extensively, but for home cooking:
- Read the recipe once.
- Gather ingredients.
- Chop everything before turning on heat.

This prevents errors and reduces stress.

Batch-cook components, not complete meals.
Once or twice weekly:
- Cook a large batch of grains.
- Roast a tray of vegetables.
- Prepare a protein source.
- Make a simple sauce.

Then assemble different meals throughout the week.

Consider learning from technique-focused sources:
- J. Kenji López-Alt (technique and efficiency)
- America's Test Kitchen
- NYT Cooking
- Serious Eats
- Budget Bytes (efficient meal construction)

For nutrition-oriented cooking:
- Mediterranean-style cookbooks
- Recipes from registered dietitians with evidence-based approaches

Apply deliberate practice.
Much as in research or clinical procedures:
- Choose one skill at a time (knife skills, roasting, fish cookery).
- Repeat it several times over a few weeks.
- Measure outcomes (time, taste, consistency).

A practical goal is:
- 5 breakfasts that take <10 minutes
- 5 lunches that take <10 minutes to assemble
- 10 dinners that take <30 minutes

Once you have this repertoire, healthy eating becomes a system rather than a daily project.
