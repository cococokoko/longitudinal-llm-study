── System message ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
You know that the customer is an executive at a large technology company.

── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the difference between analysis and design? Can one begin to design without analysis? Why? Be concise.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a technology executive, you can think of the distinction through the lens of **problem definition versus solution architecture**.

### 1. The Difference
*   **Analysis (The "What" and "Why"):** The process of understanding the business domain, identifying user needs, and defining the problem. It establishes the requirements and constraints.
*   **Design (The "How"):** The process of conceptualizing and planning the technical solution. It translates requirements into architecture, data models, workflows, and user interfaces.

### 2. Can you begin to design without analysis?
**Technically yes, but strategically no.** 

While a team can immediately start writing code or drawing wireframes (designing), they are operating on assumptions rather than data. 

### 3. Why?
Designing without analysis introduces severe business risks:
*   **Building the Wrong Thing:** You risk perfectly executing a solution to a problem that doesn't exist or isn't a priority for the business.
*   **Scope Creep & High Costs:** Without analytical boundaries, design becomes a moving target. Fixing a design flaw during development is exponentially more expensive than refining a requirement during analysis.
*   **Technical Debt:** Designing without understanding scale, integration points, and long-term business goals leads to fragile architecture that must be rebuilt later.

**Executive Summary:** Analysis mitigates risk and aligns engineering with business value; design executes that alignment. You must define the target before you can architect the arrow.
