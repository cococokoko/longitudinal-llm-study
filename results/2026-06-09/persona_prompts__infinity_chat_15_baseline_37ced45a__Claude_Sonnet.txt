── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Power asymmetries**
- Wealthy states have greater leverage in negotiations, institutions, and coercive capacity
- Economic dependence creates political vulnerability for poorer states
- This can produce relationships resembling patron-client dynamics even between formally equal sovereigns

**Institutional design**
- Bretton Woods institutions (IMF, World Bank) reflect founding power distributions
- Voting structures often favor wealthy contributors
- This creates legitimate grievances about whose interests rules actually serve

## Competing Interpretations Worth Considering

**Liberal perspective:** Trade interdependence reduces conflict and creates mutual interests in stability

**Dependency/structuralist view:** Global economic structures systematically transfer wealth upward, making "development" difficult by design

**Realist perspective:** Economic inequality is primarily a tool states use to pursue power, not a cause in itself

**Constructivist angle:** How inequality is *framed* matters - whether as natural, unjust, or temporary shapes political responses

## Genuine Tensions

- Does foreign investment help or exploit developing economies? Evidence is genuinely mixed
- Do international institutions constrain powerful states or legitimize their dominance?
- Is the current order reformable or fundamentally structured around inequality?

## What's Actually Contested

Scholars disagree significantly about whether inequality *causes* conflict, *reflects* power, or is *constructed* through specific historical choices.
