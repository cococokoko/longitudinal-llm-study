── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Many high-performing executives spend years getting very good at achieving goals without spending much time distinguishing between:

- What they truly want
- What they are expected to want
- What they have become accustomed to wanting

One useful way to understand what you want is to stop asking "What should I do next?" and instead investigate your patterns of attention, energy, and regret.

Consider these questions:

- When do you feel most alive during a typical month?
  - Strategy?
  - Building products?
  - Leading people?
  - Solving difficult problems?
  - Creating something new?
  - Teaching?
  - Winning?
- Which activities leave you with more energy afterward rather than less?
- If a major promotion became impossible tomorrow, which parts of your work would you still want to do?
- What do you envy in others?
  - Envy is often a clue to an unacknowledged desire.
- What opportunities have you repeatedly declined, even when they looked prestigious?
  - Those decisions may reveal values more accurately than your stated goals.
- Looking back ten years, which decisions are you proud of independent of their outcomes?
  - Those often point toward your underlying motivations.

A practical framework is to separate wants into three categories:

1. Achievement
- Status
- Compensation
- Influence
- Recognition
- Scale

2. Experience
- Freedom
- Challenge
- Creativity
- Learning
- Adventure

3. Contribution
- Building institutions
- Developing people
- Advancing a field
- Solving important problems

Many executives discover they have spent years optimizing for achievement while quietly caring more about experience or contribution.

Another useful exercise:

Imagine three futures five years from now.

- Future A: Maximum prestige.
- Future B: Maximum autonomy.
- Future C: Maximum impact.

Write a detailed description of each. Then ask:

- Which future am I most drawn to?
- Which future would disappoint me least if no one knew I achieved it?

The second question is often more revealing than the first.

Finally, pay attention to recurring thoughts. Most people assume their deepest wants are hidden. Often they are not hidden at all. They are simply recurring signals that have been overridden by obligations, incentives, and momentum.

A useful test is this:

"If success were guaranteed and judgment were absent, what would I choose?"

The answer may not be practical. But it is often informative. Understanding what you want and deciding what to do about it are two separate steps. The first requires honesty; the second requires trade-offs.
