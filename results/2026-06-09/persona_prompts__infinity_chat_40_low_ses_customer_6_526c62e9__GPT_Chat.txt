── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on how you want to balance finances, risk, and your future plans.

A few key points:

- If the house is purchased solely in your girlfriend's name, and you're not on the deed or mortgage, then money you put toward the mortgage is generally helping build her equity, not yours.
- If you move in and live there, it is still reasonable for you to contribute to housing costs, because you're receiving the benefit of living there.
- The question is less "Should I pay?" and more "What am I paying for, and what rights do I have in return?"

Common approaches:

1. Pay rent, not the mortgage
- Treat her as the homeowner and yourself as a tenant/partner.
- Agree on a monthly amount that contributes to housing costs.
- Often people split costs in a way that reflects income and expenses.
- This is simple and avoids confusion about ownership.

2. Split household expenses but not equity-building costs
- You pay part of utilities, groceries, internet, maintenance, etc.
- She pays the mortgage and property taxes.
- This recognizes that the house is her asset.

3. Create an ownership arrangement
- If you contribute substantially to the purchase, mortgage, or renovations, you could discuss being added to the deed or having a legal agreement that defines your ownership interest.
- This should be documented by a lawyer, especially before marriage.

4. Wait until marriage and then reassess
- If you're planning to marry in about a year, some couples keep the house as the purchasing partner's separate asset until marriage and then decide how future payments and ownership will work.

Questions worth discussing together:
- Will you be living there?
- Are you contributing to the down payment?
- Will your name be on the deed or mortgage?
- How do your incomes compare?
- What would happen if you broke up before marriage?
- What would happen after marriage regarding ownership and finances?

Given that you're planning to marry in roughly a year, a practical middle ground is often:
- She buys the house in her name.
- You contribute a reasonable amount toward living expenses (essentially rent and shared costs).
- You do not make large undocumented contributions toward the down payment or mortgage principal unless you've both agreed in writing on how ownership will be handled.

That approach usually balances fairness with the reality that you're not yet married and the house is being purchased by her.
