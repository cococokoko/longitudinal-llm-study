Best practices for organic gardening.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Organic gardening focuses on building healthy soil, encouraging biodiversity, and minimizing synthetic inputs. Some widely recommended practices include:

- Start with healthy soil
  - Add compost regularly to improve soil structure and fertility.
  - Use well-rotted manure, leaf mold, or other organic matter when appropriate.
  - Test your soil periodically to understand nutrient levels and pH.

- Mulch generously
  - Apply organic mulch such as straw, shredded leaves, or wood chips.
  - Mulch helps retain moisture, suppress weeds, and moderate soil temperature.

- Choose suitable plants
  - Select varieties adapted to your local climate and growing conditions.
  - Use disease-resistant cultivars when available.

- Practice crop rotation
  - Avoid planting the same plant family in the same location year after year.
  - Rotation can reduce pest and disease buildup.

- Encourage beneficial insects
  - Grow flowering plants that attract pollinators and natural predators of garden pests.
  - Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides that can harm beneficial species.

- Water efficiently
  - Water deeply and less frequently rather than shallow, frequent watering.
  - Use drip irrigation or soaker hoses when possible.
  - Water early in the day to reduce disease risk.

- Manage weeds naturally
  - Remove weeds before they set seed.
  - Use mulch and dense planting to reduce weed growth.
  - Hand-pulling and hoeing are often effective in smaller gardens.

- Use organic pest management
  - Monitor plants regularly for early signs of problems.
  - Remove heavily infested plant material when necessary.
  - Consider physical barriers such as row covers and insect netting.
  - Use approved organic controls only when needed and according to instructions.

- Compost garden waste
  - Recycle plant trimmings and kitchen scraps into compost.
  - Avoid composting diseased material unless your compost system reaches temperatures that can destroy pathogens.

- Promote biodiversity
  - Include a variety of plants rather than large areas of a single crop.
  - Incorporate native plants where appropriate to support local ecosystems.
