── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different Christian traditions and thinkers understand discipleship in different ways, though most agree that it involves following Jesus and being shaped by his teachings. Here are some major views:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus.
  - Discipleship often involves Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and spiritual growth.
  - Focuses on becoming more like Christ through faith and obedience.
  - Small groups, mentoring, and accountability are commonly used.

• Reformed view
  - Stresses God's sovereignty and the role of Scripture.
  - Discipleship is seen as lifelong growth in holiness through God's grace.
  - Often emphasizes doctrinal understanding and participation in the local church.

• Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Highlights sanctification (growth in holiness).
  - Discipleship involves both personal piety and acts of mercy and justice.
  - Encourages intentional spiritual disciplines and accountability groups.

• Roman Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as participation in the life of Christ through the Church.
  - Strong emphasis on the sacraments, especially the Eucharist.
  - Involves prayer, moral formation, service, and spiritual direction.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on theosis, or becoming united with God by grace.
  - Discipleship is a process of spiritual transformation through prayer, worship, ascetic practices, and participation in the Church's life.
  - Emphasizes tradition alongside Scripture.

• Anabaptist view
  - Stresses radical obedience to Jesus' teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Often emphasizes peace, simplicity, community, and practical discipleship.
  - Following Jesus is viewed as central, sometimes more than doctrinal precision.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Highlights the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, and mission.
  - Often emphasizes personal spiritual experience alongside biblical teaching.

• Liberation theology perspective
  - Understands discipleship in relation to justice and solidarity with the poor and oppressed.
  - Following Jesus includes social transformation and advocacy for marginalized people.

• Bonhoeffer's perspective
  - Dietrich Bonhoeffer emphasized costly discipleship.
  - Following Jesus requires sacrifice, obedience, and willingness to suffer for faith.
  - Contrasts "cheap grace" (forgiveness without transformation) with true discipleship.

• Contemporary disciple-making movements
  - Focus on reproducing disciples who make more disciples.
  - Often emphasize mentorship, simple church structures, and mission.
  - Measure discipleship by obedience and multiplication rather than only knowledge.

Some key questions that divide these approaches include:
• Is discipleship primarily about personal spiritual growth or social action?
• What role do the Church and sacraments play?
• How important are doctrine, experience, and obedience?
• Is discipleship mainly individual, communal, or both?
• Is the goal moral transformation, union with God, evangelism, justice, or a combination of these?

Most Christian traditions ultimately see discipleship as a lifelong process of following Jesus, but they differ in what aspects they emphasize and how that process is practiced.
