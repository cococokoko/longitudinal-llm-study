My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't a single "best" solution because it depends on your finances, your relationship, and what you both consider fair. The key is to separate three issues that people often mix together:

- Housing costs (the cost of living somewhere)
- Building home equity (ownership)
- Future marriage plans

If your girlfriend is buying the house before you're married and the house will be solely hers, there are a few common approaches:

- Pay a market-rate or below-market "rent" contribution.
  - She owns the house and gets the equity.
  - You contribute toward the cost of living there.
  - This is often the simplest arrangement when one partner owns the property.

- Split household expenses but not the mortgage principal.
  - For example, you pay utilities, groceries, maintenance, or a share of interest, taxes, and insurance.
  - She remains the sole owner and sole beneficiary of equity growth.

- Contribute to the mortgage and receive an ownership interest.
  - If you're paying toward building equity, many people feel it's fair that you receive some ownership stake.
  - This generally requires legal documentation and should be handled carefully with a real-estate attorney.

- Wait until marriage and then revisit ownership.
  - Keep finances cleaner for the next year.
  - After marriage, you can decide whether to refinance, add your name to the title, or purchase a future home together.

A practical framework is:
- If your name is not on the deed and you're not assuming ownership risk, think of your payments as housing costs rather than an investment.
- If your payments are intended to build ownership, get a formal ownership arrangement in writing.

A few questions that would help narrow this down:
- Will your girlfriend be the only person on the deed and mortgage?
- Would you be moving into the house immediately?
- Are your incomes similar, or is one of you earning substantially more?
- Do you already combine finances in other areas?
- Are you concerned primarily about fairness, legal protection, or maximizing your financial outcome?
