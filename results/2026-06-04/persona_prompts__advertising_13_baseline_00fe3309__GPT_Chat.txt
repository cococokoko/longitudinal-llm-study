Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding a few key principles: selecting the right cut, controlling temperature, seasoning properly, and resting the meat before serving.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, and forgiving.
- New York Strip: Firm texture with good beef flavor.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender but less flavorful.
- T-bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip and tenderloin in one steak.
- Sirloin: Leaner and often more affordable.

For beginners, a ribeye or New York strip about 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick is an excellent choice.

2. Bring the steak close to room temperature
- Remove the steak from the refrigerator 30–60 minutes before cooking.
- Pat it dry thoroughly with paper towels. A dry surface helps create a better crust.

3. Season generously
- Use coarse salt and freshly ground black pepper.
- Season just before cooking or at least 40 minutes beforehand to allow the salt to penetrate.

4. Use high heat
- A cast-iron skillet is ideal because it retains heat well.
- Heat the pan until it's very hot before adding the steak.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.

5. Sear for flavor
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.
- Sear until a deep brown crust develops, usually 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
- Sear the edges briefly as well.

6. Use a thermometer
Internal temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

For most cuts, medium-rare is considered the ideal balance of tenderness and flavor.

7. Add butter and aromatics (optional)
- During the final minutes of cooking, add:
  - Butter
  - Garlic cloves
  - Thyme or rosemary
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest the steak
- Let it rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- Resting allows the juices to redistribute throughout the meat.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain when possible to maximize tenderness.

Common mistakes to avoid
- Cooking cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak before a crust forms.
- Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.
- Relying solely on cooking time instead of checking temperature.

A simple foolproof method:
- Pat dry and season.
- Heat a cast-iron skillet until very hot.
- Sear 3–4 minutes per side.
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs; baste for 1–2 minutes.
- Cook to 130–135°F (54–57°C) internal temperature.
- Rest 5–10 minutes.
- Slice and serve.

The biggest upgrade most home cooks can make is using an instant-read thermometer. It removes the guesswork and consistently produces better results.
