── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any meaningful sense, it is impossible for a human being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- Cognitive limits: Human memory, attention, processing speed, and lifespan are finite. The amount of available knowledge already vastly exceeds what any individual can learn.

- Ongoing discovery: Knowledge is not a fixed inventory. New observations, technologies, theories, and data are constantly being generated. Even if someone somehow mastered all current knowledge, new knowledge would continue to appear.

- Complexity and specialization: Modern fields have become so deep that even experts know only a fraction of their own discipline. As a neurosurgeon, you likely know this firsthand; no one can simultaneously maintain mastery of all of neurosurgery, neuroscience, molecular biology, genetics, imaging, critical care, statistics, and every other adjacent field at the highest level.

- Fundamental uncertainty: Some things may be unknowable in practice or even in principle. Physics, mathematics, and philosophy all contain examples of limits on what can be predicted, proven, or measured.

- Information scale: The amount of information in the world is enormous and continually changing. A finite mind cannot contain an effectively unbounded body of facts.

A more interesting question may be what "knowing everything" means. If it means possessing every true fact, then the answer appears to be no. If it means understanding the fundamental principles that explain a vast range of phenomena, that is more achievable—but even then, complete understanding remains elusive.

Medicine often provides a useful perspective: the deeper one's expertise, the more visible the boundaries of one's knowledge become. Many highly accomplished clinicians and scientists report that increased expertise makes them more aware of what they do not know, not less.
