How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on the cause of the problem, but these steps usually provide the biggest gains:

- Move your router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large objects.
  - Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phones, and thick walls when possible.

- Restart and update your equipment.
  - Reboot the router occasionally.
  - Install the latest router firmware updates.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band if available.
  - 2.4 GHz reaches farther but is slower and more crowded.
  - 5 GHz and 6 GHz offer higher speeds with less interference, though shorter range.

- Change Wi‑Fi channels.
  - In crowded apartment buildings, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Many routers can automatically select the best channel.

- Upgrade security settings.
  - Use WPA3 if supported, or WPA2 if not.
  - Remove unknown devices from the network.

- Reduce congestion.
  - Disconnect devices you do not use.
  - Enable Quality of Service (QoS) if your router supports it to prioritize video calls or gaming.

- Add coverage if needed.
  - For larger homes, a mesh Wi‑Fi system often works better than a single router.
  - A Wi‑Fi extender can help, though mesh systems generally provide a better experience.

- Use wired connections for demanding devices.
  - Connect desktop PCs, gaming consoles, TVs, or workstations via Ethernet when possible.

- Consider upgrading your router.
  - If your router is more than 4–6 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router may improve performance.

- Test your speeds.
  - Run speed tests near the router and in problem areas.
  - If speeds are poor even next to the router, the issue may be your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.
