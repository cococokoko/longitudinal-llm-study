In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is that, in humans, there is no strong, consistent correlation between general intelligence and compassion.

A few distinctions matter:

- Intelligence is usually measured as cognitive ability (e.g., reasoning, problem-solving, learning, abstraction).
- Compassion generally refers to concern for others' suffering combined with a motivation to help.
- Compassion overlaps with, but is not identical to, empathy.

The research literature suggests:

- General intelligence (IQ or g) shows little to no robust relationship with compassionate behavior across populations.
- Highly intelligent individuals can be exceptionally compassionate, indifferent, exploitative, or anywhere in between.
- Likewise, people of average intelligence can be deeply compassionate.

One reason is that different social-cognitive capacities are involved:

- Cognitive empathy: understanding another person's perspective or mental state.
- Affective empathy: sharing or resonating with another person's emotions.
- Compassion: caring about another person's welfare and being motivated to alleviate suffering.

Higher intelligence may contribute somewhat to cognitive empathy in some contexts because perspective-taking can involve complex reasoning. However:
- Understanding another person's feelings does not necessarily produce concern for them.
- Individuals can possess excellent social insight without being compassionate.
- Some forms of manipulation require sophisticated perspective-taking.

Personality variables tend to predict compassion better than intelligence does. Traits associated with compassion include:
- Agreeableness
- Empathic concern
- Prosocial values
- Moral identity
- Certain attachment and developmental factors

There is also evidence that emotional intelligence and compassion are related, but emotional intelligence is only partially connected to traditional measures of cognitive intelligence.

From a neuroscience perspective, the systems commonly associated with higher-order reasoning and those associated with empathy/compassion overlap only partially. Networks involved in mentalizing, executive function, affective processing, and prosocial motivation are distinguishable and can dissociate clinically. For example, some patients retain high intellectual function despite profound social-emotional deficits, while others show the opposite pattern.

So the best summary is:
- Intelligence and compassion are not opposites.
- Intelligence and compassion are not strongly linked.
- The ability to understand others and the motivation to care about them are related but separable human capacities.
