# Different Views on Discipleship

Discipleship — the process of following, learning from, and being formed by a teacher or tradition — is understood quite differently across theological, philosophical, and practical frameworks.

---

## Christian Perspectives

### Traditional/Catholic View
- Formal, structured formation through the Church
- Sacraments central to the discipleship process
- Emphasis on **obedience, tradition, and community**
- Discipleship as lifelong sanctification

### Protestant/Evangelical View
- Personal relationship with Christ as foundation
- Bible study and accountability central
- Often emphasizes **intentional one-on-one mentoring**
- Goal: reproducing disciples (multiplication model)

### Missional View
- Discipleship happens **in everyday life contexts**
- Less program-driven, more organic
- Formation through mission and service
- Associated with thinkers like **Alan Hirsch, Dietrich Bonhoeffer**

### Contemplative View
- Spiritual disciplines (prayer, silence, fasting)
- Inner transformation emphasized
- Associated with **Thomas à Kempis, Henri Nouwen**

---

## Key Theological Tensions

| Question | Views Differ On |
|----------|----------------|
| Who leads? | Clergy vs. every believer |
| Where does it happen? | Church programs vs. daily life |
| What is the goal? | Knowledge vs. character vs. mission |
| How long does it take? | Courses vs. lifelong journey |

---

## Broader/Secular Perspectives

- **Philosophical tradition** (Socrates, Plato): discipleship as intellectual formation through questioning
- **Eastern traditions**: guru-student relationships emphasizing surrender and wisdom transmission
- **Leadership development**: mentoring focused on skill and character building

---

## Core Debates

- **Programmatic vs. relational** — Can discipleship be systematized?
- **Individual vs. communal** — Is it primarily personal or corporate?
- **Cost of discipleship** — Bonhoeffer's challenge against "cheap grace"
- **Cultural adaptation** — How much should discipleship models change across cultures?
