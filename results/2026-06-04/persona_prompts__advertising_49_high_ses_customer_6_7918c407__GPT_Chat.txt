How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to a combination of better placement, reducing interference, and upgrading equipment when necessary.

Start with the highest-impact steps:

- Place the router centrally in the home.
  - Keep it elevated (on a shelf or table rather than the floor).
  - Avoid closets, cabinets, and behind TVs.
  - Position it away from large metal objects and appliances.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band.
  - 5 GHz (or 6 GHz if available) provides higher speeds but shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz provides longer range but is more susceptible to interference.
  - Use band steering or separate network names if you want more control.

- Update router firmware.
  - Manufacturers frequently release updates that improve performance, stability, and security.

- Optimize channel selection.
  - In crowded neighborhoods or apartment buildings, neighboring Wi‑Fi networks can cause congestion.
  - Many modern routers automatically select channels, but manual optimization can sometimes help.

- Upgrade older equipment.
  - If your router is more than 4–5 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E model can provide significant improvements.
  - Ensure devices also support newer standards where possible.

For larger homes:

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system.
  - Mesh systems are often the best solution for multi-story homes or homes with dead zones.
  - Place nodes where they still receive a strong signal from the main unit.

- Use wired backhaul if possible.
  - Connecting mesh nodes or access points via Ethernet often delivers much better performance than wireless links.

- Add access points instead of simple extenders.
  - Dedicated access points connected by Ethernet generally outperform traditional Wi‑Fi extenders.

Reduce interference:

- Keep the router away from:
  - Microwaves
  - Cordless phone bases
  - Bluetooth hubs
  - Baby monitors
  - Thick concrete, brick, or stone walls

Improve internet performance beyond Wi‑Fi:

- Run a speed test directly from a device connected by Ethernet.
  - This helps determine whether the bottleneck is your internet service or the Wi‑Fi network.
- Check whether your internet plan matches your household's usage.
