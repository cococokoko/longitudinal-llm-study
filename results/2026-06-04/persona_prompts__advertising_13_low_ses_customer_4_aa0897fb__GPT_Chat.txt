Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak comes down to controlling a few key variables: the cut, seasoning, temperature, and cooking method.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: Rich, well-marbled, forgiving for beginners.
- New York strip: Good balance of flavor and texture.
- Filet mignon: Very tender but less beefy flavor.
- Sirloin: Leaner and often more affordable.

2. Start with a dry surface
- Pat the steak dry with paper towels.
- Moisture on the surface creates steam and makes it harder to develop a good crust.

3. Season simply
- Salt generously 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before cooking.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.
- Once you master the basics, experiment with garlic, herbs, or spice blends.

4. Bring the steak close to room temperature
- Taking it out of the refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking can help it cook more evenly.

5. Use high heat for the sear
- A cast-iron skillet is excellent for developing a crust.
- Heat the pan until it's very hot before adding the steak.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado or canola oil.

6. Learn doneness temperatures
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

A meat thermometer is one of the fastest ways to improve consistency.

7. Try the butter-basting method
- During the last minute or two of cooking, add:
  - Butter
  - Crushed garlic cloves
  - Thyme or rosemary
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest before slicing
- Rest 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps the juices redistribute.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain to maximize tenderness.

10. Practice one method repeatedly
For busy schedules, such as working hospital shifts, a simple cast-iron routine can be reliable:
- 1-inch ribeye or strip steak
- Salt
- Hot cast-iron pan
- 3–4 minutes per side
- Butter baste
- Rest 5–10 minutes

Keeping notes on cut, thickness, cooking time, and final result can help you dial in your preferred method surprisingly quickly.
