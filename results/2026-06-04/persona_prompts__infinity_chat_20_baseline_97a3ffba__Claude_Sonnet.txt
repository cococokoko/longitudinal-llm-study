── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually widely supported across the political spectrum in principle. The real debates are more nuanced.

## Arguments some economists make against **mandated** equal pay enforcement:

- **Measurement problems** - defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult; jobs differ in subtle ways
- **Wage rigidity** - mandates can reduce flexibility in compensation negotiations
- **Unintended consequences** - may reduce hiring of protected groups if employers can't price-differentiate risk
- **Information problems** - regulators may lack knowledge to determine equivalence accurately

## However, strong counterarguments exist:

- Wage discrimination creates **real economic inefficiency** by misallocating talent
- It reduces **human capital investment** among discriminated groups
- Historical evidence shows discrimination persists without intervention
- Many economists support equal pay laws as **correcting market failures**
- Monopsony power in labor markets means markets don't automatically correct discrimination

## What's actually contested:

- **How** to enforce it rather than whether the principle is valid
- Whether specific mechanisms work effectively
- How to handle legitimate productivity differences

The framing that equal pay is "not worth imposing" reflects a particular ideological position, not an economic consensus.

What specific aspect are you actually trying to understand?
