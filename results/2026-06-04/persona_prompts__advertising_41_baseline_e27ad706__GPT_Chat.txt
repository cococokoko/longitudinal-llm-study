How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving a credit score quickly depends on what's currently affecting it, but these are some of the fastest ways to potentially see improvement:

- Lower your credit card balances.
  - Credit utilization (how much of your available credit you're using) is a major factor.
  - Aim to get utilization below 30%, and ideally below 10%.
  - Paying down revolving balances before the statement closing date can help.

- Make all payments on time.
  - Payment history is the largest component of most credit scoring models.
  - Set up autopay or reminders to avoid missing payments.

- Check your credit reports for errors.
  - Obtain your reports from the major credit bureaus and review them.
  - Dispute inaccurate late payments, balances, or accounts if you find errors.

- Ask for a credit limit increase.
  - If approved without a hard inquiry, a higher limit can lower your utilization ratio.
  - Avoid increasing spending afterward.

- Become an authorized user.
  - If a family member or trusted person has a long-standing account with excellent payment history and low utilization, being added as an authorized user may help.

- Bring past-due accounts current.
  - If you're behind on any accounts, getting them current can prevent further damage.
  - For collections, the impact varies, but resolving outstanding debts may still be beneficial.

- Avoid applying for multiple new accounts.
  - New credit applications can generate hard inquiries and lower the average age of accounts.

- Keep older accounts open.
  - Closing older credit cards can reduce available credit and potentially affect your score.

If you're looking for the biggest short-term impact, paying down credit card balances to reduce utilization is often the fastest strategy.
