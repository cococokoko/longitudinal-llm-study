Improving a credit score quickly usually means focusing on the factors that credit scoring models update most frequently. As a single parent balancing childcare and work, it may help to prioritize the actions that can have the biggest impact with the least ongoing effort.

Here are the fastest ways to potentially improve your score:

- Lower your credit card balances
  - Credit utilization (how much of your available credit you're using) is a major factor.
  - Aim to keep each card below 30% of its limit, and below 10% if possible.
  - If you can make an extra payment before your card issuer reports to the credit bureaus, your score may improve relatively quickly.

- Make every payment on time
  - Payment history is the largest scoring factor.
  - Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment if possible.
  - Even one missed payment can significantly hurt your score.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Review your reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute any inaccurate late payments, balances, or accounts you don't recognize.
  - Correcting errors can sometimes produce meaningful score improvements.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If your income and payment history support it, a higher limit can lower your utilization ratio.
  - Before requesting one, ask whether it requires a "hard inquiry," which can temporarily affect your score.

- Become an authorized user
  - If a trusted family member has a long-standing credit card with a strong payment history and low balance, being added as an authorized user may help your credit profile.

- Avoid opening several new accounts
  - Multiple applications in a short period can lower your score temporarily.
  - Only apply for new credit if there's a clear benefit.

- Pay down accounts that are near their limits
  - Bringing a maxed-out card down can have a larger impact than spreading payments evenly across multiple cards.
