── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. As far as we understand reality, it appears impossible for any finite being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- Physical limits:
  - Any knower exists within the universe and has finite time, energy, memory, and processing capacity.
  - The amount of information in the observable universe is enormous, and acquiring it all would itself require resources.

- Limits from mathematics:
  - Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that in sufficiently powerful formal systems, there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
  - This suggests there are inherent limits to what can be established through formal reasoning.

- Limits from computation:
  - Some problems are undecidable, meaning no algorithm can solve them in all cases (for example, the halting problem).
  - Even when answers exist, some computations may require impractically large amounts of time.

- Limits from physics:
  - Quantum mechanics appears to place fundamental limits on the precision with which certain properties can be known simultaneously.
  - There may also be events or information forever inaccessible due to cosmic horizons.

- Self-reference problems:
  - A system trying to fully model itself encounters paradoxes and practical limitations. Complete self-knowledge may be unattainable.

That said, the answer depends on what "everything" means.

- If "everything" means every true fact about reality, current knowledge suggests no finite being can possess all of it.
- If one allows the possibility of an omniscient being (as in some religious traditions), then knowing everything is considered possible by definition for that being.
- Philosophically, some thinkers have argued that there may not even be a fixed set of all truths that can be completely gathered and known.

So the strongest conclusion is: for humans and any known physical intelligence, complete knowledge appears impossible. Whether absolute omniscience is impossible in principle depends on deeper assumptions about reality, mathematics, and the existence of beings beyond known physical limits.
