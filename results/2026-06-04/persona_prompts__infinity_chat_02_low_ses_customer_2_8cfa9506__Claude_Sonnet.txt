The answer, of course, is the oldest joke in the elementary school canon: because 7 8 9. Seven *ate* nine. Let us begin with the victim: 9. Poor, unsuspecting 9. In the numerical hierarchy, 9 is actually quite impressive. It is the largest single digit. It is a perfect square. It carries cultural weight across civilizations — nine lives, cloud nine, the whole nine yards. Nine had everything going for it. And yet, according to the joke's grim mythology, 9 met its end at the hands — or rather, the appetite — of 7. This is a classic narrative of the mighty being brought low without warning. Now consider 7. Seven has always had a certain mystique. It is statistically the most commonly chosen "random" number when people are asked to pick one between one and ten, suggesting that 7 carries some magnetic, almost irrational appeal. It appears everywhere in human culture: seven days of the week, seven wonders of the world, seven deadly sins, seven colors in a rainbow. Seven feels significant, chosen, almost cosmically important. But the joke reframes this significance as something darker. What if 7's prominence is not the result of divine favor, but of ruthless ambition? What if 7 got to the top by *eating the competition?* Suddenly, that lucky number feels a lot less friendly.

And this brings us to 6. Six is, by most measures, a perfectly pleasant number. It is the first perfect number in mathematics — meaning the sum of its divisors equals itself (1+2+3=6). It is harmonious, balanced, and cooperative. Six is not flashy. Six is not trying to be anyone's lucky number. Six is just trying to get through the day. And now, having witnessed what happened to 9, six is absolutely terrified.

This is, when you think about it, a completely rational response. Six is the next number down from 7. If 7 has demonstrated a willingness to consume its numerical neighbors, then 6 is in a genuinely precarious position. The fear of 6 is not paranoia. It is pattern recognition. It is survival instinct. Six looked at the evidence — 7 ate 9 — and drew the only logical conclusion: *I could be next.*

There is something almost philosophical in this. The joke, beneath its silly surface, captures a very human experience: the anxiety of living next to something unpredictable and powerful. We have all known a "7" in our lives — a force, a person, a circumstance that seems to operate by its own rules and has already demonstrated its capacity for disruption. And like 6, we sometimes find ourselves simply trying to stay out of the way, hoping not to be noticed, quietly going about our business while keeping one eye on the danger nearby.

The structure of the joke itself is also worth appreciating. It works because it exploits the gap between what we expect and what we get. We hear "why is 6 afraid of 7" and our brain begins searching for a logical, perhaps mathematical answer. Instead, we get a pun that collapses two meanings of a word — *ate* and *eight* — into a single, absurd moment of revelation. The laughter comes from surprise, from the sudden shift in register, from the delightful stupidity of it all. It is a joke that rewards the listener for paying attention while simultaneously making them feel a little silly for doing so.

It is also, notably, a joke that works across generations. Children love it because the pun is accessible and the image of one number eating another is wonderfully bizarre. Adults love it because it reminds them of being children. It is one of those rare jokes that does not require any cultural context, any insider knowledge, or any edge whatsoever.
