Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty typically do so for several reasons. These arguments can be presented strongly even though each is debated by opponents.

- Retribution and proportional justice
  - Some believe that certain crimes, especially premeditated murder or acts of terrorism, are so severe that the only punishment proportionate to the harm caused is death.
  - The argument is that justice requires a penalty that matches the gravity of the offense, not merely the protection of society.

- Deterrence
  - Supporters argue that the possibility of execution may discourage some individuals from committing the most serious crimes.
  - Even if the deterrent effect is difficult to measure, proponents contend that preventing even a small number of murders could justify the policy.

- Protection of society
  - Execution permanently prevents a convicted offender from committing future crimes, escaping prison, or harming others while incarcerated.
  - Advocates see this as the strongest possible guarantee of public safety.

- Moral accountability
  - Some argue that individuals who knowingly commit particularly heinous crimes have forfeited their own right to life through their actions.
  - In this view, the death penalty recognizes the offender's moral agency and responsibility.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Supporters often argue that the punishment acknowledges the seriousness of the harm done to victims.
  - Some families of victims feel that a death sentence delivers a sense of justice that lesser penalties cannot provide.

- Social contract theory
  - According to this argument, citizens benefit from legal protections and agree to obey the law.
  - Those who commit extreme crimes violate that social contract so fundamentally that society may be justified in imposing the ultimate penalty.

- Prevention of repeat violent offenses
  - While life imprisonment can incapacitate offenders, supporters note that prisoners can sometimes kill fellow inmates, prison staff, or others after release if sentences are altered.
  - Execution removes these risks entirely.

- Democratic legitimacy
  - In some countries or jurisdictions, the death penalty remains legal because elected representatives and portions of the public support it.
  - Advocates argue that, if enacted through lawful democratic processes and constitutional safeguards, it reflects society's judgment about punishment.

- Cost arguments (in some contexts)
  - Some supporters claim that permanently incarcerating offenders for decades can be expensive.
  - However, this argument is highly contested because death penalty cases often involve lengthy and costly appeals.

It's worth noting that each of these arguments has corresponding counterarguments, including concerns about wrongful convictions, unequal application, human rights, and questions about whether executions actually deter crime.
