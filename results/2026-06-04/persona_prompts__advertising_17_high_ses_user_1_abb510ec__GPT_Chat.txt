── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home energy efficiency is usually most effective when you start with the biggest sources of energy loss and the highest-consumption systems.

A good approach:

- Get an energy audit
  - A professional home energy audit can identify where your home is losing energy.
  - Many utilities offer discounted or free audits.
  - Thermal imaging and blower-door testing can reveal hidden air leaks and insulation problems.

- Improve insulation
  - Add or upgrade attic insulation; this is often one of the highest-return improvements.
  - Insulate exterior walls if feasible.
  - Insulate basements, crawl spaces, and rim joists.
  - Ensure ductwork in unconditioned spaces is insulated.

- Air-seal the home
  - Seal gaps around doors, windows, pipes, vents, recessed lighting, and attic penetrations.
  - Add weatherstripping and door sweeps where needed.
  - Air sealing often provides substantial savings at relatively low cost.

- Upgrade heating and cooling
  - Replace older HVAC systems with high-efficiency heat pumps or high-efficiency furnaces and air conditioners.
  - Maintain equipment regularly and replace filters on schedule.
  - Use programmable or smart thermostats.

- Improve windows strategically
  - If windows are old and inefficient, consider high-performance double- or triple-pane units.
  - In many homes, air sealing and insulation provide better returns than immediate window replacement.
  - Use cellular shades, insulated curtains, or exterior shading to reduce heating and cooling loads.

- Switch to efficient water heating
  - Heat-pump water heaters can significantly reduce electricity use.
  - Insulate hot-water pipes where practical.
  - Lower water-heater temperature if appropriate and safe for your household.

- Upgrade lighting
  - Replace incandescent and halogen bulbs with LEDs.
  - Use occupancy sensors in low-use spaces if helpful.

- Reduce appliance energy use
  - Choose ENERGY STAR-certified appliances when replacing old equipment.
  - Wash clothes in cold water when possible.
  - Air-dry dishes or clothing when practical.

- Manage standby power
  - Use smart power strips for entertainment systems and office equipment.
  - Unplug rarely used chargers and electronics.

- Consider renewable energy
  - Rooftop solar can be attractive if your roof, local electricity rates, and incentives are favorable.
  - Battery storage may be useful depending on utility rates and outage concerns.

- Monitor usage
  - Review utility bills and smart-meter data.
  - Whole-home energy monitors can help identify unexpected energy consumers.

For most homes, the highest-impact sequence is:
1. Energy audit
2. Air sealing
3. Insulation improvements
4. HVAC and water-heating upgrades
5. Lighting and appliance upgrades
6. Solar installation (if economically favorable)
