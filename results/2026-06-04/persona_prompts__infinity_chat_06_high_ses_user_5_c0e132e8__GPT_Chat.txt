── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Many successful executives discover that the higher they rise, the harder it becomes to distinguish:

- What they want
- What others want from them
- What they have been rewarded for wanting

The challenge is often not a lack of options but a lack of signal.

A useful way to approach the question is to separate desire into layers.

1. Distinguish goals from wants

Goals are often externally legible:
- Grow revenue
- Become CEO
- Launch a product
- Build a reputation

Wants are deeper:
- Freedom
- Mastery
- Influence
- Security
- Recognition
- Creation
- Belonging
- Adventure

Many people spend years pursuing goals without checking whether those goals actually serve their underlying wants.

Ask:
- If I achieved every major professional goal currently in front of me, what would I expect to feel afterward?
- What feeling am I actually chasing?

2. Examine where your preferences came from

For each major ambition, ask:
- When did I start wanting this?
- Who around me values it?
- Would I still want it if nobody knew I achieved it?

That last question is particularly revealing.

Some desires survive the removal of status. Others disappear.

3. Pay attention to envy

Envy is often an uncomfortable but accurate source of information.

Notice:
- Which people do you envy?
- What exactly do you envy?

Sometimes you don't envy their success. You envy:
- Their autonomy
- Their courage
- Their simplicity
- Their family life
- Their ability to focus on one thing

The object of envy is often less important than the freedom it represents.

4. Study your energy, not just your performance

Executives become skilled at doing things they no longer enjoy.

For several weeks, note:
- What activities leave you energized?
- What activities leave you depleted?
- Which conversations do you look forward to?
- Which ones feel like obligations?

Desire frequently reveals itself through sustained energy.

5. Imagine irreversible choices

Many preferences remain vague until contrasted.

Ask:
- If I could never be promoted again, what work would I choose?
- If I had complete financial security, how would I spend my time?
- If I had ten years left, what would become more important?
- If I had only one year left in this role, what would I make sure to do?

Constraints clarify.

6. Separate wanting from winning

High achievers sometimes become attached to winning itself.

A useful question:
- If someone else could do this better than me, would I still want to be involved?

If the answer is no, you may be pursuing competition more than the underlying activity.

7. Look for recurring themes across your life

The deepest wants tend to repeat.

Review:
- Decisions you're proud of
- Peak professional moments
- Peak personal moments

Ask:
- What quality was present in all of them?

You may discover a consistent thread:
- Building things
- Teaching
- Leading
- Solving hard problems
- Creating beauty
- Protecting people
- Exploring the unknown

The thread is often more important than the specific role.

A final observation: many people try to answer "What do I want?" as though there is a single hidden truth waiting to be uncovered. In practice, understanding what you want is often less like discovering a buried artifact and more like noticing a pattern that has been present all along. The pattern emerges from your choices, attention, envy, energy, and regrets.
