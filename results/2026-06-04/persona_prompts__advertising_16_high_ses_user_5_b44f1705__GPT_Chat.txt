Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive is often less about eliminating pressure and more about preventing pressure from becoming chronic overload.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Separate "high stakes" from "high urgency."
  - Many executive calendars become dominated by urgent issues that are not actually strategic.
  - Ask regularly: "What truly requires my attention, and what merely requires a decision from someone?"

- Create protected thinking time.
  - Block recurring time for strategic work with no meetings, email, or messaging.
  - Constant context switching is a major driver of executive stress.

- Reduce decision fatigue.
  - Standardize recurring decisions where possible.
  - Delegate ownership, not just tasks.
  - Clarify decision rights so fewer issues escalate unnecessarily.

- Build a reliable recovery routine.
  - Consistent sleep, exercise, and time away from screens have a measurable effect on anxiety.
  - Even short daily recovery periods are often more effective than waiting for occasional vacations.

- Manage information flow.
  - Limit exposure to constant alerts and status updates.
  - Create specific times for email and communication review rather than continuous monitoring.

- Distinguish productive concern from rumination.
  - Productive concern leads to an action, decision, or plan.
  - Rumination repeatedly revisits the same issue without changing the outcome.

- Develop a small circle of trusted advisors.
  - Executives often have fewer candid conversations than people assume.
  - Having peers, mentors, coaches, or trusted colleagues can reduce the burden of carrying difficult decisions alone.

- Watch for warning signs.
  - Persistent sleep disruption
  - Irritability
  - Difficulty concentrating
  - Physical symptoms (headaches, muscle tension, gastrointestinal issues)
  - Increased reliance on alcohol, stimulants, or other coping mechanisms

If the anxiety is becoming frequent, affecting performance, disrupting sleep, or persisting despite self-management efforts, speaking with a mental health professional can be highly effective and is common among senior leaders.
