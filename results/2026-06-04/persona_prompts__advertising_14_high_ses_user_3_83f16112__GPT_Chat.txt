── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a tenured professor at a prestigious university.

Creating a successful YouTube channel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you already have a major advantage: deep expertise and institutional credibility. The challenge is translating that expertise into content that people want to watch regularly.

A practical framework:

1. Define your audience
- General public interested in your field
- University students
- Professionals and practitioners
- Policymakers
- Academic peers

The most successful educational channels usually focus on one primary audience rather than trying to serve everyone.

2. Choose a clear channel promise
Viewers should immediately understand what they will gain.

Examples:
- "Explaining the latest discoveries in neuroscience."
- "Economics explained through current events."
- "History behind today's biggest headlines."
- "University-level physics for curious people."

3. Prioritize topics over credentials
Many academics lead with their CV. Viewers care more about the question being answered.

Less effective:
- "Lecture 7: Advanced Theoretical Frameworks"

More effective:
- "Why Experts Keep Getting This Prediction Wrong"
- "The Surprising Science Behind Memory Loss"
- "What Most People Misunderstand About Inflation"

4. Develop a repeatable content format
Consistency matters more than complexity.

Possible formats:
- 8–15 minute explainers
- Myth-versus-fact episodes
- Analysis of current events through your discipline
- Reactions to news stories
- Q&A videos
- Interviews with leading experts

5. Publish consistently
A realistic schedule is better than an ambitious one that collapses.

Examples:
- One high-quality video per week
- Two videos per month

6. Learn YouTube packaging
Academic quality alone rarely drives growth.

Focus on:
- Titles that create curiosity
- Clear thumbnails
- Strong first 30 seconds
- Simple language

7. Build an intellectual brand
People often subscribe to individuals rather than subjects.

Viewers should gradually associate you with:
- A specific expertise
- A particular style of explanation
- Reliable analysis

8. Use current events strategically
Connecting your expertise to ongoing public conversations can dramatically increase reach.

Example:
- A political scientist explaining an election.
- A climate scientist analyzing a major weather event.
- A historian explaining the background of a geopolitical conflict.

9. Repurpose content
One video can become:
- Short clips for YouTube Shorts
- LinkedIn posts
- X posts
- Newsletter content
- Course material

10. Think in years, not months
Many successful educational channels grow slowly for the first 50–100 videos before reaching a larger audience.

A strong starting plan:
- Publish one 10–15 minute video each week.
- Create 3–5 Shorts from each long video.
- Focus on a single audience.
- Measure watch time and audience retention.
- Commit to producing at least 50 videos before making major judgments about success.
