In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, there is some correlation between intelligence and certain forms of compassion, but the relationship is modest and more complicated than "more intelligent people are more compassionate."

Research generally finds:

- Cognitive intelligence (IQ, reasoning ability) has a weak positive relationship with perspective-taking—the ability to understand what another person is thinking or feeling.
- Compassion and empathy are not the same thing. A person may be very good at understanding others' emotions (cognitive empathy) without feeling much concern for them (compassion), and vice versa.
- Emotional intelligence, which includes recognizing and managing emotions, tends to be more strongly associated with compassionate behavior than IQ alone.
- Personality traits, especially agreeableness, are often better predictors of kindness, compassion, and prosocial behavior than intelligence.
- Some studies suggest that higher intelligence is associated with broader moral concern (for example, concern for distant people, future generations, or social issues), but the effect sizes are typically not large.

There are also reasons the correlation is not stronger:

- Intelligence can help someone understand suffering, but it does not automatically motivate them to help.
- Compassion can be driven by temperament, upbringing, culture, values, and life experiences independently of intelligence.
- Highly intelligent people vary enormously in character. Some are exceptionally compassionate; others are not.

So the most accurate summary is that intelligence may slightly increase a person's ability to understand others, but compassion depends heavily on personality, values, and emotional development. The two traits overlap somewhat, but they are far from the same thing.

You mentioned working long hours in a warehouse. If your question comes from observing coworkers or managers and wondering whether smarter people tend to be more considerate, everyday experience often reflects the research: competence and intelligence don't reliably predict how kindly someone treats others. Personality and incentives frequently matter more.
