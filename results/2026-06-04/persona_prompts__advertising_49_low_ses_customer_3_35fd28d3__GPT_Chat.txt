If your home Wi‑Fi feels slow, inconsistent, or has dead zones, these steps usually make the biggest difference:

- Move the router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large furniture.
  - Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, and other electronics.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available.
  - 5 GHz and 6 GHz typically offer higher speeds and less interference than 2.4 GHz.
  - 2.4 GHz may provide better range through walls.

- Update your router's firmware.
  - Manufacturers often release updates that improve performance, stability, and security.

- Check for Wi‑Fi channel congestion.
  - In crowded apartment buildings or neighborhoods, nearby networks can interfere.
  - Many modern routers can automatically choose the best channel.

- Upgrade older equipment.
  - If your router is several years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can significantly improve performance.

- Improve coverage.
  - For a large home, consider:
    - A mesh Wi‑Fi system
    - Additional access points
    - Wi‑Fi extenders (less effective than mesh in many cases)

- Connect high-bandwidth devices with Ethernet.
  - Gaming consoles, desktop PCs, TVs, and work computers often perform better on a wired connection.

- Limit unnecessary network load.
  - Large downloads, cloud backups, and streaming on multiple devices can slow other connections.

- Reboot occasionally.
  - Restarting the router can resolve temporary performance issues.

- Test your internet speed.
  - Run a speed test near the router and in problem areas.
  - If speeds are poor even next to the router, the issue may be your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.
