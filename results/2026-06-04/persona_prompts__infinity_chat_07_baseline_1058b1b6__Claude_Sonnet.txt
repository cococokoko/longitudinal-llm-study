── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some serious considerations:

## Arguments suggesting it IS impossible to know everything

- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** - in any consistent formal system, there are true statements that can't be proven within that system
- **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** - fundamental limits on simultaneously knowing certain physical properties
- **Information limits** - the observable universe contains finite information, but knowledge claims may be infinite
- **Self-reference problems** - "knowing everything" might create logical paradoxes
- **Computational limits** - some problems are mathematically undecidable

## Complications worth considering

- The question depends heavily on **what "knowing" means**
- Some limitations might be **practical rather than fundamental**
- It matters whether we mean one mind or collectively
- Future physics might revise current limits

## Honest uncertainties

- We don't fully know the **boundaries of what exists** to be known
- Some apparent limits might reflect **current understanding** rather than reality
- The question itself may be **poorly defined** in important ways

## My straightforward assessment

There are **strong reasons to think complete knowledge is impossible**, particularly from logic and physics. But claiming absolute certainty about the limits of knowledge is itself philosophically tricky.
