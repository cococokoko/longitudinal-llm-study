You know that the customer is a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving productivity while working from home.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For a senior attorney working from home, productivity often depends less on working harder and more on protecting attention, managing communication, and structuring the day around high-value legal work.

Some practical approaches:

- Time-block deep work
  - Reserve uninterrupted blocks for drafting, strategy, complex research, and case analysis.
  - Schedule these periods during your peak cognitive hours and treat them like court appearances or client meetings.

- Separate reactive and proactive work
  - Designate specific times for email, internal messages, and administrative matters.
  - Avoid allowing inbox activity to dictate the day's priorities.

- Create a clear matter-priority system
  - Review active matters each morning or the evening before.
  - Identify:
    - Urgent deadlines
    - High-value client work
    - Business development tasks
    - Administrative obligations

- Use a dedicated home office
  - A space with a door, reliable technology, and minimal distractions can significantly improve concentration and professionalism during client interactions.

- Standardize routine legal tasks
  - Develop templates, checklists, and workflows for recurring documents, intake procedures, discovery management, and client communications.
  - Small efficiencies compound over time.

- Conduct brief daily team check-ins
  - A 10–15 minute meeting can reduce interruptions throughout the day and keep matters moving efficiently.

- Protect meeting quality
  - Require agendas when possible.
  - Decline or shorten meetings that do not require real-time discussion.
  - Consider whether an email or shared document would achieve the same objective.

- Track where time actually goes
  - For a week or two, compare billable work, administrative work, business development, and interruptions.
  - Many professionals discover productivity leaks that are not obvious in real time.

- Build deliberate transition rituals
  - One challenge of remote work is the absence of physical separation between office and home.
  - A consistent start-of-day review and end-of-day wrap-up can improve focus and reduce lingering work concerns after hours.

- Invest in technology
  - Reliable video conferencing, dual monitors, document-management systems, dictation tools, and secure remote-access solutions often generate substantial returns in efficiency.

For attorneys specifically, one of the highest-impact changes is often reserving uninterrupted drafting and analysis time before opening email or responding to messages. That protects the work that typically creates the most value for clients and the firm.
