In Christianity, “discipleship” generally means following Jesus and growing in faith, but different traditions emphasize different aspects of it.

Some common views include:

- Evangelical view
  - Focuses on a personal relationship with Jesus.
  - Emphasizes Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and mentoring relationships.
  - Discipleship is often seen as a lifelong process of spiritual growth.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as following Christ through faith, participation in the sacraments, prayer, and service.
  - Emphasizes growth in holiness within the life of the Church.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation and union with God (often called theosis).
  - Emphasizes worship, prayer, fasting, and participation in the sacramental life of the Church.

- Mainline Protestant view
  - Often highlights both personal faith and social responsibility.
  - Discipleship includes justice, compassion, community involvement, and ethical living.

- Anabaptist view
  - Places strong emphasis on actively obeying Jesus’ teachings.
  - Often stresses peace, simplicity, community, and service.

- Liberation theology perspective
  - Emphasizes following Jesus by standing with the poor and oppressed.
  - Views discipleship as involving social and political action for justice.

- Radical discipleship movements
  - Encourage a more countercultural lifestyle modeled closely on Jesus.
  - May involve intentional community, voluntary simplicity, and sacrificial service.

Despite these differences, most Christian traditions agree that discipleship involves learning from Jesus, becoming more like him, and living out faith in everyday life.
