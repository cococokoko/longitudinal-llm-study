Improving mental health is usually most effective when approached as a combination of daily habits, relationships, mindset, and professional support when needed. As an executive, it can also help to recognize that high responsibility, constant decision-making, and sustained pressure create unique mental demands.

Some areas to consider:

- Prioritize sleep
  - Aim for a consistent sleep schedule.
  - Limit late-night work and excessive alcohol.
  - Treat sleep as a performance and health requirement, not a luxury.

- Maintain physical health
  - Exercise regularly, even if it's just walking, strength training, or cardio several times per week.
  - Eat consistently and stay hydrated.
  - Physical activity is one of the most reliable ways to improve mood and reduce stress.

- Create recovery time
  - Schedule periods with no meetings, emails, or major decisions.
  - Build genuine downtime into your calendar instead of waiting for it to appear.
  - Take vacations and fully disconnect when possible.

- Strengthen relationships
  - Spend time with people you trust and enjoy.
  - Invest in family, friendships, and community.
  - Social connection is a major protective factor for mental health.

- Manage stress proactively
  - Notice recurring sources of stress and identify which can be reduced, delegated, or eliminated.
  - Consider practices such as mindfulness, meditation, journaling, or reflective thinking.
  - Build routines that help you transition out of work mode.

- Set boundaries
  - Define when you are available and when you are not.
  - Avoid allowing work to occupy every hour of the day.
  - Model healthy boundaries for your team when possible.

- Pay attention to your thinking patterns
  - Watch for persistent self-criticism, catastrophizing, or all-or-nothing thinking.
  - Ask whether your interpretation of a situation is supported by evidence.
  - Developing self-awareness can reduce unnecessary psychological strain.

- Seek professional support when useful
  - Therapy, counseling, or executive coaching can provide valuable perspective and tools.
  - You do not need to wait for a crisis to benefit from professional help.

- Monitor for signs that additional support may be needed
  - Persistent sadness, anxiety, irritability, burnout, loss of motivation, sleep disruption, or difficulty functioning are worth taking seriously.
  - If symptoms are significant or lasting, speaking with a mental health professional is advisable.

A useful starting point is to assess four areas on a scale from 1–10:
- Sleep
- Physical activity
- Stress management
- Relationships

The lowest-scoring area is often where the next improvement will have the greatest impact.
