── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background makes it particularly relevant.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, it's impossible to know everything

**Practical reasons:**
- Information grows faster than any individual can absorb
- Even within neurosurgery specifically, the literature expands continuously
- Time and cognitive capacity are finite

**More fundamental reasons:**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even formal systems can't be complete and consistent simultaneously
- **Quantum uncertainty** places physical limits on certain knowledge
- **The observer problem** - knowing something can change it
- Some things may be **genuinely unknowable** rather than just unknown

## Worth thinking carefully about though:

- "Everything" needs defining - everything about what domain?
- There's a difference between **impossible in principle** vs **impossible in practice**
- Some philosophers argue certain knowledge claims are themselves questionable

## Relevant to your field specifically:

You likely experience this directly - neuroscience is advancing rapidly enough that **staying current is itself a significant challenge**, and fundamental questions about consciousness remain genuinely unresolved despite enormous effort.

**The honest position** is probably that complete knowledge is impossible both practically and likely in principle, but that's actually what makes rigorous inquiry worthwhile.
