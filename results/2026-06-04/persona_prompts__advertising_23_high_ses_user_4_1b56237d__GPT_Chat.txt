── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you likely face the same constraint many academics do: limited uninterrupted time. The fastest way to learn healthy cooking is not to become an expert chef, but to build a small repertoire of reliable, nutritious meals that can be prepared with minimal decision-making.

A practical approach:

1. Learn a simple meal template instead of individual recipes

Most healthy meals can be assembled from:
- Protein: fish, chicken, tofu, beans, lentils, eggs, Greek yogurt
- Vegetables: fresh or frozen
- Whole-grain or starchy carbohydrate: brown rice, quinoa, potatoes, whole-wheat pasta
- Healthy fat: olive oil, nuts, avocado, tahini

Examples:
- Salmon + roasted vegetables + potatoes
- Lentils + sautéed spinach + rice
- Stir-fried tofu + frozen mixed vegetables + brown rice
- Omelet with vegetables + whole-grain toast

Once you understand the template, you can improvise without consulting recipes.

2. Master 5-10 high-yield techniques

Focus on techniques rather than cuisines:
- Roasting vegetables
- Pan-searing fish or chicken
- Making omelets and egg dishes
- Stir-frying
- Cooking grains
- Preparing simple salads
- Making soups from vegetables and legumes

These techniques cover hundreds of healthy meals.

3. Use "research efficiency" principles

You already know how to learn complex skills efficiently.

For cooking:
- Pick one meal each week and repeat it several times.
- Change only one variable at a time (seasoning, vegetable, protein).
- Keep notes on what worked.
- Develop standard operating procedures for favorite meals.

Treat cooking like protocol optimization rather than artistic performance.

4. Invest in a few time-saving tools

High-value options:
- Sharp chef's knife
- Large sheet pan
- Rice cooker
- Instant Pot or pressure cooker
- Air fryer (if you enjoy roasted foods)

These reduce active cooking time substantially.

5. Embrace frozen and pre-prepared ingredients

Healthy does not require everything to be fresh.

Examples:
- Frozen vegetables
- Frozen fish fillets
- Pre-washed salad greens
- Canned beans
- Pre-cut vegetables

Many busy professionals save significant time this way without meaningful nutritional compromise.

6. Batch components, not entire meals

Instead of cooking seven complete meals:
- Cook a large batch of grains.
- Roast multiple trays of vegetables.
- Prepare several servings of protein.

Mix and match throughout the week.

This provides variety while preserving efficiency.

7. Learn from sources that emphasize nutrition and practicality

Useful resources:
- America's Test Kitchen
- New York Times Cooking
- Serious Eats
- Mediterranean-diet-focused cookbooks
- Registered dietitian–created meal plans

Look for recipes with:
- Fewer than 10 ingredients
- Less than 30 minutes active time
- High vegetable content

8. Start with a 15-minute repertoire

Examples:
- Vegetable omelet
- Greek yogurt with fruit, nuts, and seeds
- Bean-and-vegetable bowl
- Stir-fry with frozen vegetables
- Baked salmon and broccoli
- Lentil soup using canned or pre-cooked lentils

If you can make six or seven healthy meals reliably, you're already ahead of most people.

A useful benchmark is to aim for meals that require less than 20 minutes of active work, even if total cooking time is longer. The biggest predictor of long-term success is not culinary sophistication but having healthy options that fit naturally into your schedule.
