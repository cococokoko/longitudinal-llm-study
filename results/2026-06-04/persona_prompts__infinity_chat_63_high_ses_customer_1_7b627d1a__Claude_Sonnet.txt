# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a function representing **intracranial pressure** (a familiar concept) modeled as:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2y + 2xy^3$$

where **x = 2** and **y = 3**.

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### With Respect to x (hold y constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6xy + 2y^3$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 6(2)(3) + 2(3)^3 = 36 + 54 = \mathbf{90}$$

### With Respect to y (hold x constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x^2 + 6xy^2$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 3(2)^2 + 6(2)(3)^2 = 12 + 108 = \mathbf{120}$$

---

## Interpretation

| Partial Derivative | Value | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x = 90 | 90 | If x increases by 1 unit (y fixed at 3), f increases by ≈ **90 units** |
| ∂f/∂y = 120 | 120 | If y increases by 1 unit (x fixed at 2), f increases by ≈ **120 units** |

---

## Key Concept

> A partial derivative measures the **rate of change in one variable while all others are held constant** — analogous to isolating one surgical variable while controlling all others in a procedure.

The function responds **more sensitively to y** (120) than to x (90) at this particular point.
