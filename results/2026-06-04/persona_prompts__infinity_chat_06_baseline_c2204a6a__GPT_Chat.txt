── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experiences.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice what energizes you.
  - After activities, conversations, projects, or experiences, ask:
    - Did this give me energy or drain me?
    - Would I willingly do more of this?
  - Over time, patterns emerge.

- Pay attention to envy.
  - When you're jealous of someone, the useful question is often not "Why do they have that?" but "What does this reveal that I value?"
  - The answer might be freedom, recognition, creativity, stability, connection, or something else.

- Separate your desires from expectations.
  - Ask:
    - What do I want if nobody judged me?
    - What do I want if success were guaranteed?
    - What do I want if nobody ever found out?
  - Different answers can reveal different influences.

- Experiment instead of overthinking.
  - Many people try to think their way into clarity when clarity often comes from action.
  - Try things in small, low-risk ways and observe your reactions.
  - Information from experience is usually more reliable than speculation.

- Look at recurring themes.
  - Review the moments in your life that felt meaningful, exciting, or satisfying.
  - What values were present? Learning? Achievement? Adventure? Service? Intimacy? Independence?

- Distinguish wants from goals.
  - A goal might be "become a doctor" or "start a company."
  - Underneath that may be deeper wants such as helping people, intellectual challenge, status, security, or autonomy.
  - Understanding the deeper layer gives you more options.

- Accept uncertainty.
  - Sometimes the honest answer is, "I don't know what I want yet."
  - That isn't failure. It is a stage of the process.

If you're feeling especially stuck, try answering these three questions without overthinking:
1. What do I spend time thinking about when I don't have to?
2. What kinds of people do I admire most?
3. What would I regret not trying in the next five years?

Your answers don't have to be perfect. They just need to be honest enough to point you in a direction. What makes you ask this question right now?
