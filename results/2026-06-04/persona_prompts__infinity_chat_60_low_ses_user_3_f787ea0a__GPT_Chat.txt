── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a very long line of dominoes set up in a strange pattern.

You ask, "If I knock over the first domino, what will the 1,000th domino be doing later?"

For some domino setups, you can figure out the answer quickly without watching all the dominoes fall.

But for other setups, the only way to know what happens at domino #1,000 is to actually watch domino #1, then #2, then #3, and so on, all the way to #1,000.

Computational irreducibility is the idea that some things are like that second kind of domino setup:

- There is no shortcut.
- You can't jump ahead and calculate the answer much faster.
- The fastest way to know what happens is to let every step happen, one after another.

A simple way to think about it:

- Easy puzzle: "What's 2 + 2?" You can answer right away.
- Irreducible puzzle: A giant maze where the only way to know where a path leads is to follow it.

So, computational irreducibility means: for some systems, the future can't be predicted without doing all the work of creating that future step by step.
