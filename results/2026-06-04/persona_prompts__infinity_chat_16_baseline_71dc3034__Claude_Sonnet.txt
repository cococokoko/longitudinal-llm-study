── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the future of work in an increasingly automated economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question worth thinking through carefully rather than just optimistically or pessimistically.

## What We Actually Know

**Historical pattern:** Previous automation waves displaced specific jobs but created new ones overall - though transitions were often painful for affected workers.

**Current situation differs in some ways:**
- AI can now affect cognitive work, not just physical/routine tasks
- Speed of change may exceed workers' ability to adapt
- Benefits have concentrated rather than distributed broadly

## Reasonable Concerns

- Middle-skill jobs face particular vulnerability
- Geographic concentration of new jobs creates real displacement problems
- Retraining programs have historically underperformed expectations
- Some workers genuinely may not transition successfully

## Legitimate Counterarguments

- New job categories consistently emerge that we can't predict
- Productivity gains could fund broader prosperity
- Many jobs involve human judgment, relationships, and accountability that resist automation
- Labor shortages in many sectors suggest demand for workers remains strong

## Honest Uncertainties

- Whether this wave is fundamentally different from previous ones
- How quickly adaptation can realistically happen
- Whether policy responses will be adequate or timely

## What Seems Most Important

- **Policy choices matter enormously** - outcomes aren't predetermined
- Education, safety nets, and profit distribution are key variables
- Worker bargaining power significantly affects who benefits

What specific aspect concerns you most? The economic, social, or policy dimensions each deserve separate consideration.
