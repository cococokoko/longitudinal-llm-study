Stress is the body’s natural response to challenges or demands. In small amounts, it can help people stay alert and focused. However, when stress becomes frequent or long-lasting, it can affect both physical and mental health.

Physically, stress triggers the release of hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol. These hormones increase heart rate, raise blood pressure, and prepare the body for action. Over time, chronic stress can contribute to headaches, muscle tension, fatigue, sleep problems, digestive issues, and a weakened immune system. People who work in fast-paced environments or have unpredictable schedules may experience these effects more often if they do not have enough time to recover.

Mentally, stress can affect mood, concentration, and decision-making. It may lead to feelings of anxiety, irritability, frustration, or being overwhelmed. Long-term stress can also increase the risk of depression and burnout. When stress levels are high, it can become harder to focus on tasks, remember information, or maintain motivation.

Managing stress is important for overall well-being. Healthy coping strategies include regular exercise, adequate sleep, relaxation techniques, social support, and setting aside time for enjoyable activities. By recognizing the signs of stress early and taking steps to address them, people can protect both their physical and mental health.
