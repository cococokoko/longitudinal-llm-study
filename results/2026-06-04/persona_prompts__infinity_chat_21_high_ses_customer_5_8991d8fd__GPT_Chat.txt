People who support allowing the death penalty typically make several arguments. Even among supporters, there is disagreement about which argument is strongest.

Common arguments in favor include:

- Retribution and proportional justice
  - Some believe that certain crimes, such as premeditated murder or acts of terrorism, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - This view is based on the idea that justice should reflect the gravity of the offense, not only focus on rehabilitation or deterrence.

- Deterrence
  - Supporters argue that the possibility of execution may discourage some people from committing the most serious crimes.
  - The reasoning is that a punishment perceived as more severe than life imprisonment could influence potential offenders' decisions.
  - Empirical evidence on deterrence remains heavily debated, but the argument continues to be influential.

- Protection of society
  - Execution permanently prevents a convicted offender from committing future crimes, escaping prison, directing criminal activity from prison, or harming fellow inmates and staff.
  - Some supporters see this as the highest level of public safety.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Some argue that the death penalty provides a sense of closure or justice to victims' relatives.
  - The view is that society acknowledges the seriousness of the harm by imposing its most severe punishment.

- Moral accountability
  - Supporters may contend that individuals who knowingly commit especially heinous crimes should bear the fullest consequences of their actions.
  - In this view, respecting human agency includes holding people fully responsible for deliberate wrongdoing.

- Social condemnation
  - The death penalty can be seen as a statement that certain acts are fundamentally unacceptable.
  - Advocates argue that it expresses society's strongest moral condemnation of extreme crimes.

- Incapacitation without lifelong incarceration
  - Some supporters argue that keeping offenders imprisoned for decades imposes ongoing costs and security risks.
  - They view execution as a definitive resolution in cases involving the most dangerous offenders.

- Historical, cultural, or religious grounds
  - Certain legal traditions, cultural norms, or religious interpretations have supported capital punishment for specific offenses.
  - Supporters may see the death penalty as consistent with longstanding concepts of justice.

It is worth noting that each of these arguments has corresponding counterarguments. For example, opponents often raise concerns about wrongful convictions, unequal application of the law, human rights, moral objections to state execution, and questions about whether the death penalty actually deters crime more effectively than life imprisonment. The debate largely turns on how people weigh justice, public safety, human rights, and the risk of irreversible error.
