── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no objectively "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

The highest absolute profits in the history of short-term trading have generally come from strategies that are inaccessible to most traders, including:

- High-frequency market making
  - Firms continuously quote bid and ask prices and earn the spread.
  - Requires sophisticated technology, infrastructure, and risk management.
  - Historically among the most consistently profitable categories of day trading.

- Statistical arbitrage
  - Exploits small, temporary pricing discrepancies among related securities.
  - Used by quantitative firms such as Renaissance Technologies, Citadel, Two Sigma, and others.
  - Relies on large datasets, quantitative research, and automation.

- Latency arbitrage and microstructure strategies
  - Seek tiny, fleeting market inefficiencies.
  - Depend heavily on speed and technology advantages.

For an individual trader, the question changes from "most profitable ever" to "best risk-adjusted strategy that can actually be executed."

Among retail-accessible approaches, the most durable categories are often:

- Trend-following momentum
  - Buy strength, sell weakness.
  - Simple concept with extensive academic support across many markets.
  - Requires strict risk controls because win rates can be modest.

- Opening range breakout
  - Trades breakouts from the first segment of the trading day.
  - Common among active equity traders.

- VWAP-based institutional flow trading
  - Uses volume-weighted average price as a benchmark.
  - Attempts to align with intraday institutional order flow.

- Mean reversion
  - Bets that extreme short-term moves will partially reverse.
  - Can have high win rates but is vulnerable to large losses if risk management is poor.

If your definition of "best" is maximizing long-term wealth rather than trading profits, the historical evidence is much less favorable to day trading. Broad-market investing has outperformed the vast majority of day traders after costs and taxes.

If your definition is "which day-trading approach has generated the largest aggregate profits in financial history," the answer is likely some form of quantitative market making or statistical arbitrage conducted by elite institutional firms rather than a discretionary chart-based strategy.
