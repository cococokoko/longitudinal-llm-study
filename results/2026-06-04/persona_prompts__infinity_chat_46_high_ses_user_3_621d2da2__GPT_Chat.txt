── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term "linear graph" is sometimes used (especially in older literature) to mean a simple graph, but many people may actually be referring to a line graph.

Example of a line graph:

Start with the graph G, which is a path on four vertices:

A — B — C — D

The edges of G are:
- e1 = AB
- e2 = BC
- e3 = CD

The line graph L(G) is constructed by:
- Creating one vertex for each edge of G.
- Connecting two vertices whenever the corresponding edges in G share a common endpoint.

Thus L(G) has vertices:
- e1, e2, e3

and edges:
- e1—e2 (because AB and BC meet at B)
- e2—e3 (because BC and CD meet at C)

So the line graph is:

e1 — e2 — e3

which is itself a path on three vertices.

If by "linear graph" you mean something else (for example, a graph consisting of vertices arranged in a line, i.e., a path graph), a simple example is:

v1 — v2 — v3 — v4 — v5

This is the path graph P5.
