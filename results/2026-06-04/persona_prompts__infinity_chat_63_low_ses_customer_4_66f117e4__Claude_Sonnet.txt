# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Let's use a function that relates to something practical. Suppose your **hourly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **x** = hours worked per week
- **y** = years of experience

$$f(x, y) = 2x^2 + 3xy + y^2$$

---

## Evaluate at a Specific Point

Let's say you work **x = 40 hours/week** and have **y = 5 years** of experience.

$$f(40, 5) = 2(40)^2 + 3(40)(5) + (5)^2$$
$$= 2(1600) + 600 + 25 = \mathbf{3225}$$

---

## Calculate the Partial Derivatives

### ∂f/∂x — *How earnings change with hours, holding experience fixed*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 4x + 3y$$

At (40, 5): &nbsp; $4(40) + 3(5) = 160 + 15 = \mathbf{175}$

> ✅ Each **additional hour** worked adds approximately **175 units** to earnings

### ∂f/∂y — *How earnings change with experience, holding hours fixed*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x + 2y$$

At (40, 5): &nbsp; $3(40) + 2(5) = 120 + 10 = \mathbf{130}$

> ✅ Each **additional year** of experience adds approximately **130 units** to earnings

---

## Key Idea

| Partial Derivative | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x = 175 | Vary hours → freeze experience |
| ∂f/∂y = 130 | Vary experience → freeze hours |

**The partial derivative isolates the effect of ONE variable while treating all others as constants.**
