── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The first question I would challenge is the premise: what counts as "achieving anything"?

Many people reach 60 having never become famous, wealthy, influential, or exceptional in a way that society readily recognizes. Yet they may have:
- Raised children.
- Been a reliable friend or partner.
- Cared for parents.
- Done work that helped others.
- Endured hardship without much recognition.
- Learned things, traveled, created memories, or simply lived with integrity.

Those things often don't feel like "achievements" when compared against public measures of success, but they are a large part of what most human lives actually consist of.

If you genuinely feel that you've achieved very little by your own standards, then the harder question is: what do you think you missed out on?

Possibilities include:
- Mastery: becoming truly skilled at something.
- Impact: leaving a mark on other people's lives or on a field.
- Adventure: taking risks, exploring, building, creating.
- Recognition: being admired or respected.
- Wealth and security.
- Meaningful relationships.
- A sense that you used your abilities fully.

Different people mourn different absences.

It's also worth remembering that when you imagine the life you "could have had," you're usually comparing your actual life to a fantasy version that contains the successes but not the failures, sacrifices, disappointments, illnesses, bad luck, and tradeoffs that would have come with it.

At 60, some opportunities are gone. You will never be 20 again. Certain careers, athletic goals, or life paths may no longer be realistic. But many of the things people most regret missing—deep relationships, learning, creating, contributing, mentoring, writing, teaching, making art, starting projects, helping others—are still available.

A useful question isn't only "What have I missed out on?" but also "What experience would make the next 10 years feel less like a continuation of regret?"

Your answer to that may reveal what you feel was missing all along.
