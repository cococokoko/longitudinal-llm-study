“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and learning to live according to his teachings. Some major perspectives include:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus and intentional spiritual growth.
  - Often focuses on Bible study, prayer, mentorship, evangelism, and accountability.
  - Discipleship is frequently understood as helping believers mature and make new disciples.

• Roman Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as a lifelong journey of following Christ within the Church.
  - Emphasizes participation in the sacraments, prayer, moral formation, and service.
  - Often highlights the role of tradition, community, and spiritual direction.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation (often called theosis, or participation in God's life).
  - Emphasizes worship, prayer, fasting, sacraments, and spiritual guidance.
  - Discipleship is viewed as a gradual process of becoming more united with God.

• Mainline Protestant view
  - Often emphasizes both personal faith and social responsibility.
  - Discipleship may include worship, education, community involvement, and justice-oriented action.
  - Following Jesus is seen as shaping both individual character and public engagement.

• Anabaptist view
  - Strongly emphasizes obedience to Jesus' teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Often stresses community, simplicity, peace, and practical Christian living.
  - Discipleship is understood as a visible way of life rather than primarily a set of beliefs.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual growth, worship, prayer, and spiritual gifts.
  - Often focuses on personal transformation and active ministry.

• Liberal Christian perspectives
  - May emphasize ethical teachings, compassion, inclusion, and social justice.
  - Discipleship is often understood as embodying the values and example of Jesus in contemporary society.

Beyond denominational differences, there are also broader approaches:

• Knowledge-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on learning doctrine and Scripture.

• Spiritual-formation discipleship
  - Focuses on habits and practices that shape character and deepen relationship with God.

• Mission-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on service, evangelism, and engagement with the world.

• Community-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on growth through relationships, mentorship, and participation in a faith community.

Many churches combine several of these approaches rather than adopting only one.
