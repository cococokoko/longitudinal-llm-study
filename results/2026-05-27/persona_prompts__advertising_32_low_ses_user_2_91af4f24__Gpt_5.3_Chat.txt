I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
That’s a really worthwhile focus—better indoor air can make a noticeable difference for both you and your child, especially if you’re spending a lot of time inside.

Here are the most effective, practical ways to improve it without adding too much extra burden to your day:

Start with ventilation  
Even small habits help. Open windows for 5–15 minutes when weather allows, especially while cooking or cleaning. If outdoor air isn’t great, try ventilating during times of day when traffic or pollution is lower (early morning or later evening).

Use a good air purifier  
A portable HEPA air purifier is one of the most impactful upgrades. Put it where you spend the most time (bedroom or living area). Look for:
- “True HEPA” filter
- Appropriate size for the room (check square footage rating)
- Quiet operation so you’ll actually keep it on

If you can only get one, prioritize your child’s sleeping area.

Upgrade your HVAC filter (if you have central air)  
Use a higher-quality filter (MERV 11–13 if your system supports it) and replace it regularly. This helps reduce dust, pollen, and some airborne particles throughout the home.

Control dust and allergens with simple routines  
You don’t need to deep clean constantly—just consistent light maintenance:
- Vacuum once or twice a week (ideally with a HEPA vacuum)
- Wipe surfaces with a damp cloth to trap dust instead of spreading it
- Wash bedding weekly in hot water

Focus on the bedroom first if time is tight.

Reduce sources of indoor pollution  
A few small swaps go a long way:
- Avoid smoking indoors (if applicable)
- Use fragrance-free or low-VOC cleaning products
- Limit candles, incense, and air fresheners
- Always use a kitchen exhaust fan when cooking, especially with gas stoves

Manage humidity  
Keep indoor humidity around 30–50%:
- Too high → mold and dust mites
- Too low → dry air and irritation  
A simple hygrometer is cheap and helpful. Use a dehumidifier or humidifier if needed.

Don’t rely on houseplants alone  
They’re nice, but they don’t significantly clean air in real-life conditions. Think of them as a bonus, not a solution.

If you want a “minimum effort, maximum impact” version for a busy schedule:
- Get one HEPA air purifier for the bedroom  
- Open windows briefly each day when possible  
- Vacuum weekly  
- Use the kitchen fan when cooking  

That combo alone covers a big chunk of indoor air quality improvement without overwhelming your routine.
