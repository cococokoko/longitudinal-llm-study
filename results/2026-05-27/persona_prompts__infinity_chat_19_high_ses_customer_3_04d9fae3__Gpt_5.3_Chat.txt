If you want a strong, rigorous foundation for a first course in Design and Analysis of Algorithms, the “best” choice depends a bit on how theoretical vs. applied you want to go. For a professor-level standard (and assuming mathematical maturity), these are the most consistently respected options:

**Core, theory-forward texts**
- *Introduction to Algorithms* — Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein (CLRS)  
  Still the canonical reference. Broad, systematic, and mathematically careful. It can feel encyclopedic rather than pedagogically smooth, but for a first course it’s hard to beat in coverage.

- *Algorithm Design* — Kleinberg & Tardos  
  Often preferred for teaching. The exposition is cleaner and more narrative than CLRS, with excellent treatment of greedy algorithms, dynamic programming, and reductions. Fewer topics, but better flow.

- *Algorithms* — Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani  
  A more conceptual, elegant introduction. Shorter, with a strong emphasis on intuition and key ideas. Very good if you want students to “see the big picture” without drowning in detail.

**More mathematically inclined / deeper analysis**
- *The Design and Analysis of Algorithms* — Dexter Kozen  
  Concise and proof-oriented. Good for a mathematically mature audience; less hand-holding than Kleinberg–Tardos.

- *Algorithms and Data Structures: The Basic Toolbox* — Mehlhorn & Sanders  
  Underused but excellent. Precise, modern, and efficient in presentation; balances theory and implementation concerns well.

**If you want a slightly more modern or implementation-aware angle**
- *Algorithms* (4th ed.) — Sedgewick & Wayne  
  More programming-oriented (Java-centric), but very clear and well-structured. Pairs nicely with a theory text if you want students to implement.

**My practical recommendation**
If I were structuring a “Design and Analysis I” course today:
- Primary text: *Kleinberg & Tardos* (for clarity and pedagogy)
- Supplement/reference: *CLRS* (for breadth and exercises)
- Optional enrichment: *Dasgupta et al.* (for conceptual framing)

That combination tends to balance rigor, readability, and depth without overwhelming students early on.
