I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your income level, “retirement planning” is less about finding a clever product and more about structuring taxes, liquidity, and risk over time. The basics still matter, but the optimization layer is where most of the gains are.

Start with the tax-advantaged core:
- Max out all qualified plans available to you. That usually means a 401(k) (or equivalent), and if your firm offers a cash balance plan or defined benefit plan, those can be extremely powerful at your level because contribution limits are much higher.
- If you have any 1099 or side income, a solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA can add another tax-deferred bucket.
- Backdoor Roth IRA contributions are still worth doing annually. If your plan allows mega backdoor Roth (after-tax 401(k) contributions with in-plan conversion), that’s one of the highest-impact moves available.

Then think in “tax buckets,” not just accounts:
- Tax-deferred (401k, DB plans): great for high-income years, but you’re creating future ordinary income.
- Tax-free (Roth): extremely valuable for flexibility later, especially if tax rates rise or your retirement income is still high.
- Taxable brokerage: underrated. This gives you liquidity, long-term capital gains treatment, and step-up in basis at death. For someone at your level, this often becomes the largest and most flexible pool.

Within taxable accounts, focus on tax efficiency:
- Use low-turnover index funds or direct indexing to harvest losses.
- Municipal bonds can make sense depending on your state and bracket.
- Avoid unnecessary churn and high ordinary-income distributions.

Equity compensation and concentration risk:
- If a large portion of your wealth is tied to your firm (or a single company), have a deliberate diversification plan. Emotional attachment or tax avoidance often leads to overconcentration.
- Structured sales, 10b5-1 plans, or staged diversification can help manage taxes while reducing risk.

Alternative investments:
- You’ll likely have access to private equity, credit, or hedge funds. These can diversify and enhance returns, but fees, illiquidity, and tax drag are real. Be selective rather than defaulting into them because of access.
- Real estate (direct or via funds) can provide tax advantages (depreciation, leverage) and income diversification, but it adds complexity.

Insurance and downside protection:
- At high income, your future earnings are a huge asset. Own-occupation disability insurance is often more important than people think.
- Life insurance is situational; it’s mainly useful for estate liquidity or dependents, not as a primary “investment.”

Estate and long-term planning:
- You’re likely to exceed estate tax thresholds if you haven’t already. Start using trusts (SLATs, GRATs, etc.) and annual gifting strategies early.
- A good estate attorney is as important as a good investment plan here.

Timing and flexibility:
- Keep enough liquid assets to give yourself optionality. Many high earners become “asset rich but cash constrained” because everything is locked in retirement accounts or illiquid investments.
- Think about sequencing risk: having a few years of safe/liquid assets protects you if markets are down when you want to scale back work.

Finally, don’t overcomplicate the core portfolio:
- Even at your level, a disciplined allocation to global equities, fixed income, and some diversifiers—rebalanced and tax-managed—does most of the heavy lifting.
- The real edge comes from tax strategy, cost control, and behavior, not picking exotic investments.
