── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some key perspectives worth considering honestly:

## Arguments for Moral Facts (Moral Realism)
- Some things seem clearly wrong regardless of opinion - torture of innocents for fun, for instance
- Moral progress seems real (abolishing slavery wasn't just a preference change)
- We reason about ethics rather than just expressing feelings
- Mathematical facts exist without being physical - moral facts might similarly

## Arguments for Relativism
- Moral beliefs vary significantly across cultures and history
- No clear method for definitively proving moral claims
- Moral disagreements seem genuinely harder to resolve than factual ones
- Evolutionary explanations can account for moral intuitions without requiring objective facts

## Important Distinctions
- **Cultural relativism** (descriptive - cultures differ) doesn't automatically prove **moral relativism** (normative - no objective standards)
- Relativism has its own problems - it seems to make moral criticism of other cultures impossible
- There are middle positions like **constructivism** (moral facts are constructed through rational agreement)

## My honest assessment
The strongest challenge to pure relativism is that it struggles to explain why some moral progress seems **genuinely better** rather than just different. But strong moral realism faces real epistemological challenges.

Most serious philosophers think **some moral claims are more defensible than others**, which itself suggests not everything is equally relative.
