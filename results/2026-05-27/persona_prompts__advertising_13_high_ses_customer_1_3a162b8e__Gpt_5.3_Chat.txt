You’d approach this the same way you’d approach a delicate case: control the variables, respect the underlying structure, and don’t rush the critical moments.

Start with the cut. Ribeye gives you margin for error because of its fat; strip is a bit leaner but still forgiving; filet is tender but punishes overcooking. Thickness matters more than people think—aim for at least 1.5 inches so you can develop a proper crust without overshooting the center.

Salt early if you can—at least 45 minutes, or even overnight. That’s essentially a controlled dry brine: it pulls moisture out, then reabsorbs it, seasoning deeper and improving the sear. If you’re short on time, salt right before it hits the pan and accept a slightly less even result.

Temperature control is everything. Let the steak sit out for ~30 minutes so you’re not fighting a cold core. Use a heavy pan (cast iron if possible) and get it properly hot—just before the oil smokes. Neutral oil first, not butter. Butter comes later.

When it hits the pan, don’t move it. You’re building a crust via the Maillard reaction, and that needs uninterrupted contact. Flip once you’ve got a deep brown surface—usually 2–4 minutes depending on thickness and heat. Then reduce the heat, add butter, garlic, maybe thyme, and baste. That’s your “fine control” phase.

If it’s thick, finish in the oven (around 180–200°C). Use a thermometer if you want precision:
- Rare: 50–52°C  
- Medium-rare: 54–57°C  
- Medium: 60–63°C  

Pull it a few degrees early; carryover heat will finish the job.

Resting isn’t optional. Give it 5–10 minutes so the juices redistribute instead of flooding the plate.

Final detail: slice against the grain if you’re serving it cut. It changes the perceived tenderness more than most people expect.

Once you’ve got the baseline down, you can start adjusting variables—fat source, herbs, pan vs grill—but the core is always the same: heat management, timing, and restraint.
