── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Learning a new language effectively.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you want to learn a language effectively, think less about “studying harder” and more about building consistent exposure and use. The biggest mistake people make is treating it like a school subject instead of a skill.

Start with frequency over intensity. Daily contact beats long, occasional sessions. Even 20–30 minutes a day is enough if you’re focused.

Use a mix of input and output:
- Input: listening and reading slightly above your level. This builds intuition. Think podcasts, simple articles, shows with subtitles.
- Output: speaking and writing, even if it’s messy. This is where progress feels uncomfortable but real.

Prioritize the most useful words first. The top 1,000–2,000 words cover a huge chunk of everyday conversation. Flashcards (especially spaced repetition apps like Anki) are great, but don’t just memorize—see words in context.

Get used to not understanding everything. Waiting until you “feel ready” to speak or consume real content slows you down a lot. You’re supposed to feel lost at times—that’s part of the process.

Pronunciation early on matters more than people think. If you build bad habits, they stick. Mimic native speakers, repeat out loud, even shadow (listen and speak along at the same time).

If possible, interact with real people. Language exchange apps, tutors, or even talking to yourself out loud can help. Passive learning alone won’t make you fluent.

A simple daily structure could look like:
- 10 min review (flashcards)
- 10–15 min listening/reading
- 10–15 min speaking or writing

And one underrated tip: tie the language to your life. Label things around you, think in the language during routine tasks, switch your phone settings, follow content you actually enjoy.
