Nice, that’s a demanding job—respect. Learning an instrument online can actually work really well if you set it up right. The main difference between people who succeed and people who stall isn’t talent, it’s structure and consistency.

First, pick one instrument and commit to it for a while. Don’t bounce around early on. If you’re unsure, piano/keyboard and guitar are the most beginner-friendly for online learning because there’s a huge amount of good content.

For actually learning:

Start with a structured path, not random videos  
A lot of people stall because they just hop between YouTube videos. Use a course or app that builds skills step-by-step. Good options:
- Justin Guitar (free, very structured)
- Yousician (interactive, paid but motivating)
- Simply Piano / Flowkey (great for piano basics)
- Fender Play (for guitar/bass)

You can still use YouTube, but treat it as a supplement, not your main plan.

Keep sessions short and consistent  
With shift work, long daily sessions aren’t realistic. Aim for something like 15–30 minutes most days instead of 2-hour bursts once a week. Consistency beats intensity.

A simple session could be:
- 5 min: warm-up (scales, basic drills)
- 10–15 min: learning new material
- 5–10 min: playing something you enjoy

Use “micro-practice” on busy days  
Even 5–10 minutes counts. On a rough shift day, just pick up the instrument and do something small. This keeps the habit alive.

Focus on actual songs early  
Don’t get stuck only doing exercises. Learn simplified versions of songs you like. It keeps motivation high and helps you connect skills to real music.

Slow things down  
Use tools to slow songs or lessons down (YouTube speed control, apps like Transcribe+). Playing slowly and cleanly is way better than rushing and getting sloppy.

Record yourself occasionally  
It feels awkward, but it’s one of the fastest ways to improve. You’ll hear timing issues and mistakes you don’t notice while playing.

Expect plateaus  
You’ll hit phases where it feels like you’re not improving. That’s normal. Usually it means your brain is consolidating skills. Stick through it.

Optional but powerful: occasional feedback  
Even if you’re mostly self-taught, getting feedback once in a while (online lesson, forum, or even posting a clip) can correct mistakes early.

Since you work shifts, one extra tip: anchor practice to something stable in your day (like right after waking up or before bed), instead of tying it to a specific clock time.
