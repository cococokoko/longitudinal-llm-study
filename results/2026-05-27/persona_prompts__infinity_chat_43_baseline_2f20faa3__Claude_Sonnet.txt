── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of philosophy's most contested questions. Let me lay out the main positions honestly.

## Major Positions

**Moral Realism** (objective facts exist)
- Some things seem genuinely wrong regardless of opinion (torturing children for fun)
- Moral progress appears real - we actually *corrected* slavery, not just *changed preference*
- Convergence across cultures on some basics suggests something objective

**Moral Relativism** (culturally/individually constructed)
- Significant cross-cultural variation in moral beliefs
- No clear mechanism for detecting moral facts
- Explains why moral disagreements persist without obvious resolution

**Error Theory** (moral claims aim at facts but are all false)
- We *talk* as if moral facts exist but they don't
- Mackie's "queerness" argument - moral facts would be metaphysically strange

**Non-cognitivism** (moral statements aren't fact-claims at all)
- "Torture is wrong" expresses attitude, not describes reality

## Honest Assessment

There are **genuine tensions** worth acknowledging:
- Strong intuitions *feel* like fact-tracking
- But intuitions vary and can be explained evolutionarily
- "Relative to what?" is a serious problem for relativism
- Moral progress is hard to explain without some objectivity

## My actual take
The relativist position has **serious underappreciated weaknesses** - it struggles to explain moral progress and makes disagreement meaningless. But strong realism faces real metaphysical challenges.
