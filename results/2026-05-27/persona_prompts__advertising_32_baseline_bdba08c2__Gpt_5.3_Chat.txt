── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality is mostly about reducing pollutants at the source, increasing ventilation, and filtering what remains. The best mix depends on your space, but here are the most effective moves:

Start with ventilation. Fresh air is underrated. Open windows regularly if outdoor air is decent, even for 10–15 minutes a day. Use exhaust fans in kitchens and bathrooms to push out moisture and pollutants. If you have an HVAC system, make sure it’s bringing in some outdoor air if possible.

Upgrade filtration. A good HVAC filter (look for MERV 11–13 if your system can handle it) can trap fine particles like dust, pollen, and some smoke. Replace it on schedule. For specific rooms, a portable HEPA air purifier is one of the most effective upgrades—especially in bedrooms and living areas.

Control moisture. Mold and dust mites thrive in humidity. Aim for indoor humidity around 30–50%. Use a dehumidifier in damp areas or a humidifier in very dry climates—but keep them clean so they don’t become sources of contamination.

Cut pollution at the source.  
- Avoid smoking indoors.  
- Use a vented range hood when cooking, especially with gas stoves.  
- Choose low-VOC paints, cleaners, and furniture when possible.  
- Store chemicals (paints, solvents) outside living areas or tightly sealed.  
- Take shoes off at the door to reduce tracked-in pollutants.

Clean smarter. Regular vacuuming (ideally with a HEPA vacuum) and dusting reduces particle buildup. Wash bedding weekly in hot water. Don’t forget soft surfaces like curtains and rugs.

Add plants? Nice, but limited impact. Houseplants look great, but they don’t significantly clean air in real-world conditions. Think of them as decor, not filtration.

Watch for hidden sources. New furniture, carpets, and pressed wood products can off-gas VOCs. Let new items air out if possible. Check for gas leaks or poorly vented appliances—those are serious and need immediate attention.

If you want a quick “best bang for buck” setup: a HEPA air purifier in your main room + good HVAC filter + consistent ventilation + humidity control. That combo covers most of the problem.
