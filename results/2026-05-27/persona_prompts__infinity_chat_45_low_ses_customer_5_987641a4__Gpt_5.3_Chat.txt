Short answer: people disagree because there’s evidence pointing both ways.

If you mean “are there moral truths that are true regardless of what anyone thinks?” that’s moral objectivism. The case for it is that some things seem wrong no matter the culture—like gratuitous cruelty or harming others for no reason. Also, we argue about morality as if there are better and worse answers, not just preferences, which suggests we’re trying to discover something, not just invent it.

If you mean “is morality dependent on human beliefs, cultures, or feelings?” that’s moral subjectivism (or relativism in a cultural sense). The case here is the huge variation in moral norms across societies and history. What counts as “right” often tracks social needs, power structures, and shared values, which makes morality look constructed rather than discovered.

A middle position a lot of people find plausible is that morality is grounded in objective features of human life—like our capacity to suffer, our need for cooperation, and the kinds of societies that let people flourish—but our specific rules are shaped by culture and can evolve. So there may be better and worse moral systems relative to human well-being, even if there isn’t a single timeless rulebook everyone agrees on.
