# The Cloud Who Couldn't Rain

High above the green hills and the tall oak trees, there lived a small cloud named Cleo.

All the other clouds were very good at their job. Big Barry could pour down buckets of rain in minutes. Old Greta could rumble with thunder that shook the whole sky. Even tiny Tim, who had only been a cloud for one season, could sprinkle a perfectly good drizzle.

But Cleo couldn't rain at all.

She tried and tried. She puffed herself up as big as she could. She squeezed her edges tight. She held her breath and then let it all out at once. But nothing came out. Not a single drop.

"Don't worry," said Big Barry kindly. "It took me a while too."

But Cleo did worry. Every morning the farmer down below looked up at the sky with tired eyes. His fields were dry and dusty. His sunflowers drooped their heavy heads. His carrots were thin and sad.

"He needs rain," Cleo said to herself. "And I can't give it to him."

One afternoon, Cleo drifted sadly over a mountain. She felt so heavy and full, like she was carrying something she just couldn't let go of. She sat down on top of the mountain peak and sighed the longest, deepest sigh a cloud had ever sighed.

And then something magical happened.

A little girl was sitting on a rock near the top of the mountain. She was painting a picture, but she had stopped and was staring up at Cleo with wide brown eyes.

"Hello up there!" the girl called out. "You look sad."

Cleo was so surprised that someone was talking to her that she forgot to feel embarrassed. "I can't rain," she called back. "I'm a terrible cloud."

The girl tilted her head. "But you're the most beautiful cloud I've ever seen. You look like a big fluffy rabbit."

Cleo looked down at herself. She had never thought about what she looked like before. She had only ever thought about what she couldn't do.

She laughed.

It started as a small giggle, then grew into a big, rolling, rumbling laugh that shook her from her wispy top all the way down to her soft, round bottom.

And when she laughed, she rained.

It wasn't a little drizzle. It wasn't a sprinkle. It was a wonderful, warm, rushing rain that tumbled down the mountain, through the valley, and all the way across the farmer's dry fields.

The sunflowers lifted their heads. The carrots plumped up happily. The farmer stood in his doorway, smiled, and held out his hands.

Cleo floated over the valley feeling lighter than she ever had before.

From that day on, whenever Cleo needed to rain, she thought about the little girl on the mountain. She thought about fluffy rabbits. And she laughed.

And it always, always worked.
