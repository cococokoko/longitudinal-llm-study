# The Orderly's Apprentice

Marcus had worked the night shift at Riverside General for eleven years. He knew every squeak in the linoleum, every flickering fluorescent tube, every patient who called out in the dark hours between two and four in the morning.

He was good at his job. Quiet, steady, reliable.

Nobody would have guessed he was about to become something else entirely.

---

It started with the old man in Room 14.

He had been admitted on a Tuesday with no identification, no emergency contact, and no explanation for the strange silver burns that traced patterns up both his forearms like circuit boards drawn by a careful hand. The attending physician had documented them as unusual scarring and moved on. There were always more pressing things.

Marcus noticed them differently.

He noticed them the way he noticed everything — slowly, carefully, without making a fuss about it.

"Those marks," Marcus said one night while adjusting the man's pillow. "They look like they mean something."

The old man opened one eye. It was extraordinarily blue.

"Most people don't see them at all," he said. His voice was dry as old paper but surprisingly strong. "They look right at them and their minds smooth it over. Like water filling a hole."

"I see a lot of things people smooth over," Marcus said. "Comes with the job."

The old man opened both eyes then. He studied Marcus the way Marcus had learned to study patients — looking past the surface, reading the small signals that told you what was really going on.

"Sit down," the old man said.

"I have other patients."

"They're all sleeping. You checked twelve minutes ago. Sit down, Marcus."

Marcus sat down. He didn't ask how the man knew his name. His badge was right there on his chest.

"What's your name?" Marcus asked.

"Aldric," the man said. "And I am running out of time."

---

Over the following week, Aldric talked and Marcus listened.

He listened the way he always listened to patients — without judgment, without rushing, giving the words room to breathe. He had learned that people said the most important things when they didn't feel hurried.

Aldric talked about things that should have sounded impossible.

He talked about a tradition older than the hospital, older than the city, older than most things Marcus could name. A lineage of people who had learned to perceive the world at a different frequency — to see the structure beneath the surface of things, the patterns that connected cause to effect, the invisible architecture that most people walked through without ever knowing it was there.

"You call it magic," Aldric said, with mild distaste. "We call it *reading*. Because that's what it is. The world is written. Most people are illiterate."

"And you can read it," Marcus said.

"I could," Aldric said. "Past tense is becoming relevant."

His hands, Marcus had noticed, were getting weaker. The silver marks were fading.

"What happens when you're gone?" Marcus asked.

Aldric looked at him steadily. "That depends entirely on you."

---

Marcus was not a man who believed in destiny. He believed in showing up. He believed in doing the work in front of you. He believed in small dignities — a warm blanket, a glass of water at the right moment, remembering that the person in the bed was a whole human being and not just a collection of symptoms.

"I'm not special," he told Aldric.

"No," Aldric agreed. "You're not. Special is the wrong word entirely. You're *attentive*. Do you understand the difference?"

Marcus thought about it. "Special is something you are. Attentive is something you do."

Aldric smiled for the first time. It changed his whole face.

"Eleven years," he said. "Eleven years of watching. Of noticing. Of being present in rooms where most people only pass through. You have been practicing without knowing you were practicing."

He reached out and took Marcus's hand. His grip was surprisingly firm.

"I'm not going to give you anything," Aldric said. "I want to be clear about that. I have nothing to give. What I'm going to do is show you what you already have."

---
