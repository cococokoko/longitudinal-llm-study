The town of Grey Hollow sat where the road forgot itself.

If you drove in, you might miss it entirely—just a scattering of houses, a shuttered diner, a post office with a flag that never quite reached the top of its pole. The kind of place where people waved whether they knew you or not, and where everyone claimed nothing ever happened.

That wasn’t true, of course. It just depended on who you asked—and how long you stayed.

Mara Ellison arrived on a Thursday, just before the rain.

She had come for the house.

It stood at the far edge of town, leaning slightly as if it were tired of standing. White paint peeled in long curls, and the porch sagged at the center. Her aunt June had lived there alone for thirty years, until she was found one morning in her armchair, eyes open, as though she’d been waiting for someone who never came.

The official cause was heart failure.

The unofficial cause—if you listened closely enough in Grey Hollow—was something else entirely.

Mara didn’t believe in unofficial causes.

Not yet.

The first night, the house settled around her like it was remembering how to breathe.

Old wood creaked. Pipes whispered. The wind threaded through gaps in the window frames with a low, mournful hum. Mara unpacked only what she needed—a suitcase, her laptop, a kettle—and ignored the rest.

She found her aunt’s journal in the kitchen drawer.

It wasn’t hidden. That was the strange part. Just lying there beneath a stack of unopened mail, as if June had meant for it to be found.

The handwriting inside was tight and careful at first, then looser, more urgent as the pages went on.

*April 3 — It’s back again. Not the sound. The feeling. Like someone standing just behind me.*

*April 9 — I asked Mr. Ketter if he’s noticed anything. He said no, but he wouldn’t look at me when he said it.*

*April 12 — The clock stopped at 2:17 last night. I wound it, but it won’t move past that time.*

Mara glanced up at the wall clock above the sink.

2:17.

It wasn’t ticking.

---

The rain came hard after midnight.

Mara woke to the sound of something knocking—not on the door, but from inside the house. A hollow, rhythmic thud, like wood against wood.

She followed it down the hallway, her bare feet cold against the floor.

The sound was coming from the spare bedroom.

The door was closed.

She hesitated, hand hovering over the knob, and for a moment she felt it—that strange pressure her aunt had described. The unmistakable sensation that someone stood just behind her, close enough to reach out.

Mara turned quickly.

Nothing.

Just the dark hallway, stretching back toward her bedroom.

The knocking stopped.

She opened the door.

The room was empty, except for an old rocking chair in the corner, gently swaying.

By morning, the town knew she’d heard it.

Mara discovered this at the diner—one of the few places open—where conversations dipped into silence as she entered.

The waitress, a woman with tired eyes and a name tag that read *LENA*, poured her coffee without asking.

“You’re June’s niece,” Lena said.

Mara nodded. “Just got in yesterday.”

Lena glanced toward the window, where rain streaked the glass. “You staying long?”

“I’m deciding whether to sell the house.”

A pause.

“People don’t usually stay long,” Lena said.

“Why not?”

Lena’s mouth tightened, like she’d bitten into something bitter. “They don’t sleep well.”

Mara leaned forward slightly. “My aunt—did she ever say anything? Before she died?”

Lena hesitated.

Then, quietly: “She said the house keeps time wrong.”

That afternoon, Mara visited Mr. Ketter.

He lived two houses down, in a place so neat it felt staged. The lawn was trimmed with surgical precision, and the curtains hung perfectly straight.

He answered the door after a long delay.

“Yes?” His voice was thin, cautious.

“I’m Mara. June’s niece.”

Something flickered in his expression—recognition, maybe, or dread.

“You spoke with her,” Mara said. “Before she died.”

Mr. Ketter glanced over his shoulder, then stepped outside, closing the door behind him.

“Your aunt asked too many questions,” he said.

“About what?”

He studied her face, as if deciding how much truth she could carry.

“About the night the clocks stopped,” he said.
