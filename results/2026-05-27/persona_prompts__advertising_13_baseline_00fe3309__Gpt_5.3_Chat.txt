Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cooking a great steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control: of heat, timing, and seasoning. Once you get those right, everything else is optional flair.

Start with the steak itself. Thicker cuts (about 1–1.5 inches) are much easier to cook well than thin ones. Ribeye, strip, and filet are the usual go-tos. If you can, let the steak sit out for 20–30 minutes before cooking so it’s not fridge-cold in the center.

Seasoning is simple: salt generously, right before cooking (or up to a day ahead if you want a deeper effect). Pepper can go on before or after—if your heat is very high, add it after so it doesn’t burn.

Now the key: heat. You want a very hot pan or grill. Cast iron is ideal because it holds heat well. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like avocado or canola), then lay the steak down and don’t touch it for a couple minutes. That first contact builds the crust.

Flip once you’ve got a deep brown sear. From there, you’ve got two main paths:
- For thinner steaks: just keep flipping every 30–60 seconds until done.
- For thicker ones: after searing both sides, lower the heat and finish more gently, or move it to an oven around 375°F (190°C).

Butter basting is where things get restaurant-level. In the last couple minutes, toss in butter, garlic, maybe thyme or rosemary, tilt the pan, and spoon that melted butter over the steak repeatedly. It builds flavor fast.

Doneness matters, so use a thermometer if you can:
- Rare: ~120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: ~130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: ~140–145°F (60–63°C)

Pull it off a few degrees early—it keeps cooking while resting.

Rest the steak for 5–10 minutes before cutting. That keeps the juices inside instead of spilling out onto the plate.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Not enough heat (you’ll steam it instead of searing)
- Moving it too much (kills the crust)
- Skipping the rest (dry steak)
- Overcooking (hard to undo)

Once you’ve got this down, you can play with extras—pan sauces, compound butters, dry brining, reverse searing. But honestly, salt, heat, and timing already get you 90% of the way there.
