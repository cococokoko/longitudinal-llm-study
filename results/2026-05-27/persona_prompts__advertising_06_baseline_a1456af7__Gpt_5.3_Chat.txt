Nice, that’s a great skill to build—photography rewards both creativity and technical understanding. If you want to actually *master* digital photography (not just take decent shots), it helps to think in layers:

**1. Nail the fundamentals first**
You can’t skip these:
- Exposure triangle: aperture, shutter speed, ISO—know how each affects brightness *and* look (depth of field, motion blur, noise)
- Focus: single vs continuous autofocus, focus points, back-button focus
- White balance: not just “fix it later”—understand how light color affects mood

**2. Learn to see light**
This is where people level up.
- Direction: front, side, backlight (backlight = drama)
- Quality: hard vs soft light
- Time of day: golden hour is forgiving, midday is harsh but workable
Start noticing light even when you don’t have your camera.

**3. Composition isn’t rules—it’s intent**
Stuff like rule of thirds, leading lines, framing—use them, but don’t worship them.
Ask: *what do I want the viewer to look at, and how do I force their eye there?*

**4. Get comfortable with manual mode**
Not because it’s “pro,” but because it gives you control.
Try this:
- Set ISO first (lowest possible unless you need more light)
- Pick aperture based on depth of field
- Adjust shutter speed for proper exposure

**5. Shoot with purpose**
Don’t just take random photos. Do mini-projects:
- “Shadows only” for a week
- “One focal length” challenge
- “Street portraits” or “textures”

Constraints build skill fast.

**6. Editing is half the craft**
Lightroom or similar:
- Exposure and contrast first
- Then color
- Then fine tuning (clarity, sharpening)
Don’t over-edit—aim for consistency and style.

**7. Study other photographers**
Not casually—actively:
- Why does this image work?
- Where is the light coming from?
- What lens might they have used?

**8. Get feedback and iterate**
Your eye improves faster when someone points out what you missed.
