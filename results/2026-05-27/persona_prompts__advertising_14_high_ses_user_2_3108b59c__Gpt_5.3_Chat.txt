── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Creating a successful YouTube channel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good instinct—YouTube can work very well for a senior attorney, but only if you treat it as a long-term trust-building channel, not a quick client funnel.

First, the uncomfortable truth: most lawyer YouTube channels fail because they’re too generic (“What is a contract?”) or too self-promotional. That won’t differentiate you, especially given your level of experience. Your advantage is depth, judgment, and real-world perspective—lean into that.

Here’s a practical way to approach it:

Start with positioning, not content volume.  
Pick a clear lane that reflects your actual practice and the kinds of clients you want. For example:
- “Behind the scenes of high-stakes litigation”
- “Legal strategy for founders and executives”
- “What actually happens in major deals/cases (from someone who’s done them)”

If your topic could be delivered by a junior associate or a generic explainer blog, it’s too shallow.

Focus on insight, not definitions.  
Skip basic legal education unless you’re adding a strong point of view. Better angles:
- “What clients misunderstand about X—and how it costs them”
- “The moment cases are really won or lost (that no one talks about)”
- “How I think about risk when advising on [specific scenario]”

You’re selling judgment, not information.

Use “case-style” storytelling (without breaching confidentiality).  
People engage with stories. You can anonymize and generalize:
- “I worked on a matter where…”
- “A client once assumed… and here’s what went wrong”
- “In a negotiation like this, here’s what each side is really thinking”

This is where seniority becomes a huge asset.

Keep production simple but sharp.  
You don’t need a studio. You do need:
- Clear audio (non-negotiable)
- Clean background (office is fine)
- Tight editing (remove rambling)

Aim for 5–10 minutes per video to start.

Consistency beats intensity.  
One video per week for 6 months will outperform a burst of 10 videos followed by silence.

Titles matter more than you think.  
Avoid academic phrasing. Instead of:
- “An Overview of Indemnification Clauses”
Try:
- “The clause that quietly shifts millions in risk (and how to read it)”

Distribution is where most professionals underperform.  
Don’t just upload and hope:
- Share on LinkedIn with a sharp takeaway
- Send to clients/contacts when relevant (“thought you might find this useful”)
- Repurpose clips into short-form video

Compliance and firm considerations:  
Before you start, be very clear on:
- Advertising rules in your jurisdiction
- Confidentiality boundaries
- Your firm’s marketing policies

You don’t want your first viral video to trigger an internal issue.

Finally, expectations:  
You’re unlikely to get clients directly from early videos. What you *will* get, if you do this right, is:
- Warmer inbound conversations
- Faster trust in pitches
- A stronger personal brand within your niche

If you want, I can help you map out your first 10 video ideas based on your specific practice area—that’s usually the hardest part to get right.
