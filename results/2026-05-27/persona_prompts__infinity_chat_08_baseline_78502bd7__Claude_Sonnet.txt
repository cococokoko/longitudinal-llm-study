# The Last Letter

The envelope had been sitting on Margaret's kitchen table for three days.

She walked past it every morning when she made her coffee, every afternoon when she ate her lunch alone, and every evening when she poured herself a small glass of red wine before bed. She never touched it. She never moved it. She simply looked at it the way you might look at something dangerous — with a careful distance that felt like protection.

The handwriting on the front was unmistakable. She had known that handwriting for forty-seven years.

It belonged to her brother, Daniel.

---

Margaret was sixty-three years old and lived in a small house on the edge of a town that most people drove through without stopping. She had a garden that she tended with obsessive care, a cat named Oliver who slept eighteen hours a day, and a routine so precise that her neighbors could set their clocks by her movements. She liked her life this way. Predictable. Controlled. Safe.

Daniel had always been the opposite.

He was the kind of man who quit jobs on impulse, who fell in love with strangers on trains, who once drove from Ohio to New Mexico because he had a dream about the desert and decided the dream was a message. He was loud where Margaret was quiet, reckless where she was careful, warm where she had learned to be cool.

They had not spoken in eleven years.

The falling out had been spectacular and ugly, the way only family arguments can be. It involved their mother's funeral, a disputed inheritance, words that were chosen specifically because they would cause the most damage, and a silence that had hardened over the years into something that felt permanent. Margaret had told herself she preferred it this way. She had almost believed it.

And then the envelope arrived.

---

On the fourth morning, Oliver jumped onto the kitchen table and sat directly on top of the envelope, staring at Margaret with his yellow eyes as if delivering a verdict.

"Fine," she said out loud.

She picked up the envelope. Her name was written in Daniel's familiar looping script — *Maggie*, he had written, which was what he had called her when they were children, before everything became complicated. Just that one word made her chest tighten in a way she hadn't expected.

She made herself sit down before she opened it.

The letter inside was two pages long, written on plain white paper in the same blue ink. Daniel's handwriting was slightly shakier than she remembered, but still recognizably his — big and careless and somehow cheerful-looking even when the words were serious.

*Dear Maggie,*

*I've started this letter about fourteen times. I have a folder on my computer with twelve different versions, and I've thrown away at least two notebooks trying to get this right on paper. *I'm sick. I don't want you to panic — I'm not writing to tell you I'm dying next week or anything dramatic like that.
