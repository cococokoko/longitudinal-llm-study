Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: it’s not settled, but the strongest positions today cluster around three options—robust moral realism (there are stance‑independent moral facts), constructivism (moral truths are grounded in rational procedures or shared practical standpoint), and various forms of anti‑realism (error theory or noncognitivism). Each can explain some of our moral practices, and each pays a price.

Why people think there are moral facts:
- The “deliberative indispensability” point: in serious moral reasoning we treat some considerations as genuinely counting for or against actions, not merely as reports of preference. Abolition of slavery, condemnation of genocide—these look like discoveries of error, not shifts in taste.
- Convergence under idealization: across cultures and time, reflection tends to favor norms about harm, fairness, promise‑keeping. Realists take that as tracking mind‑independent truths.
- Explanatory work: moral facts seem to explain why certain actions are wrong and why we ought to feel guilt or resentment.

Why people resist realism:
- The metaphysical worry: what kind of entities are moral facts, and how do we know them? Mackie’s “queerness” objection presses both ontology and epistemology.
- Disagreement and underdetermination: persistent, deep disagreement suggests there may be no single fact of the matter.
- Naturalistic scruples: many prefer accounts that reduce or eliminate sui generis normative properties.

Middle paths:
- Constructivism (Kantian or Humean variants): moral truths are what would be endorsed by agents under conditions of rational deliberation or what no one could reasonably reject. This preserves objectivity without positing mind‑independent moral properties.
- Quietist or pragmatic approaches: treat moral claims as indispensable tools for coordination and critique without heavy metaphysical commitments.

A few clarifications often missed:
- Rejecting realism doesn’t force crude relativism. You can deny stance‑independent facts yet still have robust, criticizable standards (e.g., contractualism).
- Accepting realism doesn’t solve disagreement by itself; you still need an epistemology explaining why we’re justified in particular judgments.
- Evolutionary debunking cuts both ways: it pressures realism (our moral faculties were shaped for fitness), but also any view that relies on those same faculties for justification.

If you want a clean way to test your own leanings, ask which cost you find more tolerable: positing irreducible normative facts (and explaining access to them), or explaining away the apparent objectivity and authority of moral judgment without losing its practical grip.
