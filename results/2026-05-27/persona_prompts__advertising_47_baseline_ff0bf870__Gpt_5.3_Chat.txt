Running a marathon is less about guts on race day and more about months of boring, consistent prep. A few key things that actually move the needle:

First, build your base slowly. Most people get injured because they ramp up too fast. Aim to increase your weekly mileage gradually (around 10% per week is a common guideline), and keep most of your runs easy enough that you could hold a conversation.

Do a weekly long run. This is the backbone of marathon training. Start at a distance that feels manageable and extend it over time, eventually getting up to 18–22 miles depending on your plan. These runs teach your body to handle time on your feet, not just distance.

Don’t skip recovery. Easy days should be truly easy, and rest days are part of the plan, not a failure of it. Sleep and nutrition matter just as much as mileage.

Practice fueling and hydration. During long runs, test what you’ll use on race day (gels, drinks, timing). A common starting point is fueling every 30–45 minutes, but you’ll want to see what your stomach tolerates.

Mix in some quality workouts. Once you’ve built a base, add one or two sessions a week with some structure—like tempo runs or intervals—to improve efficiency and pacing. But don’t turn every run into a hard run.

Dial in your gear early. Shoes, socks, clothes—nothing new on race day. Blisters and chafing are avoidable if you test everything in advance.

Pace conservatively on race day. The biggest rookie mistake is going out too fast. If you feel amazing at mile 5, that’s normal—hold back anyway. The race really starts around mile 20.

Have a mental strategy. Break the race into chunks, expect some discomfort, and don’t panic if you hit a rough patch—it usually passes.

Taper before the race. Reduce mileage in the last 2–3 weeks so your body is fresh, not fatigued, at the start line.

And one underrated thing: pick a realistic goal for your first marathon. Finishing strong beats chasing an aggressive time and blowing up halfway through.
