Nice, that’s a great skill to build—especially if you’ve got a demanding job and want something creative but still structured.

If you want to *actually master* digital photography (not just dabble), think of it less like a hobby and more like a layered system you build over time:

Start with the fundamentals that control every photo:
- Exposure triangle: aperture, shutter speed, ISO — not just what they do, but how they trade off
- Light: direction, softness, color temperature (this matters more than your camera)
- Composition: framing, leading lines, subject isolation, negative space

You don’t need weeks of theory—get these to “working fluency” quickly, then move on.

Then shift into deliberate shooting, not random shooting:
- Pick one constraint per week (only shoot in aperture priority, only shoot reflections, only shoot people, etc.)
- Force yourself to solve problems within that constraint
- Review your photos critically (not emotionally): what worked, what didn’t, why

Gear matters less than you think, but lenses matter more than bodies:
- A solid starter setup: one camera + one prime lens (like 35mm or 50mm)
- Prime lenses force you to think, which accelerates learning

Editing is half the craft:
- Learn Lightroom or Capture One properly
- Focus on exposure correction, color grading, and consistency—not filters
- Study how professionals create a “look” across a set

To accelerate fast (this is where your consulting mindset helps):
- Do “projects,” not just practice  
  Example: “10 portraits of strangers with natural light” or “document a neighborhood over 2 weeks”
- Reverse engineer photos you like: break down lighting, composition, lens choice
- Get feedback from people who are better than you (this compresses years into months)

A realistic path if you stay consistent:
- 0–2 months: you stop taking bad photos
- 3–6 months: you can reliably create good ones
- 6–18 months: you develop a recognizable style

One more thing: your edge, given your background, is taste and storytelling. Lean into that. Anyone can learn settings—far fewer people can make images that *say something*.
