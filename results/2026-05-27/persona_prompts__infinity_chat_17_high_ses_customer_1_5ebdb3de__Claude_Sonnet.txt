# The Millhaven Cipher

## A Mystery

---

The body was found on a Tuesday, which Deputy Carla Reyes always thought was the worst day for anything to happen. Not dramatic enough for a Monday, not far enough into the week to feel inevitable.

Ed Marsh lay face-down in the shallow end of Cutter's Creek, his flannel shirt soaked through, his reading glasses still folded neatly in his breast pocket. The water was only eight inches deep. Ed was seventy-two years old and had lived in Millhaven his entire life.

The medical examiner from the county seat would later confirm what Carla already suspected standing there in the cold morning air, her boots sinking into the muddy bank.

Ed Marsh had not drowned.

---

Millhaven had 2,300 residents, one diner, one hardware store, and the kind of collective memory that made secrets both impossible and inevitable. Everyone knew everything, which meant everyone knew how to hide things in plain sight.

Carla had grown up here. Left for eight years — college, then the police academy, then three years working homicide in a city large enough to swallow Millhaven whole. She'd come back when her mother got sick and stayed after her mother recovered, which surprised everyone including herself.

She was thirty-four. She was the only deputy who had ever worked a murder investigation.

Sheriff Dale Pruitt, sixty-one, kind-hearted, and deeply uncomfortable with conflict, handed her the case with visible relief.

"You've got the experience," he said.

"I've got *some* experience," she said.

"More than me." He handed her his notepad. He'd written three things on it. *Ed Marsh. Creek. Tuesday.* 

She drove to the diner.

---

Millhaven Diner operated on the principle that coffee solved most problems and gossip solved the rest. Agnes Tully, seventy, had owned it for forty years and knew things about this town that would never appear in any official record.

Carla slid onto a stool at the counter. Agnes appeared with coffee before Carla had removed her jacket.

"Terrible thing," Agnes said.

"It is."

"Ed was in here Friday. Sat right where you're sitting." Agnes refilled a cup that didn't need refilling. "He was agitated."

Carla looked up. "Agitated how?"

"Kept looking at the door. Ordered pie and didn't eat it." Agnes lowered her voice, though the diner held only two other customers — old Walt Briggs, who was deaf, and a young woman Carla didn't recognize. "He asked me something strange."

"What?"

"He asked if I still had the old town ledgers. From when my father ran the general store." Agnes set down the coffee pot. "I told him they were in my basement somewhere. He asked if he could look at them. I said sure, someday. He said he meant that afternoon."

"Did he come?"

Agnes nodded slowly. "Spent three hours down there. Left without telling me what he found." She paused. "Left without his hat, too. Ed never forgot his hat."

Ed Marsh had been the town historian. Not officially — Millhaven had no budget for such things — but practically. He'd spent thirty years compiling records, photographs, and documents about the town's founding and growth. His house on Birch Street contained filing cabinets, bookshelves, and cardboard boxes organized in a system only he fully understood.

Carla spent an afternoon there with his daughter, Ruth, who had driven up from the city and moved through the rooms with the careful grief of someone trying not to fall apart in front of a stranger.

"He called me last week," Ruth said, standing in the doorway of his study. "Said he'd found something in his research. Something that would upset people."

"Did he say what?"

"I asked. He said he wanted to be sure first." She picked up a framed photograph from his desk — Ed as a young man, standing in front of a building Carla didn't recognize. "He said, *some things that are buried should stay buried, and some things that are buried should be dug up. I'm still figuring out which kind this is.*"

Carla looked at the filing cabinets. Three of the four drawers had been left open. The fourth was locked.

She found the key taped to the underside of Ed's desk chair.
