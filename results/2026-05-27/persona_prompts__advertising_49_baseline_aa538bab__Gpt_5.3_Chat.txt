── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A few upgrades can make a big difference, and you don’t need to do all of them—start with the easiest wins:

**1. Router placement matters more than people think**  
Put it in a central, open spot (not in a closet, basement corner, or behind a TV). Higher is better. Avoid thick walls, metal, and appliances.

**2. Reboot + update**  
Restart the router and check for firmware updates. Old firmware can slow things down or cause drops.

**3. Use the right band (2.4 vs 5 vs 6 GHz)**  
- 2.4 GHz: longer range, slower  
- 5 GHz: faster, shorter range  
- 6 GHz (if you have it): fastest, shortest range  
Use 5 GHz for most devices close to the router, 2.4 for far rooms.

**4. Change the channel**  
If neighbors’ WiFi overlaps, you get interference. Use your router settings or a WiFi analyzer app to pick a less crowded channel.

**5. Upgrade the router (big impact if it’s old)**  
If it’s 4–5+ years old, a newer WiFi 6 or 6E router can massively improve speed and stability, especially with many devices.

**6. Consider mesh if your home is big or multi-floor**  
Instead of one router, mesh systems (like Eero, Nest, Orbi) spread coverage evenly without dead zones.

**7. Reduce interference**  
Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, Bluetooth hubs.

**8. Secure your network**  
If neighbors or unknown devices are connected, that slows you down. Use WPA2/WPA3 with a strong password.

**9. Limit heavy usage**  
Streaming, downloads, and gaming all at once can choke bandwidth. Some routers let you prioritize devices (QoS).

**10. Ethernet where possible**  
For things like gaming consoles, PCs, or TVs—wired connections are faster and free up WiFi bandwidth.
