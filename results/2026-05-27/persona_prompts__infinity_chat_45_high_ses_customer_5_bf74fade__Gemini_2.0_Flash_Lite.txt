That's a question that philosophers have debated for centuries! There's no single, universally accepted answer. Here's a breakdown, keeping in mind your executive background:

*   **Objective Morality:** This view holds that moral truths exist independently of individual opinions or cultural norms. Think of it like the laws of physics – they're true whether we believe in them or not. Proponents of objective morality might point to things like the inherent wrongness of murder or the importance of honesty as universal principles. This perspective often aligns with religious or philosophical systems that posit a higher power or a set of fundamental moral laws.

*   **Subjective Morality:** This perspective argues that moral judgments are based on individual feelings, beliefs, and experiences. What's right or wrong is a matter of personal opinion. This view emphasizes the diversity of human values and the influence of culture and upbringing. A subjective view might acknowledge that different cultures have different moral codes, and that's okay because morality is ultimately a matter of personal preference.

*   **Relativism:** This is a related concept that suggests moral truths are relative to a particular culture or society. What's considered moral in one society might not be in another. This is often seen as a middle ground between objective and subjective morality.
