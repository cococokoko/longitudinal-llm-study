# The Last Light at Harrow's Mill

The diner closed at nine, but Mabel Pruitt never left before ten.

That was the thing about Harrow's Mill — everyone knew everyone's schedule. Which was exactly why, on the Tuesday morning that Roy Callahan's truck sat cold and dark in his driveway at seven AM, half the town already knew something was wrong.

---

Sheriff Dana Oakes pulled up to the Callahan property with her coffee still steaming. Roy was a creature of habit. Forty years of opening the hardware store at six-thirty, rain or drought or blizzard. His truck should have been gone an hour ago.

"Door's unlocked," said Deputy Hector Vance, appearing from around the side of the house. He was young, still wore his badge like it meant something. Dana appreciated that about him.

"Everything's unlocked in Harrow's Mill," she said.

"Was," Hector corrected quietly.

Dana stepped inside.

The kitchen was ordinary enough. Coffee maker still warm. One mug on the counter, half-filled. A chair pushed back from the table at an angle that suggested hurry rather than intention. The back door stood open two inches, letting in the cold October air and the smell of pine and wet earth.

Roy Callahan was simply gone.

---

The town had a population of eight hundred and twelve, or eight hundred and eleven, depending on how you counted Roy now. Dana had grown up here, left for twelve years, and returned to find the place exactly as she'd left it — which was either comforting or troubling, she'd never quite decided.

She started with the neighbors.

Phyllis Marsh, seventy-three, lived next door and missed nothing. She sat Dana down at her kitchen table and produced cookies that hadn't been asked for.

"I heard his back door," Phyllis said, wrapping both hands around her own mug. "Around midnight. I thought it was the wind, but the wind doesn't make footsteps."

"More than one set?"

Phyllis considered this with the seriousness it deserved. "I couldn't say for certain. But it didn't sound like Roy walking alone. Roy walks heavy. Forty years on a concrete floor will do that." She paused. "Whatever I heard was lighter."

Dana wrote that down.

---

The hardware store held its own secrets.

Hector had opened it up with Roy's spare key — kept, naturally, at the diner, because this was Harrow's Mill — and Dana walked the aisles slowly, looking for the thing that was out of place.

She found it in the back office.

Roy kept meticulous records. Forty years of ledgers, organized by year, stacked on shelves with the quiet dignity of a man who believed that numbers told the truth when people wouldn't. The current ledger sat open on his desk.

The last entry was dated three days ago.

A cash payment. Twelve thousand dollars. No name in the customer column, which was the only blank customer column in forty years of records.

Dana photographed it and said nothing to Hector yet.

---

The town had three places where information actually lived: the diner, the barbershop, and the small Catholic church where Father Dominic had been hearing confessions for thirty years and had developed, Dana always thought, the careful eyes of a man who understood that secrets had weight.

She visited all three.

At the diner, Mabel Pruitt refilled Dana's coffee without being asked and said, "Roy had a visitor last week. Thursday, I think. Sat in the corner booth. Didn't order anything, which I found rude, but Roy seemed nervous, so I didn't push it."

"Description?"

"Younger than Roy. Maybe forty. City clothes — you know the kind, trying too hard to look casual. Dark jacket." Mabel lowered her voice, though the diner was empty. "He and Roy talked for maybe twenty minutes. When the man left, Roy sat there another ten minutes just staring at his coffee."

"Did they seem angry?"

Mabel thought carefully. "No. That's what bothered me. Roy looked *scared*. And Roy Callahan hasn't been scared of anything since his wife passed and he decided he had nothing left to lose."
