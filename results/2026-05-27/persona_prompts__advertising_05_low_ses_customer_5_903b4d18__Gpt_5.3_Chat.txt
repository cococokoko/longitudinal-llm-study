Totally doable—you don’t need fancy ingredients to eat well. The trick is building a small rotation of cheap, flexible meals and shopping with a plan.

Start with a simple formula for most meals:  
protein + grain + vegetables + flavor.  
If you keep those four covered, you’ll stay full and balanced.

Budget-friendly staples to lean on:
- Proteins: eggs, canned beans, lentils, peanut butter, canned tuna, whole chicken (often cheaper per pound than parts)
- Grains: rice, oats, pasta, potatoes, tortillas
- Vegetables: frozen veggies (just as nutritious, cheaper, no waste), carrots, cabbage, onions
- Extras for flavor: garlic, soy sauce, canned tomatoes, spices, bouillon

A few low-cost meal ideas you can rotate:
- Rice + beans + sautéed veggies + a fried egg on top  
- Lentil soup with carrots, onions, and canned tomatoes  
- Pasta with garlic, oil, frozen spinach, and beans  
- Chicken and rice with roasted vegetables (use leftovers for multiple meals)  
- Breakfast-for-dinner: eggs, toast, and fruit  
- Stir-fry using whatever vegetables you have + rice  

To stretch your budget further:
- Cook once, eat multiple times. Big pots of soup, chili, or rice dishes save time and money.  
- Use ingredients across meals. For example, a bag of rice can go with stir-fry, beans, or soup.  
- Buy in bulk when it makes sense (oats, rice, dried beans).  
- Don’t sleep on frozen or store-brand items—they’re often much cheaper.  
- Plan 4–5 core meals per week instead of trying to cook something new every day.  
- Keep a “use-it-up” night for leftovers so food doesn’t get wasted.  

If you're feeding multiple people or juggling a busy household, it can also help to anchor meals around one big, affordable base (like rice, potatoes, or pasta) and vary the toppings so it doesn’t feel repetitive.
