Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people have argued both sides for centuries, and there isn’t a settled proof either way. But the interesting part is how each view explains the same facts differently.

If morality is objective, then some actions are right or wrong independent of what anyone thinks—like facts about gravity. On this view, things like “torturing innocent people for fun is wrong” would be true even if a society approved of it. Philosophers who take this route (moral realists) often argue that moral disagreement doesn’t undermine objectivity—after all, people disagree about science too—and that our strong intuitions about certain harms point to real moral facts.

If morality is subjective, then moral judgments depend on human minds—individual preferences, cultural norms, or evolved psychology. This explains why moral codes vary across cultures and time, and why moral change happens. On this view, saying something is “wrong” is more like expressing a stance or a shared rule than describing a mind-independent fact.

There are also middle positions that executives tend to find practical:
- “Intersubjective” views: morality isn’t built into the universe, but stable norms emerge from shared human needs (cooperation, trust, reducing harm). These can feel objective because they’re widely convergent and functionally necessary.
- “Constructivist” views: moral truths are what rational agents would agree to under fair conditions (think contracts, rights frameworks, governance).

In practice, most institutions behave as if some moral claims are non-negotiable (e.g., against coercion or fraud) while acknowledging that many norms are context-dependent. So the working answer in policy and leadership is often: treat core protections as if they’re objective, and handle the rest as negotiated, evidence-informed norms.

If you’re deciding how to act or set policy, the question becomes less “which metaphysics is true?” and more “which framework produces outcomes we can justify, defend, and sustain?”
