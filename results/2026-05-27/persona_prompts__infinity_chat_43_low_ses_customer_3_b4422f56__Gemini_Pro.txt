The question of whether there are objective moral facts or if morality is entirely relative is one of the oldest and most fiercely debated topics in philosophy. There isn't a single consensus, but philosophers generally fall into a few main camps. 

Here is a breakdown of the two main perspectives, along with a practical way to look at it:

**1. Moral Realism (There are moral facts)**
Moral realists argue that moral truths exist independently of what we think or feel about them, much like mathematical or scientific facts. From this view, a statement like "torturing an innocent person for fun is wrong" is an objective fact. It doesn't matter if a specific culture or individual believes it's okay; they are simply incorrect, just as they would be incorrect if they said 2 + 2 = 5. Realists believe that through reason, intuition, or human nature, we can discover these universal moral truths.

**2. Moral Relativism (It's all relative)**
Moral relativists argue that moral judgments are true or false only relative to a specific standpoint (like a culture, a historical period, or an individual). From this perspective, there is no universal standard of "right" and "wrong" that floats in the universe. For example, a relativist would say that eating certain animals is "wrong" in one culture but "right" in another, and neither culture is objectively more correct than the other.
