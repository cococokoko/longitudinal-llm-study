# The Hollow Bell

## A Mystery of Millhaven

---

The bell above Carver's Hardware had not rung in eleven years.

Everyone in Millhaven knew this. It was one of those facts that small towns collect the way attics collect furniture — unremarkable until the day someone notices it's gone.

So when the bell rang at 3:47 on a Tuesday morning in October, half the town heard it.

---

**Chapter One: What the Postmaster Found**

Ruth Adler had been delivering mail in Millhaven for twenty-two years, which meant she knew the town the way a doctor knows a patient — every scar, every secret, every symptom that didn't quite add up.

She found the body at 6:15 AM, before the fog had lifted off Shepherd's Creek.

Martin Carver, seventy-one years old, owner of the hardware store that bore his family name, was sitting in the wooden chair on his front porch. His hands were folded in his lap. His eyes were closed. He might have been sleeping, except that his skin had gone the color of old candle wax, and the October air was thirty-eight degrees, and Martin Carver had been sitting there long enough for frost to form on the collar of his flannel shirt.

Ruth set down her mail bag. She did not scream. She had grown up in Millhaven, and people who grow up in small towns learn early that screaming rarely helps.

She called Sheriff Diane Marsh from the sidewalk.

"Diane," she said, when the sheriff picked up on the second ring. "You need to come to Carver's."

A pause. "Ruth, it's six in the morning."

"I know what time it is."

Another pause, longer. "I'll be there in five minutes."

Ruth waited on the sidewalk, watching the fog move through the empty street like something alive. She did not look at Martin Carver again. But she noticed, before she looked away, that there was something in his lap beneath his folded hands. A small object she couldn't quite make out.

She also noticed that the bell above the hardware store door was perfectly still.

---

**Chapter Two: The Sheriff**

Diane Marsh had been sheriff of Millhaven for six years, elected on the strength of her reputation for being the only person in town who could remain calm when the Hendersons' barn caught fire, the Kowalski twins went missing in the woods for two days, and the selectmen held their annual budget meeting — sometimes all in the same week.

She was forty-four, with close-cropped gray hair she'd stopped apologizing for at thirty-eight, and a habit of going very quiet when she was thinking hard.

She went very quiet when she saw Martin Carver.

"Don't touch anything," she told Ruth, though Ruth had already stepped back to the sidewalk and showed no intention of touching anything at all.

Diane pulled on latex gloves and climbed the porch steps carefully. She checked for a pulse she didn't expect to find. She noted the temperature of the skin, the position of the body, the absence of any visible injury.

Then she looked at what was in Martin's lap.

It was a brass key, old and heavy, attached to a tag made of yellowed cardboard. Written on the tag in faded ink were two words:

*Forgive me.*

Diane stared at it for a long moment.

"Ruth," she said, without turning around. "Did you hear the bell last night?"

"I did," Ruth said. "Woke me right up. Checked the clock. Three forty-seven."

"Did you look out the window?"

"I did. Couldn't see much. Fog was thick. But I thought I saw a light on in the hardware store." She paused. "Thought it was Martin working late. He did that sometimes."

Diane stood up slowly. She looked at the bell above the door. She looked at the key in Martin's lap. She looked at the fog still drifting down the empty main street of Millhaven.

"Who else is going to have heard that bell?" she asked.

Ruth almost smiled, despite everything. "Diane, honey. In this town? Everybody."
