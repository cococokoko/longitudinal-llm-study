── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: it’s not obviously one or the other. There are serious arguments that some moral claims are objectively true, and equally serious arguments that morality depends on human perspectives or practices.

If you lean toward moral realism, the idea is that at least some moral statements are true independent of what anyone thinks—like “causing unnecessary suffering is wrong.” Realists point out how we argue about morality as if there are right answers, how we condemn things like torture or slavery even across cultures, and how moral reasoning often feels like discovering something, not just inventing it.

On the other side, moral anti-realists say morality is grounded in human attitudes, emotions, or social agreements. Different cultures have had very different moral codes, and there’s no obvious “moral detector” the way we have for physical facts. From this view, saying something is wrong often expresses disapproval or reflects shared norms rather than stating an objective fact.

There are also middle positions. Some think morality is constructed but not arbitrary—built from human needs, rational constraints, and cooperation. For example, if you care about living with others, avoiding harm, and solving conflicts, certain moral rules follow pretty naturally, even if they’re not written into the universe itself.

So it depends on what you find more convincing: that moral truths exist independently of us, or that morality emerges from human life and perspective. A lot of philosophers end up in that middle space—seeing morality as not purely subjective whim, but not a set of cosmic facts either.
