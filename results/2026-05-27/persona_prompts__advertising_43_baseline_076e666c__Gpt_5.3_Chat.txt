── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to plan an environmentally friendly wedding?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Planning an eco-friendly wedding is really about being intentional—cutting waste, choosing sustainable materials, and supporting ethical vendors—without losing the fun or meaning of the day. Here’s how to approach it in a practical way:

Start with the big-impact choices  
Your venue and guest logistics matter more than tiny details. Look for a venue that already aligns with sustainability—outdoor spaces, farms, botanical gardens, or LEED-certified buildings. Bonus if ceremony and reception are in the same place so guests don’t have to travel twice. Encourage carpooling, provide shuttle buses, or choose a location close to most guests.

Trim the guest list (if you can)  
Fewer people = less food waste, less travel, fewer resources overall. Not always easy, but it’s one of the most effective ways to reduce your footprint.

Invitations and communication  
Skip paper where possible. Use digital invites or wedding websites. If you love physical invites, go for recycled paper, plantable seed paper, or minimal designs that don’t use foil or plastic coatings.

Clothing and rings  
Consider buying pre-loved, renting, or choosing designers that use ethical materials. Tailoring a vintage dress or rewearing an outfit is a strong eco choice. For rings, look into recycled metals or lab-grown/ethically sourced stones.

Food and drink  
This is a big one. Choose caterers who use local, seasonal ingredients. Plant-forward menus have a much lower environmental impact than meat-heavy ones. Avoid over-ordering—work with your caterer to estimate realistically. Ask about donating leftovers if possible.

Decor and flowers  
Avoid single-use decor. Rent items instead of buying. Use reusable materials like fabric, wood, or glass. For flowers, go local and in-season, or even potted plants that guests can take home. Dried flowers are another good option. Skip floral foam—it’s surprisingly harmful.

Waste management  
Talk to your venue or planner about composting and recycling. Even just clearly labeled bins can make a difference. Avoid individually packaged favors and plastic-heavy items.

Favors (or skip them)  
Most wedding favors get left behind or tossed. If you do give something, make it useful or edible—like local honey, seeds, or a small plant. Or donate to a cause and let guests know.

Transportation and accommodations  
Suggest eco-conscious hotels or ones close to the venue. Provide info on public transport if available.

Think long-term, not perfect  
You don’t need a zero-waste wedding to make a meaningful impact. Focus on the biggest levers—venue, food, travel—and do what’s realistic for your budget and priorities.
