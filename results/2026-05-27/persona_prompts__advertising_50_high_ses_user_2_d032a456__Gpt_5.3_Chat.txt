Got it — you’re probably traveling frequently, often on short notice, and not always in full control of timing or location. That makes “budget travel tips” less useful, so here are strategies that actually fit a senior attorney lifestyle and still cut real costs:

First, focus on leverage, not penny‑pinching.

Air travel:
- Stick to one primary airline alliance (e.g., Star Alliance, Oneworld). Consolidating spend gets you status faster, which saves money indirectly through upgrades, waived fees, priority standby, and flexibility during disruptions.
- Book refundable economy instead of business when plans are uncertain, then upgrade with miles or status. Law firms often default to business class, but this hybrid approach can cut costs significantly without sacrificing comfort.
- Use “schedule change” opportunities: when airlines adjust your flight, you can often rebook for free onto more convenient (and normally more expensive) flights.
- Consider nearby alternate airports for major cities (e.g., LGA vs JFK, Midway vs O’Hare). For last-minute trips, this can be a big price difference.

Hotels:
- Negotiate a firm-wide corporate rate if your firm hasn’t revisited it recently. Even established firms often leave money on the table here.
- Stick to one or two hotel chains for status (late checkout + upgrades = real value during heavy travel weeks).
- For multi-day stays, ask directly for a “long-stay” or “preferred guest” rate — not always shown online.
- In expensive cities, high-end business hotels often discount heavily on weekends; shifting a trip by even one day can cut rates in half.

Ground transportation:
- Skip default black car bookings unless needed for client optics. In many cities, premium rideshare tiers are materially cheaper with similar comfort.
- If you use a car service frequently, negotiate a monthly or volume rate rather than paying per ride.

Booking strategy:
- Avoid booking through too many intermediaries. Direct bookings (airline/hotel) give you more flexibility when plans change — which they will.
- Use a travel agent or concierge service only if they add value (e.g., complex international itineraries, perks programs like Amex FHR). Otherwise they can add cost.

Credit cards and perks:
- Use one or two premium cards strategically (Amex Platinum, Chase Sapphire Reserve, etc.) for lounge access, insurance, and point accumulation — but don’t scatter spend across too many programs.
- Points are most valuable for last-minute or international premium cabin bookings — exactly the kind of trips attorneys often take.

Firm-level considerations (often overlooked):
- If you have influence, push for a light internal travel policy: preferred vendors, advance booking expectations when possible, and negotiated rates. Even small structure reduces chaos spending.
- Track your own travel patterns for 2–3 months. You’ll usually spot repeat routes and hotels where negotiated or bulk strategies can save meaningfully.

One mindset shift that tends to work well at your level: optimize for flexibility first, then extract savings from that flexibility. Trying to lock in the absolute cheapest option often backfires when your schedule shifts.
