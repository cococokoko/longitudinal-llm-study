How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You don’t need to “become a cook” to do this—you need a small system you can repeat under pressure. Fast, healthy cooking is mostly about preparation, constraints, and a handful of reliable patterns.

Start by lowering the bar: aim for 15–20 minute meals using 5–7 ingredients. If a recipe has 18 steps, it’s not for your weeknights.

A practical approach:

- Pick 3–4 “default meals” you rotate. Example: stir-fry, grain bowl, sheet-pan roast, omelet/tacos. These cover most nutrition needs and are endlessly variable.
- Use a formula, not recipes. For example:  
  protein + vegetables + sauce + carb (optional).  
  Once you internalize that, you can improvise quickly.
- Buy pre-prepped ingredients without guilt. Washed greens, frozen vegetables, rotisserie chicken, pre-cut stir-fry mixes—these are leverage, not shortcuts.
- Cook once, eat twice. Make extra protein or grains so tomorrow’s meal is assembly, not cooking.
- Learn 5 high-impact techniques: sautéing, roasting at high heat (425°F / 220°C), boiling grains, making a quick pan sauce, and using a knife efficiently.

If you want a concrete starting point, here are three fast “templates” you can memorize:

1) Stir-fry (10–15 min)  
Heat oil, cook protein (chicken, shrimp, tofu), remove.  
Cook vegetables (fresh or frozen), add garlic/ginger.  
Return protein, add a simple sauce (soy sauce + a little honey + vinegar or citrus).  
Serve over rice or skip carbs.

2) Sheet-pan meal (5 min prep, 20 min cook)  
Toss protein + chopped vegetables with olive oil, salt, pepper.  
Roast at 425°F. Optional: finish with lemon or yogurt sauce.

3) Grain bowl (15 min if grains are pre-cooked)  
Base: rice/quinoa.  
Top with protein, raw or roasted vegetables, something crunchy (nuts/seeds), and a sauce (tahini, vinaigrette, or even a good store-bought dressing).

A few leverage points that matter more than people expect:

- Keep a tight pantry: olive oil, soy sauce, vinegar, mustard, garlic, citrus, salt, pepper, one or two spice blends. That’s enough to make almost anything taste good.
- Frozen is your friend: vegetables and even proteins save time and reduce waste.
- Timing beats complexity: start the longest-cooking item first (usually grains or oven items), then layer everything else.

If your schedule is intense, consider a weekly 60–90 minute prep block: cook a batch of protein, a pot of grains, chop vegetables, and make one sauce. That turns your week into mix-and-match assembly in under 10 minutes per meal.
