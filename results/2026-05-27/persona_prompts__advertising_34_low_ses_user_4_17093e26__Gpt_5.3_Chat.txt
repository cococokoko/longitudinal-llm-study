── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work shifts at a local hospital as a patient care aide.

What are the best techniques for effective online teaching?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nice—your job already gives you a big advantage. You’re used to explaining things clearly, reading people, and adapting on the fly. Good online teaching builds on exactly that, just with a screen in between.

Here are the techniques that actually make a difference:

Start with structure, not content  
People learn better when they know where they’re going. Open every session with:
- what they’ll learn
- why it matters
- what they’ll be able to do after  
Then keep a predictable flow each time (intro → explain → practice → recap). It reduces mental load.

Keep explanations short and layered  
Avoid long lectures. Break things into small chunks, then build:
- simple explanation first
- example
- then a slightly more complex version  
Think of it like how you’d explain a procedure to a new patient vs. a trainee.

Make it interactive early and often  
Online attention drops fast. Every 5–10 minutes, pull people in:
- ask a question (even simple ones)
- use polls or chat responses
- quick “type yes if this makes sense”  
Silence doesn’t mean understanding—assume you need to check.

Use “show, then do”  
Don’t just explain—demonstrate, then have them try:
- you model the task
- they practice (even briefly)
- you give quick feedback  
This is one of the strongest learning methods, especially for practical topics.

Read the room differently  
You won’t get body language easily, so look for:
- chat activity dropping off
- delayed responses
- cameras off + no interaction  
When that happens, pause and re-engage instead of pushing forward.

Design for shorter attention spans  
Online sessions work best in tighter segments:
- 20–30 minutes of teaching max before a reset
- switch activities (talk → question → example → discussion)

Use names and direct prompts  
Calling on people (gently) increases engagement:
- “Alex, what do you think about this step?”
- “Jamie, want to walk us through your answer?”  
It keeps people present without being aggressive.

Make materials visually clean  
Avoid cluttered slides. Aim for:
- one idea per slide
- minimal text
- clear visuals  
You are the explanation—the slide just supports you.

Build psychological safety  
People won’t participate if they’re worried about being wrong:
- normalize mistakes
- respond positively to attempts, not just correct answers
- say things like “good try—let’s build on that”

End with a recap and action  
Always close with:
- 2–3 key takeaways
- one thing they should try or review after  
This helps retention more than adding extra content.

Don’t think “how do I cover this material?” Think “what will they *do* during this session?”

That shift turns a passive lecture into something people actually remember.
