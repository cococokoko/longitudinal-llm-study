── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people disagree, and there isn’t a universally accepted proof either way. But there are a few main ways to think about it.

**Moral objectivism** says some moral claims are true or false independent of what anyone thinks. For example, “torturing innocent people for fun is wrong” would be true even if a society approved of it. Objectivists argue that moral disagreement doesn’t prove there’s no truth—people disagree about science too—and that some moral intuitions feel more like discoveries than preferences.

**Moral subjectivism (or relativism)** says moral judgments depend on human attitudes—individual or cultural. On this view, something is “wrong” because people (or a society) disapprove of it. This explains why moral codes vary across cultures and time, and why morality often tracks human needs, emotions, and social cooperation.

There are also **middle positions**:
- Some argue morality is grounded in facts about human well-being (so it’s objective in a practical sense, even if rooted in human nature).
- Others think moral statements express attitudes (“boo to cruelty”) rather than facts, but still allow for reasoning and debate.
- Some say there are objective moral truths, but we only access them imperfectly, which is why disagreement persists.

If you want a grounded takeaway: in everyday life, people tend to act as if some moral claims are more than just personal preference (e.g., condemning genocide), even while recognizing that many norms are shaped by culture.

If you’re curious, I can walk through which view is strongest depending on how you define “truth” or what kind of evidence you think morality should have.
