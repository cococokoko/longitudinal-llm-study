Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely contested philosophical question. Here's an honest breakdown:

## Arguments for Objectivity
- Some moral claims seem clearly true regardless of opinion (torturing children for fun is wrong)
- Moral progress appears real - we didn't just *change*, we seemingly *improved*
- Mathematical and logical truths are objective without being physical
- Moral intuitions converge across cultures on basics

## Arguments for Subjectivity
- Significant cross-cultural moral disagreement exists
- Moral beliefs correlate strongly with upbringing/culture
- No clear mechanism for how moral facts would exist independently
- Evolutionary psychology can explain moral intuitions without objective grounding

## Middle-ground positions worth considering
- **Intersubjectivism** - morality is constructed through rational agreement
- **Pragmatism** - moral frameworks are tools evaluated by outcomes
- **Moral realism lite** - some constraints are objective, details are constructed

## My honest assessment
The strongest tension is between:
- Our *experience* of moral claims feeling genuinely true/false
- The *difficulty* explaining what makes them objectively true

**Most serious philosophers lean toward some form of realism**, but the question remains genuinely open.

As someone making decisions affecting many people, the practical question might matter more: **which framework produces better accountability and reasoning** in your context?
