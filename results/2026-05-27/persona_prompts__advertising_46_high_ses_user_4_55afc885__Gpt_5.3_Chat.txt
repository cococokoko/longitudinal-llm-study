── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great question—and a good sign you’re thinking about this with some rigor. The “best” resources depend a bit on whether you want practical implementation, clinical evidence, or behavior change tools, so I’ll point you to a mix that tends to hold up well.

First, for evidence-based clinical grounding:
- The NutritionFacts.org site (run by Michael Greger) is very comprehensive and heavily cited. It’s advocacy-leaning, but the primary literature links are useful if you want to dig in yourself.
- The American College of Lifestyle Medicine (ACLM) has position statements, CME content, and clinical handouts specifically on plant-predominant diets.
- The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics’ position paper on vegetarian/vegan diets is still one of the more balanced, mainstream summaries of adequacy and risks.
- For primary literature, review articles in journals like Nutrients, JAMA Network Open, and Circulation on plant-based dietary patterns (Mediterranean, DASH, vegetarian, vegan) are worth skimming—especially umbrella reviews and meta-analyses.

For practical “how to actually do this” (both for you or for patients):
- “How Not to Die” (Greger) and “Eat to Beat Disease” (Li) are accessible but still science-referenced; useful if you want something translational rather than purely academic.
- “The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners” (Gabriel Miller) or similar guides can be helpful just to see how meal construction is operationalized.
- The Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM) has very usable starter kits, meal plans, and patient-facing materials, though like NutritionFacts, it has a strong advocacy stance.

For clinical implementation and counseling:
- ACLM and PCRM both offer patient handouts, 21-day kickstarts, and structured programs you can adapt for clinic use.
- Kaiser Permanente and some academic centers (e.g., Stanford Lifestyle Medicine) have open-access toolkits and slide decks for counseling on plant-forward diets.
- Motivational interviewing resources are surprisingly important here—adherence is usually the bottleneck, not knowledge.

For nutrient adequacy (where things can quietly go wrong if you’re not paying attention):
- Key nutrients to stay on top of: B12 (non-negotiable supplementation for fully plant-based diets), vitamin D, iodine, iron (especially in premenopausal women), calcium, omega-3s (ALA vs EPA/DHA), and possibly zinc.
- The NIH Office of Dietary Supplements fact sheets are a solid, neutral reference for each of these.
- Cronometer is a practical tool (more precise than most apps) if you or your patients want to sanity-check micronutrient intake.

A couple of framing points that tend to help in practice:
- “Plant-based” doesn’t have to mean fully vegan to confer benefit. Many of the strongest outcome data are for plant-predominant patterns (Mediterranean, DASH).
- Ultra-processed vegan foods can muddy outcomes; emphasizing whole or minimally processed foods matters.
- Rapid transitions can cause GI issues (fiber load), so a stepped approach is often more sustainable.
