# The Weight of a Shift

The alarm went off at 5:47 AM, thirteen minutes before it was supposed to. Maya had been lying awake anyway, staring at the ceiling fan as it made its slow, lazy circles in the dark. She had learned long ago that her body refused to sleep properly before a morning shift. Some internal alarm system always kicked in, some quiet anxiety that whispered *what if you miss it, what if someone needs you and you're not there.*

She dressed in the dark, pulling on her navy blue scrubs without turning on the bedroom light. Her husband, Daniel, stirred beneath the blankets.

"Early again?" he mumbled.

"Always," she said softly, and kissed his forehead.

---

The hospital parking lot was half empty at 6:30 AM. Maya liked this time of morning, the strange in-between hour when the night shift was winding down and the day shift hadn't fully arrived yet. The building hummed with a quiet energy, like a held breath. She badged through the side entrance, nodded to the security guard named Pete who was always there, always drinking terrible coffee from a paper cup, and made her way to the third floor.

Medical-surgical. Her floor.

"Morning, Maya." Denise, the outgoing night aide, looked exhausted in the way that only twelve-hour overnight shifts could produce. Her eyes had that particular hollowness, that polite vacancy of someone running entirely on willpower and vending machine snacks.

"Rough night?" Maya asked, pulling on her gloves.

"Room 312 had a fall risk situation around three. We caught him in time." Denise shook her head. "Mr. Calloway. He keeps trying to get up on his own. Thinks he's fine."

"He's never fine," Maya said.

"None of them think they're not fine." Denise gathered her bag. "Good luck. You've got eight patients today. Staffing called out two people."

Maya closed her eyes for exactly one second. Then she opened them and smiled. "Okay. Go home. Sleep."

---

Room 308 first. Mrs. Eleanor Briggs, seventy-four years old, admitted three days ago with a hip fracture. She was a small woman with white hair pinned neatly despite everything, and she always had the television tuned to the morning news with the volume too low to actually hear.

"Good morning, Mrs. Briggs. How are we feeling today?"

"My daughter called last night," Eleanor said, which was not an answer to the question but was somehow more important. "She's flying in from Portland. She'll be here by noon."

"That's wonderful," Maya said, and she meant it completely. She had watched Eleanor spend two days pretending she wasn't lonely, pretending the empty chair beside her bed didn't bother her. "Let's get you cleaned up and looking your best then."

Eleanor smiled, and it transformed her entire face.

Maya helped her wash, changed her gown, repositioned her carefully, checked her skin for any signs of pressure injury. She recorded everything on the tablet, vitals, intake, output, the small clinical details that formed the invisible architecture of a person's recovery. But she also talked. She asked about Portland, about Eleanor's daughter, about whether Eleanor had ever been to the Pacific Northwest herself.

"Once," Eleanor said. "For my anniversary. Forty-second. We drove the coast."

"That sounds beautiful."

"It was." Eleanor paused. "He passed four years ago."

Maya rested her hand briefly on Eleanor's. "I'm sorry."

"Don't be sorry. Just keep talking to me like I'm a person and not a problem to be solved."

Maya looked at her. "You are absolutely a person, Mrs. Briggs."

---

By ten o'clock, she had seen six of her eight patients. She had helped Mr. Calloway in 312 use the bathroom safely, standing close enough to catch him if needed, talking about baseball the entire time because it distracted him from his embarrassment. She had repositioned Mr. Torres in 315, a large man recovering from surgery, a task that required genuine physical effort and left her lower back aching. She had sat with a young woman in 309, only twenty-six years old, newly diagnosed with something that had not yet been fully explained to her, and held her hand while she cried without asking why or offering hollow reassurances.

Sometimes the job was just being present. Just being a body in the room that said *you are not alone in this.*
