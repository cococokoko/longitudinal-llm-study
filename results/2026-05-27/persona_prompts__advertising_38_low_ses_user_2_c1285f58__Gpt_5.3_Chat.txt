── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Balancing freelancing with childcare is tough, so the “best” platforms aren’t just about pay—they’re about flexibility, reliability, and how quickly you can land steady clients. Here’s a practical breakdown based on what tends to actually work for freelance writers:

**1. Upwork (best overall for steady work)**
- Huge range: blog posts, ghostwriting, copywriting, technical writing  
- You can find both quick gigs and long-term clients  
- Good if you can invest time upfront in proposals  
- Downside: competitive, fees (10%), and slow start

**2. Fiverr (good for flexible, productized services)**
- You create fixed “packages” (e.g., “I’ll write a 1000-word blog post”)  
- Clients come to you once you gain traction  
- Easier to manage around unpredictable schedules  
- Downside: takes time to build visibility, pricing pressure early on

**3. ProBlogger Job Board (great for quality writing gigs)**
- Focused on blogging and content writing  
- Fewer low-paying gigs than big marketplaces  
- No bidding system—you just apply  
- Downside: competitive, fewer listings overall

**4. Freelance Writing Jobs (FWJ) + SolidGigs (curated leads)**
- Aggregated or curated job listings sent to you  
- Saves time scrolling multiple sites  
- Good if your schedule is tight  
- SolidGigs is paid, FWJ is free

**5. LinkedIn (underrated but powerful)**
- Clients post jobs or reach out directly  
- Posting your own content can attract inbound leads  
- Best for higher-paying, long-term work  
- Takes consistency, but less “race to the bottom” than marketplaces

**6. Content mills (use strategically, not forever)**
Examples: Textbroker, WriterAccess, Verblio  
- Easy entry, fast approval  
- Reliable if you need immediate income  
- Downside: lower pay, but predictable workflow
