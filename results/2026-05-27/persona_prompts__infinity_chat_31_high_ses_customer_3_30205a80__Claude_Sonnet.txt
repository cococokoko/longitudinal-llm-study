── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great question. There are several common pathways:

## Formal Pathways
- **MBA or advanced degrees** from business schools, which provide credentials and networks
- **Climbing internal ranks** - starting in entry-level roles and advancing over years/decades
- **Functional expertise** - becoming a recognized leader in finance, operations, marketing, etc.

## Key Factors Research Points To
- **Networks and sponsorship** - having senior advocates who recommend you for opportunities, often more important than pure merit
- **Visibility** - being associated with high-profile projects or successes
- **Timing and luck** - being in the right place when opportunities open
- **Early career momentum** - initial advantages tend to compound

## Structural Realities
- **Homophily** - executives tend to promote people similar to themselves, which perpetuates demographic patterns
- **Elite educational credentials** signal status and open doors disproportionately
- **Social capital** from family background, schools, and prior connections matters significantly

## What the Research Suggests
Competence matters, but it's probably **not the primary driver** compared to networks, visibility, and organizational politics. Studies on CEO selection suggest boards often prioritize confidence and narrative over demonstrated performance.
