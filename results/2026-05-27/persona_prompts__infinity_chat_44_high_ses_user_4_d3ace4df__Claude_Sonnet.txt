── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

Can you write me a series of three questions that can determine if something is morally right or wrong?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here are three questions designed to systematically evaluate moral rightness or wrongness:

---

**1. Does it cause harm, and to whom?**
Consider whether the action causes physical, psychological, financial, or social harm — to individuals, groups, or society broadly. Also weigh whether *inaction* causes harm.

---

**2. Is it fair and would it withstand impartial scrutiny?**
Ask whether the action treats people with equal dignity and respect. Would you defend it transparently to those affected, to peers, or to a neutral outside observer?

---

**3. Does it violate a duty or right that should not be compromised, regardless of outcome?**
Consider whether the action crosses a fundamental ethical line — such as deception, exploitation, or violation of consent — even if it produces some benefit.

---

**Why these three?**
They deliberately draw from complementary ethical frameworks:
- Question 1 reflects **consequentialist** thinking
- Question 2 reflects **fairness/contractualist** thinking
- Question 3 reflects **deontological** thinking

This is particularly relevant in a **research context**, where actions can appear beneficial in outcome while still violating rights or fairness — making all three questions necessary together.
