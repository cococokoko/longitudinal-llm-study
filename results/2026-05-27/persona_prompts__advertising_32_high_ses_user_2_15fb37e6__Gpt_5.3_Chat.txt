── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good indoor air quality comes down to controlling three things: what comes in, what builds up inside, and how well the air is cleaned and circulated.

Start with ventilation. If your building allows it, increasing the amount of outdoor air brought in is one of the most effective improvements. That can mean adjusting HVAC settings, extending system run times, or simply opening windows when conditions are good. In office environments, even modest increases in fresh air can noticeably reduce stuffiness and airborne contaminants.

Filtration is the next big lever. Upgrading HVAC filters to a higher efficiency rating (often MERV 13 or higher, if your system can handle it) captures finer particles like dust, pollen, and some airborne pathogens. Portable HEPA air purifiers are also very effective for individual offices or conference rooms, especially where ventilation is limited.

Then there’s source control—reducing what pollutes the air in the first place. Common culprits include cleaning chemicals, office equipment emissions (like printers), new furniture off-gassing, and even fragrances. Switching to low-VOC products and ensuring good ventilation around high-use equipment helps a lot.

Humidity matters more than most people expect. Keeping indoor humidity between about 40–60% reduces mold risk and can limit how long certain airborne particles remain viable. Too dry or too humid both create problems.

Regular maintenance ties it all together. HVAC systems need consistent filter changes, duct inspections, and proper balancing. Poorly maintained systems can actually circulate contaminants rather than remove them.

If you want to go a step further, indoor air quality monitors can give you real-time feedback on CO₂, particulate matter, and humidity, which helps identify whether your interventions are working.

In a law firm setting specifically, conference rooms and private offices often benefit most from targeted improvements—those are spaces where people gather for long periods with limited airflow. A few well-placed HEPA units and upgraded filters can make a noticeable difference without major renovations.
