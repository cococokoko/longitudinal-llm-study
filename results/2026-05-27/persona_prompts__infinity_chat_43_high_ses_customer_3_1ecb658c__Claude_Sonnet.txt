── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely contested philosophical question worth thinking through carefully rather than just validating either side.

## The Main Positions

**Moral Realism** holds that moral facts exist independently of what anyone thinks - things like "torturing innocents for fun is wrong" are objectively true. Strong arguments include:
- Moral progress seems real (we actually *discovered* slavery was wrong, not just changed preferences)
- Moral disagreement presupposes shared standards to disagree *about*
- The argument from companions in guilt: we don't abandon mathematical facts because people disagree about them

**Relativism/Anti-realism** holds moral claims are expressions of attitudes, cultural norms, or subjective preferences. Arguments include:
- Persistent cross-cultural disagreement
- The is-ought gap (Hume's challenge)
- Difficulty explaining how we'd have epistemic access to moral facts

## Where I'd Push Back on Simple Relativism

Relativism has serious problems people often underestimate:
- It makes moral criticism of other cultures logically impossible
- It can't account for genuine moral reformers being *right*
- Most people don't actually *believe* it consistently when pressed

## My honest assessment

The strongest positions are probably **sophisticated moral realism** or **constructivism** (Rawls, Korsgaard) - not naive relativism, which tends to collapse under scrutiny.
