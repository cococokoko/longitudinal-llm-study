── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best practices for data security in small businesses?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Small businesses don’t need enterprise-level complexity, but they do need consistent, disciplined basics. Most breaches happen because of simple gaps, not sophisticated attacks.

Start with access control. Give employees only the data and systems they actually need (least privilege), and require strong authentication. That means unique passwords plus multi-factor authentication (MFA) wherever possible, especially for email, cloud tools, and admin accounts.

Keep software up to date. Turn on automatic updates for operating systems, apps, and any plugins. A lot of attacks exploit known vulnerabilities that already have patches.

Backups are your safety net. Use the “3-2-1 rule”: three copies of your data, on two different types of storage, with one copy offsite or in the cloud. Test backups occasionally to make sure you can actually restore them.

Encrypt sensitive data. That includes data stored on devices (laptops, phones, servers) and data in transit (use HTTPS, secure email, VPNs if needed). If a device is lost or stolen, encryption can prevent exposure.

Train your people. Phishing emails are still the #1 entry point. Teach staff how to spot suspicious links, attachments, and requests for sensitive info. Regular, short training sessions are more effective than one long annual seminar.

Secure your network. Change default router passwords, use a firewall, and separate guest Wi-Fi from your business network. If employees work remotely, make sure they’re using secure connections.

Limit and monitor third-party access. Many small businesses rely on external tools and vendors. Only grant necessary access and review it periodically. Choose reputable providers with clear security practices.

Have a simple incident response plan. Know who to contact, how to isolate affected systems, and how to communicate if something goes wrong. You don’t need a giant manual—just a clear set of steps.

Finally, comply with relevant regulations (like GDPR, HIPAA, etc.) if they apply to your business. Even if they don’t strictly apply, they’re a good benchmark for responsible data handling.
