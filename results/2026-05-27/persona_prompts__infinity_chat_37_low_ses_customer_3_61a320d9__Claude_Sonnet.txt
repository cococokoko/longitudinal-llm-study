# The Little Cloud Who Couldn't Rain

High up in the sky, where the air was cool and breezy, lived a small white cloud named Pip.

All the other clouds in the sky were very good at their jobs. Big Bertha could pour down buckets of rain that filled rivers and lakes. Old Grumble could rumble and boom with the most magnificent thunder. Even tiny Wisp could produce a perfect drizzle that made the flowers smile.

But Pip couldn't rain at all.

Every morning, Pip would puff up as big as possible, scrunch up tight, and try with all his might.

Nothing.

Not a single drop.

"Don't worry," said Big Bertha kindly. "It took me a whole season to get my first raindrop out."

But Pip did worry. He drifted sadly across the sky, watching the farmers wave thankfully at the other clouds. He watched children splash in puddles that other clouds had made. He watched sunflowers stretch up tall after a good soaking.

"I'm a useless cloud," Pip sighed.

One hot summer afternoon, Pip floated over a small village. Down below, he spotted a little girl sitting in a garden, crying. Beside her was a row of tiny seedlings in dry, cracked soil. They were drooping and brown at the edges.

"My seeds are dying," the girl wept. "I planted them myself and now they'll never grow into flowers."

Pip looked at the sad little seedlings. He looked at the crying girl. Something strange happened in the middle of his fluffy chest. It felt warm and tight and urgent all at once.

Pip puffed up. He scrunched tight. He thought about the little girl and her flowers and how much he wanted to help.

And then...

*Drip.*

One tiny raindrop fell from Pip onto the dry soil.

Then another. And another.

Soon Pip was raining properly — a soft, gentle shower that soaked right down to the roots of every single seedling.

The little girl looked up in surprise and laughed, holding out her hands to catch the drops. "Thank you, cloud!" she called out.

Pip had never felt so light and happy in all his life.

When he floated back up to join the other clouds, Big Bertha was beaming. "I saw that!" she boomed proudly.

"But how did I do it?" Pip asked, still amazed.

Old Grumble chuckled, which sounded like distant thunder. "You stopped thinking about yourself," he said wisely, "and started thinking about someone else. That's where rain comes from, young Pip. It always has been."

From that day on, Pip rained regularly. Not always the biggest storm or the longest shower. But always at exactly the right moment, for exactly the right person.

And somewhere in that little village, a garden full of beautiful flowers bloomed all summer long.
