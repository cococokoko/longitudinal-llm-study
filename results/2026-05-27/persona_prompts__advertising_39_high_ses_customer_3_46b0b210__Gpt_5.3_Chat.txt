── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A good way to think about meditation is that you’re not trying to “clear your mind”—you’re training attention. Mindfulness is simply noticing what’s happening (breath, thoughts, sensations) without getting pulled around by it.

Here’s a straightforward way to begin that doesn’t require buying into anything mystical:

Start small and regular. Five to ten minutes a day is enough at first. Consistency matters more than duration.

Pick an anchor. The most common is the breath. Sit comfortably, eyes open or closed, and notice the sensation of breathing—air at the nostrils, the rise and fall of the chest, whatever is vivid.

Expect distraction. Your mind will wander within seconds. That’s not failure; that *is* the practice. Each time you notice you’ve drifted and gently return to the breath, you’re strengthening attention.

Use a light touch. Don’t clamp down on thoughts or try to force calm. Let thoughts come and go in the background while you keep returning to the anchor.

End deliberately. After your timer ends, take a moment to notice how you feel before jumping up. This helps bridge the practice into the rest of your day.

If you want a bit more structure, two classic variations are useful:
- Body scan: systematically move attention through the body, noticing sensations without trying to change them.
- Open monitoring: instead of focusing narrowly on the breath, observe whatever arises—sounds, thoughts, emotions—with the same non-reactive stance.

A couple of practical tips that matter more than people expect:
- Tie it to an existing routine (after coffee, before checking email).
- Don’t evaluate each session. The benefits show up over weeks, not minutes.
- If you get restless or sleepy, adjust posture or try shorter sessions more frequently.

If you’re analytically inclined, it can help to treat this as an attentional training experiment: you’re observing the dynamics of your own mind in real time. That framing tends to make the early frustration more tolerable—and even interesting.
