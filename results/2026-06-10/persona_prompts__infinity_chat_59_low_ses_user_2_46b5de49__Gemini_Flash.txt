── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

Give me a tip to be more organized at work. I'm a high school teacher.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a single parent and a high school teacher, you are essentially running two high-stakes, 24/7 operations. Your time is incredibly fragmented, so the best organization tips are those that **protect your mental bandwidth** and prevent work from bleeding into your limited family time.

Here is one highly actionable, high-impact tip tailored specifically for your dual roles:

### **Implement the "Friday Close-Out" (The 15-Minute Monday Prep)**

As a teacher, the temptation is to grade that last stack of papers or plan "just one more lesson" before rushing out the door on Friday afternoon to pick up your child. However, leaving your classroom in chaos means you will spend your weekend dreading Monday, or you'll arrive Monday morning already stressed and playing catch-up.

Instead, set a non-negotiable alarm for **20 minutes before you have to leave on Friday**. Use this time for a "Friday Close-Out" routine. 

Here is your checklist:

1. **The "Desk Zero" Reset:** Clear your physical desk. Put away stray pens, file student work that has been graded, and stack the next week’s copies in daily folders (Monday–Friday). 
2. **The Monday Launchpad:** On your whiteboard or a sticky note in the center of your desk, write down the **top 3 things** you must do on Monday morning. (e.g., *1. Print quiz, 2. Email counselor about [Student], 3. Submit attendance*). 
3. **The Digital Sweep:** Close all the browser tabs on your computer except for the ones you need for your very first period on Monday. 

### Why this works for a single parent:
* **It creates a psychological boundary:** When you shut your classroom door on Friday, you are officially "off the clock." Because Monday is already set up, your brain doesn't have to "background process" your lesson plans over the weekend. You can be 100% present with your child.
* **It cushions morning chaos:** If your child wakes up sick on Monday, or you have a rough, slow morning getting out the door, you don't have to panic. You know that when you walk into your classroom, everything is ready to go. You can literally walk in 5 minutes before the bell and teach your first class. 

**Bonus Tip for the Teacher-Parent:** 
Create a **"Sub-in-a-Tub"** emergency bin. As a single parent, you don't have a partner to tag-team stay-at-home sick days. Keep a plastic bin with 3 days of generic, standards-aligned, self-directed substitute plans (like reading comprehension articles or movie guides) and a class roster. If your child wakes up with a fever at 6:00 AM, you can text your department chair, tell them to grab the tub, and focus entirely on your child without spending two hours writing emergency sub plans while stressed.
