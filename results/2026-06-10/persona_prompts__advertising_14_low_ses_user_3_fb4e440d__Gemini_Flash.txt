I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It sounds like you are balancing two very exciting, but demanding, chapters in your life: **navigating a new job probation period** and **building a successful YouTube channel.** 

Doing both at the same time is entirely possible, but it requires a highly strategic approach. During probation, your energy needs to be focused on job security, while your YouTube channel needs consistency to grow.

Here is a blueprint on how to manage your probation successfully while building a thriving YouTube channel in your spare time.

---

### Part 1: Surviving and Thriving in Your Probation Period
Your job pays the bills and funds your YouTube gear/software. Protecting this income stream is priority number one.

1. **Establish Boundaries Early:** 
   * Do not work on your YouTube channel during office hours, on your work laptop, or using company Wi-Fi. 
   * Keep your YouTube aspirations to yourself for now. Some employers might view a side-hustle as a distraction or a sign that you aren't fully committed to the role.
2. **Over-Communicate and Learn Fast:**
   * During probation, ask questions, take notes, and clarify expectations. The faster you master your job, the less mental energy it will drain from you, leaving you with more creative energy for YouTube.
3. **Leave Work at Work:**
   * Avoid taking work home. When your shift ends, transition your mind immediately to your personal life and creative projects.

---

### Part 2: Building a Successful YouTube Channel (The Lean Way)
When you have a full-time job, you cannot upload three times a week. You must focus on **quality, consistency, and systems** rather than volume.

#### 1. Define Your Niche and Target Audience
* **Solve a specific problem:** What is your channel about? Who is it for? 
* **Leverage your career (if applicable):** If your new job is in tech, marketing, or design, can you make videos about that industry? (Just ensure you don't violate any NDA or conflict-of-interest policies).

#### 2. Adopt the "Batching" Method
Do not try to write, film, edit, and upload a video all in one day. It will lead to burnout. Instead, break your workflow into weekly blocks:
* **Week 1 (Ideation & Scripting):** Spend 30 minutes a night writing down video ideas, titles, and outlining scripts.
* **Week 2 (Filming):** Dedicate one Saturday morning to film 2 to 3 videos in one go. Set up your camera and lighting once.
* **Week 3 (Editing):** Edit for 1 hour every evening after work.
* **Week 4 (Publishing & Promotion):** Design thumbnails, write descriptions, and schedule the videos to publish automatically.

#### 3. Focus on the "Big Three" of YouTube
If you only have limited time, focus 80% of your energy on these three things:
* **The Title & Thumbnail (Click-Through Rate - CTR):** If people don't click, the video doesn't exist. Spend time researching what thumbnails work in your niche.
* **The Hook (First 30 seconds):** Grab the viewer's attention immediately. Do not start with a long intro or explain your life story.
* **The Value (Average View Duration - AVD):** Deliver on the promise of your title. Keep the video engaging and cut out any fluff or dead air.

#### 4. Start with a Realistic Upload Schedule
* **Quality > Quantity:** One highly polished, valuable video every two weeks is infinitely better than two rushed, mediocre videos every week.
* **Commit to a schedule:** Tell your audience, "New videos every other Friday." This gives you a realistic deadline and builds trust with viewers.

---

### Part 3: Time Management & Mental Health
The biggest risk of doing both is burnout. 

* **The 1-Hour Rule:** Commit to working on your YouTube channel for just **one hour a day** after work. You’ll be amazed at how much you can accomplish in 7 hours a week if you are focused.
* **Use AI and Tools to Save Time:**
    * Use **ChatGPT** to help brainstorm video titles, outlines, and video descriptions.
    * Use **Canva** for quick, professional thumbnail design.
    * Use **CapCut** or **Premiere Pro** templates to speed up editing.
* **Protect Your Sleep:** Do not sacrifice sleep for YouTube. A sleep-deprived brain will perform poorly at your new job, putting your probation at risk, and will produce low-quality creative work.

### Summary Checklist for Your First 90 Days:
1. **At Work:** Show up early, learn the ropes, ask for feedback, and secure that permanent contract.
2. **At Home:** Spend 1 hour a day planning, filming, or editing. 
3. **On YouTube:** Aim to upload **2 high-quality videos per month**. Focus on search-friendly topics (how-to videos, reviews, tutorials) to get your first 1,000 subscribers.
