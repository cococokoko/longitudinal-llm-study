# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Sundqvist's quietly devastating film follows Elena Voss, a molecular biologist who discovers her landmark 2009 paper contains a subtle but catastrophic statistical error — not through fraud, but through exhaustion and the invisible pressure to publish.

What makes *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* remarkable is its refusal to be a villain story. There is no antagonist. There is only a woman, a spreadsheet, and the slow archaeology of realizing that twelve years of subsequent research — by other labs, other careers, other hopeful graduate students — was built on a cracked foundation.

The film is almost unbearably procedural in the best sense. Sundqvist clearly did her homework. The retraction process feels *real* in a way cinema rarely achieves with science.

The third act, in which Voss must present her findings at a conference before the formal retraction publishes, is among the most tension-filled scenes I've watched in years — and nothing explodes.

**The one criticism:** the film slightly romanticizes her isolation. Real researchers have department chairs, compliance offices, and lawyers involved far earlier than depicted.

Still, this is the rare film that treats science as a *moral practice*, not just a backdrop.

*Recommended strongly for anyone who has ever submitted a manuscript at 2 a.m.*
