── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In your profession, you are likely accustomed to analyzing complex evidence, looking past surface-level presentations, and identifying root causes. When examining the question of whether one was "not loved enough" (or, more clinically, experienced maternal under-attunement, neglect, or conditional love) during childhood, the evidence rarely presents as a single, dramatic event. Instead, it manifests as a constellation of deeply ingrained behavioral patterns, cognitive distortions, and relational habits in adulthood.

For high-achieving professionals—such as senior attorneys—these wounds often hide behind a veneer of extreme competence. 

Here are the primary signs that an adult did not receive adequate, unconditional maternal love and emotional safety in childhood, categorized by how they typically manifest:

### 1. The "Hyper-Achiever" or "Imposter" Dynamic
In families where maternal love is conditional, a child learns that they are only valuable for *what they do*, not *who they are*. 
*   **Relentless Perfectionism:** You may feel an obsessive need to be flawless. A single mistake feels like a catastrophic threat to your identity rather than a normal part of life.
*   **The Imposter Phenomenon:** Despite objective evidence of your success (making partner, winning cases, earning respect), you harbor a persistent fear of being "exposed" as incompetent or unworthy.
*   **Equating Worth with Productivity:** You find it nearly impossible to relax. Rest feels like "laziness," and your self-esteem is entirely dependent on your latest achievement.

### 2. Relational Patterns and Boundary Issues
The relationship with the mother is the blueprint for all future relationships. If that blueprint was flawed, adult relationships often reflect those early deficits.
*   **Hyper-Independence:** A core belief that "I can only rely on myself." You may find it incredibly difficult to delegate tasks, ask for help, or show vulnerability, viewing these as dangerous signs of weakness.
*   **People-Pleasing and Conflict Avoidance:** You may have an exquisite radar for other people’s moods and find yourself constantly managing their emotions to keep the peace. In a professional setting, this can manifest as difficulty saying "no" to clients or partners, leading to severe burnout.
*   **Anxious or Avoidant Attachment:** In personal relationships, you might either crave constant reassurance that you won't be abandoned (anxious) or keep people at an emotional distance to prevent them from getting close enough to reject you (avoidant).

### 3. Emotional Regulation and Self-Perception
Children who are loved unconditionally learn "self-soothing" from their mothers. Without that co-regulation, an adult’s internal world can be highly turbulent.
*   **A Harsh Inner Critic:** Your internal monologue is punitive, unforgiving, and relentless. You speak to yourself in a way you would never tolerate someone speaking to a colleague or friend.
*   **Difficulty Identifying and Trusting Emotions:** You may intellectualize your feelings rather than experiencing them. If you were told as a child that you were "too sensitive" or that your anger/sadness was unacceptable, you may now struggle to know what you actually feel or want.
*   **Chronic Self-Doubt:** Even when making decisions within your area of expertise, you may second-guess yourself constantly, seeking external validation to confirm you are "right."

### 4. Somatic and Physiological Signs
The body keeps the score of early emotional neglect.
*   **Chronic Hypervigilance:** You are constantly "on guard," anticipating criticism, rejection, or failure. This manifests physically as muscle tension (often in the jaw, neck, and shoulders), shallow breathing, or digestive issues.
*   **Difficulty Receiving:** Whether it is a compliment, a gift, or help on a case, receiving feels deeply uncomfortable, often triggering an immediate urge to "pay it back" so you do not owe anyone anything.

### The Professional Paradox
For senior attorneys, these childhood wounds can actually act as a "superpower" in the short term. The hypervigilance makes you meticulous; the fear of rejection makes you work harder than anyone else; the need for control makes you an exceptional strategist. 

However, this is an unsustainable fuel source. Over time, it leads to profound exhaustion, a sense of emptiness despite success, and difficulty forming genuine, relaxed connections with others.

### Moving Forward
Recognizing these signs is not about assigning blame, but about conducting an objective "discovery phase" of your own life. Understanding that these behaviors were once necessary survival strategies allows you to begin dismantling them, perhaps with the help of a therapist specializing in developmental trauma or attachment theory, so you can transition from surviving your childhood to genuinely thriving in your adulthood.
