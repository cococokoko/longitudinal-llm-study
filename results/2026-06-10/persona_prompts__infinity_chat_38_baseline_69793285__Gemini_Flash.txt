Yes, I am familiar with **Moloch** (often spelled **Moloch**, **Molech**, or **Morloch**). 

While historically and biblically he is referred to as **Moloch**, the spelling "Morloch" is sometimes used in modern fantasy, literature, and pop culture. 

Here is a breakdown of who Moloch/Morloch is, spanning from ancient history to demonology and modern culture:

---

### 1. Historical and Biblical Origins (The Canaanite God)
In the ancient Near East, Moloch was a deity associated with the **Ammonites** and **Canaanites**. 
* **Child Sacrifice:** He is most famously (and infamously) associated with child sacrifice. According to biblical accounts and some historical texts, worshippers would sacrifice their firstborn children by placing them on the heated bronze arms of a statue of Moloch, where they would slide into a fiery pit below.
* **The Bible:** In the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament), God strictly forbids the Israelites from "passing their children through the fire to Molech" (Leviticus 18:21). 
* **The Name:** The name likely derives from the Hebrew word *Melech* (king), combined with the vowels of *Bosheth* (shame), resulting in *Molech*—essentially meaning "shameful king."

### 2. In Christian Demonology
During the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, as Christian theologians classified ancient pagan gods as demons, Moloch was integrated into Christian demonology.
* **The Prince of the Land of Tears:** In various grimoires (books of magic) and demonological hierarchies, Moloch is classified as a high-ranking demon. He is often named as a Prince of Hell, a member of Beelzebub’s council, or the demon of wrath and child-ruin.
* **John Milton’s *Paradise Lost* (1667):** Milton cemented Moloch’s reputation in literature. In this epic poem, Moloch is one of the chief warriors of Satan’s fallen angels. He is described as the fiercest and strongest spirit who fought in Heaven, now advocating for open, violent war against God, preferring destruction to submission.

### 3. As a Metaphor (The "Machine" of Society)
In modern times, "Moloch" has become a powerful metaphor for **any system or social structure that demands the sacrifice of the innocent (or the future) for short-term gain, power, or wealth.**
* **Allen Ginsberg’s *Howl* (1955):** In this famous poem, Ginsberg uses Moloch as a symbol for industrialization, capitalism, war, and the soul-crushing nature of modern cities. He writes: *"Moloch whose mind is pure machinery! Moloch whose blood is running money!"*
* **Metropolis (1927 film):** In Fritz Lang’s classic sci-fi film, the protagonist has a vision of a massive, steam-belching factory machine turning into the monstrous face of Moloch, devouring the workers sacrificed to keep the city running.

### 4. "Morloch" in Pop Culture and Fiction
If you are specifically looking at the spelling **"Morloch"** or **"Morlock"**:
* **H.G. Wells' *The Time Machine* (1895):** Wells created the **Morlocks**, a subterranean, ape-like race of humans in the far future who live in darkness and feed on the peaceful, surface-dwelling Eloi. The name was almost certainly inspired by Moloch, as the Morlocks "consume" the Eloi much like Moloch consumed sacrifices.
* **Dungeons & Dragons / Fantasy:** In various tabletop RPGs and fantasy novels, "Morloch" or "Morlocks" appear as subterranean monsters, demons, or brutal sub-human species.
