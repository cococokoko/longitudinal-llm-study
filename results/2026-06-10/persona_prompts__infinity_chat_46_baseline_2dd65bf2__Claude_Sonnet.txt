## Example of a Linear Graph

A **linear graph** (also called a **path graph**) is a graph where vertices are connected in a single straight sequence, with each vertex connected to the next by exactly one edge.

### Example: P₄ (Path Graph with 4 vertices)

```
A --- B --- C --- D
```

### Components:
| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Vertices** | A, B, C, D |
| **Edges** | {A,B}, {B,C}, {C,D} |
| **Degree sequence** | A=1, B=2, C=2, D=1 |

### Key Properties:
- **4 vertices**, **3 edges**
- The two **end vertices** (A and D) have degree **1**
- The **interior vertices** (B and C) have degree **2**
- There is **exactly one path** between any two vertices
- It is a **tree** (connected, acyclic)

### General Rule:
For a path graph **Pₙ** with *n* vertices:
- Number of edges = **n − 1**
- It is the simplest connected graph with no cycles

This is one of the most fundamental structures in graph theory, used in modeling roads, pipelines, and sequential processes.
