── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at a well-established law firm, building a personal brand on social media is often most effective when it reinforces three things:

- Credibility: Why people should trust your expertise.
- Visibility: Making sure the right audience sees your insights.
- Distinction: Giving people a clear reason to remember you.

A practical framework:

1. Define your positioning

Instead of trying to be known for "law," narrow your focus.

Ask:
- What type of legal work do I want to attract?
- What industries do I know particularly well?
- What legal issues are clients most concerned about right now?
- What do colleagues and clients already seek my advice on?

Examples:
- Employment attorney focused on workplace investigations.
- Corporate attorney advising founder-led businesses.
- Litigation attorney focused on complex commercial disputes.
- Privacy attorney helping healthcare organizations manage compliance risks.

The more specific your positioning, the easier it is to build recognition.

2. Choose the right platform

For most senior attorneys:
- LinkedIn should be the primary platform.
- X (Twitter) can work for legal commentary and media visibility.
- YouTube can be powerful if you're comfortable speaking on camera.
- Podcasts are often highly effective for relationship building.

You do not need to be active everywhere.

3. Create content that demonstrates expertise

Useful content generally outperforms promotional content.

Topics:
- Recent legal developments.
- Regulatory changes.
- Practical business implications of court decisions.
- Common client mistakes.
- Risk management strategies.
- Industry-specific legal trends.
- FAQs you hear from clients.

A simple content mix:
- 50% educational insights.
- 30% commentary on current developments.
- 20% personal perspective and professional experiences.

4. Show the person behind the attorney

Professional audiences increasingly engage with people, not institutions.

Appropriate examples:
- Speaking engagements.
- Conference observations.
- Career lessons.
- Mentoring younger lawyers.
- Community involvement.
- Professional milestones.

The goal is not oversharing. It is demonstrating authenticity and accessibility.

5. Develop a consistent publishing cadence

Consistency matters more than volume.

For LinkedIn:
- 2–4 posts per week is sufficient.
- One longer article or newsletter per month can establish authority.
- Engage with others' content regularly.

6. Build relationships, not just an audience

Many attorneys focus on posting and overlook engagement.

Consider:
- Commenting thoughtfully on industry leaders' posts.
- Congratulating clients and colleagues on achievements.
- Participating in discussions within your practice area.
- Connecting with journalists, executives, and referral sources.

Strong professional relationships often generate more opportunities than large follower counts.

7. Stay compliant with ethics rules

Review:
- Attorney advertising regulations.
- Confidentiality obligations.
- Client consent requirements.
- Firm social media policies.
- Jurisdiction-specific professional conduct rules.

Avoid discussing client matters unless clearly authorized and appropriate.

8. Measure outcomes that matter

Track:
- Speaking invitations.
- Media requests.
- Referral inquiries.
- New professional relationships.
- Newsletter subscribers.
- Business development conversations.

These indicators are often more valuable than likes and follower counts.

Example LinkedIn post structure:

- Hook: "Three lessons from a recent appellate decision affecting employers."
- Insight: Summarize the issue in plain language.
- Business impact: Explain why executives should care.
- Takeaway: Offer practical guidance.
- Question: Invite discussion.

Given your seniority, your strongest advantage is likely not producing large amounts of content but translating complex legal developments into practical business guidance. That is often what clients, executives, and referral sources find most valuable.
