── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive at a large technology company, you are likely surrounded by noise: quarterly earnings, product roadmaps, talent retention issues, board demands, and the relentless pace of technological change (especially in the era of AI). 

In this environment, your brain is constantly wired for **optimization, problem-solving, and external demands**. This makes it incredibly difficult to hear your own voice. You are likely conflating *what you are good at*, *what is expected of you*, and *what you actually want*.

To cut through the noise and understand what you truly want, you need to apply a structured, executive-level framework to your own psyche. Here is a guide to diagnosing your actual desires.

---

### Phase 1: The "Audit" (Deconstruct the Present)
Before you can figure out what you want next, you must audit your current state. Executives often suffer from "hedonic adaptation"—you’ve achieved the title, the comp, and the influence, but the satisfaction has evaporated.

*   **The Energy Audit:** For two weeks, keep a micro-journal. At the end of every meeting, presentation, or task, note your energy level on a scale of -5 to +5. 
    *   *The Goal:* Separate **competence** from **energy**. You might be world-class at resolving cross-functional political battles (competence), but it might leave you at a -4 (draining). What tasks leave you at a +3 or higher?
*   **The "Should" vs. "Want" Filter:** Look at your current goals. Write them down. For each one, ask: *"If I achieved this, but could never tell anyone about it, would I still want it?"* If the answer is no, you don’t want the thing; you want the status or validation that comes with it.
*   **The "Resentment" Map:** What or who do you currently resent? Resentment is a powerful diagnostic tool. It usually points directly to a boundary that is being crossed or a personal need that is being ignored. If you resent a peer who just launched a new venture, you might want entrepreneurial freedom. If you resent your CEO’s schedule, you likely want time autonomy.

### Phase 2: The "Constraint-Free" Simulations
In tech, we use sandboxes to test code without breaking the system. You need to run mental sandboxes.

*   **The "Post-Economic" Scenario:** Assume you have just had a massive liquidity event. You have $50M in the bank. You have taken a six-month vacation. The novelty of doing nothing has worn off. 
    *   *The Question:* It is Tuesday morning at 9:00 AM. What are you doing? Who are you talking to? What problem are you trying to solve? (If your answer is "nothing," you are just exhausted, not lacking desire. Rest first, then answer).
*   **The "Alternative Lives" Portfolio:** Write down three radically different versions of your life five years from now.
    *   *Path A (The Current Trajectory):* You stay on the ladder. You become SVP, C-suite, or CEO. What does that look like?
    *   *Path B (The Pivot):* You stay in tech but change the modality. You become a VC, start a boutique advisory, or found a seed-stage startup.
    *   *Path C (The Wildcard):* You leave the ecosystem. You write a book, teach at a university, buy a winery, or join a non-profit.
    *   *The Diagnostic:* Which of these paths makes you feel a sense of *relief*? Which one makes you feel *dread*, even if it is prestigious?

### Phase 3: Isolate the "Core Currency"
What is the primary driver of your satisfaction? Executives usually optimize for one of four "currencies." You need to know yours, because optimizing for the wrong one leads to deep misery.

1.  **Impact/Scale:** You want to build things that affect millions of lives. (You need large tech companies).
2.  **Autonomy/Control:** You want to be the ultimate decision-maker and own your time. (You need to be a founder, a solo GP, or retired).
3.  **Craft/Mastery:** You love the actual work—coding, designing, architecting, strategizing—and hate the politics and people management. (You need to downshift to an IC role or a highly technical niche).
4.  **Status/Recognition:** You want the prestige, the title, and the respect of your peers. (You need the traditional corporate ladder).

*There is no shame in any of these.* But if your core currency is **Autonomy**, and you are chasing **Status** at a Big Tech company, you will always feel trapped.

### Phase 4: The "Anti-Goal" Exercise
Sometimes it is easier to define what you want by explicitly defining what you *do not* want. 

Write down your absolute worst day. 
*   Who are you talking to? (e.g., "Micromanaging board members," "Underperforming direct reports").
*   What are you doing? (e.g., "Sitting in 8 hours of back-to-back status updates," "Dealing with HR compliance issues").
*   How does your body feel?
*   *Now, design your life to systematically eliminate those elements.*

### Phase 5: Take Actionable Micro-Bets
As a tech executive, you know that you don't launch a massive product without beta testing. Don't make a massive life change without testing it first.

*   If you think you want to be a **VC/Angel**, start by mentoring three startups or making two small angel investments. Does the process energize you, or do you find it boring to be on the sidelines?
*   If you think you want to **write/create**, commit to publishing one essay a week on LinkedIn or Substack for a month. Do you actually enjoy the craft, or did you just like the idea of being a "thought leader"?
*   If you think you want **more time with family**, ruthlessly block out your calendar from 5:00 PM to 8:00 PM for two weeks. See if you can tolerate the anxiety of being offline, or if you find yourself sneaking looks at Slack.

### Summary: The Executive Diagnostic
To understand what you want, you must stop looking at your career as a ladder to climb, and start looking at it as a portfolio of resources (Time, Energy, Capital, Attention) that you are currently allocating. 

If you are unhappy, it is a **resource allocation problem**. 

Ask yourself: **"If I were advising a client with my exact profile, assets, and frustrations, what would my recommendation be?"** Often, the executive brain is brilliant at solving other people's problems. Turn that analytical engine inward.
