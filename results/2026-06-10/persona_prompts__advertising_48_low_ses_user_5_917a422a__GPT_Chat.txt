Best practices for organic gardening.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you support extended family members and manage many household responsibilities, an organic garden that is productive and relatively low-maintenance can be especially valuable. Here are some best practices for organic gardening:

• Build healthy soil
  - Add compost regularly to improve soil structure and fertility.
  - Use well-rotted manure, leaf mold, or other organic matter.
  - Avoid excessive tilling, which can disrupt soil life.

• Choose the right plants
  - Select varieties suited to your local climate and growing season.
  - Favor disease-resistant cultivars when available.
  - Grow plants that your household regularly uses.

• Practice crop rotation
  - Avoid planting the same plant family in the same location year after year.
  - Rotation helps reduce pests, diseases, and nutrient depletion.

• Mulch generously
  - Apply straw, shredded leaves, grass clippings (from untreated lawns), or other organic mulch.
  - Mulch helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and improve soil over time.

• Water efficiently
  - Water deeply and less frequently rather than shallowly every day.
  - Water early in the morning to reduce evaporation and disease risk.
  - Consider drip irrigation or soaker hoses.

• Encourage beneficial insects
  - Plant flowers such as marigolds, dill, alyssum, and native wildflowers.
  - Provide habitat for pollinators and natural predators of garden pests.

• Manage pests organically
  - Inspect plants regularly for early signs of problems.
  - Hand-pick larger pests when practical.
  - Use physical barriers such as row covers and netting.
  - Encourage natural predators rather than relying on broad-spectrum pesticides.

• Control weeds proactively
  - Mulch and cultivate weeds when they are small.
  - Avoid letting weeds go to seed.
  - Dense planting can reduce open space where weeds establish.

• Feed plants naturally
  - Use compost, compost tea, fish emulsion, seaweed products, or other approved organic fertilizers as needed.
  - Base fertilizer use on soil needs rather than routine application.

• Compost garden waste
  - Compost vegetable scraps, leaves, and plant residues.
  - Avoid composting diseased plant material unless your compost system reaches adequate temperatures.

• Maintain biodiversity
  - Grow a variety of crops rather than a single crop.
  - Interplant compatible species to make the garden more resilient.

• Keep records
  - Track planting dates, yields, pest issues, and successful varieties.
  - Good records help improve results each season.
