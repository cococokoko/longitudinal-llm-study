── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a tenured professor at a prestigious university.

Creating a successful YouTube channel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you already have two assets many creators spend years building: subject-matter expertise and credibility. The challenge is translating those into content that people actually choose to watch.

A useful approach is to think of your channel as education packaged for attention.

Key principles

- Optimize for audience curiosity, not academic completeness.
- Teach one idea per video whenever possible.
- Focus on problems, questions, debates, and surprising findings.
- Assume viewers can leave at any second.

Channel positioning

Choose a clear identity such as:

- "A professor explains the most important ideas in [field]."
- "What the public gets wrong about [field]."
- "The science behind everyday life."
- "Academic research translated into practical insights."
- "University-level thinking without the jargon."

The more specific your niche, the easier it is to build an audience.

Video formats that often work well

- Myth-busting:
  - "5 misconceptions about climate economics"
- Explainers:
  - "What people misunderstand about inflation"
- Current events:
  - "What this new study actually found"
- Historical deep dives:
  - "The idea that changed an entire discipline"
- Research reviews:
  - "I read 100 papers so you don't have to"
- Professor reacts:
  - Analyze claims from news, social media, films, or public debates

Content structure

For a 10–15 minute video:

- First 15 seconds:
  - Present a compelling question or surprising claim.
- First minute:
  - Explain why the topic matters.
- Main section:
  - Deliver the core insight.
- Final minute:
  - Summarize and connect to a larger idea.

Avoid lengthy introductions about your credentials. Viewers care most about the value they'll receive.

Production quality

You do not need a studio initially.

Prioritize:

- Clear audio
- Good lighting
- Clean background
- Strong editing pace

Poor audio hurts growth more than modest video quality.

Publishing strategy

- Publish consistently (e.g., weekly).
- Create 20–30 videos before judging results.
- Study retention data rather than focusing only on views.
- Double down on topics that outperform.

Titles and thumbnails

Many academics underestimate how important these are.

Instead of:
- "Lecture on Cognitive Biases"

Try:
- "The Mental Error That Changes Your Decisions Every Day"

The content can remain rigorous while the packaging becomes more engaging.

Leverage your academic position

Advantages you can use:

- Access to cutting-edge research
- Colleagues for interviews
- University resources and archives
- Ability to explain complex topics accurately

These can become recurring series.

Common mistakes professors make

- Turning videos into lectures
- Using excessive jargon
- Covering too many concepts at once
- Prioritizing precision over clarity
- Making titles that sound like journal articles

A useful rule: if a first-year undergraduate would struggle to follow a sentence, simplify it.

Growth timeline

Many educational channels grow slowly at first. A realistic path is:

- First 20 videos: learning production and audience preferences
- First 50 videos: identifying repeatable formats
- First 100 videos: significant growth potential if the content resonates
