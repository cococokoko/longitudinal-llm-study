── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at a well-established firm, your social media strategy must be fundamentally different from that of a solo practitioner or a junior lawyer. You are not just representing yourself; you are representing the prestige of your firm, maintaining the trust of high-value clients, and adhering to strict ethical guidelines. 

Your goal is not "virality." Your goal is **authority, trust, and influence.**

Here is a strategic blueprint for building a high-impact personal brand on social media as a senior attorney.

---

### 1. Platform Selection: Where to Play
As a senior attorney, your time is highly valuable. Do not try to be everywhere. Focus on where your clients, peers, and referral sources actually are.

*   **LinkedIn (Primary - 80% of effort):** This is your digital office. It is the premier platform for B2B networking, client acquisition, talent recruitment, and industry thought leadership.
*   **X/Twitter (Secondary - Optional):** Useful if your practice area intersects with politics, public policy, tech, or finance. It is a great place to interact with journalists.
*   **Instagram/TikTok (Avoid or highly curate):** Generally not suitable for senior B2B attorneys, unless you specialize in entertainment, sports, or family law—and even then, the ROI is often low compared to the risk of looking unprofessional.

---

### 2. Defining Your "Content Pillars"
To build a cohesive brand, you should focus on 3 to 4 specific topics. This prevents your feed from looking cluttered and establishes you as a specialist.

*   **Pillar 1: Industry Insights & Analysis.** Don’t just report the news; explain *what it means* for businesses. (e.g., "The Supreme Court just ruled on [X]. Here are the three implications for tech CEOs.")
*   **Pillar 2: Leadership & Firm Culture.** Share insights on mentorship, diversity in law, the evolution of the legal profession, or how you manage high-performing teams.
*   **Pillar 3: Case Studies & Wins (Anonymized).** Share the *strategy* behind a successful negotiation or trial without violating attorney-client privilege. Focus on the "how," not just the "what."
*   **Pillar 4: Behind-the-Scenes/Human Element.** Occasional personal posts (charity work, speaking engagements, a book recommendation) to make you approachable.

---

### 3. The "Senior Attorney" Tone of Voice
Your tone must strike a balance between **authoritative** and **accessible**.
*   **No Legalese:** Write for the C-suite, not for judges. Use clear, punchy, and persuasive language.
*   **Nuanced, Not Reactive:** Avoid "hot takes." As a senior attorney, your audience expects measured, analytical, and objective commentary.
*   **Generous with Knowledge:** Give away 90% of your knowledge for free. Clients pay for the 10%—the application of that knowledge to their specific, complex problems.

---

### 4. Content Formats That Work on LinkedIn
*   **The "Text + Image" Post:** A photo of you speaking at a conference or at your desk, paired with a story about a lesson learned. (Images of *people* perform 3-4x better than text alone).
*   **The PDF Carousel:** A slide deck summarizing a complex legal change. (e.g., "A 5-Slide Guide to the New FTC Non-Compete Ban"). These are highly shareable.
*   **The Short-Form Video:** 60-second videos answering a common client question. Keep production clean but simple—no flashy TikTok transitions, just high-quality audio and lighting.

---

### 5. Navigating Firm Politics and Ethics
This is the biggest hurdle for senior attorneys at established firms. 
*   **Get Internal Buy-In:** Align with your firm’s marketing/PR department and managing partner. Frame your personal branding as a **business development tool for the firm**, not just a vanity project.
*   **The Disclaimer:** Put a standard disclaimer in your bio (e.g., "Opinions mine. Not legal advice. No attorney-client relationship formed.").
*   **Strict Privilege Control:** Never post about active cases. When posting about past cases, change identifying details or use hypothetical scenarios.
*   **Avoid Controversy:** Stay away from partisan politics, unless your practice area specifically dictates it. 

---

### 6. A 30-Minute Weekly Workflow (For Busy Attorneys)
You do not have hours to spend on social media. Use this leveraged system:

1.  **The "Brain Dump" (10 mins):** Once a week, write down 3 questions clients asked you this week. Those are your next 3 posts.
2.  **Drafting (15 mins):** Write the posts. Keep them under 200 words. Use bullet points for readability.
3.  **Scheduling (5 mins):** Use a tool like Buffer or Publer (or LinkedIn’s native scheduler) to schedule them to go out Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday mornings.
4.  **Engagement (10 mins/day):** Do not just post and leave. Spend 10 minutes a day commenting on posts by clients, prospective clients, and industry leaders. **Engagement is where the relationships are built.**
