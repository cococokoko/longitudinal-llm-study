── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Thank you for the work you do! Hospital staff, especially patient care aides, are the backbone of healthcare. 

In graph theory, a **linear graph** is most commonly referred to as a **Path Graph** (usually denoted as $P_n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices). It is a graph where the vertices (nodes) are connected in a single, straight line with no branches, loops, or cycles.

Here is a simple example of a linear graph with 4 vertices ($P_4$):

### The Visual Graph
```text
(A) —————— (B) —————— (C) —————— (D)
```

### Mathematical Definition of this Example:
*   **Vertices (V):** $\{A, B, C, D\}$ (These represent the points/nodes)
*   **Edges (E):** $\{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$ (These represent the connections between them)

---

### A Hospital Analogy for Your Work
To make this relatable to your job as a patient care aide, you can think of a linear graph as a **sequential step-by-step workflow** or a **chain of command** during a shift. 

Imagine a patient's admission process represented as a graph:

```text
[Admissions Desk] ───> [Triage Nurse] ───> [Patient Care Aide (You)] ───> [Doctor]
```

*   **Vertices:** The different staff members/stations.
*   **Edges:** The transfer of the patient from one person to the next. 

Because the patient must move strictly from one person to the next in a single line without skipping steps or looping back, this workflow forms a **linear graph**.
