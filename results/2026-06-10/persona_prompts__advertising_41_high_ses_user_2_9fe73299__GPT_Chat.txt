How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If your goal is to improve your credit score as quickly as possible, focus on the factors that can change within one or two reporting cycles:

- Lower your credit utilization ratio
  - This is often the fastest lever.
  - Pay down revolving credit card balances, especially cards that are near their limits.
  - Aim for utilization below 30%, and ideally below 10% of available credit.

- Make all payments on time
  - Payment history is the largest scoring factor.
  - If you have any accounts that are currently past due, bring them current as soon as possible.
  - Set up automatic payments where practical.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Obtain reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute inaccurate late payments, incorrect balances, duplicate accounts, or fraudulent accounts.
  - Correcting errors can sometimes produce meaningful score improvements.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If approved without a hard inquiry (policies vary by issuer), a higher limit can reduce utilization immediately.
  - Avoid increasing spending after receiving the higher limit.

- Pay credit card balances before the statement closing date
  - Credit scores generally use the balance reported to the bureaus, not necessarily the balance due date amount.
  - Reducing the reported balance can improve utilization more quickly.

- Avoid applying for new credit unless necessary
  - New applications can generate hard inquiries and reduce the average age of accounts.
  - Multiple applications in a short period can temporarily depress scores.

- Do not close older credit accounts
  - Closing accounts can increase utilization and may eventually reduce the average age of your credit history.

- Address collections or derogatory items strategically
  - Depending on the scoring model and the specific item, paying or settling collections may help.
  - The impact varies, so evaluate each account individually.
