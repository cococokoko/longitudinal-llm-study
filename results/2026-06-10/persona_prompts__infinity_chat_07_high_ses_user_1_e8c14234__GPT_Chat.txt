── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any practical and, very likely, any absolute sense relevant to humans, it is impossible to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- Finite cognitive capacity:
  - A human brain has limited storage, attention, and processing resources.
  - The amount of information that could potentially be known about the universe is vastly larger than what any individual could learn.

- Knowledge is continually expanding:
  - New observations, discoveries, technologies, and events occur constantly.
  - Even if someone somehow mastered all current human knowledge, new knowledge would emerge.

- Some knowledge may be inaccessible:
  - There are physical limits on what can be observed, such as regions beyond our cosmological horizon.
  - Certain facts may be lost forever if the relevant evidence no longer exists.

- Fundamental limits in mathematics and logic:
  - Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that in sufficiently powerful formal systems, there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
  - Results such as Turing's halting problem demonstrate limits on what can be computed or decided.

- Scientific knowledge is provisional:
  - Science produces models that are increasingly accurate, but they remain open to revision in light of new evidence.
  - Knowing "everything" would require certainty that no future observation could alter understanding.

For someone in a field like neurosurgery, there is also a more local version of this reality. The field expands faster than any individual can absorb it. Expertise increasingly depends not on possessing all knowledge, but on:
- Knowing foundational principles deeply.
- Recognizing patterns effectively.
- Knowing where and how to find reliable information.
- Judging evidence and uncertainty well.

In that sense, wisdom in a mature discipline often comes from understanding the boundaries of one's knowledge as clearly as the knowledge itself.

A deeper philosophical question is whether an omniscient being is even conceptually possible. Different philosophical and theological traditions answer that differently. But for humans, the impossibility of knowing everything is about as close to a settled conclusion as any broad epistemological claim can be.
