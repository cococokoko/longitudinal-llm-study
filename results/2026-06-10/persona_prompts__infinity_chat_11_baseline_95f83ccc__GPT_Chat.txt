That depends on what you mean by "haven't achieved anything."

At 60, people often compare themselves to a mental list of milestones:
- Career success
- Wealth
- Marriage or family
- Recognition or status
- Creative accomplishments
- Travel and adventures
- Owning a home
- Leadership or influence

If you haven't had some of those experiences, then yes, there are specific things you may have missed. It's reasonable to feel grief about that. Certain opportunities are tied to particular stages of life, and not every path remains equally available forever.

But there are two important things to keep in mind.

First, achievement is not the same as having lived. Many people who have impressive résumés still feel regret. They may have earned money but missed close relationships. They may have raised families but never pursued their interests. They may have gained status but lived with chronic anxiety. There is no version of life that includes every worthwhile experience.

Second, when people say they have achieved nothing, they are often using a very narrow definition of achievement. Consider:
- Have you helped anyone?
- Have you worked, even if the work wasn't prestigious?
- Have you endured difficulties?
- Have you learned things?
- Have you cared for family, friends, pets, or a community?
- Have you shown kindness, loyalty, or integrity at times when it mattered?

Those may not earn awards, but they are not nothing.

The harder question might be: what do you feel you've missed?

For example:
- If you wanted a family and never had one, that's a specific loss.
- If you wanted a career you never pursued, that's a specific loss.
- If you spent decades afraid to try things, that's a different kind of loss.
- If you actually accomplished many things but they don't feel meaningful, that's yet another issue.

At 60, some possibilities are gone, but many are not. People start businesses, write books, find partners, build friendships, learn instruments, travel, volunteer, earn degrees, and reinvent themselves well into their 60s, 70s, and beyond. The future is shorter than it was at 20, but it is not necessarily small.
