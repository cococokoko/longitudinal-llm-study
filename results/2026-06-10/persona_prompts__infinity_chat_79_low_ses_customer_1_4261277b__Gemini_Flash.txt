### Project Title: *The Clay Pot Filter: Upcycling Local Waste to Clean Vietnam’s Water*

#### **The Real-World Vietnam Connection**
Many communities in Vietnam—from the Mekong Delta to rural areas outside major cities—struggle with access to clean drinking water due to heavy metal contamination (like arsenic), agricultural runoff, and industrial waste. 

#### **The STEM Concept**
You will build and test a low-cost, gravity-driven water filtration system using **terracotta clay** mixed with different local organic waste materials (like **rice husks** or **coffee grounds**, both of which are incredibly abundant and cheap/free in Vietnam). 

When clay is mixed with rice husks or coffee grounds and fired (or baked), the organic material burns away, leaving behind microscopic pores. These pores act as a natural filter that traps bacteria and sediment. 

---

### **Why this fits a busy, unpredictable schedule:**
*   **No living subjects:** Unlike projects with plants or bacteria cultures, this doesn't require daily watering or precise feeding times. If you get called into a shift unexpectedly, your project can sit on your counter for days without any issue.
*   **Batch testing:** You can do the building on one day off, and the testing on another day off. 
*   **Low cost:** The materials are either free waste products or very cheap household items.

---

### **Materials Needed (Under $15–$20 total):**
1.  **Terracotta Clay:** (Available at any craft store or online for a few dollars).
2.  **Organic Waste:** Rice husks (you can get these free from Asian markets or pet stores) or spent coffee grounds (free from any local cafe or your own kitchen).
3.  **Dirty Water:** You can make your own "polluted" water using dirt, food coloring (to represent chemical dyes from Vietnam's textile industry), and vinegar (to represent acidity).
4.  **Plastic Bottles:** Cut in half to act as the funnels/stands for your filters.
5.  **A simple water testing kit:** (Optional, around $5-$10 online to test for pH or heavy metals, or you can just measure "turbidity"—how clear the water is—using a phone camera and a flashlight).

---

### **The Experiment (Step-by-Step):**

1.  **Create the Filters:** 
    *   Mix your clay with different ratios of your organic material. For example:
        *   Filter A: 100% Clay (Control)
        *   Filter B: 80% Clay + 20% Rice Husks
        *   Filter C: 80% Clay + 20% Coffee Grounds
    *   Shape them into small bowls or cups. 
    *   *The "Baking" Step:* Traditionally, these are fired in a kiln. For a budget/home version, you can bake them in a standard home oven at its highest temperature for a few hours, or use a simple backyard fire pit/charcoal grill to get them hotter. (Even if they aren't fully vitrified like professional ceramics, they will still function for a short-term science experiment).

2.  **Set Up the Test:**
    *   Place each clay cup inside the top half of a cut plastic bottle.
    *   Pour your "polluted" water into the clay cups.
    *   Let gravity do the work. The water will slowly drip through the clay into the bottom of the bottle.

3.  **Measure the Results:**
    *   **Flow Rate:** How fast does the water filter through? (Too fast means the pores are too big; too slow means it's not practical).
    *   **Clarity (Turbidity):** Hold a flashlight behind the filtered water. Which filter made the water the clearest?
    *   **pH/Contaminants:** Use a cheap test strip to see if the filters changed the acidity of the water.
