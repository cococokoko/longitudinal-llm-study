── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes—at least based on everything we currently understand, it appears impossible for any finite being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The universe may contain more information than any single mind could store or process.
- Knowledge is constantly changing. New events happen all the time, creating new facts to know.
- Some things may be fundamentally unknowable. In physics, for example, certain properties cannot be measured with unlimited precision simultaneously.
- Mathematics contains limits on what can be proven. Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that in sufficiently powerful formal systems, there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
- A knower trying to know "everything" would need complete knowledge of itself, including every future state of its own reasoning, which leads to deep logical and practical difficulties.

There's also a distinction between:
- Knowing everything that is true.
- Being able to answer every question.
- Being able to prove every truth.

Those are not necessarily the same thing.

Some philosophical and religious traditions posit an omniscient being that knows everything. Whether such a being is possible depends on one's metaphysical assumptions. From a scientific and human perspective, however, complete knowledge appears unattainable.

A related question is whether there is a fixed set of all truths that could, in principle, be known. Even that is debated. If the universe is infinite, or if new truths come into existence as time passes, then "everything" may not be a completed collection that can be fully possessed at all.
