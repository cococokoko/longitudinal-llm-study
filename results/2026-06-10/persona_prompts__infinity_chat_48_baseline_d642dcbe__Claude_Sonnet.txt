The Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) witnessed remarkable economic development that transformed China into one of the ancient world's most prosperous civilizations. The early Han emperors adopted a policy of light taxation and minimal government interference, allowing the agricultural sector to recover from the devastation of the preceding Qin Dynasty and the civil wars that followed it. As the population grew and agricultural output increased, the government gradually introduced more active economic policies, including state control over key industries such as salt and iron production under Emperor Wu (141–87 BCE), which generated significant revenue for the imperial treasury. The Silk Road, which expanded considerably during this period, facilitated long-distance trade between China and Central Asia, the Middle East, and eventually Rome, allowing Chinese merchants to export silk, ceramics, and other luxury goods in exchange for gold, glass, and exotic products. Domestically, improvements in technology, including the invention of the iron plow and the collar harness for horses, greatly enhanced agricultural productivity, while advances in metallurgy and textile production strengthened manufacturing. The Han government also standardized currency, weights, and measures, creating a more unified and efficient marketplace across the vast empire. Together, these developments established an economic foundation that supported a population of roughly 60 million people and left a lasting legacy on Chinese civilization.
