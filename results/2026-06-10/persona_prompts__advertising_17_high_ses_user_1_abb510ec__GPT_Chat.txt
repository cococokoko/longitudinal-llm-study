── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home energy efficiency is usually most effective when you focus on the biggest sources of energy loss first: heating/cooling, air leakage, insulation, and major appliances.

Some of the highest-impact steps include:

- Get a home energy audit
  - A professional audit can identify where your home is losing energy.
  - Many utilities offer subsidized or free audits.
  - Thermal imaging and blower-door testing can reveal hidden leaks.

- Seal air leaks
  - Caulk gaps around windows and doors.
  - Weather-strip exterior doors.
  - Seal openings around plumbing, wiring, vents, and attic penetrations.
  - Air sealing is often one of the most cost-effective improvements.

- Improve insulation
  - Add insulation in the attic if levels are below current recommendations.
  - Insulate crawl spaces, basements, and exterior walls where practical.
  - Proper attic insulation can significantly reduce heating and cooling costs.

- Upgrade heating and cooling systems
  - Replace older furnaces, boilers, or air conditioners with high-efficiency models when they reach end of life.
  - Consider a heat pump, which can provide both heating and cooling efficiently in many climates.
  - Maintain HVAC equipment with regular filter changes and servicing.

- Use a smart or programmable thermostat
  - Automatically reduce heating or cooling when you're asleep or away.
  - Even modest temperature adjustments can lower energy use.

- Improve windows strategically
  - If windows are old and drafty, replacement may help, but it is often less cost-effective than air sealing and insulation.
  - Consider weather-stripping, storm windows, or window films before full replacement.
  - Use blinds, shades, or curtains to reduce heat gain and heat loss.

- Upgrade water heating
  - Install a heat-pump water heater if suitable.
  - Insulate hot-water pipes.
  - Lower the water-heater temperature to a safe, efficient setting.
  - Fix leaks and install low-flow fixtures.

- Switch to efficient lighting
  - Replace remaining incandescent or halogen bulbs with LEDs.
  - LEDs use substantially less electricity and last much longer.

- Reduce appliance and electronics consumption
  - Choose ENERGY STAR–rated appliances when replacing old equipment.
  - Use advanced power strips to reduce standby ("phantom") power consumption.
  - Unplug rarely used devices.

- Consider solar energy
  - Rooftop solar can reduce electricity bills, particularly in areas with favorable incentives or high utility rates.
  - Battery storage may add resilience but often has a longer financial payback.

- Optimize landscaping
  - Shade trees can reduce cooling loads in warm climates.
  - Windbreaks may reduce heating demand in colder regions.

To prioritize effectively, it helps to know:
- Your climate (hot, cold, mixed).
- Whether the home is a house, townhouse, or apartment.
- Approximate age of the home.
- Whether your main concern is lowering utility bills, reducing emissions, or increasing comfort.

With those details, I can suggest the highest-return improvements for your specific situation.
