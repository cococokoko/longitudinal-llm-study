What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

If such a strategy existed and remained profitable indefinitely, large traders would exploit it until the edge disappeared. Markets are competitive and constantly changing.

That said, some of the most consistently successful day trading approaches have shared characteristics:

- They exploit a small, measurable market edge.
- They use strict risk management.
- They are tested over thousands of trades.
- They adapt to changing market conditions.
- They focus on preserving capital during unfavorable periods.

Examples of historically successful day trading styles include:

- Trend-following
  - Buy strong stocks or markets moving upward and sell weak ones moving downward.
  - Often uses moving averages, breakouts, or momentum indicators.
  - Can produce large winners but has many small losses.

- Opening Range Breakout (ORB)
  - Trade when price breaks above or below the first 5–30 minutes of the trading session.
  - Popular among professional futures and stock traders.
  - Works best in volatile, trending markets.

- Momentum Trading
  - Focus on assets showing unusually strong volume and price movement.
  - Common in stocks, futures, and crypto.
  - Can be highly profitable but also carries substantial risk.

- Mean Reversion
  - Bet that extreme price moves will revert toward an average.
  - Often used by quantitative firms.
  - Tends to have a high win rate but occasional large losses.

- Market Making / Statistical Arbitrage
  - Historically among the most profitable trading businesses.
  - Used by firms such as Citadel Securities, Jane Street, and Two Sigma.
  - Requires advanced technology, data, and significant capital.
  - Not realistically accessible to most retail traders.

If your goal is maximum profitability with realistic access as an individual trader, many professional traders would argue that:
- A disciplined trend-following or momentum strategy,
- Combined with strict risk limits (e.g., risking 0.25%–1% of capital per trade),
- And executed consistently over hundreds of trades,

has a better chance of long-term success than searching for a "holy grail" setup.

One important point: studies of retail day traders generally find that most lose money after costs, taxes, slippage, and emotional mistakes. The difference between profitable and unprofitable traders is often risk management and discipline rather than the entry signal itself.
