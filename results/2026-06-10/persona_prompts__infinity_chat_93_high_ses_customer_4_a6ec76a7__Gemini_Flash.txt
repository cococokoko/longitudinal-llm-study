At the physiological level, the stress response is mediated by the rapid activation of the sympathetic-adrenal-medullary (SAM) axis, followed by the slower, sustained engagement of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Acute stressors trigger a cascade of catecholamines—epinephrine and norepinephrine—resulting in immediate systemic changes: elevated heart rate, vasoconstriction, bronchodilation, and mobilized glucose reserves. 

However, chronic stress leads to sustained hypercortisolemia, which exerts profound deleterious effects across multiple organ systems. In the periphery, chronic glucocorticoid exposure promotes systemic low-grade inflammation via the upregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-α), accelerating atherogenesis and endothelial dysfunction. It also impairs insulin sensitivity, predisposing patients to metabolic syndrome. 

Neurologically, prolonged HPA axis dysregulation induces structural remodeling in the brain. High levels of circulating glucocorticoids promote dendritic atrophy and loss of dendritic spines in the CA3 region of the hippocampus and the prefrontal cortex, impairing memory consolidation and executive function. Conversely, stress induces hypertrophy and hyper-reactivity in the basolateral amygdala, shifting the neural architecture toward a state of hypervigilance, anxiety, and emotional dysregulation. Over time, this neuroendocrine wear-and-tear—or allostatic load—compromises both systemic homeostasis and cognitive resilience, bridging the gap between psychological distress and somatic pathology.
