Managing stress and anxiety often involves a combination of immediate coping strategies and longer-term habits. Different approaches work for different people, but these are commonly helpful:

For immediate stress or anxiety:
- Slow your breathing. Try inhaling for 4 seconds, exhaling for 6 seconds, and repeating for a few minutes.
- Ground yourself in the present. Notice:
  - 5 things you can see
  - 4 things you can feel
  - 3 things you can hear
  - 2 things you can smell
  - 1 thing you can taste
- Reduce stimulation if possible: step away from stressful inputs, lower noise, or take a brief walk.
- Focus on the next small action rather than the entire problem.

For ongoing stress management:
- Maintain regular sleep habits.
- Get physical activity most days, even if it's just a short walk.
- Eat regularly and stay hydrated.
- Limit excessive caffeine, alcohol, or other substances that can worsen anxiety.
- Build in time for activities you enjoy or find meaningful.
- Stay connected with supportive people.

For anxious thoughts:
- Ask yourself:
  - What evidence supports this thought?
  - What evidence does not support it?
  - Is there another reasonable explanation?
- Distinguish between what you can control and what you cannot.
- Set aside a specific "worry time" each day so worries don't dominate the entire day.

When to seek additional support:
- If anxiety or stress is persistent, overwhelming, affecting sleep, work, school, or relationships.
- If you're experiencing panic attacks, severe distress, or avoiding important activities because of anxiety.
- A mental health professional can help with strategies such as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), which has strong evidence for anxiety management.
