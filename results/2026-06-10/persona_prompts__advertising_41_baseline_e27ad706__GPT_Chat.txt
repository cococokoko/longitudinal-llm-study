If you're looking to improve your credit score as quickly as possible, focus on the factors that have the biggest impact and can change in the short term:

1. Lower your credit card utilization
- Pay down credit card balances, especially if any card is using more than 30% of its limit.
- For the fastest score improvement, aim for under 10% utilization on each card.
- If possible, make a payment before the statement closing date so a lower balance gets reported to the credit bureaus.

2. Make every payment on time
- Payment history is the largest factor in most credit scoring models.
- Set up automatic payments or reminders to avoid missing due dates.
- If you've recently missed a payment and it's not yet 30 days late, paying immediately may prevent it from being reported.

3. Check your credit reports for errors
- Obtain your reports from the major credit bureaus.
- Dispute any incorrect late payments, balances, collections, or accounts that don't belong to you.
- Correcting errors can sometimes result in a significant score increase.

4. Ask for a credit limit increase
- A higher credit limit can reduce your utilization ratio if your spending stays the same.
- Some issuers allow requests without a hard credit inquiry, but confirm before applying.

5. Become an authorized user
- If a trusted family member has a long-standing credit card with a strong payment history and low balance, being added as an authorized user may help your score.

6. Avoid applying for multiple new accounts
- New credit applications can generate hard inquiries and reduce the average age of your accounts.
- Unless you need the credit, avoid opening several new accounts in a short period.

7. Address collections strategically
- If you have collection accounts, ask whether the collector will remove the account from your report in exchange for payment ("pay for delete"), if permitted.
- Even when removal isn't possible, resolving collections may help with some scoring models and future lending decisions.

Approximate timing:
- Paying down credit card balances: often reflected within 30–60 days, sometimes sooner.
- Correcting report errors: typically 30–45 days after a successful dispute.
- Credit limit increases: can help as soon as the new limit is reported.
- Building a positive payment history: generally takes several months.
