── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty typically make several arguments in its favor. The strongest arguments are usually based on justice, public safety, deterrence, and moral reasoning. Here are some of the most common and substantial ones:

- Retributive justice ("just deserts")
  - Some believe that certain crimes, such as premeditated murder or mass murder, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - The argument is not primarily about utility but about moral accountability: a person who intentionally takes innocent lives forfeits their own right to life.
  - Supporters often argue that justice requires punishments that match the gravity of the offense.

- Protection of society
  - Executed offenders cannot commit further crimes, escape prison, or orchestrate violence from within prison.
  - Supporters point to rare cases where convicted murderers kill again, including while incarcerated.
  - The death penalty is seen as providing the highest possible level of public protection from the most dangerous offenders.

- Deterrence
  - Some argue that the possibility of execution discourages at least some people from committing murder.
  - The logic is that a harsher penalty should create a stronger disincentive than life imprisonment.
  - While evidence on deterrence is heavily debated among researchers, many supporters view deterrence as a plausible benefit.

- Moral accountability and respect for victims
  - Some believe failing to impose the harshest punishment for the worst crimes diminishes the value of the victims' lives.
  - They argue that executing a murderer recognizes the seriousness of the harm done and affirms society's moral condemnation of the act.

- Closure for victims' families
  - Some families of victims report feeling that an execution brings a sense of finality or justice.
  - Supporters argue that lengthy imprisonment can leave wounds unresolved, whereas carrying out the sentence may help some families move forward.
  - It is worth noting that not all victims' families agree with this argument.

- Democratic legitimacy
  - In many places, support for the death penalty has at times been substantial among the public.
  - Some argue that if citizens, through democratic processes, choose to authorize capital punishment, governments may legitimately use it within constitutional and legal limits.

- Incapacitation beyond life imprisonment
  - Supporters sometimes argue that life imprisonment is not always guaranteed to mean lifelong confinement because laws can change, sentences can be reduced, or convictions overturned.
  - Execution permanently eliminates those possibilities for offenders who are unquestionably guilty.

- Historical and philosophical traditions
  - Some ethical, religious, and legal traditions have long accepted capital punishment for especially grave offenses.
  - Supporters may argue that these traditions reflect enduring intuitions about justice and punishment.

The strongest criticisms of these arguments include the risk of executing innocent people, concerns about unequal application of the law, questions about whether executions actually deter crime, and the belief that the state should not take human life. These criticisms are a major reason why many countries have abolished the death penalty.
