There is no single day trading strategy that is objectively the "best" or "most profitable of all time."

That said, some of the most consistently studied and widely used day-trading approaches include:

- Trend-following
  - Buy assets showing strength and sell those showing weakness.
  - Uses tools such as moving averages, VWAP, price structure, and momentum indicators.
  - Many professional traders prefer trend-following because large market moves can outweigh many small losses.

- Momentum trading
  - Focuses on stocks or assets experiencing unusually high volume, news, or volatility.
  - Traders attempt to ride short-term price acceleration.
  - Potentially high returns, but also high risk.

- Breakout trading
  - Enter when price moves beyond a well-defined support, resistance, or consolidation range.
  - Works best when accompanied by strong volume.
  - False breakouts are a common challenge.

- Mean reversion
  - Assumes prices that move too far from their average tend to revert.
  - Often used in stocks, ETFs, and some futures markets.
  - Can perform poorly during strong trends.

- Market-making and statistical arbitrage
  - Common among quantitative firms.
  - Relies on technology, automation, and very large datasets.
  - Generally inaccessible to most retail traders.

- Most day traders do not outperform simple long-term investing after costs and taxes.
- Day trading requires substantial time, emotional control, and risk tolerance.
- Consistent risk management is usually more important than the entry strategy itself.
- Many successful traders risk only a small percentage of their capital on any single trade.
