My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is less a question of fairness in the abstract and more a question of aligning money, ownership, and risk.

A few key facts matter:
- Will only her name be on the deed?
- Will only her name be on the mortgage?
- Will you live there together before marriage?
- Are you contributing to the down payment, monthly payments, renovations, or all three?
- Do you expect your contributions to build ownership, or are you comfortable treating them as rent?

In general, if she owns the house and you are not on the title, there are a few common approaches:

- Pay rent.
  - She owns the property, so you contribute a reasonable amount toward housing costs, similar to what you would pay elsewhere.
  - This is often the simplest arrangement before marriage.
  - The amount doesn't necessarily have to equal half the mortgage; it can be based on your incomes and local market rents.

- Split household expenses but not the mortgage.
  - She pays the mortgage, taxes, and major ownership costs.
  - You cover a larger share of utilities, groceries, or other living expenses.
  - This avoids ambiguity about whether you're acquiring equity.

- Become a co-owner.
  - If you contribute to the purchase price or mortgage with the expectation of ownership, talk to a lawyer about putting your name on the deed or creating a written ownership agreement.
  - Without documentation, people sometimes contribute substantial amounts and later discover they have no legal claim.

- Wait until marriage and revisit.
  - Some couples keep assets separate until they're married, then decide whether to refinance, add a spouse to the title, or otherwise combine finances.

One thing I would be cautious about: paying a significant portion of the down payment, mortgage principal, or major renovations on a house that you do not own, unless you've both explicitly agreed in writing how those contributions will be treated.

A practical compromise many couples use:
- The homeowner pays ownership-related costs (mortgage, property taxes, insurance, major repairs).
- The non-owner pays a fair monthly amount for living there (rent or a share of expenses).
- After marriage, they revisit ownership and financial arrangements.
