Improving home Wi‑Fi is usually a combination of better placement, reducing interference, and ensuring the equipment matches your needs.

Start with the highest-impact steps:

- Place the router centrally in the home rather than in a corner.
- Elevate it (on a shelf or table) and keep it out in the open.
- Avoid placing it near:
  - Microwaves
  - Cordless phone bases
  - Large metal objects
  - TVs and AV cabinets
  - Thick concrete or brick walls

Optimize your Wi‑Fi settings:

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available for faster speeds and less interference.
- Use 2.4 GHz for devices that are far from the router or need maximum range.
- Ensure WPA2 or WPA3 security is enabled.
- Update the router's firmware regularly.

Check whether your router is the bottleneck:

- If your router is more than 4–5 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can provide significant improvements.
- If you have gigabit internet, verify that your router supports those speeds.

For larger homes:

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system (e.g., Eero, Google Nest WiFi, Asus ZenWiFi, Orbi).
- Mesh systems generally outperform traditional range extenders because they create a unified network and manage roaming more effectively.

Improve device connectivity:

- Connect stationary, high-bandwidth devices (gaming consoles, desktop PCs, smart TVs) via Ethernet when possible.
- Reduce the number of unnecessary connected devices.

Diagnose before spending money:

- Run speed tests near the router and in problem areas.
- If speeds are poor even next to the router, the issue may be your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi coverage.
- Use a Wi‑Fi analyzer app to identify congested channels in dense apartment buildings.

A useful rule of thumb:
- Small apartment: a modern single router is often enough.
- Medium house (2,000–3,000 sq ft / 185–280 sq m): a high-quality router or 2-node mesh.
- Large or multi-story house: a 3+ node mesh system, ideally with wired Ethernet backhaul between nodes.
