How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what's causing the problem, but these are the most effective steps, roughly in order of impact:

- Move the router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets, behind TVs, or near large metal objects.
  - Position it closer to where you use devices most often.

- Reboot and update your equipment.
  - Restart the router and modem.
  - Install the latest firmware updates from the manufacturer.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band.
  - 5 GHz (or 6 GHz, if available) offers higher speeds but shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz offers longer range but more interference and lower speeds.

- Change Wi‑Fi channels.
  - Nearby networks can cause congestion.
  - For 2.4 GHz, channels 1, 6, or 11 are usually best.
  - Many routers can automatically choose the least crowded channel.

- Upgrade security and settings.
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 encryption.
  - Ensure your router isn't using outdated legacy modes that slow the network.

- Reduce interference.
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and Bluetooth-heavy devices when possible.

- Add more coverage if needed.
  - For larger homes, a mesh Wi‑Fi system often works better than a single router.
  - A Wi‑Fi extender can help, but mesh systems generally provide a better experience.

- Connect stationary devices with Ethernet.
  - PCs, gaming consoles, smart TVs, and streaming boxes perform better on wired connections.
  - This also frees wireless capacity for phones and tablets.

- Upgrade older hardware.
  - If your router is more than 5–7 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router may significantly improve performance.

- Check your internet plan.
  - If Wi‑Fi is working well but everything is still slow, your internet service itself may be the bottleneck.
