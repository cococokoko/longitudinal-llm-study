# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose your **hourly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **x** = number of hours worked per week
- **y** = hourly wage rate ($/hr)

The total weekly pay function is:

$$f(x, y) = x \cdot y + 0.5x^2 \cdot 0.1$$

But let's use a cleaner, realistic example for a hospital shift worker.

---

## The Function

Your **weekly fatigue-adjusted pay** is modeled as:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^2$$

where:
- **x** = overtime hours worked (beyond 36 hrs)
- **y** = base hourly wage ($)

---

## Compute the Partial Derivatives

### 1. Partial derivative with respect to **x** (holding y constant):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

### 2. Partial derivative with respect to **y** (holding x constant):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 2y$$

---

## Plug In Numbers

Suppose you work **x = 4** overtime hours at **y = $18/hr**:

| Partial Derivative | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x | 6(4) + 2(18) | **60** |
| ∂f/∂y | 2(4) + 2(18) | **44** |

---

## Interpretation

| Result | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **∂f/∂x = 60** | If you work **1 more overtime hour** (wage held at $18), your output measure increases by **60 units** |
| **∂f/∂y = 44** | If your **wage increases by $1** (overtime hours held at 4), your output measure increases by **44 units** |

---

## Key Concept

> A partial derivative measures the rate of change of a function with respect to **one variable at a time**, while **all other variables are held constant** — just like asking *"what happens to my pay if only my hours change, nothing else?"*
