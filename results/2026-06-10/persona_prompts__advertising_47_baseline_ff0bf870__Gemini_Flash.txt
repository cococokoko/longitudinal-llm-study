── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon is a monumental achievement that requires physical endurance, mental toughness, and smart strategy. To help you succeed, here is a comprehensive guide broken down into four phases: **Training, The Week Before, Race Day, and Post-Race.**

---

### Phase 1: Training (The Foundation)

The work you do in the months leading up to the race determines your success on race day.

*   **Respect the Long Run:** Your weekly long run is the most important workout. It builds cardiovascular endurance, strengthens muscles/joints, and teaches your body to burn fat for fuel.
*   **Follow the 10% Rule:** Never increase your weekly mileage or the length of your long run by more than 10% from the previous week. This is the golden rule for preventing injury.
*   **Practice Your Fueling:** Do not wait until race day to try energy gels, chews, or sports drinks. Use your long runs to experiment with different brands and timing (e.g., taking a gel every 30–45 minutes) to see what your stomach tolerates.
*   **Don’t Skip Rest Days:** Muscle recovery is when you actually get stronger. Sleep 7–8 hours a night, and incorporate active recovery (walking, swimming, yoga) on non-running days.
*   **Trust the Taper:** In the 2–3 weeks before the race, you will reduce your mileage. You might feel restless or "sluggish"—this is normal. Your body is storing energy and repairing micro-tears. Trust the process.

---

### Phase 2: The Week Before (Preparation)

This week is all about logistics, rest, and hydration.

*   **Carb-Loading (Done Right):** You don’t need to gorge yourself on pasta the night before (which will just make you feel bloated). Instead, gradually increase the *percentage* of carbohydrates in your meals starting 3 days before the race (rice, potatoes, oatmeal, pasta).
*   **Hydrate Early:** Hydration on Friday and Saturday matters more than what you drink on Sunday morning. Sip water and electrolyte drinks throughout the week.
*   **Nothing New on Race Day:** This is the golden rule of running. Do not wear new shoes, new socks, or a new shirt. Do not eat a new breakfast. Stick strictly to what worked during your training.
*   **Flat Runner:** Lay out all your gear the night before (shoes, bib pinned to your shirt, socks, gels, hat, watch). This prevents panic on race morning.
*   **Cut Your Toenails:** Clip them short a few days before the race to prevent them from bruising or falling off from hitting the front of your shoe.

---

### Phase 3: Race Day (Execution)

The atmosphere will be electric, but you must stay disciplined.

*   **Arrive Early:** Give yourself at least 60–90 minutes before the start to use the restroom (the lines are always long), check your bag, and warm up.
*   **Start Slow (The "Negative Split" Goal):** The biggest mistake runners make is letting adrenaline carry them too fast in the first 3–5 miles. You will pay for this later. Run the first half of the race slightly *slower* than your target pace. Save your energy for the second half.
*   **Chafe Prevention:** Apply Vaseline or Body Glide to areas prone to chafing (underarms, inner thighs, nipples for men, sports bra lines for women) before you start.
*   **Walk the Water Stations:** You don’t have to run through water stations. Walking for 10–15 seconds while you drink ensures you actually swallow the liquid rather than spilling it down your shirt.
*   **Chunk the Distance:** Don't think about 26.2 miles. Break the race into manageable segments: "Just get to 10 miles," "Now run a 10k," "Now it's just a 5k left."
*   **Prepare for "The Wall":** Around mile 20, your body’s glycogen stores will deplete, and your brain will tell you to stop. This is where mental toughness comes in. Dedicate miles 20–26 to loved ones, repeat a mantra (e.g., "I am strong, I am ready"), and focus on just putting one foot in front of the other.

---

### Phase 4: Post-Race (Recovery)

You crossed the finish line! Now, take care of your body.

*   **Keep Moving:** Do not immediately sit down. Walk for 10–15 minutes to let your heart rate come down gradually and prevent your muscles from seizing up.
*   **Get Warm:** Your body temperature will drop rapidly once you stop running. Put on the foil space blanket provided by the race, or change into dry clothes immediately.
*   **Refuel Quickly:** Eat a mix of carbohydrates and protein within 30–45 minutes of finishing (chocolate milk, a banana, a bagel, or a recovery shake) to kickstart muscle repair.
*   **Elevate and Ice:** Later in the day, lie down and put your legs up on a wall for 10 minutes to help drain pooled blood. Avoid hot baths on race day (they can increase inflammation); opt for a cool shower or ice bath instead.
*   **Celebrate:** You just ran 26.2 miles. Wear your medal proudly all weekend!
