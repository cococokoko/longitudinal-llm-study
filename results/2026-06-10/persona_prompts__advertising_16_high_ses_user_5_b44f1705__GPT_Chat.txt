Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stress and anxiety are common challenges in senior leadership roles because the demands are often continuous, high-stakes, and difficult to compartmentalize. The goal is usually not to eliminate stress entirely, but to keep it from degrading decision quality, health, relationships, and performance.

Some approaches that tend to be effective for executives:

- Separate "signal" from "noise"
  - Keep a short list of the 3–5 issues that genuinely require your attention.
  - Delegate, defer, or ignore items that create urgency without strategic importance.
  - Review this list daily to prevent reactive decision-making.

- Create decision boundaries
  - Avoid making important decisions when exhausted, angry, or under severe time pressure whenever possible.
  - Establish predefined criteria for recurring decisions to reduce cognitive load.

- Schedule recovery as deliberately as meetings
  - Protect time for exercise, sleep, and uninterrupted personal time.
  - Many executives treat recovery as optional, but it directly affects judgment and resilience.

- Manage information intake
  - Constant exposure to messages, dashboards, news, and internal updates can sustain anxiety.
  - Designate specific windows for email and communication rather than remaining continuously reactive.

- Use structured thinking for anxiety
  - When worried about a situation, write down:
    - What am I concerned about?
    - What evidence supports this concern?
    - What actions are available?
    - What is outside my control?
  - This often converts vague anxiety into concrete decisions.

- Build a trusted sounding board
  - A coach, mentor, peer executive, or therapist can help you process challenges that are difficult to discuss internally.
  - Isolation often amplifies stress.

- Watch for signs that stress is becoming harmful
  - Persistent sleep disruption
  - Irritability
  - Difficulty concentrating
  - Increased alcohol or substance use
  - Physical symptoms such as headaches, digestive issues, or elevated blood pressure
  - Loss of enjoyment in work or life

- Consider professional support
  - If anxiety is frequent, intense, or affecting your functioning, a licensed mental health professional can provide evidence-based tools that are highly effective for many high-performing leaders.
