── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Starting a new role is an exciting time, and thinking about retirement savings right now is incredibly smart. 

Being on probation introduces a few unique constraints (like waiting periods for company benefits), but it shouldn't stop you from saving. Here is a step-by-step guide on the best ways to save for retirement, tailored to your current situation.

---

### Phase 1: What to Do *Right Now* (During Probation)

While you are on probation, you may not yet be eligible for your employer’s retirement plan (like a 401k or pension). Here is how you can start saving immediately on your own:

#### 1. Open an IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
An IRA is a retirement account you open on your own through a brokerage (like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Charles Schwab). You don't need an employer to do this.
*   **Traditional IRA:** You contribute pre-tax or tax-deductible dollars. Your money grows tax-deferred, and you pay taxes when you withdraw it in retirement.
*   **Roth IRA:** You contribute after-tax dollars. Your money grows **100% tax-free**, and your withdrawals in retirement are also tax-free. 
    *   *Recommendation:* If you are young or in a lower tax bracket now than you expect to be in retirement, a **Roth IRA** is usually the best starting point.
    *   *Limit:* In 2024, you can contribute up to $7,000 per year (or $8,000 if you are 50 or older).

#### 2. Build a "Probation Emergency Fund"
Before aggressively investing, ensure you have a cash cushion. Being on probation means you have less job security. 
*   Keep **3 to 6 months of living expenses** in a **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)**. Currently, HYSAs are paying around 4-5% interest. 
*   If the job doesn't work out, this fund ensures you won't have to dip into retirement accounts (which carry heavy penalties for early withdrawal).

---

### Phase 2: What to Do *After* Probation (Once Benefits Kick In)

Once you pass probation, you will likely gain access to your employer’s retirement benefits. This is where you should shift your focus.

#### 3. Capture the Employer Match (The "Free Money")
If your employer offers a 401(k), 403(b), or similar plan with a **matching contribution**, this is your absolute highest priority.
*   *How it works:* If your employer matches 100% of your contributions up to 4% of your salary, you should contribute at least 4%. 
*   *Why:* This is an instant 100% return on your money. Never turn down free money.

#### 4. Understand the "Vesting Schedule"
Since you are new, look at the company's **vesting schedule**. 
*   Your contributions to a 401(k) are always 100% yours.
*   The *employer’s* matching contributions may "vest" over time (e.g., 20% per year of service). If you leave the job before you are fully vested, you may lose some or all of the employer's match. Keep this in mind if you don't plan on staying long-term.

---

### Phase 3: How to Invest the Money

Putting money into a retirement account is only step one; you must then **invest** that money so it grows. If you leave it in cash, inflation will eat its value.

*   **The Easy Route: Target Date Funds (TDFs)**
    *   Choose a fund with the year closest to when you plan to retire (e.g., "Target Date 2060"). 
    *   The fund automatically manages your investments, starting out aggressive (mostly stocks) when you are young, and becoming conservative (more bonds) as you get closer to retirement.
*   **The DIY Route: Index Funds**
    *   Invest in low-cost index funds that track the total stock market (like an S&P 500 index fund). These have very low fees (expense ratios) and historically perform very well over the long term.

---

### Summary: The "Order of Operations" for Saving

To maximize your savings, follow this priority list:

1.  **Now:** Put money into a High-Yield Savings Account until you have a comfortable emergency fund.
2.  **Now:** Open a Roth IRA and start contributing what you can.
3.  **Post-Probation:** Contribute to your employer’s 401(k) *just enough* to get the maximum company match.
4.  **Next:** Go back to your Roth IRA and try to max it out ($7,000/year).
5.  **Finally:** If you still have money to save, go back to your 401(k) and increase your contributions there.

**Pro-Tip:** Set up **automatic transfers** on your payday. If the money goes straight to your savings or IRA before you see it in your checking account, you won't miss it!
