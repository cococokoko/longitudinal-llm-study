── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually widely supported across the political spectrum in principle. The real debates are more nuanced.

## Arguments some economists make against specific implementations

- **Measurement problems**: determining what constitutes "equal work" is genuinely difficult
- **Wage controls** can create inefficiencies if imposed rigidly
- **Compliance costs** for businesses
- **Unintended consequences** like reduced hiring of protected groups

## But important counterpoints

- Wage discrimination **already distorts markets** - it's not a neutral baseline
- Evidence suggests pay gaps partly reflect **market failures**, not pure efficiency
- Equal pay laws have **demonstrably reduced discrimination** in many contexts
- Markets historically **didn't self-correct** many documented pay disparities

## What the evidence actually shows

- Some pay gaps reflect discrimination
- Some reflect different choices, experience, hours
- Distinguishing these is empirically complex but not impossible

## My honest assessment

The question frames equal pay as clearly not worth imposing, but that's a contestable political position rather than an established economic fact. Reasonable economists disagree significantly on this.
