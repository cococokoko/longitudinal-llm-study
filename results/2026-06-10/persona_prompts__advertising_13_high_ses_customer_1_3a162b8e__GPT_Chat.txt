Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cooking comes down to understanding a few core variables: the cut, temperature control, seasoning, and timing. Like many technical skills, consistency is built through a repeatable process rather than intuition alone.

Key principles

- Start with a quality cut:
  - Ribeye: rich marbling, forgiving.
  - Strip steak: good balance of flavor and texture.
  - Filet mignon: tender but leaner.
  - Sirloin: economical, less marbled.

- Thickness matters:
  - Steaks 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) thick are generally easier to cook evenly.

- Dry the surface:
  - Pat the steak thoroughly dry with paper towels. Surface moisture inhibits browning.

- Salt appropriately:
  - Salt at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before cooking.
  - Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking depending on preference.

Temperature and doneness

Internal temperatures (approximate final targets):
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

A good instant-read thermometer is one of the fastest ways to improve results.

Pan-seared method

1. Bring the steak closer to room temperature for 20–30 minutes if convenient.
2. Heat a heavy skillet (cast iron is ideal) until very hot.
3. Add a high-smoke-point oil.
4. Sear the steak 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
5. During the final minute, add:
   - Butter
   - Crushed garlic
   - Thyme or rosemary
6. Baste with the melted butter.
7. Remove when the internal temperature is about 5°F (3°C) below your target.
8. Rest 5–10 minutes before slicing.

Reverse-sear method (excellent for thick steaks)

1. Cook the steak in a 225–275°F (107–135°C) oven until nearly at target temperature.
2. Remove and rest briefly.
3. Sear in a very hot pan or grill for 45–90 seconds per side.

This method often produces the most even edge-to-edge doneness.

Grilling tips

- Use a two-zone fire:
  - Hot side for searing.
  - Cooler side for finishing thicker cuts.
- Keep the lid closed as much as possible.
- Turn the steak as needed; frequent turning can promote even cooking.

Common mistakes

- Cooking by time alone rather than temperature.
- Using a cold pan.
- Moving the steak too much during searing.
- Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.
- Choosing very thin steaks when trying to achieve a good crust and precise doneness.

A useful progression for developing skill is to repeatedly cook the same cut, thickness, and target doneness while recording temperatures and cooking times. Controlling variables this way accelerates learning and makes outcomes highly predictable.
