Improving a credit score quickly depends on what's currently affecting it, but some actions can produce results within a few weeks to a few months.

Here are the highest-impact steps:

- Pay down credit card balances
  - Credit utilization (how much of your available credit you're using) is a major factor.
  - Aim to keep balances below 30% of each card's limit, and ideally below 10%.
  - If you can make multiple payments throughout the month, that can help reduce reported balances.

- Make every payment on time
  - Payment history is one of the most important credit score factors.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders to avoid missing due dates while managing work and childcare responsibilities.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Obtain your credit reports from the major credit bureaus available in your country.
  - Dispute any incorrect late payments, accounts that aren't yours, or inaccurate balances.
  - Correcting errors can sometimes lead to relatively fast score improvements.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If your income and payment history support it, a higher limit can lower your utilization ratio.
  - Ask whether the lender will perform a soft inquiry or a hard inquiry before requesting the increase.

- Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts
  - New applications can generate hard inquiries and lower the average age of your accounts.
  - If you're trying to improve your score quickly, limit new credit applications unless necessary.

- Bring past-due accounts current
  - If any accounts are behind, catching them up can prevent further damage and improve your standing over time.

- Become an authorized user (if appropriate)
  - If a trusted family member has a long-standing account with low balances and excellent payment history, being added as an authorized user may help, depending on the lender and credit scoring model.

For a busy single parent, a simple routine can be effective:
- Turn on autopay for at least the minimum payment.
- Schedule one monthly 15-minute review of your accounts.
- Direct any extra funds toward reducing the balance on the card with the highest utilization.
