── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is there life on other planets?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is: **We do not know yet, but the scientific consensus is that it is highly likely.**

To date, we have not found definitive, universally accepted evidence of life beyond Earth. However, the search is one of the most active and exciting areas of modern science. 

Here is a breakdown of where we stand, why scientists are optimistic, and where we are looking.

---

### 1. The Argument for Optimism: The Numbers Game
The primary reason scientists believe life exists elsewhere is sheer mathematics.
* **Trillions of Planets:** There are an estimated 100 billion to 400 billion stars in our galaxy (the Milky Way) alone, and billions of galaxies in the observable universe. 
* **The "Goldilocks" Zone:** NASA’s Kepler space telescope revealed that about 20% to 50% of the stars visible in the night sky likely have small, potentially rocky planets in their "habitable zone"—the distance from a star where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface.
* **Common Ingredients:** The chemical building blocks of life (carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus, and sulfur) are among the most common elements in the universe.

Given these numbers, the probability that Earth is the *only* planet to have ever developed life is incredibly small.

---

### 2. Where We Are Looking (Our Solar System)
We don’t have to look to other star systems to find potential life. There are several candidates in our own cosmic backyard:

* **Mars:** Mars was once warm, wet, and had a thick atmosphere. NASA’s Perseverance and Curiosity rovers are currently searching for signs of ancient microbial life (fossils) in dried-up lakebeds.
* **Europa (Jupiter’s Moon):** Europa is covered in a thick crust of ice, but beneath that ice lies a global liquid-water ocean containing more water than all of Earth's oceans combined. Hydrothermal vents at the bottom of this ocean could provide the heat and chemistry needed for life.
* **Enceladus (Saturn’s Moon):** Like Europa, Enceladus has a subsurface ocean. Spacecraft have actually flown through geysers of water blasting out of its ice crust and detected organic molecules, salts, and silica, suggesting a habitable environment.
* **Titan (Saturn’s Moon):** Titan has a thick atmosphere and liquid lakes on its surface. However, these lakes are made of liquid methane and ethane, not water. If life exists there, it would be radically different from life on Earth.

---

### 3. How We Are Searching (Beyond Our Solar System)
For planets orbiting other stars (exoplanets), we cannot send probes yet. Instead, we use advanced telescopes like the **James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)**.
* **Biosignatures:** Scientists look at the light filtering through the atmospheres of distant planets. They are searching for "biosignatures"—gases like oxygen, ozone, methane, and carbon dioxide in combinations that would suggest the presence of living organisms.
* **SETI (Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence):** This initiative listens for artificial radio or optical signals coming from deep space, which would indicate the presence of an advanced, technological civilization. So far, we have heard only silence.

---

### 4. The Fermi Paradox: "Where is everybody?"
If life is likely, why haven't we found it? This contradiction is known as the **Fermi Paradox**. Possible explanations include:
* **Space is too big:** The distances between stars are so vast that communication or travel is incredibly difficult and takes thousands of years.
* **We’ve just started looking:** Humans have only had the technology to detect radio signals or exoplanets for a few decades—a blink of an eye in cosmic time.
* **Life is common, but intelligent life is rare:** Simple microbial life might be everywhere, but the evolution of intelligent, technology-using species might be an extremely rare fluke.

### Conclusion
While we have **no proof** yet, we are closer to finding an answer than ever before. Many astrobiologists believe we may find evidence of microbial life (either on Mars or the icy moons of the outer solar system) within the next few decades.
