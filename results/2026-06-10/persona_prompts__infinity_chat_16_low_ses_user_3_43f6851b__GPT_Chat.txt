  - Communication
  - Leadership
  - Negotiation
  - Creativity
  - Ethical judgment
  - Building trust with customers and colleagues
  - These are areas where automation remains less reliable and where organizations often derive significant value.

- Career paths may become less linear.
  - Workers may change roles, industries, or specialties more frequently.
  - Continuous learning is likely to become a normal part of professional life rather than something reserved for occasional retraining.

- Productivity gains may create new types of work.
  - Previous technological shifts eliminated some jobs but also created entirely new occupations that were difficult to predict beforehand.
  - AI-related governance, human-AI collaboration design, data stewardship, and new service industries are examples of emerging areas.

- There may be economic and social challenges.
  - Some occupations could face significant disruption.
  - Wage inequality could increase if the benefits of automation are concentrated among a small number of firms or workers.
  - Governments and organizations may need to address issues such as retraining, labor-market transitions, and social safety nets.

For someone early in a new role, a practical approach is often:
- Learn your core job well.
- Identify which parts of the work are becoming automated.
- Become proficient with the tools driving that automation.
- Develop skills involving judgment, communication, and decision-making.
- Build a reputation for adaptability and learning.
