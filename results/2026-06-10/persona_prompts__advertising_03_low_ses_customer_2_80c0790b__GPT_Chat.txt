Working from home can be productive, but it often requires more structure than working in an office—especially when you're balancing work responsibilities with caregiving.

Some approaches that tend to help:

- Create a "minimum viable schedule"
  - Identify your most important work tasks for the day.
  - Block dedicated focus periods around times when interruptions are least likely.
  - Keep the schedule flexible enough to accommodate unexpected needs.

- Prioritize outcomes over hours
  - Choose 1–3 high-impact tasks each day.
  - Complete those before spending time on lower-priority work such as routine email or administrative tasks.

- Use time blocking
  - Reserve specific periods for focused work, meetings, email, and household responsibilities.
  - Even short blocks of 25–45 minutes can be effective.

- Reduce context switching
  - Batch similar tasks together.
  - Check email and messaging platforms at scheduled times instead of continuously.

- Create a dedicated workspace if possible
  - A separate room is ideal, but even a consistent corner of a room can help signal "work mode."
  - Keep frequently used materials within reach.

- Build interruption-friendly systems
  - Keep a running task list so you can quickly resume work after being interrupted.
  - Write down your next step before stepping away from a task.

- Communicate availability clearly
  - Share your working hours and response expectations with colleagues.
  - Use calendar blocks or status indicators when you're focusing.

- Use technology selectively
  - Task managers, calendar reminders, and focus timers can help.
  - Avoid adopting too many tools at once.

- Protect your energy
  - Schedule demanding work during your peak concentration hours.
  - Take short breaks to maintain focus throughout the day.

- Review and adjust weekly
  - Spend 10–15 minutes each week identifying what worked and what created friction.
  - Make small improvements rather than overhauling your entire routine.

A simple daily framework could look like:
- Morning: Most important task
- Midday: Meetings and communication
- Afternoon: Secondary tasks and follow-ups
- End of day: Plan tomorrow's priorities

If you share what specifically is affecting your productivity—distractions, time management, childcare interruptions, motivation, meetings, or something else—I can offer more targeted suggestions.
