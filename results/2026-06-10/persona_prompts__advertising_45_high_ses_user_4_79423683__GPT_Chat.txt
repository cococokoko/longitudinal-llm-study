── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you're already accustomed to learning complex systems, evaluating evidence, and developing procedural skills. DIY home repair is a different domain, but many of the same learning principles apply.

A structured approach works better than randomly watching repair videos.

1. Learn the house as a system

Start with a broad understanding of:
- Structural components (foundation, framing, roof)
- Electrical systems
- Plumbing systems
- HVAC
- Insulation and moisture control
- Interior finishes (drywall, flooring, paint)

Good resources:
- "How Your House Works" by Charlie Wing
- "The Visual Handbook of Building and Remodeling" by Charlie Wing
- Family Handyman website
- This Old House website and YouTube channel

2. Build a core tool set and learn tool use

Learn:
- Measuring and layout
- Drilling and driving fasteners
- Cutting wood
- Finding studs and utilities
- Basic electrical testing
- Plumbing shutoffs and connections

Core tools:
- Tape measure
- Cordless drill/driver
- Stud finder
- Level
- Utility knife
- Hammer
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Multimeter
- Flashlight/headlamp

Focus initially on safe, accurate tool handling rather than acquiring many tools.

3. Follow a progression of projects

Begin with low-risk tasks:
- Caulking
- Painting
- Replacing door hardware
- Installing shelves
- Replacing faucets
- Weatherstripping
- Patching drywall

Intermediate:
- Installing light fixtures
- Replacing outlets and switches
- Minor trim carpentry
- Toilet replacement
- Garbage disposal installation

Advanced:
- Flooring installation
- Building cabinets
- Deck repairs
- Significant plumbing modifications

Avoid major structural, roofing, gas, or service-panel electrical work until you have substantial experience.

4. Use apprenticeship-style learning

The fastest path is often:
- Help a skilled friend on projects
- Hire contractors and observe
- Ask tradespeople questions
- Volunteer with organizations such as Habitat for Humanity

Many physicians learn procedural skills efficiently through supervised repetition; DIY is similar.

5. Learn building science

Many DIY failures come from misunderstanding moisture, ventilation, and heat transfer rather than poor workmanship.

Look into:
- Building Science Corporation resources
- Green Building Advisor
- Fine Homebuilding

Understanding moisture management can prevent expensive mistakes.

6. Develop a "diagnosis before treatment" mindset

Your medical training gives you an advantage.

When something breaks:
- Define the problem precisely
- Identify possible causes
- Gather evidence
- Test hypotheses
- Repair only after understanding the failure mechanism

For example, a ceiling stain may indicate:
- Roof leak
- Plumbing leak
- Condensation
- HVAC issue

The visible symptom is not necessarily the root cause.

7. Learn local codes

Read:
- Local permitting requirements
- Electrical code summaries
- Plumbing code summaries

Even when doing DIY work, code knowledge helps ensure safety and resale compliance.

8. Build a reference library

Useful sources:
- This Old House
- Fine Homebuilding
- Family Handyman
- Essential Craftsman (YouTube)
- Home Renovision DIY (YouTube)
- The Honest Carpenter (YouTube)
- See Jane Drill (YouTube)

When learning a task:
- Read one article
- Watch 2–3 experienced tradespeople perform it
- Compare approaches before starting

9. Practice on noncritical projects

Create opportunities to make mistakes cheaply:
- Build a workbench
- Construct garage shelving
- Repair drywall in a utility area
- Install trim in a closet

This is analogous to simulation before operating on patients.

10. Keep a homeowner's logbook

Document:
- Shutoff locations
- Paint colors
- Filter sizes
- Appliance models
- Repairs completed
- Contractor contacts
- Photos before walls are closed

Over time, you'll build a detailed understanding of your home's infrastructure.

For someone with a demanding academic career, a particularly efficient strategy is to choose one project every month or two and learn deeply from it. Within a year, you'll acquire competence in the most common household repairs without needing to become an expert carpenter, electrician, or plumber.
