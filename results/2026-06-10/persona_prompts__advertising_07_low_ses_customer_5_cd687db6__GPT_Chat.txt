── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Effective ways to reduce household energy use.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reducing household energy use can lower utility bills and reduce environmental impact. If you're managing a busy household or supporting extended family members, small changes across many areas can add up significantly.

Some effective strategies:

• Improve heating and cooling efficiency
  - Set thermostats a few degrees lower in winter and higher in summer.
  - Use programmable or smart thermostats to avoid heating or cooling an empty home.
  - Replace or clean HVAC filters regularly.
  - Seal drafts around doors, windows, and vents.
  - Use ceiling fans to improve air circulation.

• Reduce water-heating costs
  - Lower the water heater temperature to a safe, efficient setting (often around 120°F / 49°C).
  - Install low-flow showerheads and faucet aerators.
  - Fix leaking faucets promptly.
  - Wash clothes in cold water when appropriate.

• Use appliances efficiently
  - Run dishwashers and washing machines with full loads.
  - Air-dry clothes when possible.
  - Clean refrigerator coils and ensure door seals are tight.
  - Replace older appliances with energy-efficient models when it's time to upgrade.

• Cut lighting energy use
  - Switch to LED bulbs.
  - Turn off lights in unused rooms.
  - Make use of natural daylight whenever possible.
  - Install motion sensors in infrequently used areas.

• Reduce standby power consumption
  - Unplug devices that aren't being used.
  - Use smart power strips for electronics and entertainment systems.
  - Enable energy-saving settings on computers, TVs, and other devices.

• Improve insulation
  - Add insulation in attics, walls, or crawl spaces if needed.
  - Use insulated curtains or shades.
  - Close blinds during hot days to reduce cooling needs.

• Kitchen energy savings
  - Keep refrigerator and freezer temperatures at recommended levels.
  - Avoid frequently opening refrigerator doors.
  - Use microwave ovens, pressure cookers, or air fryers when suitable, as they often use less energy than a full-size oven.

• Monitor energy use
  - Review utility bills to identify trends.
  - If available, use a home energy monitor or utility app to track consumption.
  - Conduct a home energy audit, many of which are offered by utilities at low cost or free.

For larger households, it can also help to create simple shared habits—such as turning off unused lights, limiting unnecessary heating or cooling, and running full laundry loads—to spread energy-saving efforts across everyone in the home.
