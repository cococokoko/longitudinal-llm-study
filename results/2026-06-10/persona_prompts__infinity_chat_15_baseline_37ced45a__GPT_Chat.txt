── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities shape international relations in many ways because differences in wealth, resources, technology, and economic power influence how states interact with one another.

Some key effects include:

• Power and influence
  - Wealthier countries often have greater political and diplomatic influence in international organizations such as the United Nations, World Bank, and International Monetary Fund (IMF).
  - Economic strength allows states to use tools such as foreign aid, investment, sanctions, and trade agreements to advance their interests.

• Trade relationships
  - Poorer countries often depend on exporting raw materials or low-cost labor, while wealthier countries tend to dominate higher-value industries and advanced technologies.
  - This can create unequal trade relationships where benefits are distributed unevenly.

• Development assistance and debt
  - Developing countries may rely on loans, aid, and investment from wealthier nations and international institutions.
  - While these resources can support development, they can also create dependencies and give lenders influence over domestic policies.

• Global governance
  - Economic inequality affects whose interests are represented in international decision-making.
  - Wealthier countries generally have more resources to shape global rules on trade, finance, climate policy, and security.

• Migration
  - Large differences in living standards encourage migration from poorer to wealthier countries.
  - Migration can create economic benefits but may also become a source of political tension and debate over borders, labor markets, and national identity.

• Conflict and instability
  - Severe poverty and economic inequality can contribute to political instability, social unrest, and conflict within states.
  - These issues can spill across borders, affecting regional and international security.

• Competition for resources
  - Countries may compete for access to energy, minerals, food supplies, and strategic trade routes.
  - Wealthier states often have greater capacity to secure these resources through investment, diplomacy, or military presence.

• Climate change and environmental politics
  - Economic inequalities influence debates about responsibility for environmental damage and the costs of addressing climate change.
  - Developing countries often argue that wealthier nations, which historically produced more emissions, should bear a larger share of the burden.

• Rise of emerging economies
  - As countries such as China, India, Brazil, and others increase their economic power, they gain greater influence in international affairs.
  - This can shift global power balances and challenge institutions historically dominated by Western countries.

Different theories of international relations interpret these dynamics differently. Realists emphasize how economic power supports national power and security. Liberals focus on trade, cooperation, and institutions. Marxist and dependency theorists argue that global economic inequalities are built into the structure of the world economy and help maintain unequal relationships between richer and poorer countries.

Overall, global economic inequalities shape who has influence, how resources are distributed, which countries set international rules, and how states cooperate or compete on the world stage.
