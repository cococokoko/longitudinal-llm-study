── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different Christian traditions and theologians understand discipleship in somewhat different ways, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and living according to his teachings. Here are some major views:

1. Discipleship as Following Jesus Personally
- Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus.
- Focuses on faith, prayer, Bible reading, and obedience.
- Common in many evangelical and non-denominational churches.
- Key idea: A disciple is someone who actively follows Christ in everyday life.

2. Discipleship as Spiritual Formation
- Sees discipleship as a lifelong process of becoming more like Christ.
- Emphasizes character transformation, spiritual disciplines, and inner growth.
- Found in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, and many Protestant traditions.
- Key idea: The goal is not merely learning about Jesus but being transformed into Christlikeness.

3. Discipleship as Apprenticeship
- Popularized by writers such as Dallas Willard and John Mark Comer.
- Views discipleship as learning to live as Jesus lived.
- Includes being with Jesus, becoming like Jesus, and doing what Jesus did.
- Key idea: A disciple is an apprentice who learns from a master.

4. Discipleship as Participation in the Church
- Emphasizes the role of the Christian community.
- Growth occurs through worship, sacraments, service, and fellowship.
- Strongly emphasized in Catholic, Orthodox, and some Protestant traditions.
- Key idea: One cannot fully be a disciple apart from the body of Christ.

5. Discipleship as Mission
- Focuses on evangelism, social action, and spreading the gospel.
- Draws heavily from the Great Commission (Matthew 28:18–20).
- Common in missionary movements and many evangelical churches.
- Key idea: Disciples make disciples.

6. Discipleship as Radical Commitment
- Emphasizes sacrifice, obedience, and costly following of Jesus.
- Associated with figures such as Dietrich Bonhoeffer and Anabaptist traditions.
- May stress simplicity, peacemaking, and willingness to suffer for faith.
- Key idea: Following Jesus requires total commitment.

7. Discipleship as Liberation and Justice
- Found in liberation theology, Black theology, and some progressive Christian movements.
- Emphasizes Jesus' concern for the poor, oppressed, and marginalized.
- Sees discipleship as participation in God's work of justice and liberation.
- Key idea: Following Jesus includes confronting social and systemic injustice.

8. Discipleship as Theosis (Eastern Orthodox View)
- Theosis means participation in the divine life through God's grace.
- Discipleship is a process of union with God while remaining fully human.
- Emphasizes prayer, worship, ascetic practices, and sacramental life.
- Key idea: The goal of discipleship is communion with God and transformation into Christ's likeness.

Areas of agreement:
- Jesus is the model and teacher.
- Discipleship involves learning and obedience.
- Growth is expected over time.
- Community is important, though traditions differ on how central it is.
- Disciples are called to love God and neighbor.

Areas of disagreement:
- The role of the church and sacraments.
- Whether discipleship is primarily personal, communal, or societal.
- The balance between evangelism and social justice.
- The extent to which discipleship requires radical separation from mainstream culture.

These views are often complementary rather than mutually exclusive. Many churches combine elements from several of them.
