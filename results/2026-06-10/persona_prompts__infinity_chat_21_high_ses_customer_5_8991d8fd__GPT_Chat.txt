Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty generally do so for a few major reasons. The strength of these arguments depends on one's moral, legal, and empirical assumptions, but the most common arguments include:

- Retributive justice ("just deserts")
  - Some believe that certain crimes, especially premeditated murder or acts of terrorism, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - This view holds that punishment should reflect the seriousness of the offense, not merely serve practical goals such as rehabilitation.
  - Supporters argue that failing to impose the highest penalty for the worst crimes can undermine justice.

- Deterrence
  - Some argue that the possibility of execution discourages potential offenders from committing murder or other capital crimes.
  - Even if the deterrent effect is debated, supporters contend that a sufficiently severe punishment may prevent at least some crimes.
  - They often argue that the death penalty sends a strong societal signal about the consequences of certain acts.

- Incapacitation and public safety
  - Execution permanently prevents a convicted offender from committing further crimes.
  - Supporters note that people serving life sentences have, in some cases, committed violence against prison staff, other inmates, or escaped custody.
  - From this perspective, execution removes any future risk posed by the offender.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Some believe the death penalty acknowledges the gravity of the harm suffered by victims and their families.
  - They argue that the punishment can provide a sense of closure or moral accountability.
  - This argument is often rooted in the idea that society has an obligation to respond decisively to extreme wrongdoing.

- Moral accountability and personal responsibility
  - Supporters may argue that individuals who knowingly commit especially heinous crimes should bear the full consequences of their actions.
  - In this view, respecting human agency includes holding people accountable for deliberate choices.

- Social contract theory
  - Some political philosophers have argued that by committing certain severe crimes, offenders violate the basic rules that make civil society possible.
  - Under this reasoning, individuals can forfeit some of the rights normally protected by the state, including, in some views, the right to life.

- Protection of societal order
  - Supporters sometimes contend that a legal system must demonstrate its capacity to respond proportionately to the most serious offenses.
  - They argue that capital punishment can reinforce public confidence that justice is being carried out.

- Cost arguments (in some jurisdictions)
  - Some supporters believe that executing offenders is less costly than housing them for life.
  - In practice, this argument is heavily disputed because death penalty cases often involve lengthy appeals and higher legal costs.

It is worth noting that many of these arguments are contested. Opponents often respond by citing the risk of wrongful convictions, concerns about unequal application of the law, questions about deterrence evidence, human rights principles, and the availability of life imprisonment without parole. The debate ultimately involves both empirical questions (what effects the policy has) and moral questions (what justice requires).
