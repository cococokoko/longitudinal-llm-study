── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common, yet highly sensitive situation. Because you are working long, physically demanding hours in a warehouse, your hard-earned money represents a massive amount of physical labor and time spent on your feet. It is completely reasonable that you want to protect your financial well-being while also being a supportive partner.

Since you are not married yet, the "best" solution is one that **protects both of you legally and financially right now**, while allowing room for your finances to merge smoothly once you do get married. 

Here are the three best ways to handle this, ranging from the safest legal option to a more collaborative approach.

---

### Option 1: The "Tenant-Landlord" Model (Safest & Simplest)
Since she is buying the house in her name before you are married, the cleanest approach is for **her to cover the down payment and the mortgage payments, while you pay her "rent" or cover other living expenses.**

*   **How it works:** She is the sole owner. You pay a agreed-upon monthly amount that goes toward utilities, groceries, or a portion of the mortgage. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** You are not investing your hard-earned savings into an asset you don't legally own. If the relationship were to end before the wedding, she keeps her house, and you simply move out without having lost a massive lump sum of capital. 
*   **The Warehouse Worker Perspective:** Your body takes a beating at work to earn your paycheck. Paying a reasonable "rent" keeps your housing costs predictable and protects your savings from being tied up in a property you have no legal claim to.

### Option 2: The "Co-Ownership" Model (If you want to buy it *together*)
If you want to be a true co-owner from day one, you should not just "pay for the house" informally. You need to be on the deed and the mortgage.

*   **How it works:** You both put money toward the down payment and both of your names go on the title. You should have a real estate attorney draft a **Cohabitation Agreement**. This document outlines exactly who gets what if you split up (e.g., "If we split, the house is sold, and Partner A gets their $20k down payment back, Partner B gets their $10k back, and we split the remaining equity 50/50").
*   **Why it’s fair:** It legally protects your financial contribution. You aren't just giving her money; you are buying a share of the asset.

### Option 3: The "Post-Marriage Transition" Model (The Compromise)
You treat the house as *hers* for the next year. Once you get married, you transition it to *ours*.

*   **How it works:** 
    *   **Year 1 (Before Marriage):** She pays the mortgage and down payment. You pay for groceries, utilities, and maybe a small, agreed-upon amount of rent. 
    *   **Year 2 (After Marriage):** Once you are legally married, you can refinance the mortgage to put your name on it, or draft a legal agreement adding you to the deed. At that point, you begin contributing fully to the mortgage payments, and the home becomes a shared marital asset.
*   **Why it’s fair:** It keeps things clean while you are dating, but acknowledges that you are building a life together once you take your vows.

---

### Crucial Rules of Thumb for Your Situation:

1.  **Never pay for major renovations on a house you don't own.** If she wants to remodel the kitchen or put on a new roof before you are married, she should pay for it. If you split up, you cannot take a new kitchen with you, and you won't get that money back.
2.  **Do not put down payment money toward a house if your name is not on the deed.** If you write a check for $10,000 toward her down payment and your name isn't on the paperwork, legally, that money is considered a "gift" in many jurisdictions. If you break up, you have no legal right to get it back.
3.  **Value your labor.** If you are working 10-12 hour shifts on your feet, your recovery time is precious. If she expects you to do heavy DIY renovations on the house because "you live there too," that needs to be factored into your financial agreement. Your physical labor has monetary value.
