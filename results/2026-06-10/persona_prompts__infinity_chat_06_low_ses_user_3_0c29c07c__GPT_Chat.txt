── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job, especially while on probation, can make it surprisingly hard to tell what you actually want. You're learning the role, trying to make a good impression, adjusting to a new environment, and dealing with uncertainty. In that situation, it's common for your immediate concerns ("Can I succeed here?") to drown out your deeper preferences ("Do I actually want this?").

A useful approach is to separate three different questions:

- What do I feel right now?
- What do I want in the short term?
- What do I want in the longer term?

For example:
- Right now: "I want to get through probation successfully."
- Short term (6–12 months): "I want to build confidence and learn the role."
- Longer term (2–5 years): "I want work that is intellectually challenging, financially rewarding, flexible, meaningful, or whatever matters most to me."

You can also gather evidence from your daily experience. For a few weeks, notice:
- When do you feel energized at work?
- When do you feel drained?
- Which tasks make time pass quickly?
- Which tasks do you procrastinate on?
- Which colleagues' jobs make you think, "That looks interesting"?
- Which aspects of the job are you tolerating versus genuinely enjoying?

Try writing down brief observations rather than forcing a conclusion. Patterns often emerge before clarity does.

Another helpful distinction is:
- "What do I want?" versus
- "What am I afraid of?"

People sometimes think they want to leave a job when they're actually anxious about being new. Others think they want to stay because leaving feels risky, even though the work isn't a good fit. Understanding whether a feeling is attraction or avoidance can clarify things.

You don't necessarily need a definitive answer immediately. Since you're still new, it may be enough to ask:
- "What information do I need to learn before I can make a good judgment about this job?"

Often, after a few months, you'll have a much clearer sense of whether the discomfort comes from being a beginner or from a genuine mismatch.
