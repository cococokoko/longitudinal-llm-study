There isn't a single "best" solution, because it depends on your finances, your relationship, and how you want to handle ownership and risk. The key principle is that money contributed and ownership should be aligned and explicitly agreed upon before you move in.

A few common approaches:

- She buys the house herself, and you pay rent.
  - She remains the sole owner.
  - You contribute a fair monthly amount toward housing costs, similar to what you would pay elsewhere.
  - This is often the simplest arrangement when one partner buys before marriage.
  - Be clear whether your payment is rent, a contribution to utilities, or something else.

- You contribute to expenses but not the mortgage principal.
  - For example, split utilities, groceries, insurance, maintenance, and perhaps some housing costs.
  - She builds equity because she owns the house.
  - This avoids ambiguity about ownership interests.

- You buy into the house.
  - If you're contributing significantly to the down payment or mortgage, you could receive a defined ownership stake.
  - This should be documented legally.
  - The ownership percentage should reflect actual contributions and any future agreements.

- Wait until marriage and then revisit ownership.
  - If marriage is likely within a year, some couples keep the purchase separate for now and decide later whether to retitle the property or make other arrangements.

Questions to discuss together:
- If you broke up before marriage, what outcome would feel fair?
- If the house appreciates in value, who should benefit?
- Will you be contributing to the down payment, renovations, maintenance, or mortgage?
- Do both of you view your contributions as rent, shared living expenses, or investment?
