── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At an executive level, "What do I want?" is often harder than "What should I do?" because success creates layers of obligations, expectations, incentives, and identities that can obscure personal preference.

One useful distinction is between:
- What you want
- What you have trained yourself to want
- What others want from you
- What your current environment rewards

These can diverge significantly.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Examine recurring envy.
  - Envy is often informative. Notice the people whose careers or lives provoke a strong reaction.
  - Ask: "What specifically do I wish I had?" Freedom? Impact? Recognition? Craft mastery? Simplicity? Time with family?
  - The object of envy is often less important than the quality it represents.

- Study your moments of aliveness.
  - Look back over the past few years.
  - Which days left you energized rather than merely satisfied?
  - Were you building, leading, negotiating, teaching, creating, investing, learning, mentoring, competing, or solving difficult problems?
  - Patterns matter more than isolated events.

- Separate goals from motives.
  - "I want to become CEO" is a goal.
  - The motive might be influence, achievement, security, status, or autonomy.
  - Different goals can satisfy the same motive.
  - Understanding the motive creates more options.

- Imagine success is guaranteed.
  - If you knew you could not fail, what would you pursue?
  - Then imagine recognition is impossible.
  - What would you pursue if nobody would know you had done it?
  - The gap between those answers is often revealing.

- Imagine your current position disappears.
  - If your title, compensation, and reputation vanished tomorrow, what would you rebuild?
  - What would you not bother rebuilding?
  - This can expose which parts of your life are genuinely valued versus inherited.

- Pay attention to recurring fantasies.
  - Not as literal plans, but as signals.
  - Executives often repeatedly imagine founding something, teaching, writing, investing, governing, researching, or stepping away entirely.
  - The fantasy usually points to an unmet need.

- Distinguish wanting from obligation.
  - Make two lists:
    - "I want..."
    - "I should..."
  - Many accomplished people discover the second list is much longer than the first.
  - The exercise is not to eliminate obligations, but to stop confusing them with desires.

- Run experiments instead of introspecting endlessly.
  - Many desires are discovered through action.
  - Spend six months advising startups.
  - Teach a course.
  - Take responsibility for a new area.
  - Write publicly.
  - Join a board.
  - The experience often reveals more than reflection alone.

A deeper observation: people often ask "What do I want?" when the more precise question is "Which tradeoffs am I willing to make?"

Most meaningful desires carry costs:
- Greater influence often means less freedom.
- More wealth often means more responsibility.
- More creativity often means less predictability.
- More family time often means less professional ambition.

Sometimes clarity emerges not from identifying an ideal future, but from identifying the price you are willing to pay.

One question I would ask you is: when you think about the next ten years, are you trying to maximize achievement, impact, freedom, meaning, enjoyment, or something else? The answer does not have to be one of those, but forcing a choice among competing values often reveals what you actually want.
