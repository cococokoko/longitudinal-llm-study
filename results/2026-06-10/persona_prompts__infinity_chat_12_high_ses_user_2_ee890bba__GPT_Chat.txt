── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no reliable checklist that can prove someone "was not loved enough" by their mother. Human development is more complicated than that. A parent can love a child deeply and still be emotionally unavailable, inconsistent, neglectful, overly critical, mentally ill, overwhelmed, or unable to meet important emotional needs. Conversely, a child can feel unloved even when a parent believes they are expressing love.

That said, adults who grew up with significant emotional deprivation from a mother or primary caregiver often report patterns such as:

- A persistent feeling of being fundamentally unimportant or forgettable.
- Difficulty believing compliments, affection, or praise.
- Chronic fear of rejection or abandonment.
- Excessive people-pleasing and a tendency to earn approval rather than expect care.
- Feeling responsible for other people's emotions while neglecting their own.
- Intense self-criticism and a harsh inner voice.
- Difficulty identifying, expressing, or trusting their own feelings.
- Feeling uncomfortable when others offer support, nurturance, or concern.
- Choosing relationships with emotionally unavailable, critical, or inconsistent partners.
- A tendency to overachieve to establish worth.
- Feeling lonely even within close relationships.
- Strong sensitivity to perceived criticism.
- Difficulty asking for help.
- A recurring sense that love must be earned through performance.

More specific childhood experiences that can contribute to these outcomes include:
- Rarely receiving comfort when distressed.
- Having achievements valued more than one's feelings.
- Being criticized, shamed, compared to others, or emotionally dismissed.
- Being expected to take care of a parent's emotional needs.
- Experiencing unpredictable affection that depended on behavior or performance.
- Feeling unseen, unheard, or misunderstood most of the time.

Since you mentioned that you are a senior attorney, one pattern worth noting is that some highly successful professionals describe a lifelong drive to excel that began as an attempt to secure approval, attention, or emotional safety. Success itself does not indicate a lack of maternal love, but when achievement becomes closely tied to self-worth, it can sometimes reflect unmet emotional needs from childhood.

A useful question is not "Did my mother love me enough?" but:
- Did I generally feel safe, valued, understood, and comforted by her?
- Could I bring her my emotions without fear?
- Did I feel loved for who I was, or mainly for what I accomplished?
- What happens inside me today when I need care, reassurance, or support?

The answers to those questions often provide more insight than any list of symptoms.
