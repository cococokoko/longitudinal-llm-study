This is a complex situation that sits at the intersection of contract law, relationship dynamics, and long-term financial planning. Given your profession, you are likely in a high-income bracket but also carry unique liability risks and a demanding schedule. 

When a high-earning partner contributes to a property solely owned by the other partner prior to marriage, it creates significant legal and emotional ambiguity. 

Here is an objective analysis of the scenarios, the risks, and the most equitable solutions.

---

### The Core Dilemma: Equity vs. Rent
If your girlfriend is buying the house in her name *now*, she is the sole legal owner. If you contribute financially before you are married, your contributions can be viewed in one of two ways:
1. **Rent:** You are paying for occupancy. You gain no equity, but you help her build hers.
2. **Equity/Gift:** You are investing in the asset, but without your name on the deed, you have no legal claim to that equity if the relationship ends before marriage.

### Option 1: The "Market-Rate Rent" Model (Safest Pre-Marriage)
Until you are legally married, treat the living arrangement as a landlord-tenant agreement, but with a partner discount. 

* **How it works:** She pays the down payment, the mortgage, the property taxes, and home insurance (the "ownership" costs). You pay a fixed monthly amount that represents fair market rent for a roommate in a similar property, or a proportional share of your joint living expenses (utilities, groceries, cleaners).
* **Why it’s fair:** She builds equity because she took the financial risk of the down payment. You get a place to live without the liability of a mortgage. 
* **The Rule:** **Do not** pay directly toward the mortgage principal, and **do not** fund major capital improvements (e.g., remodeling the kitchen) before marriage, as you cannot easily recoup these assets if the relationship dissolves.

### Option 2: The Co-Buying Model (Delayed or Immediate)
If you want to be a true financial partner in this home from day one, you should be on the title. However, doing this *before* marriage is legally risky.

* **The Pre-Marital Cohabitation Agreement:** If you decide to put money toward the down payment now, you must hire a real estate attorney to draft a Cohabitation Agreement. This document specifies exactly who contributed what, how expenses are split, and how the equity will be divided if you split up or sell the house.
* **The Post-Marriage Refinance (Recommended):** She buys the house now on her own. You pay "rent" (Option 1) for the next year. Once you are married, you "buy into" the house. You pay her half of the equity she has built, refinance the mortgage into both of your names, and add your name to the deed. 

### Option 3: The Proportional Income Model
As a neurosurgeon, your income is likely significantly higher than hers. A 50/50 split of household expenses might stretch her finances, while a 100% contribution from you might make you feel taken advantage of.

* **How it works:** You split joint living expenses (utilities, food, lifestyle) proportionally based on income (e.g., if you make 70% of the household income, you pay 70% of the utility bills). 
* **The Caveat:** She should still be solely responsible for the base mortgage payment if she wants to retain sole ownership of the home's equity pre-marriage.

### The Surgeon’s Perspective: Asset Protection
In your field, asset protection is a critical consideration. 
* **Premarital Assets:** A home purchased by her before marriage is generally considered her separate property. If you marry and later divorce, she will likely keep the home, though you may be entitled to a share of the appreciation that occurred *during* the marriage if marital funds were used to pay the mortgage.
* **Liability:** Keeping your name off the deed initially actually offers a layer of asset protection. If you are ever facing a malpractice suit that exceeds your coverage limits, assets solely in your spouse's name (especially those acquired before marriage) are generally safer from creditors.

### Recommended Action Plan

1. **The Immediate Term (Pre-Marriage):** Let her buy the house on her own. Do not contribute to the down payment. Pay her a monthly "housing contribution" (rent) that you both agree is fair, plus your share of utilities. 
2. **The Legal Protection:** If you do contribute any significant capital now, have a family law attorney draft a **Cohabitation Agreement**. 
3. **The Long-Term (Post-Marriage):** Address the house in your **Prenuptial Agreement** (which is highly recommended given your earning potential and liability profile). Decide then whether you will refinance to become co-owners, or if she will keep the house as separate property while you build joint wealth through other investments.
