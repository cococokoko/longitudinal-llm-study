“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and helping others do the same. Here are some major perspectives:

- Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus.
  - Discipleship often involves Bible study, prayer, evangelism, accountability, and spiritual growth.
  - Many churches use one-on-one mentoring or small groups.
  - Influenced by passages such as Matthew 28:19–20 (“make disciples of all nations”).

- Reformed (Calvinist) view
  - Focuses on discipleship as lifelong sanctification under God's grace.
  - Strong emphasis on Scripture, doctrine, and participation in the local church.
  - Views spiritual growth as empowered by the Holy Spirit rather than human effort alone.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as participation in the life of Christ through the Church.
  - Includes the sacraments, prayer, moral formation, service, and spiritual direction.
  - Often emphasizes both personal holiness and engagement in the world.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Understands discipleship as a process of theosis (growing into union with God).
  - Emphasizes worship, prayer, ascetic practices, spiritual guidance, and participation in the sacraments.
  - Focuses on transformation of the whole person.

- Mainline Protestant view
  - Often highlights discipleship through faith, community, social responsibility, and service.
  - May place greater emphasis on justice, compassion, and living out Christian ethics in society.

- Anabaptist view (e.g., Mennonites)
  - Strong focus on imitating the life and teachings of Jesus.
  - Emphasizes peace, community, simplicity, and practical obedience.
  - Discipleship is often seen as visible and communal rather than primarily doctrinal.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit, spiritual gifts, and personal transformation.
  - Discipleship often includes prayer, worship, evangelism, and experiences of spiritual empowerment.

- Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Focuses on growth in holiness and love.
  - Encourages spiritual disciplines, accountability groups, and service to others.
  - Often stresses both personal piety and social action.

Some broader debates about discipleship include:
- Is discipleship primarily about belief, behavior, or spiritual transformation?
- Is it mainly individual or communal?
- Should the focus be on doctrinal instruction, spiritual practices, evangelism, social justice, or some combination?
- Is discipleship a gradual process of growth or a series of decisive commitments?

Across traditions, a common theme is that discipleship involves learning from Jesus, becoming more like him, and living out that commitment in daily life.
