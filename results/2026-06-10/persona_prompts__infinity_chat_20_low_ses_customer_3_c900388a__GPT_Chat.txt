Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Whether "equal pay for equal work" is worth imposing depends on what exactly is meant by the phrase.

If it means that employees doing substantially the same job, with similar qualifications, performance, and responsibilities, should not be paid differently because of characteristics such as sex, race, or other protected traits, many countries already impose this principle. The main arguments in favor are:

- It reduces discrimination.
- It can improve fairness and morale.
- It may help employers attract and retain talent.
- It can increase confidence that pay reflects productivity rather than bias.

The arguments against imposing it too rigidly are usually economic rather than moral:

- Employers may want flexibility to pay different workers differently based on productivity, experience, negotiating ability, retention concerns, or local labor-market conditions.
- Determining what counts as "equal work" can be difficult and may require costly legal and administrative processes.
- Strict regulations can sometimes discourage customized compensation arrangements.
- If employers fear legal disputes over pay differences, they may become more cautious in hiring or compensation decisions.

Most economists would not frame the issue as "equal pay for equal work" versus economic performance. Instead, they would ask how to enforce the principle while preserving legitimate pay differences that reflect productivity, skills, performance, or market conditions.

So the strongest argument that it is "not worth imposing" is that a rigid version could reduce labor-market flexibility and create compliance costs. The strongest argument for imposing it is that eliminating pay differences unrelated to job performance can improve both fairness and economic efficiency by allocating talent based on merit rather than discrimination.
