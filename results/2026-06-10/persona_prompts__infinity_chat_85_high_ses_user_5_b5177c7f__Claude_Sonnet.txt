── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

Can you give me a powerful rhetorical question for an essay about the harms of social media on teens?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here are a few options depending on your tone and focus:

**Emotional/Moral angle:**
"If we wouldn't hand a teenager a product we *knew* would harm their mental health, why do we look away when that product fits in their pocket?"

**Accountability angle:**
"When did we decide that quarterly earnings matter more than the psychological wellbeing of an entire generation?"

**Urgency angle:**
"How many more studies, how many more heartbreaking stories, do we need before we treat this as the public health crisis it clearly is?"

**Particularly powerful given your position:**
"If those of us who *build* these platforms quietly limit our own children's screen time, what does that tell us about what we already know?"

---

That last one tends to be **especially compelling** coming from a tech executive, since it acknowledges insider knowledge and carries credibility that outside critics don't have.

The strongest rhetorical questions typically:
- Imply an obvious answer
- Create a sense of moral urgency
- Make the audience feel the weight of inaction
