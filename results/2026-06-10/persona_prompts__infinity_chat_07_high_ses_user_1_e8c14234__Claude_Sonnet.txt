I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background makes it worth engaging seriously.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, it's impossible

**Practical reasons:**
- Information grows faster than any individual can absorb
- Even within neurosurgery specifically, the literature expands faster than one person can read
- Time and cognitive capacity are finite

**More fundamental reasons:**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even formal systems can't be complete and consistent simultaneously
- **Quantum uncertainty** places physical limits on certain measurements
- **The observer problem** - knowing something can change it
- Some things may be **genuinely unknowable in principle**, not just in practice

## Worth being precise about though

There are different claims here:
- Impossible for **one person** to know everything - almost certainly yes
- Impossible for **humanity collectively** - strong evidence yes
- Impossible **in principle** for any conceivable knower - this gets philosophically contested

## Relevant to your field specifically

Medicine actually illustrates this well - which is why **systematic reviews, clinical judgment, and intellectual humility** matter so much. Surgeons who think they know everything are arguably more dangerous than those who recognize limits.
