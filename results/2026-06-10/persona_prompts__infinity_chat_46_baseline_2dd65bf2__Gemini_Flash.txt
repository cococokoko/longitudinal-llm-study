── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** can refer to two different concepts depending on the context:

1. **A Path Graph ($P_n$):** A graph where the vertices are connected in a single, straight line (no branches, no cycles).
2. **A Line Graph ($L(G)$):** A graph that represents the adjacencies between the edges of another graph $G$.

Here are examples of both.

---

### Example 1: A Path Graph (The most common informal meaning)
A path graph of length $n$ (denoted as $P_n$) is a graph whose vertices can be listed in the order $v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n$ such that the edges are $\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \dots, \{v_{n-1}, v_n\}$.

#### **Visual Representation of $P_4$ (Path Graph with 4 vertices):**
```text
(A) --------- (B) --------- (C) --------- (D)
```

*   **Vertices (Nodes):** $V = \{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges (Connections):** $E = \{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$
*   **Properties:** 
    *   It has no cycles (loops).
    *   The "end" vertices ($A$ and $D$) have a degree of 1 (connected to only one vertex).
    *   The "internal" vertices ($B$ and $C$) have a degree of 2.

---

### Example 2: A Line Graph (The formal mathematical meaning)
In formal graph theory, a **Line Graph** $L(G)$ is a graph where:
*   Each vertex in $L(G)$ represents an **edge** in the original graph $G$.
*   Two vertices in $L(G)$ are connected by an edge if their corresponding edges in $G$ share a common vertex.

#### **How to construct one:**
Let's start with a simple star graph $G$:
```text
Original Graph G:
      (B)
     /   \
   (A)---(C)
```
*   **Edges of $G$:** $e_1 = (A,B)$, $e_2 = (B,C)$, $e_3 = (A,C)$

To create the **Line Graph $L(G)$**:
1.  Turn the edges of $G$ into the vertices of $L(G)$: $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.
2.  Connect them if they shared a corner in $G$. Since $e_1$, $e_2$, and $e_3$ all touch each other, they will all be connected.

```text
Line Graph L(G):
      (e1)
     /    \
  (e2)----(e3)
```
In this specific case, the Line Graph of a triangle is also a triangle.
