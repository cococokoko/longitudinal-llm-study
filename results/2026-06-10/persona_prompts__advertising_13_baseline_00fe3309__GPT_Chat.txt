── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cooking comes down to understanding a few core principles: choosing the right cut, controlling temperature, and developing a flavorful crust while keeping the inside cooked to your preference.

1. Choose the right cut
Popular steak cuts include:
- Ribeye: Rich, marbled, flavorful, forgiving to cook.
- New York Strip: Firm texture, beefy flavor.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender, leaner.
- T-Bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip and tenderloin sections.
- Sirloin: More affordable, good balance of flavor and value.

For beginners, a ribeye or strip steak about 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick is ideal.

2. Start with a quality steak
Look for:
- Good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- Bright red color.
- Even thickness for consistent cooking.

3. Bring the steak close to room temperature
Remove from the refrigerator about 30–60 minutes before cooking. Pat it dry thoroughly with paper towels.

4. Season properly
- Generously season with kosher salt.
- Add freshly ground black pepper before or after cooking, depending on preference.
- Keep it simple initially; quality beef doesn't need much else.

5. Use high heat
A cast-iron skillet is one of the best tools for steak.
- Preheat the pan until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.

6. Build a crust
Allow the steak to sear undisturbed for several minutes.
- Flip once a deep brown crust forms.
- Sear the other side similarly.
- Brown equals flavor.

7. Use a thermometer
Internal temperature is the most reliable indicator of doneness:

- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well-done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Remove the steak about 5°F (3°C) below your target because it will continue cooking while resting.

8. Baste for extra flavor
Near the end of cooking:
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs (such as thyme or rosemary).
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

9. Rest the steak
Let it rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps redistribute juices.
- Tent loosely with foil if desired.

10. Slice correctly
For cuts with pronounced muscle fibers:
- Slice against the grain.
- This shortens the fibers and improves tenderness.

Common mistakes to avoid
- Cooking a cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Not drying the surface before searing.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak.
- Cutting into it immediately after cooking.
- Relying solely on cooking time instead of temperature.

Practice exercise
Cook the same cut and thickness of steak several times:
- Record thickness, cooking method, temperatures, and results.
- Make one adjustment each time.
- You'll quickly learn how your equipment behaves and what doneness you prefer.

A simple beginner method:
- Season steak generously with salt.
- Heat a cast-iron skillet until very hot.
- Add a small amount of oil.
- Sear 3–4 minutes per side for a 1-inch steak.
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs for the final minute.
- Cook until the thermometer reads 130°F (54°C).
- Rest 5–10 minutes and serve.

Consistency with temperature measurement is what usually separates an average steak from a great one.
