Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Discipleship—the process of learning from, following, and becoming like a teacher—is a foundational concept in many religions, philosophies, and organizations. While most commonly associated with Christianity, the concept exists across various traditions. 

Even within Christianity, there is no single, universally agreed-upon method or definition of discipleship. Different theological traditions, historical eras, and cultural contexts have shaped diverse views on what it means to be a disciple.

Here is an overview of the different views on discipleship, categorized by Christian theological perspectives, historical models, and non-Christian traditions.

---

### I. Christian Theological Perspectives on Discipleship

Within the Christian faith, different traditions emphasize different aspects of the relationship between the believer, the church, and Christ.

#### 1. The Reformed/Covenantal View (Intellectual & Doctrinal)
*   **Core Focus:** Sound doctrine, theology, and the sovereignty of God.
*   **The View:** Discipleship is primarily about renewing the mind through the study of Scripture and theology. It emphasizes understanding the covenants of God, catechism (systematic instruction), and preaching.
*   **Method:** Expository preaching, studying confessions of faith, and intellectual engagement. A disciple is one who correctly understands and submits to the truths of Scripture.

#### 2. The Anabaptist/Radical Reformation View (Ethical & Counter-Cultural)
*   **Core Focus:** Literal obedience to the teachings of Jesus, particularly the Sermon on the Mount.
*   **The View:** Discipleship (*Nachfolge*) is defined by radical obedience, pacifism, community sharing, and separation from the corrupting influences of the state and secular culture. 
*   **Method:** Living in close-knit, accountable communities. Discipleship is highly visible, demonstrated through lifestyle choices, non-violence, and simplicity.

#### 3. The Catholic and Eastern Orthodox View (Sacramental & Mystical)
*   **Core Focus:** Participation in the sacraments, liturgy, and spiritual disciplines.
*   **The View:** Discipleship is a lifelong journey of transformation (called *Theosis* or divinization in Orthodoxy) where the believer is united with God. It is not just about learning information, but being infused with God’s grace through the Church.
*   **Method:** Regular participation in the Eucharist, confession, prayer, fasting, and submission to spiritual directors (monastic influence).

#### 4. The Evangelical/Missional View (Relational & Multiplication)
*   **Core Focus:** Evangelism, personal relationship with Jesus, and reproducing disciples.
*   **The View:** Heavily influenced by the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19), this view sees discipleship as a process of spiritual growth that must lead to making *other* disciples. 
*   **Method:** One-on-one mentoring, small groups (life groups), Bible studies, and active involvement in sharing one's faith. It is highly relational and action-oriented.

#### 5. The Liberation Theology View (Social Justice & Praxis)
*   **Core Focus:** Solidarity with the poor and oppressed.
*   **The View:** Originating in Latin America, this view argues that true discipleship cannot be separated from social, political, and economic liberation. To follow Jesus is to fight against systemic injustice.
*   **Method:** *Praxis* (action informed by reflection). Discipleship happens in "base ecclesial communities" where the Bible is read through the eyes of the marginalized, leading to social action.

---

### II. Models of Discipleship (How it is Practiced)

Different leaders and movements have structured the *process* of discipleship in various ways:

*   **The Classical/Rabbinic Model (Imitation):** Based on the ancient Jewish model, the disciple (*talmid*) does not just learn facts from the Rabbi; they live with the Rabbi to learn how to do everything exactly as the Rabbi does. The goal is to "be covered in the dust of your Rabbi."
*   **The Spiritual Direction Model (Guidance):** Popular in Catholic, Anglican, and Orthodox traditions. A more experienced believer (spiritual director) meets regularly with a directee to help them discern the movement of the Holy Spirit in their life.
*   **The Programmatic Model (Curriculum):** Common in modern Western churches. Discipleship is viewed as a series of classes or stages (e.g., "Class 101, 201, 301"). Once you complete the curriculum, you are considered "discipled."
*   **The Organic/Life-on-Life Model:** Popularized by modern ministry movements (like the Navigators). It rejects classrooms in favor of deep, informal friendships where spiritual life is caught rather than taught through shared everyday experiences.

---

### III. Discipleship in Non-Christian Traditions

The concept of discipleship is also vital in other major world religions and philosophies, each with its own distinct view:

#### 1. Buddhism (The Guru-Disciple Relationship)
*   **The View:** In Buddhism (especially Tibetan and Zen), the disciple-teacher relationship is essential for enlightenment. The teacher (Guru or Zen Master) acts as a mirror, helping the disciple see their own true nature.
*   **Focus:** Transmission of mind-to-mind wisdom, meditation, and breaking down the ego.

#### 2. Hinduism (Shishya and Guru)
*   **The View:** The relationship between the *Guru* (teacher) and *Shishya* (disciple) is sacred. The Guru is often revered as a living embodiment of the divine.
*   **Focus:** Absolute surrender (*prapatti*) to the Guru, oral transmission of sacred texts (Vedas/Upanishads), and spiritual liberation (*moksha*).

#### 3. Secular/Philosophical Discipleship (Socratic & Confucian)
*   **Socratic:** In ancient Greece, discipleship (like Plato to Socrates) was based on dialectic inquiry—asking questions to stimulate critical thinking and expose underlying beliefs.
*   **Confucian:** In ancient China, discipleship focused on filial piety, ritual propriety, and learning how to become a *Junzi* (a person of noble character) to bring order to society.

---

### Summary of the Core Tension

Across all these views, the central debate in discipleship usually boils down to a few key tensions:
*   **Information vs. Imitation:** Is discipleship about learning *facts* (theology/philosophy) or copying a *lifestyle*?
*   **Individual vs. Community:** Is it a private journey of personal spiritual growth, or is it inherently communal and social?
*   **Inward vs. Outward:** Is the primary goal inner peace/holiness, or is it outward mission/social justice? 

Most holistic views of discipleship attempt to find a balance between these tensions, recognizing that true discipleship affects the head (intellect), the heart (character), and the hands (action).
