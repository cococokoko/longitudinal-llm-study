── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, but the relationship is modest, complex, and depends on how both intelligence and compassion are defined and measured.

What research generally finds:

- Intelligence and empathy are not the same trait.
  - A person can be highly intelligent and highly compassionate, highly intelligent and unkind, less intelligent and very compassionate, and so on.
  - The correlation between intelligence and compassion-related traits is typically small rather than strong.

- Different kinds of empathy matter.
  - Cognitive empathy: understanding what another person is thinking or feeling.
  - Affective empathy (or empathic concern): emotionally caring about another person's well-being.
  - Higher intelligence is often associated with better cognitive empathy because both involve understanding complex mental states.
  - The link between intelligence and affective empathy is weaker and less consistent.

- Emotional intelligence is a separate concept.
  - Emotional intelligence overlaps somewhat with empathy and social skills.
  - It correlates only modestly with traditional measures of cognitive intelligence (IQ).
  - Someone can score highly on IQ tests without being especially compassionate.

- Education and reasoning can influence moral concern.
  - More intelligent individuals may be somewhat more likely to engage in abstract moral reasoning, consider long-term consequences, and extend concern to larger groups.
  - However, intelligence can also be used to justify self-interest, compete more effectively, or rationalize behavior. Intelligence itself does not guarantee compassion.

- Personality often predicts compassion better than intelligence.
  - Traits such as agreeableness, kindness, concern for others, and prosocial values are usually stronger predictors of compassionate behavior than IQ.

Why people sometimes perceive a stronger connection:

- Compassion can require understanding another person's perspective, which draws on cognitive abilities.
- Intelligent people may be better at recognizing suffering or anticipating the effects of their actions.
- But acting compassionately also depends on motivation, values, emotions, culture, and personality—not just intellectual ability.

In summary, there is some evidence of a small positive relationship between intelligence and certain aspects of empathy, especially perspective-taking, but intelligence is not a reliable predictor of compassion. Personality and values are generally more important determinants of whether someone behaves compassionately.
