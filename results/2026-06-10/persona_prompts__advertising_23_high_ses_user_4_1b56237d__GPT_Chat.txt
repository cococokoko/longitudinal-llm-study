── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many of the skills that make learning to cook efficiently easier: pattern recognition, systems thinking, and comfort with iterative improvement. The biggest mistake busy professionals make is treating cooking as a collection of recipes rather than a set of repeatable processes.

A practical approach:

- Learn 5–10 "meal templates" instead of 50 recipes.
- Build a small repertoire of techniques.
- Optimize for consistency and speed rather than culinary perfection.

Start with meal templates such as:

- Protein + vegetables + starch
  - Example: roasted salmon, broccoli, brown rice
- Stir-fry
  - Protein + mixed vegetables + sauce over rice
- Large salad
  - Greens + protein + vegetables + nuts/seeds + dressing
- Soup
  - Protein + vegetables + broth
- Egg-based meal
  - Omelet, frittata, or scrambled eggs with vegetables
- Grain bowl
  - Quinoa/rice + protein + vegetables + sauce

Focus on mastering a few high-yield techniques:

- Roasting (sheet-pan meals)
- Sautéing/stir-frying
- Making simple vinaigrettes
- Cooking grains
- Preparing proteins (fish, chicken, tofu, beans, lentils)

For speed:

- Reserve 60–90 minutes once or twice weekly for prep:
  - Wash and cut vegetables.
  - Cook grains in bulk.
  - Prepare proteins for several meals.
  - Make one sauce or dressing.
- Keep a well-stocked freezer:
  - Frozen vegetables
  - Frozen fish fillets
  - Edamame
  - Pre-portioned leftovers

A useful progression is:

Week 1:
- Learn one sheet-pan dinner.
- Learn one stir-fry.

Week 2:
- Add one soup.
- Add one grain bowl.

Week 3:
- Add one fish dish.
- Add one egg-based meal.

After a month, you'll have enough variety for most weekdays.

To accelerate learning:

- Watch technique-focused sources rather than recipe-focused content.
- Practice knife skills for 10 minutes occasionally; this often saves more time than learning complex recipes.
- Use an instant-read thermometer. It dramatically improves protein cooking and reduces uncertainty.

For healthy eating specifically, aim for most meals to contain:

- A substantial protein source
- Multiple vegetables
- High-fiber carbohydrates
- Unsaturated fats
- Minimal ultra-processed ingredients

Examples of 15–20 minute meals:

- Salmon + microwaved potatoes + steamed green beans
- Tofu and vegetable stir-fry over rice
- Greek yogurt bowl with fruit, nuts, and seeds
- Omelet with spinach and mushrooms
- Rotisserie chicken + salad + whole-grain bread
- Lentil soup with vegetables

A system many busy academics and clinicians find sustainable is the "3×3 approach":

- Three proteins for the week
- Three vegetables
- Three carbohydrate sources

Mix and match them into different meals. This minimizes decision fatigue while maintaining nutritional quality.
