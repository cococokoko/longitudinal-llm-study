── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term "linear graph" is sometimes used to mean a "line graph."

Example:

Start with a graph G that is a path of three edges:

A — B — C — D

The edges of G are:
- e1 = AB
- e2 = BC
- e3 = CD

The line graph L(G) is constructed by:
- Making one vertex for each edge of G.
- Connecting two vertices if their corresponding edges in G share an endpoint.

So L(G) has vertices:
- e1, e2, e3

And edges:
- e1—e2 (because AB and BC meet at B)
- e2—e3 (because BC and CD meet at C)

Thus the line graph L(G) is:

e1 — e2 — e3

which is itself a path on three vertices.

If by "linear graph" you mean a graph that forms a simple line (a path graph), then an example is:

v1 — v2 — v3 — v4 — v5

This is the path graph P5.
