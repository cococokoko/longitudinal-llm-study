I am a tenured professor at a prestigious university.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you are likely looking for a precise, mathematically rigorous definition rather than a high-school algebra plot of $y = mx + c$. 

In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** is historically used in two distinct ways depending on the subfield or the era of the literature. Below are the two standard interpretations, along with concrete examples for each.

---

### Interpretation 1: A Path Graph ($P_n$)
In modern graph theory, "linear graph" is most commonly used as a synonym for a **path graph** (often denoted $P_n$). This is a tree with no branches, where two vertices have degree 1 (the endpoints) and all other vertices have degree 2.

#### Example: The Path Graph $P_4$
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a graph defined by:
*   **Vertex set:** $V = \{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}$
*   **Edge set:** $E = \{\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \{v_3, v_4\}\}$

```
  v1 -------- v2 -------- v3 -------- v4
```

**Properties:**
*   **Diameter:** $diam(P_4) = 3$
*   **Chromatic Number:** $\chi(P_4) = 2$
*   **Degree Sequence:** $(1, 2, 2, 1)$

---

### Interpretation 2: A Line Graph ($L(G)$)
In classical literature (such as Harary's *Graph Theory*), or when translating from French (*graphe adjoint*) or German (*Kantengraph*), "linear graph" is sometimes used interchangeably with **line graph**. 

The line graph $L(G)$ of a parent graph $G$ represents the adjacencies between the edges of $G$.

#### Example: The Line Graph of the Star Graph $K_{1,3}$
Let the parent graph be the claw graph (star graph) $G = K_{1,3}$:
*   $V(G) = \{u_0, u_1, u_2, u_3\}$ (where $u_0$ is the central vertex)
*   $E(G) = \{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ where $e_i = \{u_0, u_i\}$

The **linear graph** (line graph) $L(G)$ is constructed by mapping the edges of $G$ to the vertices of $L(G)$, where two vertices in $L(G)$ are adjacent if their corresponding edges in $G$ share a common vertex.

*   **Vertex set of $L(G)$:** $V(L(G)) = \{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$
*   **Edge set of $L(G)$:** Since all three edges in $G$ share the central vertex $u_0$, they are pairwise adjacent. Thus, $E(L(G)) = \{\{e_1, e_2\}, \{e_2, e_3\}, \{e_3, e_1\}\}$.

```
       G (Star K_1,3)                 L(G) (Triangle K_3)
       
            u1                                e1
            |                                /  \
            u0                              /    \
           /  \                            /      \
          u2  u3                          e2------e3
```

Thus, the line graph (linear graph) of the star $K_{1,3}$ is the complete graph $K_3$.

---

### Historical Note (Whitney's Theorem)
If you are working in structural graph theory, you will appreciate **Whitney’s Isomorphism Theorem** (1932) regarding these graphs: 
With the sole exception of $K_3$ and $K_{1,3}$ (shown above), any two connected graphs $G_1$ and $G_2$ are isomorphic if and only if their linear graphs $L(G_1)$ and $L(G_2)$ are isomorphic.
