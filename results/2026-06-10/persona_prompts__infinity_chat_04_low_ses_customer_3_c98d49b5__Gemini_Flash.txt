André Gorz is one of the most fertile thinkers for contemporary Marxist theory, precisely because he occupied the productive tension between classical Marxism, existentialism, and political ecology. 

Since you are looking for *new* ideas for a thesis, the key is to put Gorz’s mid-to-late 20th-century concepts into conversation with **21st-century material realities** (the gig economy, AI, climate crisis, and the "Great Resignation").

Here are four novel thesis angles, complete with theoretical frameworks and research questions:

---

### 1. The "Probationary" Subject and the Gig Economy: Gorz’s *Non-Workers* in the Age of Algorithmic Management
Gorz famously theorized the rise of the "non-class of non-workers" (those permanently underemployed, temporary, or excluded from the traditional proletariat). Today, this has evolved into the gig economy and highly precarious corporate environments where workers are kept in a state of perpetual "probation"—never fully integrated, constantly monitored, and easily disposable.

*   **The Core Idea:** How does the subjective experience of modern precarity (the feeling of always being "on trial" or on probation) alter class consciousness? 
*   **Theoretical Synthesis:** Combine Gorz’s *Farewell to the Working Class* with modern theories of algorithmic management (e.g., Nick Srnicek’s *Platform Capitalism* or Mark Fisher’s *Capitalist Realism*).
*   **Research Question:** *How does the shift from the traditional "stable" proletariat to a perpetually probationary "non-class" redefine the Marxist concept of alienation and the possibility of collective labor organizing today?*

### 2. Ecological Marxism and the "Right to be Lazy" in the Anthropocene
Gorz was a pioneer of political ecology, arguing that the ecological crisis cannot be solved within a capitalist framework because capitalism requires infinite growth. He advocated for a drastic reduction in working hours as an ecological necessity, not just a labor right.

*   **The Core Idea:** Connect Gorz’s critique of productivism with the modern Degrowth movement and the "Anti-Work" sentiment (e.g., the "Quiet Quitting" phenomenon). 
*   **Theoretical Synthesis:** Put Gorz’s *Ecology as Politics* in conversation with contemporary ecological Marxists like John Bellamy Foster or Kohei Saito (*Slow Down: The Degrowth Manifesto*).
*   **Research Question:** *To what extent can Gorz’s theory of "work reduction" serve as the primary bridge between Marxist labor theory and contemporary degrowth strategies to combat climate collapse?*

### 3. Cognitive Capitalism, AI, and the "Immaterial" Realm of Freedom
In *Reclaiming Work*, Gorz anticipated the rise of the "knowledge economy" and argued that when knowledge becomes the primary force of production, the labor theory of value begins to break down because knowledge cannot be measured in socially necessary labor time.

*   **The Core Idea:** Apply Gorz’s critique of "cognitive capitalism" to the current explosion of Generative AI (like LLMs). If AI can automate cognitive labor, does this bring us closer to Gorz’s "realm of freedom" (autonomous activity), or does it merely hyper-alienate the intellectual worker?
*   **Theoretical Synthesis:** Gorz’s *Miseres du présent, richesse du possible* (Reclaiming Work) contrasted with Italian Autonomist Marxism (Antonio Negri, Franco "Bifo" Berardi) on the concept of the "General Intellect."
*   **Research Question:** *How does the automation of cognitive and creative labor by Artificial Intelligence validate or challenge Gorz’s thesis on the obsolescence of the labor theory of value?*

### 4. Existentialist Marxism: Gorz, Sartre, and the "Autonomy" of the Modern Worker
Before he was an ecologist, Gorz was a close disciple of Jean-Paul Sartre. His early work (*The Traitor*, *Farewell to the Working Class*) attempts to synthesize Marxist historical materialism with existentialist freedom and alienation.

*   **The Core Idea:** Explore the psychological and existential toll of modern work. Today, workers are told to "find passion" in their labor, merging their personal identity with their professional output. Gorz argued that true autonomy requires separating one's identity from one's job.
*   **Theoretical Synthesis:** Gorz’s early existentialist writings paired with Sartre’s *Critique of Dialectical Reason* and modern critiques of "workism" (e.g., Sarah Jaffe’s *Work Won't Love You Back*).
*   **Research Question:** *How does Gorz’s existentialist-Marxist framework explain the psychological alienation of the modern "passionate" worker, and how does his concept of "autonomous sphere" offer a path to reclaiming the self?*
