# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a patient's **intracranial pressure (ICP)** modeled as a function of two variables:

$$P(V, R) = \frac{V^2}{R} + 3V$$

where:
- **V** = cerebrospinal fluid volume (mL)
- **R** = compliance resistance (arbitrary units)

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### ∂P/∂V (holding R constant)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = \frac{2V}{R} + 3$$

**At the point V = 4, R = 2:**

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = \frac{2(4)}{2} + 3 = 4 + 3 = \boxed{7}$$

> *Interpretation: If R is held fixed at 2, each additional 1 mL of CSF volume increases ICP by approximately **7 units**.*

---

### ∂P/∂R (holding V constant)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = -\frac{V^2}{R^2}$$

**At the point V = 4, R = 2:**

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = -\frac{16}{4} = \boxed{-4}$$

> *Interpretation: If V is held fixed at 4 mL, each unit increase in compliance resistance **decreases** ICP by approximately **4 units**.*

---

## Key Insight

| What changes | What's frozen | Effect on P |
|---|---|---|
| Volume V | Resistance R | +7 per unit V |
| Resistance R | Volume V | −4 per unit R |

The partial derivative isolates **one variable's contribution** while treating all others as constants — exactly like asking *"what happens to ICP if only this one factor changes?"*
