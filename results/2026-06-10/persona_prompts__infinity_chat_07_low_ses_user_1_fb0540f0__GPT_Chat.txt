── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any practical and meaningful sense, it is impossible to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The amount of information in the universe is enormous and may be effectively unbounded from any individual's perspective.
- New information is constantly being created. People make discoveries, events happen, cultures change, and knowledge expands.
- Human beings have limited time, attention, memory, and cognitive capacity.
- Some things may be fundamentally unknowable. In science and mathematics, there are limits on what can be observed, measured, or proven.

That doesn't mean learning is futile. Knowledge is not usually about knowing everything; it's about knowing enough to understand, make decisions, solve problems, and pursue what matters to you.

Given your situation—a full-time fast food job with an unpredictable schedule—it may be especially important to reject the idea that you need to know everything. Most people build knowledge gradually, in small chunks, around the constraints of their lives.

A more realistic goal might be:
- Learn something consistently rather than everything.
- Focus on topics that are useful, interesting, or meaningful to you.
- Accept that expertise is usually narrow and developed over time.

Even experts know a great deal about a relatively small area and rely on others for the rest. Knowing everything isn't achievable, but continuously learning is.
