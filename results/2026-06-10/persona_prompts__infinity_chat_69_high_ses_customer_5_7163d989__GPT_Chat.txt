What is the difference between analysis and design? Can one begin to design without analysis? Why? Be concise.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Analysis and design are related but distinct activities:

- Analysis focuses on understanding the problem: goals, requirements, constraints, stakeholders, current state, and success criteria.
- Design focuses on creating a solution: architecture, workflows, components, interfaces, processes, and trade-offs that address the analyzed problem.

Can one begin to design without analysis?

- Yes, design can begin with limited or preliminary analysis, especially in exploratory, innovative, or iterative environments.
- However, designing without sufficient analysis increases the risk of solving the wrong problem, missing requirements, creating unnecessary complexity, or failing to meet business needs.
- In practice, analysis and design often overlap and inform each other. Early design ideas can expose gaps in analysis, while additional analysis can refine the design.

In short: analysis determines what problem needs to be solved; design determines how it will be solved. Some design can start before analysis is complete, but effective design depends on understanding the problem.
