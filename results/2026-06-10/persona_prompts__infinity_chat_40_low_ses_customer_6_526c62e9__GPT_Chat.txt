── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer. The best solution depends on how you want to balance finances, risk, and your future plans.

A few key points:

- If the house is in her name only, and you're not married, any money you put toward the mortgage may build her equity, not yours.
- If you move in and live there, it's reasonable to contribute to housing costs. The question is how.
- Since you expect to marry within a year, it may make sense to think about both the short-term arrangement and what happens after marriage.

Common approaches:

- Pay "rent" rather than the mortgage.
  - You contribute a mutually agreed monthly amount for living there.
  - This is often the simplest and cleanest option.
  - The amount could be below market rent if she's also benefiting from building equity.

- Split household expenses but not the mortgage.
  - She pays the mortgage.
  - You cover utilities, groceries, internet, or other recurring expenses.
  - This avoids ambiguity about ownership.

- Contribute toward the mortgage and document it.
  - If you want your payments to create an ownership interest, consult a real-estate attorney and put the agreement in writing.
  - Verbal understandings often lead to conflict later.

- Wait until marriage to combine housing finances.
  - Keep ownership and finances separate for now.
  - Reevaluate after marriage and decide whether to add you to the title, refinance jointly, or handle finances another way.

Questions worth discussing together:

- Will the house be solely hers, or do you eventually expect joint ownership?
- If you break up before marriage, what happens to money you've contributed?
- How much would you pay to live somewhere else?
- Are your incomes similar, or would a proportional split be more appropriate?

Given that you're planning marriage but aren't married yet, a practical middle-ground is often:
- She buys the house in her name.
- You contribute a reasonable monthly amount as rent or toward shared living costs.
- You do not assume you're earning equity unless there's a written legal agreement saying so.

That protects both of you while still sharing the cost of living together.
