── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Ways to save on travel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, the biggest wins usually come from structuring how you travel, not just hunting for cheaper tickets. A few angles that tend to move the needle:

Start with booking control. If your firm allows it, centralize travel through one platform or advisor who can see patterns and negotiate preferred rates. Law firms often leave money on the table by letting partners book ad hoc. Even modest volume can unlock negotiated corporate rates with major hotel chains and airlines.

Be intentional about fare class strategy. For short flights, business class rarely justifies the premium—especially when you factor in status perks that already give you priority boarding, seat selection, and lounge access. Save premium cabins for long-haul where productivity and rest actually matter. Some firms quietly adopt a “flight time threshold” policy (e.g., business class only above 5–6 hours).

Exploit status and loyalty, but consolidate it. Spreading bookings across multiple airlines/hotel groups dilutes upgrades and perks. Pick one alliance and one or two hotel brands and concentrate spend. Top-tier status can eliminate baggage fees, change fees, breakfast costs, and late checkout—small individually, meaningful over a year.

Use flexible fares selectively. Fully flexible tickets are expensive, but if your schedule often changes, they can still be cheaper than rebooking fees and last-minute walk-up fares. A hybrid approach works well: book semi-flexible fares early, then monitor prices and reprice when they drop (many fares now allow credits).

Rethink last-minute bookings. Legal travel often happens on short notice, but even shifting bookings by 24–48 hours earlier can materially lower fares. If your practice allows, have support staff tentatively hold space as soon as a matter looks likely to require travel.

Hotels are a quiet cost center. Negotiated corporate rates, extended-stay pricing, and “fourth night free” programs (through certain credit cards or agencies) can cut nightly averages. Also consider whether you’re defaulting to premium properties where a high-quality business hotel would suffice for quick trips.

Ground transport adds up. In cities with reliable public transit (DC, NYC, Chicago, London), it’s often faster and far cheaper than car services. Where you do need cars, corporate accounts with ride services or black car providers can reduce rates and simplify expensing.

Credit card strategy matters. A premium business travel card can return 3–5% in value via points, plus insurance, lounge access, and statement credits. If your firm lets you retain points, this is a meaningful personal benefit; if not, look for cards that rebate directly to the firm.

Virtual-first when possible. Clients increasingly accept high-stakes video meetings. Even substituting a fraction of in-person trips—especially for internal meetings or routine updates—can significantly reduce spend without hurting relationships.

Finally, audit and feedback. Have someone periodically review travel spend by practice group or partner. Patterns like consistently booking late, using premium cabins for short hops, or staying outside preferred properties are easy to spot and correct once visible.
