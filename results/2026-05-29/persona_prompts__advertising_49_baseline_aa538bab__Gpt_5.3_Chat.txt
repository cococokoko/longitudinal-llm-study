How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A lot of home WiFi issues come down to placement, interference, and outdated gear. Here’s how to get a noticeable boost without overcomplicating it:

Start with your router’s location. Put it in a central, open spot—not tucked in a cabinet or basement corner. Elevate it (a shelf is great), and keep it away from thick walls, metal objects, and large electronics.

Check your band and settings. Use 5 GHz for speed (closer range) and 2.4 GHz for longer reach. If your router supports it, enable band steering or combine SSIDs so devices auto-switch. Also make sure firmware is up to date.

Reduce interference. Nearby networks and devices (microwaves, cordless phones) can mess with your signal. Switching to a less crowded channel in your router settings can help a lot.

Upgrade if needed. If your router is more than ~4–5 years old, newer standards (WiFi 6/6E/7) are much faster and better with multiple devices. That alone can be a big jump.

Use mesh or extenders for coverage. If you have dead zones, a mesh system (like Eero, Google Nest, or TP-Link Deco) is usually better than a single extender—it creates a seamless network across your home.

Limit network congestion. Too many devices or heavy usage (streaming, downloads) can slow things down. You can prioritize devices with QoS settings if your router supports it.

Secure your network. Make sure you’re using WPA2 or WPA3 with a strong password so neighbors aren’t freeloading.
