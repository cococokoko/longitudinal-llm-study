── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

what I should look for when I Compare Expat Insurance?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When you’re comparing expat insurance, think of it less like a standard health plan and more like a cross-border risk contract. The details matter, especially around jurisdiction, portability, and claims enforceability.

Start with scope of coverage. Look closely at what “comprehensive” actually means. Many plans differ significantly on inpatient vs. outpatient care, preventive services, mental health, maternity, and prescription drugs. Some only appear broad until you read the sub-limits and exclusions.

Geographic coverage is critical. Check whether the policy is worldwide, regional, or excludes high-cost countries like the U.S. If you expect to travel or relocate again, portability between countries without re-underwriting is a big differentiator.

Provider access and billing mechanics matter more than people expect. Prefer plans with strong direct billing networks in your host country so you’re not advancing large sums and chasing reimbursement. Also check whether you can seek care outside the network without punitive reimbursement reductions.

Pre-existing condition treatment is often the biggest trap. Review how they define “pre-existing,” whether conditions are excluded permanently, covered after a waiting period, or negotiable with underwriting. The wording here can be very restrictive.

Policy limits and structure deserve close scrutiny. Compare annual and lifetime maximums, but also sub-limits for things like cancer care, rehabilitation, or specialist visits. High headline limits can hide tight caps in specific categories.

Evacuation and repatriation coverage is essential in expat plans. Check whether medical evacuation is to the “nearest suitable facility” or to your home country, and whether repatriation of remains and family travel are included.

Legal and regulatory aspects are often overlooked. Identify the governing law of the policy, the jurisdiction for dispute resolution, and the insurer’s licensing status in your country of residence. Enforcement and recourse can be complicated across borders.

Claims process and insurer reputation are practical deal-breakers. Look at claims turnaround times, documentation requirements, and whether they offer 24/7 support. Insurers with a reputation for aggressive claims denial should raise concern, even if premiums are attractive.

Cost structure should be evaluated beyond the premium. Consider deductibles, co-pays, co-insurance, and currency exposure. Some policies fix benefits in one currency, which can create gaps if you’re treated in a higher-cost system.

Continuity and renewability are key. Ensure the policy is guaranteed renewable and won’t reprice or terminate based on claims history. This is especially important for long-term assignments.

Finally, consider alignment with your firm’s risk tolerance and your personal situation. If you have dependents, travel frequently, or may relocate again, flexibility and stability often matter more than marginal premium savings.
