── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, “saving for retirement” is less about basic vehicles and more about tax efficiency, flexibility, and protecting against concentration risk. You likely have high income, uneven bonus flows, and possibly partnership economics—so the strategy needs to layer several things together.

Start with the obvious but still critical foundation: maximize every tax-advantaged bucket available to you. That typically means:
- 401(k) (employee deferral + any profit-sharing / cash balance plan if your firm offers it)
- Backdoor Roth IRA (and possibly mega backdoor Roth via your 401k if allowed)
- HSA if you have a high-deductible plan (this is quietly one of the best long-term vehicles)

These are table stakes, but they matter because they create tax diversification: pre-tax, Roth, and taxable buckets.

From there, most of your real wealth building will happen in taxable accounts. The key here is tax efficiency, not just returns. Think broadly diversified, low-turnover strategies (index funds, direct indexing, or tax-managed portfolios). At your income level, minimizing drag from taxes can outperform chasing higher nominal returns.

If you’re a partner, the big swing factor is your firm equity or partnership units. This is where many people go wrong. It’s tempting to let this grow unchecked, but it creates massive concentration risk tied to both your income and your balance sheet. A good rule of thumb: actively manage exposure over time, even if it means paying some tax. Gradual diversification usually beats holding and hoping.

You should also be thinking about deferred compensation plans if your firm offers them. These can be powerful for smoothing income across years, but they come with counterparty risk (you’re effectively an unsecured creditor of the firm). Use them, but don’t overload them—especially if your human capital is already tied to the same entity.

Beyond that, a few higher-level levers:

- Asset location: Put tax-inefficient assets (like bonds, REITs) in tax-deferred accounts and tax-efficient equities in taxable accounts.
- Tax-loss harvesting: Particularly valuable at your bracket; direct indexing can make this systematic.
- Municipal bonds: Useful for fixed income exposure in taxable accounts at high marginal rates.
- Estate and legacy planning: Not just for later—things like trusts, gifting strategies, and life insurance can be part of long-term tax planning even now.

One thing I’d push back on preemptively: don’t over-rotate into “alternative investments” just because you have access. Private equity, hedge funds, etc., can make sense in moderation, but they often add complexity, illiquidity, and fees without dramatically improving outcomes unless you have true edge or access.

Finally, think in terms of independence, not just retirement. At your income, the goal is optionality—having enough in diversified, liquid assets that work becomes a choice rather than a requirement. That usually comes from consistently investing large amounts in a disciplined, tax-aware way rather than trying to find exotic strategies.
