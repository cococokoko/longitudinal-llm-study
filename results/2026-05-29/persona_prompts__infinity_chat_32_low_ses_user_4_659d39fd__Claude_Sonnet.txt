# The Orderly's Apprenticeship

Marcus had worked the night shift at Riverside General for eleven years. Bedpans, vital signs, repositioning patients every two hours to prevent bedsores. Honest work. Invisible work.

He didn't expect his life to change on a Tuesday.

---

The old man in Room 412 had been admitted for observation after a minor fall. No family listed. No insurance information that made any sense. His name on the chart read simply **"Aldric"** — one name, like a rockstar or a medieval king.

"You're back," the old man said, though Marcus had never met him before.

"First time visiting you, sir." Marcus checked the IV line. "How's the pain level tonight?"

"Pain is just information." Aldric's eyes were the color of deep water. "You have steady hands."

"Eleven years of practice."

"No." The old man shook his head slowly. "You were born with steady hands. You simply found a use for them."

Marcus smiled politely. Night shift brought out the philosophers.

He finished his rounds and came back an hour later to find Aldric sitting upright, which wasn't possible given the man's chart notes about mobility limitations.

"Sit down, Marcus."

"How do you know my name?"

"Your badge."

Right. Of course.

Marcus sat in the vinyl chair beside the bed because something in the old man's voice made standing feel rude.

"I'm going to tell you something," Aldric said, "and you're going to think I'm confused or medicated. I want you to hold your judgment until I finish."

"Okay."

"I am four hundred and twelve years old. I have been a keeper of certain knowledge for most of that time. I came here because I am genuinely dying — not from the fall, the fall was theater — and I need to pass what I carry to someone before I go." He paused. "I've been watching this hospital for three months. You're the one."

Marcus looked at the call button. Looked at the door. Looked back at the old man.

"Why me?"

Aldric smiled. "Because every single night, you treat people like they matter. The unconscious ones. The difficult ones. The ones who will never say thank you. You do it when no one is watching." He folded his hands. "Power should go to people who already understand what it's *for*."

---

Marcus didn't believe him.

But he came back the next night.

And the night after that.

Aldric talked. Marcus listened. The old man described a tradition older than universities, older than most religions — a discipline of understanding the world's underlying patterns. Not magic in the storybook sense. Something quieter and stranger.

"The body," Aldric said one night, "is a system of systems. You already know this. You watch it every shift. You notice when something is wrong before the monitors do. Yes?"

Marcus thought about Mrs. Okafor in 308, how he'd flagged her breathing change four hours before her oxygen levels dropped on the readout. He'd done that twice this month alone.

"I just pay attention."

"*That*," Aldric said, pointing one thin finger, "is the entire foundation."

---

On the eighth night, Aldric handed Marcus a small book. The cover was dark leather, worn smooth. The pages were dense with handwriting in several languages, diagrams that looked like anatomical drawings crossed with something else entirely.

"I can't read most of this."

"You will."

"I don't speak—"

"You will."

Marcus took the book home and fell asleep with it on his nightstand.

He dreamed in a language he'd never learned.

---

The changes were not dramatic. No lightning bolt. No glowing hands.

It started with his perception.

He began to see patterns he couldn't explain — the way a patient's color shifted subtly before a crisis, the way anxiety moved through a room like weather, the way certain people carried old pain in their posture like furniture they'd forgotten they were holding.

He started knowing things.

Not everything. Not magically. More like — the information was always there in the room, and he was finally learning to read it. A patient's breathing rhythm told him about their fear. The way someone held their jaw told him about their history. He began to understand the body's language the way a musician understands a song — not just the notes, but the *meaning*.

He went back to the book. More of it made sense each week
