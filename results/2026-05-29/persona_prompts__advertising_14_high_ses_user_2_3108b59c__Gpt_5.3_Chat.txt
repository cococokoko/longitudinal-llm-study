Got it — you’re not starting from zero in life, just on YouTube. That’s actually a huge advantage, but it comes with a trap: most senior professionals overthink positioning and end up sounding like a CLE seminar instead of something people actually choose to watch.

Let’s make this practical.

First, what will actually work for you:
You don’t win YouTube by being “informative.” You win by being *specific, opinionated, and useful fast*.

As a senior attorney, your edge is:
- pattern recognition (“I’ve seen this 100 times”)
- judgment (“this is what actually matters vs what people think matters”)
- credibility under uncertainty

If your videos feel like polished lectures, they’ll underperform. If they feel like “here’s what I’d tell a client off the record,” you’ve got something.

Now, strong channel directions that tend to work for someone in your position:

1) “What lawyers won’t tell you” angle  
   Examples:
   - “Why your lawyer won’t take your case (even if you think it’s strong)”
   - “The real reason lawsuits settle”
   - “What actually happens after you ‘send a demand letter’”

2) Decision breakdowns  
   Walk through real-world choices:
   - “Should you sue or walk away? A lawyer’s framework”
   - “Is it worth hiring a lawyer for this? Here’s how to decide”
   - “When contracts matter—and when they don’t”

3) Story-based content (very powerful if done right)  
   No confidential info, but anonymized patterns:
   - “A client ignored this clause. It cost them everything.”
   - “This deal looked perfect. Here’s where it fell apart.”

4) Myth destruction  
   - “No, you don’t ‘own your idea’ just because you thought of it”
   - “Why ‘verbal agreements are binding’ is dangerously misunderstood”

Now, format matters more than most people think:

- Open with tension in the first 5–10 seconds  
  Not: “Hi, I’m a senior attorney…”  
  Instead: “If you think sending a legal notice will scare someone into paying you, you’re probably wrong—and here’s why.”

- Keep videos tight (5–10 minutes to start)
- Cut filler aggressively
- One idea per video

Consistency beats polish early on. One solid video per week is enough.

Gear is irrelevant compared to clarity. A clean iPhone video with sharp thinking will outperform a studio setup with dull content.

A realistic growth path:
- First 20–30 videos: you’re finding your voice and topics
- Around video 30–50: patterns emerge (what people click and watch)
- After that: double down on what works

Big mistake to avoid:
Trying to appeal to “everyone who needs legal information.” That audience doesn’t exist in a usable way. Pick a lane:
- founders / startups
- employees
- small business owners
- high-income professionals
- general public (but then lean heavily into storytelling + myths)

Monetization will come later than you think, but it *will* come if the content is sharp:
- inbound clients
- speaking
- advisory roles
- digital products (templates, guides)
