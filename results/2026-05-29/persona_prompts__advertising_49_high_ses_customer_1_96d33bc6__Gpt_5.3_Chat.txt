If your WiFi feels sluggish or unreliable, it’s usually a mix of coverage, interference, and bottlenecks. Here’s how to tighten it up without overcomplicating things:

Start with placement. Your router should be central, elevated, and in the open—think a bookshelf in the middle of your home, not a basement corner or inside a cabinet. Walls (especially concrete or metal) and large appliances really do eat signal.

If your home is more than ~1500–2000 sq ft, or has multiple floors, a single router often won’t cut it. A mesh system (e.g., Eero, Google Nest WiFi, Orbi) is usually the cleanest fix—multiple nodes that hand off seamlessly as you move. It’s far more reliable than extenders.

Check your band usage. Use 5 GHz (or 6 GHz if you have WiFi 6E/7) for speed when you’re near the router, and 2.4 GHz for longer range devices. Many modern systems manage this automatically, but it’s worth confirming.

Interference is a hidden culprit. Neighboring networks, especially in apartments, can crowd channels. Logging into your router and switching to a less congested channel can help. Also keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, etc.

Upgrade hardware if it’s older than ~4–5 years. WiFi 6 or 6E routers handle multiple devices far better (important if you’ve got phones, tablets, smart TVs, etc. all active at once).

Backhaul matters. If you go mesh, wired Ethernet between nodes (if feasible) dramatically improves performance. Otherwise, place nodes close enough to each other that they still have a strong signal.

Finally, check your actual internet plan vs. expectations. If your ISP is giving you 100 Mbps, no router will magically turn that into gigabit speeds. Running a quick wired speed test at the modem helps separate WiFi issues from ISP limits.
