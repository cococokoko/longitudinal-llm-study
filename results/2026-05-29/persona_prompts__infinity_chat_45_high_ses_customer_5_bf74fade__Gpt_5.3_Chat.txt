Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people disagree because there are good arguments on both sides, and neither side fully closes the case.

If you lean objective, the idea is that some moral facts are true independent of opinion—like “unnecessary suffering is wrong.” Arguments for this often point to:
- Widespread convergence across cultures on certain norms (don’t murder, care for children).
- The way we argue about morality as if there are right answers, not just preferences.
- The possibility of grounding ethics in facts about well-being, rationality, or human flourishing.

But objectivism has challenges:
- It’s hard to explain where these moral facts “live” and how we access them.
- Persistent deep disagreements suggest they might not be mind-independent truths.

If you lean subjective, morality depends on human attitudes, cultures, or individual preferences. This explains:
- Why norms vary across time and place.
- Why moral change happens (e.g., shifts on slavery or rights).

But subjectivism has its own costs:
- It makes it difficult to say any practice is truly wrong (only “disapproved of”).
- It weakens the force of moral criticism across cultures or against power.

A common middle ground is some form of “constructivism” or “intersubjectivity”: moral truths aren’t floating in the universe, but they’re not arbitrary either—they emerge from shared human needs, constraints, and rational agreements. On this view, morality is objective enough to criticize practices like torture, but grounded in human realities rather than metaphysical absolutes.

If you want a practical stance: act as if some things are non-negotiably wrong (cruelty, coercion without justification), while staying open to revising the edges where reasonable people disagree. That tends to capture how most ethical reasoning actually works in high-stakes settings.
