# The Last Light at Harrow's Mill

The diner on Elm Street never closed before midnight, which was how Clara Voss came to be the only witness.

She'd been nursing her third cup of coffee, still wearing her uniform from the burger place across town, feet aching from a double shift that had stretched into something unrecognizable. Her manager had called her in at noon, promised she'd be done by six, and here it was — nearly eleven — and she'd only just escaped. The coffee was terrible, but it was cheap, and she needed something to hold her upright before the drive home.

That's when she saw the light go out at Harrow's Mill.

---

The old mill sat at the edge of Dunmore, population 2,400, give or take whoever had quietly given up and left. It had been abandoned for eleven years, ever since the Harrow family packed up and disappeared without so much as a forwarding address. The building was a local landmark in the way that broken things sometimes become landmarks — people pointed at it, told stories about it, and otherwise left it completely alone.

Nobody went inside.

Nobody had a reason to.

Which meant nobody should have been turning lights on in there at eleven o'clock on a Tuesday.

Clara pressed her face against the diner window, leaving a smudge she immediately felt guilty about. The light had been there — amber, flickering, like a lantern rather than anything electric — and then it was simply gone. As if someone had cupped their hand over a flame.

*You're exhausted,* she told herself. *You've been on your feet for eleven hours. You're seeing things.*

She left a dollar tip she couldn't really afford and walked to her car.

---

By morning, Dunmore had a different problem entirely.

Sheriff Dale Pruitt stood in the town square with his coffee going cold in his hand, staring at the bronze statue of the town's founder, Elias Dunmore. The statue had stood in that spot for sixty years. It was a permanent fixture, as reliable as the church steeple or the mountains beyond the tree line.

Someone had placed a key in its outstretched hand.

An old key. Ornate, iron, the kind that belonged to something serious — a vault, a gate, a door that didn't want to be opened.

"Don't touch it," Pruitt told his deputy, a young man named Garrett who had a habit of touching things. "Get me a bag."

The key had a small tag attached with a piece of twine. On the tag, written in careful, old-fashioned script, were four words:

*For what was taken.*

---

Clara heard about the key at work the next day, during the fifteen minutes between the lunch rush and the dinner crowd when the kitchen briefly resembled something human. Her coworker Benny had heard it from his cousin, who had heard it from someone at the hardware store.

"Probably just kids," Benny said, scraping the grill.

"Kids don't write like that," Clara said, though she wasn't sure how she knew this.

She almost mentioned the light at the mill. She opened her mouth, considered how it would sound — *I was staring out a window at eleven at night because I had nowhere better to be* — and closed it again.

Instead, she picked up her phone during her break and searched for everything she could find about the Harrow family.

What she found was surprisingly little, and the little she found was strange.

The Harrows had owned the mill for three generations. They'd been a quiet family, respected but not particularly liked, the way wealthy families in small towns often aren't. The patriarch, a man named Edmund Harrow, had died in 1987. His son, Richard, had run the mill until it closed. And then Richard had simply vanished, along with his daughter, a girl named Iris who would have been about seventeen at the time.

No scandal. No explanation. No trace.

The mill had been left to the town in a will that nobody had ever been able to fully authenticate, which was why it sat in legal limbo, untouched, belonging to everyone and no one.

Clara stared at the name *Iris Harrow* for a long moment.

Then her manager called her back to the counter, and she tucked the phone away.
