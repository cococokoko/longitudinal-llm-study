# The Last Letter

The envelope had been sitting on Margaret's kitchen table for three days.

She walked past it every morning when she made her coffee, every afternoon when she ate her lunch alone, and every evening when she turned off the lights before climbing the stairs to bed. She never touched it. She never moved it. She simply looked at it the way you look at something you are not yet ready to face.

The handwriting on the front was unmistakable. Forty years had not changed it one bit. The tall, looping letters that spelled her name still carried the same confident elegance she remembered from college, when notes would appear in her mailbox like small miracles. She would have recognized that handwriting anywhere in the world.

It belonged to Daniel.

---

They had met in September of 1983, during their first week at university. Margaret had been lost, literally and completely, searching for the library with a campus map held upside down. Daniel had appeared beside her, taken the map gently from her hands, turned it right side up, and pointed her in the correct direction without saying a single word about her mistake. That small kindness had meant everything to her.

They spent four years inseparable. They studied together, argued about books, shared terrible coffee from the vending machine in the science building, and fell deeply, completely in love. Everyone who knew them assumed they would get married. Margaret had assumed it too.

But life, as it often does, had other plans.

In their final year, Daniel received a scholarship to study abroad. It was the opportunity of a lifetime, the kind that could not be refused. Margaret understood this. She told him to go. She smiled when she said it, and the smile was genuine, because she loved him enough to want his dreams to come true even when those dreams carried him away from her.

They promised to write. They promised to call. They promised that distance was only distance, and that love was stronger than any ocean.

For a while, they kept those promises.

Then the letters came less frequently. The phone calls grew shorter. Life filled in the spaces between them with new people, new responsibilities, new versions of themselves that the other person had never met. By the time Daniel returned two years later, Margaret had moved to another city for work. They met once for coffee, spoke carefully and politely like two strangers who shared a history, and then went back to their separate lives.

She had married someone else. A good man named Robert, who made her laugh and built her a garden and held her hand during difficult times. They had two children and thirty-one years together before his heart gave out quietly one Tuesday morning. She had grieved deeply and honestly, and she had healed, slowly, the way people do.

She had not thought about Daniel in years.

Until the envelope arrived.

---

On the fourth morning, Margaret sat down at the kitchen table with her coffee and placed her hand flat on the envelope. She could feel the thickness of the paper inside, several pages at least. She took a long breath, picked it up, and opened it.

*Dear Margaret,*

*I have started this letter seventeen times over the past year. I know that because I kept every failed attempt in a folder on my desk, thinking I might need them for reference. I never did. Each time I started over, the words came out differently, because I was different, because time keeps changing us whether we ask it to or not.*

*I am writing because I am sick. Nothing dramatic, nothing that requires your sympathy or your worry. The doctors say I have time, and I believe them, mostly. But being sick has a way of making you honest with yourself about the things you left unfinished.*

*You are the thing I left unfinished, Margaret.*

*Not in a romantic sense. Please do not misread this. I know you built a beautiful life. I heard about Robert through mutual friends, and I was genuinely glad for you. I heard about his passing too, and I am sorry. Truly.*

*What I mean is this. When I left for my scholarship, I told myself I was being brave. I told myself I was choosing my future. But the truth, which I have only recently been able to admit, is that I was afraid. I was afraid that if I stayed, I would somehow become smaller. I was afraid that love, even good love, even the kind we had, would limit me.*

*I was wrong. I spent forty years learning that I was wrong.*

*The bravest thing I ever saw was you handing me back my map, right side up, and smiling like it cost you nothing. Except I know now that it cost you something. It always costs something to love a person well.*
