Transportation is usually the biggest lever. Replacing even a few weekly car trips with walking, cycling, or public transit has an outsized effect. If you have control over purchasing decisions, efficient appliances and LED lighting pay off both financially and environmentally.

Consumption habits are often overlooked. Buying fewer, longer-lasting goods reduces the emissions tied to manufacturing and shipping. Repairing instead of replacing, and choosing secondhand when practical, are high-impact shifts. Even something like defaulting to reusable items—bags, bottles, containers—adds up when done consistently.

Finally, there’s a meta-level habit that’s easy to miss: aligning your influence with your values. As an academic, your travel choices, institutional purchasing decisions, and even how you organize conferences or meetings (e.g., hybrid formats) can scale your impact beyond your personal footprint.
