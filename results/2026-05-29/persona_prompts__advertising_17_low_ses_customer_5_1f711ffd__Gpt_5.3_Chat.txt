How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good question—this is one of those areas where small changes add up fast, especially in a busy household.

Start with the biggest energy drains first:

• Heating and cooling: If you can, set your thermostat a few degrees lower in winter and higher in summer. Even 2–3 degrees makes a noticeable difference. If your schedule is predictable, a programmable or smart thermostat helps avoid wasting energy when no one’s home.

• Air leaks: A lot of energy literally slips out through gaps. Check around windows, doors, and vents. Weatherstripping and door sweeps are cheap and surprisingly effective.

• Lighting: Switch to LED bulbs if you haven’t already—they use a fraction of the energy and last much longer. Focus on the most-used rooms first.

• Appliances: Older appliances tend to be energy hogs. If replacing isn’t realistic right now, use them smarter—run full loads in the dishwasher and washing machine, and use cold water when possible.

• “Phantom” energy use: Devices still draw power when plugged in. Power strips make it easy to shut off multiple electronics at once (TV setups, office gear, etc.).

• Water heating: Lower your water heater temperature slightly (around 120°F / 49°C is usually enough). Also, shorter showers and fixing leaks can cut both water and energy bills.

• Insulation: If your home feels drafty or uneven in temperature, adding or improving insulation (especially in the attic) can have a big long-term payoff.

Since you’re managing a full household, it can help to focus on low-effort changes first—like LEDs, thermostat tweaks, and sealing drafts—then tackle bigger upgrades over time.
