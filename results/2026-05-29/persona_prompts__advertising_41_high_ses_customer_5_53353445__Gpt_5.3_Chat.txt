How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you want the fastest impact, focus on the factors that move scores immediately rather than the ones that take years.

First, get your credit utilization down. This is the single quickest lever. If your cards are carrying balances, pay them down aggressively—ideally below 30% of each limit, and even better below 10%. If you can make a payment before your statement closes (not just before the due date), you can lower what gets reported to the bureaus and see a bump within a month.

Second, don’t miss a payment—at all. Even one recent late payment can drag your score down hard. If there’s any risk of that, set up autopay for at least the minimums immediately.

Third, ask for credit limit increases. If your income supports it and your issuer allows soft-pull requests, this can lower your utilization without you spending a dollar. Just don’t pair it with higher spending.

Fourth, clean up any obvious errors on your report. Pull your reports (AnnualCreditReport.com in the U.S.), and dispute anything inaccurate—wrong balances, accounts that aren’t yours, or incorrectly reported late payments. Fixing errors can produce a surprisingly fast jump.

Fifth, if you have high-interest card balances and solid credit already, consider a balance transfer card with a 0% intro APR. This doesn’t directly boost your score, but it helps you pay down principal faster, which does.

A couple of things that won’t help quickly: closing old accounts (usually hurts), opening several new accounts at once (temporary dip), or expecting negative marks to disappear overnight (they age off over time).
