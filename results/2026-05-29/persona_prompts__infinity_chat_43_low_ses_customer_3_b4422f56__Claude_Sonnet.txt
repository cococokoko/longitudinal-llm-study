── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Let me lay out the main positions honestly.

## Arguments for Moral Realism (objective moral facts exist)

- Some things seem clearly wrong regardless of culture (torturing innocents for fun)
- Moral progress seems real - we recognize past practices as genuinely wrong, not just different
- Moral disagreement itself implies people are arguing about something real
- Mathematical facts are also abstract yet we accept them

## Arguments for Moral Relativism

- Significant cross-cultural variation in moral beliefs
- No clear method for settling moral disputes like scientific ones
- Moral beliefs correlate strongly with upbringing, suggesting they're constructed
- Difficulty identifying *how* we would access moral facts

## Important distinctions worth making

- **Descriptive relativism** (cultures differ) is clearly true
- **Metaethical relativism** (no objective facts) is the contested claim
- These don't automatically follow from each other
- "Relative" doesn't necessarily mean "arbitrary" - some relativist frameworks still have internal standards

## My honest assessment

The strongest challenge to pure relativism is that it struggles to explain why we think moral progress is real rather than just change. The strongest challenge to realism is explaining what moral facts *are* and how we know them.

**Most serious philosophers think this requires careful engagement rather than a quick answer either way.**
