Fast-food schedules are chaotic, so you want fixes that are quick to try and don’t require constant tinkering. Here’s the stuff that actually makes a noticeable difference, in order of impact:

**1. Router placement (biggest easy win)**
Put your router out in the open, roughly central in your home, and off the floor (like on a shelf). Avoid closets, metal cabinets, or corners. Walls—especially concrete or brick—kill signal.

**2. Restart + update**
Restart your modem and router (unplug 30 seconds). Then check the router app or settings page for a firmware update—this can quietly fix performance issues.

**3. Split or choose the right WiFi band**
If your router has 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz:
- 2.4 GHz = slower but better through walls (good for distant rooms)
- 5 GHz = faster but shorter range (good near router)
Connect important devices (phone, laptop) to 5 GHz when you’re nearby.

**4. Reduce congestion**
If your WiFi is slow at certain times (like evenings), neighbors are likely interfering. In your router settings, switch channels (especially for 2.4 GHz: try 1, 6, or 11). Many routers have an “auto” channel option—worth toggling.

**5. Upgrade if your router is old**
If it’s 4–5+ years old, it may be the bottleneck. Look for something labeled WiFi 6 (AX). You don’t need the most expensive model—mid-range is fine.

**6. Use a mesh system or extender (for dead zones)**
If certain rooms suck no matter what:
- Mesh WiFi (best, more seamless)
- Range extender (cheaper but less smooth)
Mesh is worth it if your place isn’t tiny.

**7. Limit device overload**
Too many devices (smart TVs, consoles, etc.) can bog things down. Disconnect stuff you’re not using, especially if your plan speed isn’t high.

**8. Check your internet plan**
If everything else looks good but it’s still slow, your plan might just be too low for your usage. Run a speed test near the router—if it’s already slow there, that’s your answer.
