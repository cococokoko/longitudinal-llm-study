── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of philosophy's most contested questions. Let me lay out the main positions honestly.

## Strong cases for moral facts (moral realism)
- Some things seem genuinely wrong regardless of opinion (torturing children for fun, genocide)
- Moral progress appears real - we didn't just *change*, we actually *improved* on slavery
- Moral disagreement itself implies people think there are right answers to find
- Mathematical facts exist without being physical - moral facts could similarly

## Strong cases for relativism/anti-realism
- Moral beliefs vary dramatically across cultures and history
- No clear method for definitively resolving moral disputes
- Evolutionary origins of moral intuitions suggest they're adaptive rather than truth-tracking
- The "queerness" problem - what would moral facts even *be*, and how would we detect them?

## Honest complications
- Most sophisticated positions are **neither simple realism nor simple relativism**
- Relativism has serious problems too - it implies we can't criticize slavery or genocide cross-culturally
- "Error theory" says moral facts don't exist but we act as if they do
- Constructivism argues moral facts are real but human-constructed

## My honest assessment
The strongest arguments suggest **some moral claims are more defensible than others**, which resists pure relativism, while genuine uncertainty remains about whether fully mind-independent moral facts exist.
