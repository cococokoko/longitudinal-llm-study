── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely contested philosophical question worth thinking through carefully rather than just validating either side.

## The Main Positions

**Moral Realism** holds that moral facts exist independently of what anyone thinks - things like "torturing innocents for fun is wrong" are objectively true. Strong arguments include:
- Moral progress seems real (we recognize slavery was actually wrong, not just unfashionable)
- We treat some moral disagreements as having correct answers
- Error theory seems to follow from relativism - Hitler wasn't just *different*, he was *wrong*

**Relativism/Anti-realism** holds moral claims are expressions of attitudes, cultural norms, or subjective preferences. Arguments include:
- Persistent cross-cultural disagreement
- The "queerness" of moral facts (Mackie) - what kind of thing would they even be?
- Evolutionary debunking arguments suggest our moral intuitions track survival, not truth

## Where I'd Push Back on Easy Relativism

Relativism faces serious problems people often underestimate:
- It makes moral criticism of other cultures logically impossible
- It collapses into self-contradiction when stated as a universal claim
- Most people don't actually *believe* it when confronted with genuine atrocities

## My honest assessment

The strongest positions are probably **constructivism** (Rawls, Korsgaard) or **quasi-realism** (Blackburn) - acknowledging moral facts have a different status than physical facts while preserving meaningful moral reasoning.
