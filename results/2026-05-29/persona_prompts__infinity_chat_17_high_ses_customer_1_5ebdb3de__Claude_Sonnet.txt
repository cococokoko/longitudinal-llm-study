# The Stillwater Silence

The body was found on a Tuesday, which Deputy Lena Marsh always thought was the worst day for anything to happen. Not dramatic enough for a Monday, not close enough to the weekend to feel resolved.

---

## Part One: The Discovery

Stillwater, Vermont had exactly one traffic light, two churches that hadn't spoken to each other in forty years, and a population of 2,847 people who all believed they knew everything about everyone else.

They were wrong about that. Lena had learned that much in her twelve years wearing the badge.

The call came in at 6:14 AM. Gerald Fitch, who walked his arthritic beagle along the mill pond every morning regardless of weather, had found Dr. Raymond Holt face-down in the shallow water near the old footbridge. The water was barely eight inches deep. Gerald had stood there for a full minute before calling, he admitted, because he couldn't quite believe what he was seeing.

Lena couldn't blame him.

Raymond Holt was not the kind of man you expected to find dead in a mill pond. He was the kind of man you expected to find at the head of a conference table, or accepting an award, or making other people feel quietly inadequate. He had moved to Stillwater three years ago, retired early from some prestigious position in Boston, and had immediately inserted himself into every committee, board, and civic organization the town offered. He was charming in the way that made you feel chosen when he turned his attention toward you, and dismissed when he turned it away.

Half the town adored him.

The other half had been waiting for something like this.

---

Lena crouched at the water's edge while her deputy, a twenty-six-year-old named Curtis who still got nervous around dead bodies, kept Gerald Fitch occupied near the tree line.

The county medical examiner, Dr. Patricia Osei, arrived forty minutes later, pulling on gloves as she walked.

"What do we have?" Patricia asked.

"Male, sixty-one, no obvious trauma that I can see. No wallet, no phone." Lena stood. "Water's cold but not cold enough to explain this on its own."

Patricia knelt beside the body with the focused calm that Lena had always admired. She worked methodically, speaking quietly into a small recorder.

After several minutes, she looked up.

"You see this?" She pointed to a faint discoloration along the back of the neck, partially obscured by the collar of Holt's expensive fleece jacket. "Petechial pattern. And look at the positioning of the hands."

Lena looked. The fingers were curled inward, not splayed the way they might be if someone had fallen and tried to catch themselves.

"He didn't walk in," Lena said.

"I'll confirm everything at autopsy," Patricia said carefully. "But no. I don't think he did."

---

## Part Two: The Residents

Stillwater had a way of closing around a secret like water closing over a stone. Lena had grown up here. She knew the currents.

She started with the obvious.

**Margaret Holt** received the news at her kitchen table, hands wrapped around a coffee mug, and did not cry. She was sixty-three, silver-haired, with the kind of composed bearing that could mean either great strength or great practice.

"When did you last see your husband?" Lena asked.

"Last night. Around nine. He said he was going for a walk. He did that sometimes. Couldn't sleep." Margaret set down her mug with precise care. "We weren't close anymore, Deputy. I won't pretend otherwise. But I didn't wish him harm."

"Did he have enemies?"

Margaret looked at her with something that might have been amusement. "Raymond collected enemies the way other men collect golf trophies. He considered it a sign of effectiveness." She paused. "You should speak with Owen Garrett."
