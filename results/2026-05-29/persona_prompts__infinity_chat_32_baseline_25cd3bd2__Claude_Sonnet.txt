# The Awakening of Aldric Grey

The rain had been falling for three days straight when Marcus found the book.

He hadn't been looking for anything extraordinary. He was simply a tired accountant, forty-two years old, with reading glasses perpetually smudged and a coffee mug that never seemed to stay warm. His life moved in predictable columns — numbers in, numbers out, everything balanced and accounted for.

The antique shop on Brennan Street had caught his eye only because he'd ducked under its awning to escape a particularly aggressive gust of wind. The bell above the door chimed as he stepped inside, and the smell hit him immediately — old paper, cedar wood, and something else. Something he couldn't name. Something that reminded him of lightning before it strikes.

"Browse as long as you like," said the old woman behind the counter without looking up. She was knitting something dark purple, her needles clicking in a rhythm that seemed slightly too fast for human hands.

Marcus wandered between shelves crowded with brass instruments, dusty paintings, and ceramic figures. He was about to leave when a book fell from a high shelf and landed open at his feet.

He looked up. No one was near it. No vibration had shaken the floor.

He picked it up.

---

The cover was black leather, worn smooth at the corners, with no title. The pages were thick and cream-colored, filled with handwriting in deep blue ink that seemed to shift slightly when he wasn't looking directly at it. He couldn't read the language, and yet — strangely — he understood it. Not the words exactly, but the *feeling* behind them. Like hearing music in a language you've never studied and somehow knowing it's a love song.

"How much for this?" he called to the woman.

She finally looked up. Her eyes were sharp and pale, the color of winter sky.

"That one," she said slowly, "is not for sale."

Marcus looked down at the book, then back at her. "Then why is it on your shelf?"

She studied him for a long moment, her needles going still for the first time.

"It isn't," she said quietly. "It was in the back room. Locked."

A silence stretched between them.

"Take it," she said finally, returning to her knitting. "It found you. That's not something I argue with."

---

That night, Marcus sat at his kitchen table with the book open before him, a cup of tea going cold at his elbow. He read — or rather, *received* — page after page. His mind felt like a dry field finally getting rain. Ideas soaked in and spread roots before he even fully understood them.

He learned that the world had layers, like geological strata, and that most people lived only on the surface. Beneath that surface ran currents of energy — ancient, patient, and immense — that had existed since before the first human word was spoken. Some people, across all of history, had learned to feel those currents. To redirect them. To *speak* to them.

They were called many things in many ages. Mages. Sorcerers. Wise ones. Weavers.

The book called them simply: *those who remember*.

Marcus read until three in the morning, and when he finally closed the book and went to bed, he dreamed of stars arranged in patterns he somehow knew were not random.

---

The changes began subtly.

The first morning, he woke before his alarm and lay in the dark feeling something he could only describe as *depth* — as though the floor beneath his bed extended downward not just through concrete and soil and bedrock, but through something older and stranger. He pressed his palm flat against the mattress and felt a faint pulse, like a heartbeat that belonged to the earth itself.

He told himself it was imagination.

The second day, he was standing in line at the coffee shop when the barista knocked a full cup off the counter. Marcus's hand shot out instinctively — and the cup stopped. Not caught. *Stopped.* Suspended in the air for one impossible half-second before he grabbed it.

The barista stared at him.

"Good reflexes," Marcus said, his voice perfectly steady despite the fact that his heart was hammering.

He went home and opened the book again.
