── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of philosophy's most debated questions. Here are the main positions honestly presented:

## Arguments for Objectivity
- Some moral claims seem genuinely true regardless of opinion (torturing innocents for fun seems wrong universally)
- Moral progress appears real (abolishing slavery wasn't just a preference change)
- We treat moral disagreements as having right answers, not just different tastes
- Natural law and religious frameworks ground morality in something beyond human opinion

## Arguments for Subjectivity
- Moral beliefs vary significantly across cultures and history
- No clear method exists to empirically verify moral facts
- Moral intuitions often reflect upbringing and culture
- Disagreements persist even among careful thinkers

## Middle-ground positions worth considering
- **Intersubjectivism** - morality is constructed through rational agreement
- **Moral realism lite** - some basic moral facts exist without full objectivity
- **Error theory** - moral claims aim at objectivity but fail
- **Constructivism** - moral truths are built from rational principles

## My honest assessment
The question is genuinely hard. Strong arguments exist on multiple sides, and **most serious philosophers don't think it's simply one or the other**.

The most defensible position probably acknowledges that **some moral claims are more rationally justifiable than others**, even if complete objectivity is uncertain.
