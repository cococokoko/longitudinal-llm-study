── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the biggest controversy regarding morality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn’t a single “biggest” moral controversy—what people argue about most depends on culture and era—but a few deep fault lines keep coming up again and again. If you had to boil it down, the biggest underlying controversy is this:

Are moral truths objective, or are they created by humans?

Everything else tends to branch off from that.

If morality is objective, then some actions are truly right or wrong no matter what anyone thinks (like many religious or natural law views claim). If it’s subjective or relative, then morality depends on culture, personal beliefs, or social agreements.

From that core disagreement, a bunch of major controversies emerge:

One is consequences vs rules. Some people think actions are right if they lead to the best outcomes (like reducing suffering), while others think certain actions are always wrong no matter the outcome (like lying or killing). That’s the classic utilitarian vs deontological divide.

Another is individual freedom vs collective good. How much should a society restrict personal choices for the sake of others? This shows up in debates about laws, public health, speech, and more.

There’s also the question of who or what counts morally. Do only humans matter? What about animals, future generations, or even AI? Expanding or limiting the “moral circle” is a huge source of disagreement.

And then there are applied controversies—abortion, euthanasia, capital punishment, war, inequality—where people are often really arguing from those deeper differences without always realizing it.

So the “biggest” controversy isn’t a single issue—it’s the foundation underneath all of them: whether morality is discovered or invented, and how we should reason about it.
