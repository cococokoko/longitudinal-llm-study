── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great question—there’s a lot of noise in this space, so it’s worth anchoring in resources that are both evidence-based and practical.

A good starting point is organizations that emphasize whole-food, plant-forward eating rather than highly processed “vegan” diets:

- The Nutrition Source (Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health): clear, balanced summaries on plant-based patterns, protein, fats, and micronutrients. Good for quick refreshers and patient education handouts.
- Oldways (Mediterranean/plant-forward patterns): less dogmatic than strict vegan frameworks and useful if you’re thinking in terms of dietary patterns rather than ideology.
- American College of Lifestyle Medicine (ACLM): clinically oriented, with CME, guidelines, and practical toolkits. Strong on cardiometabolic outcomes.

For more rigorous or clinician-facing material:

- Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics position paper on vegetarian/vegan diets: still one of the most cited summaries on adequacy across the lifespan.
- EPIC-Oxford and Adventist Health Studies: large cohort data on long-term outcomes (CVD, cancer, mortality) with plant-based patterns.
- Key RCT/meta-analysis authors to be aware of: Christopher Gardner (Stanford), Neal Barnard (more advocacy-leaning but still publishes RCTs), and studies on Portfolio Diet (Jenkins et al.) which is often more clinically translatable than strict veganism.

Books (variable rigor, but useful for synthesis and patient framing):

- “How Not to Die” (Greger): very comprehensive literature synthesis, though it leans strongly pro–whole-food plant-based and can overstate causality.
- “The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners” (Gabriel Miller) or similar practical guides: useful for translating theory into meals, though less academic.
- “The Proof Is in the Plants” (Simon Hill): more balanced, evidence-focused, and good on nuance (protein, omega-3s, ultra-processed foods).

If you want practical implementation (for yourself or patients):

- PCRM (Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine): strong on protocols, meal plans, and trial-based interventions (especially diabetes). Advocacy-driven, so worth balancing with other sources.
- Forks Over Knives: very accessible recipes and transition strategies, though not particularly academic.
- Dietitian-created platforms like “Plant-Based Dietitian” (Sharon Palmer) or “Pick Up Limes” (more public-facing but nutritionally sound).

Nutrients to keep front-of-mind (especially if you’re advising patients):

- B12 (non-negotiable supplementation)
- Iron (bioavailability considerations; pairing with vitamin C)
- Omega-3s (ALA vs EPA/DHA—consider algae-based supplements)
- Calcium and vitamin D
- Iodine (often overlooked if avoiding iodized salt/seafood)
- Protein adequacy is usually not an issue if caloric intake is sufficient, but distribution and leucine thresholds can matter in older adults

If your interest is research-oriented, it’s also worth distinguishing between:
- Whole-food plant-based diets (high fiber, low ultra-processed foods)
- “Vegan” diets that may be high in refined carbs and seed oils

Outcomes differ significantly, and a lot of public discourse conflates them.
