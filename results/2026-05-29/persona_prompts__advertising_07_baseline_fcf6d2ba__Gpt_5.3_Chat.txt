── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Effective ways to reduce household energy use.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Lowering household energy use is mostly about cutting waste and improving efficiency. You don’t need to overhaul everything at once—small changes add up fast.

Start with the big energy hogs:
- Heating and cooling: Set your thermostat a few degrees lower in winter and higher in summer (even 1–2° makes a difference). Use programmable or smart thermostats so you’re not heating/cooling an empty home. Seal drafts around windows and doors, and consider adding insulation if your place is older.
- Water heating: Lower your water heater to about 120°F (49°C). Wash clothes in cold water when possible and fix leaks quickly.

Tackle everyday habits:
- Turn off lights and electronics when not in use. “Standby” power from TVs, consoles, and chargers can quietly add up—use power strips to shut them off fully.
- Switch to LED bulbs—they use far less energy and last much longer.
- Run dishwashers and washing machines only with full loads, and air-dry clothes when you can.

Upgrade strategically:
- If replacing appliances, choose energy-efficient models (look for Energy Star or similar labels).
- Consider a high-efficiency heat pump for heating/cooling if you’re upgrading systems.
- Smart plugs or monitors can show you which devices are wasting the most energy.

Use your home smarter:
- Take advantage of natural light during the day.
- Close curtains/blinds to keep heat out in summer and in during winter.
- Ceiling fans can make rooms feel cooler so you can raise the thermostat setting.

If you want to go further:
- Solar panels can reduce grid energy use if they make financial sense in your area.
- Home energy audits (sometimes offered by utilities) can pinpoint the biggest opportunities.

If you tell me your home type (apartment vs house, climate, major appliances), I can suggest more targeted tweaks.
