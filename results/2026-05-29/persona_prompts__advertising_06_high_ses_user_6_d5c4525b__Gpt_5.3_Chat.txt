Nice—those two worlds actually pair really well. If you approach photography like you would a consulting skill, you’ll progress fast.

At a high level, “mastery” in digital photography comes down to three pillars: seeing, controlling, and editing.

First, seeing. This is the part most people skip because it’s not technical. You’re training your eye to notice light, shapes, contrast, and moments. Start paying attention to where light is coming from, how harsh or soft it is, and what it does to faces or textures. A good exercise: look at any scene and ask, “what’s the subject, and what’s distracting from it?” Master photographers simplify ruthlessly.

Second, control. This is the technical side—your camera is just a tool to execute what you see. You need to be fluent in:
- Exposure (aperture, shutter speed, ISO)
- Focus (single point vs continuous, depth of field)
- White balance (color temperature of light)

If you can look at a scene and *predict* your settings before raising the camera, you’re getting there. Shoot in manual or aperture priority and review your results critically, not just aesthetically but technically (“why is this soft?” “why is this blown out?”).

Third, editing. Half the image is made after the shot. Learn Lightroom (or equivalent) deeply:
- Exposure and contrast shaping
- Color grading (especially skin tones if you shoot people)
- Cropping and straightening for composition

Think of editing like slide-making in consulting: you’re not changing the truth, you’re clarifying the message.

A practical way to structure this, given your likely time constraints:
- Pick one genre for now (portraits, street, travel, or landscapes)
- Shoot with intent once or twice a week (not just casual snaps)
- Do ruthless post-mortems: pick 5 photos, ask what worked and what didn’t
- Study photographers you like and reverse-engineer their choices

Gear matters less than you think. A solid mirrorless camera and a fast prime lens (like a 35mm or 50mm) is more than enough to get to an advanced level.
