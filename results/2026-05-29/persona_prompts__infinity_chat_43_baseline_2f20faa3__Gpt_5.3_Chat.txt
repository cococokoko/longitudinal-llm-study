── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: there’s no consensus. Serious thinkers land in three main camps, and each has real arguments behind it.

**Moral realism (there are objective moral facts).**  
This view says some things are genuinely right or wrong independent of what anyone thinks—like “causing unnecessary suffering is wrong.” Arguments for it often point to how we reason about ethics (we argue, give reasons, think some views are mistaken), and to “moral progress” (e.g., slavery being wrong even when many approved of it). Critics ask: where do these facts live, and how do we know them?

**Relativism (morality depends on cultures or individuals).**  
Here, right and wrong are tied to social norms or personal commitments. It explains why moral codes vary across societies and can encourage tolerance. The downside is it can make it hard to say a whole culture is mistaken (e.g., about oppression) or to criticize harmful practices except from within that culture.

**Anti-realism / expressivism (moral claims express attitudes).**  
On this view, saying “that’s wrong” is more like expressing disapproval than stating a fact. It fits with the idea that moral judgments motivate action. But it struggles to fully explain why moral arguments feel like disputes about truth rather than just clashes of attitude.

Where does that leave things in practice? Even people who doubt objective moral facts usually act as if some reasons are better than others—appealing to harm, fairness, consent, rights, and well-being. Those shared considerations give us a workable common ground, even if we disagree about whether they’re “objectively true.”

If you want a crisp takeaway: you can be agnostic about ultimate foundations while still treating moral reasoning as serious—using evidence about harm, empathy, and consistency to argue that some actions are better or worse than others.
