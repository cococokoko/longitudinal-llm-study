── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best tools for remote team collaboration?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For a physician-scientist running a research group, the “best” stack isn’t one tool—it’s a clean ecosystem that reduces friction across communication, data, writing, and project tracking. The sweet spot is usually a small set of well-integrated tools rather than a sprawling stack.

Here’s what tends to work well in research environments:

Communication and coordination  
Slack or Microsoft Teams are the default anchors.  
- Slack is generally more flexible, integrates extremely well with research tools (GitHub, Google Drive, Notion, etc.), and is preferred in many academic labs.  
- Teams fits better if your institution is deeply tied to Microsoft 365 and compliance structures.  
For meetings: Zoom remains the most reliable for external collaborators; Teams is fine internally if already adopted.

Project and task management  
You want something lightweight but structured:  
- Asana: strong for multi-person projects, timelines, and accountability. Good for grant timelines and multi-site studies.  
- ClickUp: more customizable, but can get complex.  
- Trello: simplest option, good for smaller labs or early-stage groups.  
- Notion: doubles as both task manager and knowledge base (more on that below), but requires discipline to avoid becoming messy.

Knowledge management / “lab brain”  
This is where many groups fall apart if it’s not intentional.  
- Notion: very popular for lab wikis, protocols, onboarding docs, and meeting notes. Flexible, but needs structure.  
- Confluence: more rigid, better for larger or institutional teams.  
- Obsidian: great for individuals or small groups who want local-first, markdown-based knowledge, but less collaborative out of the box.

Document collaboration and writing  
- Google Docs/Drive: still the easiest for real-time collaboration, especially for manuscripts and grant drafts.  
- Overleaf: essential if your group writes in LaTeX (common in computational or methods-heavy work).  
- Microsoft Word (with OneDrive/SharePoint): often required for institutional workflows and tracked changes with senior collaborators.

Reference management  
- Zotero: best balance of free, powerful, and collaborative. Group libraries work well.  
- EndNote: still common in clinical environments, especially where journals or institutions expect it.

Data science and code collaboration  
- GitHub (or GitLab): essential if anyone in your group writes code. Enables version control, issues, and reproducibility.  
- JupyterHub or cloud notebooks (e.g., Google Colab): useful for shared computational work.  
- For more regulated data environments, institutional secure compute platforms may override these.

Data storage and sharing  
This is where you need to be careful given clinical data:  
- Box or OneDrive (enterprise): often the safest choice for HIPAA-aligned environments.  
- Google Drive: fine for non-PHI data, widely used in academia.  
- Avoid ad hoc Dropbox use unless institutionally approved.

Electronic lab notebooks (ELNs)  
- Benchling: excellent for wet labs, especially molecular biology.  
- LabArchives: widely used in academia, compliant and structured.  
- eLabNext: another solid option with workflow features.  
Adoption matters more than features—pick one and standardize.

Scheduling and coordination  
- Google Calendar or Outlook (depending on your ecosystem)  
- Doodle or When2Meet for multi-institution scheduling  
- Calendly for external collaborators and trainees booking time

A practical “high-functioning lab” stack might look like:  
- Slack + Zoom  
- Notion (lab wiki + light project tracking)  
- Asana (for major projects/grants)  
- Google Drive + Docs  
- Zotero  
- GitHub  
- Benchling or LabArchives  
- Box (for compliant data storage, if needed)

A few strategic points that matter more than the specific tools:  
- Standardization beats optimization. A slightly worse tool used consistently is better than a perfect tool used inconsistently.  
- Define norms early (where decisions are documented, where files live, how projects are tracked).  
- Minimize duplication (e.g., don’t track tasks in both Slack and Asana).  
- Be explicit about data governance, especially with PHI.
