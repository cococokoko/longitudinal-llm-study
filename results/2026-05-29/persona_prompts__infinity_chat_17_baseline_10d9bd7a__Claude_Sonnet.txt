# The Hollow Bell

## A Mystery of Millhaven

---

The bell above Carver's Hardware had not rung in eleven years.

Everyone in Millhaven knew this. The bell had gone silent the same morning that Douglas Carver was found dead at the bottom of his own basement stairs, and his widow, Ruth, had never wound the mechanism again. She said the silence was respectful. Most people believed her. A few people — the kind who sat too long at the counter of Delia's Diner and thought too hard about things — believed something else entirely.

On the Tuesday that the bell rang again, three people heard it.

---

**Chapter One: The Sound**

Nora Vess was forty-one years old, recently divorced, and had returned to Millhaven six months ago for reasons she described to others as *needing a change of pace* and described to herself, in the dark honesty of three in the morning, as *running away.*

She had taken a job at the county library, which suited her. Libraries were quiet. Libraries did not ask questions. Libraries did not look at you the way people in small towns looked at you — with that particular expression that was equal parts sympathy and appetite, hungry for the details of your misfortune.

She was shelving returns on Tuesday morning when she heard it.

A single, clear tone. Low and resonant, the way old iron sounds when it finally speaks after a long silence. It rolled down Main Street and slipped through the library's cracked window and settled somewhere in Nora's chest like a stone dropped in still water.

She set down the book she was holding — *A History of Coastal Navigation*, which no one had checked out in four years — and walked to the window.

Across the street and two doors down, Carver's Hardware stood exactly as it always had. Brick face, green awning, the old iron bell mounted above the door in its little wooden housing. Nothing moved. The street was nearly empty. A dog sat on the sidewalk outside the post office, looking confused.

Nora was still standing at the window when the door to the library opened and Deputy Sheriff Cal Pruitt walked in, slightly out of breath, his hat in his hand.

"You heard it," he said. It was not a question.

"I heard something," Nora said carefully.

Cal was thirty-four, broad-shouldered, and possessed of a face that was almost aggressively honest — the kind of face that could not successfully lie, which was both an asset and a liability in his profession. He had grown up in Millhaven. He had known Douglas Carver. He had been, in fact, the one to find the body, eleven years ago, when he was twenty-three and still a volunteer with the fire department.

He had never entirely made peace with what he had found.

"Ruth Carver called the station," he said. "She says someone broke into the store last night."

**Chapter Two: The Store**

Ruth Carver was seventy-three and had the posture of a woman who had decided, somewhere in her sixties, that the world owed her nothing and she owed the world even less. She was small and sharp-featured, with white hair cut practically short and eyes the color of creek water in November.

She met them at the door of the hardware store with her arms crossed and her expression arranged into something that was not quite grief and not quite anger but lived in the uncomfortable territory between them.

"Nothing's missing," she said, before either of them could speak. "I want you to understand that. Nothing is missing. But someone was here."

The store smelled of machine oil and old wood and something faintly metallic that Nora associated with her grandfather's workshop. The shelves were dense with inventory — bolts and hinges and coils of wire and cans of paint arranged with a precision that suggested Ruth ran a tight operation.

"Show me," Cal said.

Ruth led them to the back of the store, past the counter where a teenage boy named Marcus sat pretending to read a catalog and actually watching them with barely concealed fascination, through a door marked *EMPLOYEES ONLY*, and down a short hallway to the basement stairs.
