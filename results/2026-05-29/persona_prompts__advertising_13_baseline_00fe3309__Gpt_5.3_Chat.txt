Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cooking a great steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control: heat, timing, and knowing when to stop. Once you get those down, everything else is just preference.

Start with the steak itself. Thicker cuts (like ribeye, strip, filet) are much easier to cook well than thin ones. Look for good marbling—that fat is flavor and forgiveness. Let the steak sit out for 30–45 minutes before cooking so it’s not fridge-cold.

Salt it generously. This is one of the biggest differences between average and great steak. Salt at least 30 minutes before cooking if you can (it helps it penetrate), or right before it hits the pan. Pepper is optional at the start—some prefer adding it later to avoid burning.

Heat is everything. You want a very hot pan—cast iron if possible. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like avocado or vegetable oil), then lay the steak down away from you. It should sizzle loudly right away; if it doesn’t, your pan isn’t hot enough.

Don’t fuss with it. Let it sear undisturbed for a few minutes until a crust forms. Flip once. After flipping, you can add butter, garlic, and herbs (like thyme), then spoon the melted butter over the steak for extra flavor.

Use temperature, not guesswork:
- Rare: ~125°F (52°C)
- Medium-rare: ~130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: ~135–145°F (57–63°C)

Pull the steak about 5°F (3°C) before your target—it keeps cooking as it rests.

Rest it. This part gets skipped a lot, but it matters. Let it sit for 5–10 minutes so the juices redistribute.

A couple of common mistakes to avoid:
- Not drying the steak (pat it dry—moisture kills the sear)
- Overcrowding the pan
- Flipping constantly instead of letting a crust form
- Cutting it immediately after cooking

Once you’re comfortable with the basics, you can play with techniques like reverse searing (slow cook first, then sear) or pan-to-oven finishing for thick cuts.
