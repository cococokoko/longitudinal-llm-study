The town of Halcyon Ridge was the kind of place people forgot to leave.

Not because it was perfect—far from it—but because it had a way of folding time in on itself. Mornings lingered, afternoons stretched, and sunsets seemed to hesitate just long enough for you to believe something might still happen before the day was done. Most people settled into that rhythm without question. Mara Ellison never did.

She arrived on a Tuesday with a single suitcase, a dented car, and a job offer she didn’t remember applying for.

The letter had been typed, not printed—each character slightly misaligned, ink faintly pressed into thick paper. It offered her a position as archivist at the Halcyon Historical Office. Salary modest. Lodging included. Start immediately.

At first, she assumed it was a mistake. Then she noticed the address was handwritten in her own script.

That was what made her come.

The Historical Office sat at the edge of town, where the paved road thinned into gravel and the trees pressed closer together, as if eavesdropping. The building itself leaned ever so slightly, like it was tired of standing but too stubborn to sit.

Inside, it smelled of paper and something older—dust laced with rain.

“You’re late,” said a voice.

Mara turned to find a man seated behind a desk stacked with folders tied in twine. He looked neither young nor old, his face composed of lines that didn’t quite settle into age.

“I just got here,” she said.

“Yes,” he replied. “Late.”

He gestured to the chair opposite him. “Sit. We have much to not explain.”

That should have been her cue to leave. Instead, she sat.

His name was Alden Pike. He did not offer a handshake.

“The work is simple,” he said. “You record what happens.”

“What kind of things?”

He slid a folder across the desk.

Inside was a report dated three days from now.

Mara frowned. “This hasn’t happened yet.”

Alden didn’t blink. “It will.”

The report described a minor incident: a fallen tree blocking Maple Street, a stalled truck, no injuries. Mundane, except for its certainty.

“You want me to write… the future?” Mara asked.

“I want you to write what belongs here,” he said. “Time is less rigid than you think. Halcyon Ridge has… overflow.”

That word sat heavily between them.

“I think you’ve made a mistake,” Mara said, standing.

Alden nodded, as if agreeing with something else entirely. “Everyone says that at first. You’ll stay.”

“I won’t.”

“You already did.”

That night, in the small apartment above the office, Mara found more folders stacked neatly on a wooden table. Each labeled in the same typed font. Each dated days, weeks, even years ahead.

She opened one at random.

A birthday party at the lakeside. A child dropping a cake. Laughter. A sudden silence when someone noticed the water had gone still—perfectly still—despite the wind.

Mara closed it.

“This is insane,” she muttered.

But her hands didn’t let go of the folder.

The next morning, she went into town.

Halcyon Ridge was unremarkable in the way small towns often are: a diner, a general store, a post office with a bell that chimed too brightly when the door opened. People nodded at her like they knew her, though she had never been there before.

At the diner, she ordered coffee.

“First time?” the waitress asked.

Mara hesitated. “Yes.”

The woman smiled faintly. “It won’t feel like it for long.”

Mara forced a laugh. “That’s… comforting.”

“It’s not meant to be.”

The coffee tasted like every cup she’d ever had and none at all.

As she left, she noticed a boy standing across the street, staring at her. He couldn’t have been more than ten. When their eyes met, he raised his hand and waved—slowly, deliberately.

Mara felt a strange pull in her chest, like recognition without memory.

She waved back.

That evening, she returned to the office.

“I saw him,” she told Alden.

“Of course you did.”

“Who is he?”

Alden tied a string around a bundle of papers. “You’ll write about him soon enough.”

“I don’t understand what’s happening here.”

“That’s not a requirement.”

Mara exhaled sharply. “Then what is?”

Alden looked at her, really looked this time, as if measuring something unseen.

“Consistency,” he said.

He handed her a blank sheet of paper and a typewriter key.

“Write.”

“Write what?”

“What happens tomorrow.”

Mara stared at the page.
