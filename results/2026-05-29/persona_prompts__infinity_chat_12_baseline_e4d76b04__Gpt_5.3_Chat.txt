what I should look for when I Compare Expat Insurance?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When you’re comparing expat insurance, don’t just look at the monthly premium—cheap plans can get very expensive when you actually need them. Here’s what really matters:

Start with coverage scope. Check whether the plan is worldwide or limited to certain regions, and whether it includes or excludes the U.S. (plans covering the U.S. are much pricier). Make sure the countries you’ll actually live in and travel to are included.

Look closely at inpatient vs outpatient care. Some cheaper plans mainly cover big hospital stays but barely cover everyday doctor visits. If you expect regular checkups, prescriptions, or minor issues, outpatient coverage matters a lot.

Pre-existing conditions are a big one. Many plans exclude them entirely, apply waiting periods, or only cover them after a few years. If this applies to you, read the fine print carefully.

Check deductibles and co-pays. A low premium often means a high deductible or high out-of-pocket costs. Think about what you could realistically afford in a worst-case scenario, not just month-to-month.

Emergency and evacuation coverage is crucial, especially in countries with limited healthcare. Good plans will cover medical evacuation to a better facility or even back to your home country if needed.

Network vs flexibility. Some insurers require you to use specific hospitals or doctors, while others let you go anywhere and reimburse you. If you want flexibility, make sure the claims process is smooth and not a hassle.

Maternity, dental, and vision are usually optional add-ons. If you need them, check waiting periods (maternity often has 10–12 months before you can use it).

Claims process and reputation. This is underrated—look for reviews about how easy it is to get reimbursed. A cheap plan that fights every claim isn’t worth it.

Policy limits. Some plans cap total payouts per year or per condition. Make sure the limits are high enough for serious situations (like surgery or long-term illness).

Portability. If you move countries, will the policy still cover you, or do you have to start over?
