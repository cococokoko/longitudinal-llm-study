── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yeah, lots of sites let users choose a “default landing page” or customize what they see first. Here are some clear examples you can look at:

- Google (google.com)  
  You can set preferences like showing a specific language, results per page, or use services like Google News/Discover as your default experience when signed in.

- YouTube  
  Lets you tailor your homepage heavily based on subscriptions, and you can jump straight into Subscriptions or Library depending on habits.

- Gmail  
  Offers different inbox types (Primary, Priority Inbox, etc.), which effectively changes what you land on first.

- Facebook  
  You can set feeds like “Favorites,” “Most Recent,” or specific groups/pages to prioritize, shaping your landing view.

- Twitter / X  
  Lets you switch between “For You” and “Following” as your default timeline.

- Reddit  
  Logged-in users land on a personalized home feed based on subscriptions; you can also default to specific communities via bookmarks or settings.

- Slack  
  You can set which workspace/channel you land in, especially if you use multiple workspaces.

- Notion  
  Lets you set a specific page as your default landing page when you open the app.

- Trello  
  You can choose which board or workspace opens first.

- Salesforce / HubSpot (CRMs)  
  Often allow users to customize dashboards so the “home” view shows specific reports or pages.

- News sites (like NYTimes, BBC, etc.)  
  Many allow you to pick topics or sections (e.g., Business, Tech) that shape your homepage.

If you’re thinking about this from a product or design angle (especially given your schedule), a common pattern is: let users “pin” or “favorite” a page and then offer a “set as default” toggle.
