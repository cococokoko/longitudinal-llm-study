There isn’t a single “best” trading strategy—there are a few that consistently work when matched to the right temperament, time horizon, and risk discipline. The real edge tends to come from execution and consistency rather than the strategy itself.

At a high level, the most durable approaches fall into a few buckets:

• Long-term trend following: Buy assets that are going up and cut them when the trend breaks. This sounds simple, but it aligns with how markets actually move (momentum persists). Works well if you can tolerate drawdowns and avoid overtrading.

• Mean reversion: Bet on short-term overreactions snapping back. This is more technical and timing-sensitive, often used in shorter timeframes. It can work, but transaction costs and discipline matter a lot.

• Fundamental investing: Identify companies with strong earnings growth, competitive advantages, and reasonable valuations. Hold through volatility. This is essentially what most top-performing funds do over long periods.

• Event-driven / catalyst-based: Trade around earnings, M&A, macro shifts, or regulatory changes. Higher risk, but can be lucrative if you’re informed and fast.

Across all of these, the common denominators of success are:
- Risk management (position sizing, stop losses, not overconcentrating)
- A repeatable process (not reacting emotionally)
- Time horizon clarity (most people fail by mixing short-term trading with long-term ideas)
- Cost control (fees and taxes quietly erode returns)

If you’re operating at an executive level with limited time, the uncomfortable truth is that low-cost, long-term exposure (e.g., index-oriented or factor-based strategies) often outperforms active trading after costs and stress. Active trading tends to reward people who can dedicate real attention and infrastructure.
