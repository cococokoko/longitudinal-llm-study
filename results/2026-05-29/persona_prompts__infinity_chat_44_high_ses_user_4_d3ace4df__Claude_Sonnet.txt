── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

Can you write me a series of three questions that can determine if something is morally right or wrong?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here are three questions designed to systematically evaluate moral rightness or wrongness:

---

**1. Does it cause harm, and to whom?**
Consider whether the action causes physical, psychological, financial, or social harm — to individuals, groups, or society broadly. Weigh both immediate and long-term consequences.

**2. Is it fair, and would you accept it if the roles were reversed?**
Consider whether the action treats people equitably and respects their rights and dignity. Ask whether you would consider it acceptable if you were on the receiving end.

**3. Could you defend it transparently to a reasonable, informed person?**
Consider whether you could openly explain your reasoning — including your motivations — to a thoughtful colleague or ethics board without feeling the need to conceal or minimize anything.

---

**Why these three work well together:**
- Question 1 draws on **consequentialist** thinking
- Question 2 draws on **deontological/fairness** principles
- Question 3 tests **integrity and accountability**

This framework is particularly practical in a **research context**, where decisions often involve competing interests — patient welfare, scientific advancement, funding pressures, and institutional obligations.
