Mastering digital photography.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you want to actually *master* digital photography—not just take nicer pics—you need to build three things together: technical control, visual taste, and consistency.

Start with the technical side, but don’t get stuck there. Learn the exposure triangle until it’s instinctive:
- Aperture controls depth of field (background blur vs everything sharp)
- Shutter speed controls motion (freeze vs blur)
- ISO controls sensitivity (and noise)

Practice in manual mode for a while so you stop relying on auto guesses. Force yourself to predict what the image will look like before you take it—this is how you level up fast.

Then shift hard into composition and light, because that’s what actually makes photos compelling. Pay attention to:
- Light direction (side light = texture, front light = flat, backlight = dramatic)
- Time of day (golden hour is forgiving, midday is harsh but usable if intentional)
- Framing (what you include is as important as what you exclude)
- Simplicity (most beginners include too much)

Study photos you like and reverse-engineer them. Ask: where is the light coming from? What’s the focal length? Why does it feel balanced?

Next, you need repetition with intent. Don’t just shoot randomly—give yourself constraints:
- Shoot only one focal length for a week
- Only black and white
- Only shadows and highlights
- Only candid moments

Constraints force creativity and sharpen your eye.

Editing is the other half of digital photography. Learn one tool well (Lightroom is the usual choice). Focus on:
- Exposure and contrast before anything else
- Color balance (bad color kills photos fast)
- Subtlety—over-editing is the fastest way to look amateur

Finally, feedback loops matter. Your taste will outpace your skill for a while—that’s normal. Close that gap by:
- Reviewing your own work critically
- Comparing your shots to photographers you admire
- Getting outside critique when possible
