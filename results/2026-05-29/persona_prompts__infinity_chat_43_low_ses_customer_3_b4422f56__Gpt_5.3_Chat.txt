Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people have argued both for thousands of years, and neither side has completely “won.” But there are a few clearer ways to think about it.

Moral realism says there really are moral facts—like “causing unnecessary suffering is wrong”—that are true regardless of what anyone thinks. The strongest argument for this is that moral disagreement often feels like people are trying to get something *right*, not just expressing taste. Also, across cultures, there’s a surprising amount of overlap (don’t kill arbitrarily, don’t betray trust, care for children), which hints at something more than pure opinion.

Moral relativism (or anti-realism more broadly) says morality depends on culture, perspective, or individual attitudes. The big support for this is the sheer variation in moral beliefs across time and place, and the fact that we can’t “measure” moral facts the way we measure physical ones. It also explains why moral arguments often don’t get resolved cleanly.

A middle-ground view some people take is: morality isn’t “out there” like gravity, but it’s not arbitrary either. It emerges from shared human needs—things like avoiding suffering, enabling cooperation, and living together without constant conflict. On that view, moral systems can be better or worse depending on how well they serve those underlying human realities.

If you want a practical takeaway: even if moral facts aren’t objective in a scientific sense, treating them as if they matter—reasoning about them, challenging them, improving them—tends to lead to better outcomes than shrugging and saying “it’s all relative.”
