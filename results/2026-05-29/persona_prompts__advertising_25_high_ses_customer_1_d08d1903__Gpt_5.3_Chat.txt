── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You don’t need much money—just restraint, consistency, and a few smart choices that punch above their weight.

Start by limiting your palette. Pick 2–3 main colors and stick to them across textiles, art, and small objects. That alone makes a place feel intentional instead of pieced together.

Focus on scale over quantity. One larger, simple piece (like a big canvas, a floor plant, or a full-length mirror) does more for a room than a bunch of small clutter. Mirrors especially are cheap secondhand and make the space feel bigger.

Textiles are your best friend. Swap in neutral or coordinated throw pillow covers, a simple rug, and maybe linen or cotton curtains. You can often find these cheaply, and they soften a space immediately.

Lighting changes everything. Avoid harsh overheads—use a couple of warm bulbs in floor or table lamps. Even inexpensive lamps feel elevated if the light is warm and indirect.

Use negative space deliberately. Don’t fill every surface. Minimalism isn’t just aesthetic—it’s budget-friendly because you’re choosing not to buy things.

Shop secondhand, but selectively. Thrift stores, Facebook Marketplace, or hospital bulletin boards often have high-quality items. Look for solid materials (wood, metal, glass) and ignore trendy junk.

Keep surfaces clean and functional. A clear coffee table with just one object (a book, a tray, a plant) feels designed, not empty.

Finally, hide visual noise. Matching hangers, a simple cable management solution, or a storage basket can make a room feel dramatically more put together for almost no cost.
