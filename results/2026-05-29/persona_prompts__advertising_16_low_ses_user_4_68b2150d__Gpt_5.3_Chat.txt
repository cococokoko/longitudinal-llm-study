That job can get intense fast—long shifts, emotional situations, constant demands. Feeling stressed or anxious in that environment isn’t a personal failing, it’s a pretty normal response to the pressure you’re under.

A few things that tend to actually help in that kind of role:

• Short “reset” moments: even 30–60 seconds between tasks—slow your breathing, unclench your jaw/shoulders, look away from the chaos. It sounds small, but stacking these mini-resets across a shift adds up.

• Keep your body on your side: hydration, a real meal (not just snacks), and sleep when you can. Stress hits way harder when your body’s already depleted.

• Mental boundaries: patients’ situations can stick with you. Try a simple end-of-shift ritual—washing your hands slowly, changing shoes, or even just telling yourself “work stays here.” It sounds basic, but it helps your brain switch off.

• Don’t carry it solo: even one coworker you can vent to for a minute makes a difference. You don’t need a deep conversation—just “that was a rough one” can release some pressure.

• Watch your self-talk: in high-pressure care roles, people quietly beat themselves up (“I should’ve done more,” “I messed that up”). If you catch that, dial it back to something fairer: “I handled a tough situation with the resources I had.”
