Since you’re starting from zero and working long hours, the key is keeping it simple, consistent, and not overwhelming. Here’s a realistic way to approach it:

First, pick one beginner-friendly language and don’t overthink it.  
Go with Python. It’s widely used, easier to read, and great for beginners.

Next, use structured, beginner-friendly resources so you’re not guessing what to learn:
- FreeCodeCamp (free, very beginner-friendly)
- Codecademy (good structure, some paid content)
- Harvard’s CS50 (harder, but excellent if you want depth)

If you’re tired after work, CS50 might feel heavy—so starting with FreeCodeCamp or Codecademy is usually smoother.

Then, keep your daily commitment small and consistent.  
Don’t aim for “I’ll study 3 hours a day.” That burns out fast.

Instead:
- 30–45 minutes a day
- Even 20 minutes on rough days counts
- Consistency beats intensity

Focus on learning by doing, not just watching:
- Type code yourself
- Break things and fix them
- Do small exercises

After a couple of weeks, start tiny projects:
- A simple calculator
- A to-do list app (even in the terminal)
- A script that renames files or organizes folders

This is where things actually start clicking.

Also, don’t try to learn everything at once.  
You do NOT need:
- Multiple languages
- Advanced math
- Complex frameworks

You just need:
- Basic Python (variables, loops, functions)
- Problem-solving practice
- Patience

One more thing people don’t say enough:  
You’re going to feel confused a lot. That’s normal. It’s not a sign you’re bad at it—it’s literally part of learning to code.
