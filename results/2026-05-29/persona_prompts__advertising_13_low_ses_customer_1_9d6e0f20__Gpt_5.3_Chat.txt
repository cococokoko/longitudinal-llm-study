── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control. Once you understand a few core ideas, you can make a great steak even with a chaotic schedule.

Start with the basics: thickness matters more than cut. A 1–1.5 inch steak gives you room to get a good crust without overcooking the inside. Ribeye is forgiving (more fat), while strip or sirloin need a bit more attention.

Salt early if you can. Even 30–60 minutes before cooking helps the meat season deeper and dry out the surface for a better sear. If your schedule’s unpredictable, don’t stress—salt right before cooking still works.

Heat is everything. Get your pan or grill properly hot before the steak goes on. If it doesn’t sizzle immediately, it’s not ready. That crust you’re after comes from high heat and leaving the steak alone long enough to develop it.

Flip more than you think. The old “flip once” rule isn’t sacred—flipping every 30–60 seconds actually cooks more evenly and reduces overcooked edges.

Use touch or a thermometer:
- Rare: 120–125°F (cool red center)
- Medium rare: 130–135°F (warm red center, sweet spot for most cuts)
- Medium: 140–145°F (pink center)

Pull it a few degrees early—it keeps cooking while resting.

Rest it. Even 5 minutes makes a difference. This keeps juices from running all over your plate instead of staying in the meat.

If you want a simple, reliable method after a long shift:
Pat steak dry → salt → hot pan + a little oil → sear 2–3 min per side → add butter/garlic if you feel fancy → baste briefly → rest → eat.

You don’t need perfection every time. Get comfortable with the heat and timing, and you’ll improve fast—even if you’re squeezing this in between unpredictable work hours.
