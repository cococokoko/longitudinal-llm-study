# The Night Shift

The fluorescent lights hummed their familiar, tuneless song as Maya clocked in at eleven o'clock on a Tuesday night. She pulled her dark hair into a tight ponytail, tucked her ID badge into her scrub pocket, and grabbed the handoff notes from the outgoing aide, Darnell, who looked like he hadn't slept in three days.

"Room 412 is going to keep you busy," Darnell said, sliding his jacket on with the urgency of a man escaping a burning building. "Mr. Calloway. Eighty-one years old. Hip replacement. He's been calling for water every twenty minutes like clockwork."

"Got it," Maya said.

"And 408 is a new admit. Elderly woman, came in around seven. Her daughter was here but had to leave for the kids." He paused at the door. "She seemed scared, Maya. Really scared."

Maya nodded and watched him disappear down the hallway. She stood alone at the nurses' station for a moment, breathing in the antiseptic air, letting the quiet weight of the floor settle onto her shoulders. This was the part nobody talked about when they asked what her job was like. The moment before everything started, when the whole wing felt like a held breath.

She began her rounds.

Mr. Calloway in 412 was exactly as advertised. A broad-shouldered man who had probably been formidable in his younger years, he now lay propped against three pillows, his silver hair flattened on one side, his expression carrying the particular frustration of a proud person who needed help.

"Young lady," he said the moment she stepped through the door. "I've been asking for water for an hour."

"I just got here, Mr. Calloway," Maya said gently, already reaching for the pitcher on his bedside table. She filled his cup, adjusted his straw, and waited while he drank.

"Tastes like nothing," he muttered.

"Water usually does."

He looked at her sharply, then something softened in his face. "Fair point." He handed the cup back. "My wife used to put lemon in it. Every single glass. Forty-seven years, never once forgot the lemon."

Maya set the cup down carefully. "She sounds like someone who paid attention."

"Best thing about her," he said quietly. "She paid attention to everything."

Maya checked his positioning, made sure his call button was within reach, and noted his vitals on her tablet. As she moved toward the door, he spoke again.

"She passed in March."

Maya stopped. She turned back to look at him, this large, diminished man in his hospital bed, and she didn't rush the moment.

"I'm sorry," she said. And she meant it the way people rarely mean it.

He waved his hand as if brushing away smoke. "Go do your job. I'm fine."

She left him with the light on, the way he'd asked without asking.

Room 408 was quieter. The woman inside was small, almost disappearing into the white sheets, her white hair spread around her like a halo. A monitor beeped steadily beside her. Her name on the chart was Eleanor Marsh, seventy-four years old, admitted for chest pain and shortness of breath. Cardiac observation.

Her eyes were open when Maya entered.

"Hi, Mrs. Marsh. I'm Maya. I'll be your aide tonight."

The woman's eyes tracked her carefully. "Is it very late?"

"Just after eleven."

"My daughter left." It wasn't an accusation. It was simply a fact that seemed to carry enormous weight.

"She'll be back in the morning," Maya said, moving to check the woman's blanket, smoothing it at the edges. "She has little ones at home."

"Three grandchildren," Eleanor said, and a small smile crossed her face before fading. "I keep thinking about my heart." She touched her chest lightly with two fingers. "Thinking about what it's doing in there. Whether it's doing it right."

Maya pulled the chair closer to the bed and sat down. She had other rooms to check, other tasks waiting, but something in Darnell's words came back to her. *She seemed scared, Maya. Really scared.*
