I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Balancing everything on your plate is already a lot, so the goal here is simple, high-impact changes that don’t add much extra work.

Start with the biggest wins:

- Ventilation matters most. Even cracking windows for 5–10 minutes a couple times a day can clear out indoor pollutants. If you cook often, run the kitchen exhaust fan (if it vents outside) every time.

- Use a HEPA air purifier in the rooms you use most (bedroom + living area). You don’t need one in every room—just where you and your child spend the most time. Look for “true HEPA” and a size matched to the room.

- Control dust with a simple routine. Dust and vacuum once or twice a week using a vacuum with a HEPA filter if possible. If time is tight, prioritize the bedroom.

- Reduce sources of pollution:
  - Avoid indoor smoking or vaping.
  - Go easy on candles, incense, and strong cleaning sprays.
  - Choose fragrance-free or low-VOC products when you can.

- Manage humidity. Aim for about 30–50%.
  - If it’s damp, a small dehumidifier helps prevent mold.
  - If it’s very dry, a humidifier can help—but clean it regularly so it doesn’t grow bacteria.

- Change HVAC filters regularly (every 2–3 months, or more often if you have pets or allergies). If you can, use a higher-rated filter (like MERV 11–13, if your system supports it).

- Shoes-off rule at the door. It cuts down a surprising amount of dirt, pollen, and chemicals coming in.

A couple of “nice-to-have but not essential” things:
- Houseplants can help a little psychologically, but they don’t meaningfully clean air compared to ventilation or purifiers.
- If you’re worried about specific issues (like wildfire smoke or allergies), an air quality monitor can help you know when to act—but it’s optional.
