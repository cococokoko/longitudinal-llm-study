── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people disagree for good reasons—there’s no single settled answer.

If you lean **objective**, you think some moral claims are true regardless of opinion—like “causing unnecessary suffering is wrong.” This view often appeals to things like human well-being, rational consistency, or even moral facts built into reality. It explains why we argue about morality as if some people are mistaken, not just different.

If you lean **subjective**, you think moral judgments depend on human attitudes—culture, emotions, or individual preferences. This view explains why moral codes vary across societies and history, and why people can feel just as convinced while disagreeing.

There are also **middle positions**:
- **Intersubjective**: morality isn’t purely personal, but emerges from shared human needs and agreements (like norms against harm).
- **Constructivist**: moral truths are “built” through rational processes (e.g., what rules everyone could agree to under fair conditions).

A useful way to think about it: even if morality isn’t objective in the same way physics is, it might still be constrained by facts about humans—our vulnerability, need for cooperation, and capacity for suffering. That gives morality some stability without requiring it to be fully “out there” in the universe.

If you want, I can walk through how different philosophers argue each side or test a specific case (like lying or punishment) under each view.
