── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nice goal — especially while you’re still on probation at a new job, DIY can save money and give you a bit more control over your space without big expenses.

The key is to learn in layers, not all at once.

Start with the “high-frequency basics” — the stuff that breaks most often:
- fixing a leaky tap
- unclogging drains
- patching small holes in walls
- replacing light fixtures or switches (only if you’re comfortable turning off power safely)
- sealing gaps (caulk, weatherstripping)

YouTube is honestly one of the best teachers here. Channels like “See Jane Drill,” “Home Repair Tutor,” and “This Old House” are clear and beginner-friendly. Don’t just watch — pick one tiny task and copy it the same day. That’s how it sticks.

Next, build a small starter toolkit instead of buying everything upfront:
- screwdriver set
- hammer
- adjustable wrench
- tape measure
- utility knife
- pliers
- level

You can handle a surprising number of repairs with just that.

A really underrated trick: practice on “low-risk” projects first. For example, patch a hole in a closet wall before doing a visible one. Or replace a cheap fixture before touching anything expensive. You’ll mess up less where it matters.

Also, learn the “stop line.” Some jobs are great DIY, others can get risky or expensive fast. Be cautious with:
- major electrical work
- plumbing inside walls
- structural changes

Those can cost more to fix if something goes wrong.

If you like structure, you could follow a simple progression:
Week 1–2: tools + basic repairs  
Week 3–4: painting + wall fixes  
Month 2: simple installations (shelves, fixtures)  
Month 3: slightly bigger projects (minor plumbing swaps, etc.)

Lastly, since your time and energy might be limited with a new job, keep projects small and winnable. Finishing something in an hour or two builds momentum way better than starting a huge weekend project you abandon halfway.
