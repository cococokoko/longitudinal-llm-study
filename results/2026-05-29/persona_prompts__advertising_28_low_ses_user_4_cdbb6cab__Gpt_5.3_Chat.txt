Nice—shift work actually pairs pretty well with self-paced learning.

If you’re learning an instrument online, the big difference-maker isn’t the platform, it’s how you structure your practice. A few approaches that consistently work:

First, keep sessions short and frequent. 20–30 minutes most days beats long, occasional sessions—especially with an irregular schedule. Even 10 minutes before or after a shift keeps momentum.

Second, follow a clear path instead of random videos. Pick one structured course or app (like JustinGuitar, Yousician, Simply Piano, etc.) and stick with it for a while. Jumping between sources slows you down more than people expect.

Third, split practice into 3 parts:
- technique (scales, finger exercises, control)
- learning songs (keeps it fun and motivating)
- “play” time (mess around, improvise, repeat tricky bits)

Fourth, slow everything down. Most beginners try to play at full speed too soon. Use a metronome or playback speed control and go *uncomfortably slow*. That’s how accuracy builds.

Fifth, use feedback—even if you’re self-taught. Record yourself on your phone. You’ll notice timing, posture, and mistakes way more clearly than while playing.

Sixth, make your setup frictionless. Leave the instrument out and ready. If it’s in a case or another room, you’ll practice less, especially after long shifts.

Seventh, tie practice to your routine. For example:
- after waking up on day shifts
- before winding down after evening shifts  
Anchor it to something you already do so it becomes automatic.

Also, don’t underestimate songs you actually like. Even if they’re slightly above your level, working on music you enjoy keeps you consistent—which matters more than perfect technique early on.
