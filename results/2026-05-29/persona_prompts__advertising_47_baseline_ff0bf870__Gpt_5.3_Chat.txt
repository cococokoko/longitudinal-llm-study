Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon is mostly about patience and consistency, not heroics. A few core things will make or break your experience:

First, train smarter, not just harder. Build your weekly mileage gradually (the classic rule is no more than ~10% increase per week). Your long run is the backbone—do it once a week and slowly extend it to 18–22 miles. You don’t need to actually run 26 in training.

Second, slow down more than you think. Most of your runs should feel easy—like you could hold a conversation. Going too fast in training is one of the most common mistakes and leads to burnout or injury.

Third, practice fueling. During long runs, start taking in carbs (gels, chews, sports drink) around 30–45 minutes in, then every 30–45 minutes after. Also hydrate regularly. Race day is not the time to try something new—your stomach needs to be trained too.

Fourth, don’t skip strength and recovery. A couple short strength sessions a week (legs, core, hips) help prevent injury. Sleep matters more than any fancy workout. If something hurts in a sharp or persistent way, back off early.

Fifth, taper before race day. Reduce mileage in the last 2–3 weeks so your legs are fresh. You should feel a little restless—that’s normal.

Race day: start slower than your goal pace for the first few miles. The biggest rookie mistake is going out too fast and paying for it at mile 18+. Think of it as a controlled effort until halfway, then gradually push if you feel good.

And one underrated tip: plan your gear ahead of time and stick with what you’ve tested—shoes, socks, clothes, fuel. Small discomforts turn into big problems over 26 miles.
