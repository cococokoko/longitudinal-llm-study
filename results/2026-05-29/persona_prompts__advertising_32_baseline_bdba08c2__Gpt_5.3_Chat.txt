── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality is mostly about reducing pollutants at the source, increasing ventilation, and filtering what’s left. Here’s what actually makes a noticeable difference:

Start with ventilation. Fresh air dilutes everything else. Open windows when outdoor air is decent, use exhaust fans in kitchens and bathrooms, and if your place is tightly sealed, consider a mechanical system like an HRV or ERV to bring in filtered outdoor air without losing too much heat or cooling.

Control the biggest pollution sources. Cooking (especially gas stoves), smoking, candles, incense, and certain cleaning products all release particles or chemicals. Use a range hood that vents outside when cooking, switch to electric or induction if you can, and choose low-VOC or fragrance-free products. Avoid smoking indoors entirely.

Filter the air. A good HEPA air purifier can significantly reduce fine particles (dust, smoke, pollen, pet dander). Place one where you spend the most time, like a bedroom or living room. For central HVAC systems, use a high-quality filter (MERV 11–13 if your system can handle it) and change it regularly.

Manage humidity. Aim for about 30–50%. Too high encourages mold and dust mites; too low can irritate your airways. Use a dehumidifier in damp areas or a humidifier in very dry climates, but keep them clean to avoid microbial growth.

Keep dust under control. Vacuum with a HEPA-filter vacuum, damp-dust surfaces instead of dry dusting, and wash bedding regularly. Shoes-off indoors and doormats at entrances also help reduce tracked-in pollutants.

Watch for hidden issues. Mold, water damage, and poorly vented appliances can quietly degrade air quality. Fix leaks promptly, ensure dryers and combustion appliances vent outside, and consider a carbon monoxide detector.

Add plants if you like them, but don’t rely on them for purification—they help a bit psychologically and aesthetically, but real air cleaning comes from ventilation and filtration.
