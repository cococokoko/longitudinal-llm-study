If you want a solid, modern foundation for a first course in Design and Analysis of Algorithms, the “best” book depends a bit on your taste for rigor vs. intuition, but a few stand clearly above the rest:

**1. Kleinberg & Tardos — *Algorithm Design***  
This is probably the most balanced and teachable text for a first course. It emphasizes *how to think* about algorithms—greedy methods, divide-and-conquer, dynamic programming, network flow—without drowning you in formalism. The chapter narratives are especially strong for building intuition.

**2. CLRS — *Introduction to Algorithms* (Cormen et al.)**  
Still the canonical reference. It’s more encyclopedic and formal than Kleinberg–Tardos, and sometimes heavier than ideal for a first pass, but it’s unmatched in breadth. Many instructors pair selected chapters with lectures rather than assigning it cover-to-cover.

**3. Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani — *Algorithms***  
A bit underrated. It’s concise, elegant, and mathematically clean. It works very well if you want a tighter, more theory-forward presentation without the bulk of CLRS.

**4. Jeff Erickson — *Algorithms* (free online)**  
This one has become increasingly popular in the last few years. It’s rigorous but conversational, with excellent problem-driven exposition. Particularly strong for students who learn by working through ideas rather than reading polished summaries.

**5. Skiena — *The Algorithm Design Manual***  
More pragmatic and example-driven. It’s not as theoretically deep as the others, but it’s great for building problem-solving instincts and seeing how algorithms show up in practice.

---

If I had to recommend a clean pairing for a 2023-style course:
- Primary text: **Kleinberg & Tardos**
- Supplement/reference: **CLRS or Erickson**

That combination gives both intuition and depth without overwhelming students too early.
