── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you treat “personal brand” like self-promotion, it’ll feel hollow and probably won’t stick. The version that actually works—especially in academia—is closer to “making your thinking legible to people who aren’t already in your seminar room.”

Start with what you want to be known for in one crisp sentence. Not your full CV—more like a throughline. For example: “I explain how X reshapes Y, and what that means for Z.” This becomes your filter: if a post doesn’t reinforce that, it’s probably noise.

Pick one or two platforms where your audience actually is. For most tenured faculty, that’s typically:
- X (Twitter) for real-time discourse and visibility
- LinkedIn for broader professional reach and institutional stakeholders
- Possibly Substack or a personal newsletter for depth and ownership

You don’t need to be everywhere; you do need to be consistent somewhere.

Content-wise, think in three lanes:
- Translation: turn your research into accessible insights without dumbing it down
- Curation: point to interesting work (including others’) and add your angle
- Perspective: short arguments, frameworks, or “here’s how to think about this” posts

A simple rhythm helps: e.g., 2–3 short posts per week, one slightly longer piece every 1–2 weeks. Consistency matters more than volume.

Tone is where many academics stumble. You’re not writing a paper, but you also don’t need to perform internet casualness. Aim for “clear, sharp, and human.” Short sentences. Concrete examples. Occasional opinion, but grounded in your expertise.

Engagement isn’t optional. Respond to comments, join conversations, and signal intellectual generosity. Amplify junior scholars. Disagree in ways that are substantive rather than dismissive—people notice that.

A few tactical points that punch above their weight:
- Strong openings: the first line determines whether anyone reads the rest
- Threads or post series: useful for unpacking one idea over multiple posts
- Visuals: a simple chart or diagram often travels farther than text
- Pin a post that clearly communicates who you are and what you do

Also be intentional about boundaries. Decide in advance what you won’t engage with—certain topics, bad-faith debates, or time sinks. A personal brand erodes quickly if it becomes reactive or combative.

Finally, measure the right things. Follower counts are a vanity metric. More meaningful signals:
- Are people in your field citing or discussing your posts?
- Are you getting invitations—to speak, collaborate, write?
- Are students or policymakers referencing your ideas?
