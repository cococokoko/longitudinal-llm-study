If you want to travel with a lighter environmental footprint, it’s mostly about how you move, where you stay, and what you support along the way.

For getting around, trains and buses are usually the lowest-impact options, especially in regions with good rail networks like Europe or parts of Asia. If you need to fly, nonstop flights are generally better than connections, and economy seats have a smaller per-person footprint than business or first class. Some airlines and third parties offer carbon offset programs, though they’re best treated as a partial mitigation, not a full solution.

Once you’re there, public transport, biking, and walking beat taxis and rental cars. In many cities, bike-share systems or e-scooters make it easy to get around without emissions.

Where you stay matters too. Look for accommodations with credible sustainability certifications (like Green Key or EarthCheck), or smaller locally run guesthouses that use fewer resources than large resorts. Places that use renewable energy, minimize single-use plastics, and manage water responsibly are a good sign.

Your day-to-day choices add up. Carry a reusable water bottle, avoid disposable plastics, and be mindful of water and energy use (like shorter showers and turning off AC when you leave). Eating local, seasonal food—especially more plant-based meals—also reduces your impact.

Finally, consider “slow travel”: staying longer in one place instead of hopping between many destinations. It cuts down transport emissions and usually leads to a richer experience anyway.
