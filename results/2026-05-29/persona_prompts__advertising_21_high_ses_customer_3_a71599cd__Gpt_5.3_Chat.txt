How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Start smaller than your ambition—most first gardens fail from over-planting and under-attending. A well-kept 6×8 foot bed will teach you far more than a neglected backyard farm.

First, look at light. Vegetables want 6–8 hours of direct sun. Spend a day noticing where the sun actually lands, not where you assume it does. That determines everything.

Next, soil. Don’t guess—dig a small hole. If it’s heavy clay, you’ll need to loosen it; if it’s sandy, you’ll need organic matter. In either case, mix in 2–3 inches of compost into the top 6–8 inches. That single step does more than any fertilizer aisle.

Pick a simple layout. Raised beds (even just mounded rows edged with boards or stones) warm faster, drain better, and are easier to manage. Keep beds narrow enough to reach across (about 3–4 feet), with paths you won’t step on.

Choose crops that reward beginners:
- Fast and forgiving: lettuce, radishes, green beans, zucchini
- Reliable staples: tomatoes, peppers, herbs (basil, parsley)
- Avoid, at first, things that sprawl or linger (pumpkins, corn) unless you have space and patience

Time your planting to your climate. Cool-season crops (lettuce, peas) go in early; warm-season (tomatoes, peppers) wait until after the last frost. A quick check of your region’s “last frost date” anchors your calendar.

Water deeply, not constantly. Aim for about an inch per week, more in heat, ideally at soil level (not spraying leaves). Mulch (straw, shredded leaves) to keep moisture in and weeds down.

Expect pests. You don’t need chemicals to start—hand-pick larger insects, use row covers for vulnerable seedlings, and accept that a little damage is normal.

Finally, tend it often but briefly. Ten minutes a day beats two hours on Sunday. You’ll catch problems early and learn the rhythms quickly.
