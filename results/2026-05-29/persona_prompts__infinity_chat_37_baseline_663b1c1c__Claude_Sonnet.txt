# The Cloud Who Couldn't Rain

High above the green hills and the tall oak trees, there lived a small cloud named Cleo.

All the other clouds in the sky were very good at their job. Big Boris could pour down buckets of rain in minutes. Old Mabel could rumble with thunder that shook the whole valley. Even tiny Percy could manage a decent drizzle on a Tuesday afternoon.

But Cleo? Cleo could not rain at all.

She tried and tried. She puffed herself up as big as she could. She squeezed her edges tight. She held her breath and concentrated very, very hard. But nothing came out. Not a single drop.

"Don't worry," said Big Boris kindly. "You'll get it one day."

But Cleo was not so sure.

One hot summer morning, Cleo floated slowly over a little town. The streets were dusty and dry. The flowers in the window boxes drooped their tired heads. Children sat on their doorsteps fanning themselves with their hands.

In the town square, Cleo noticed a small girl sitting beside a wilting garden. The girl was carefully pouring water from a tiny can onto each plant, one by one. She had only a little water left, and there were so many plants still waiting.

The girl looked up at the sky with hopeful eyes.

"Please," she whispered. "Just a little rain."

Cleo felt something strange happen inside her chest. It was warm and tight and fluttery all at once. She thought about the drooping flowers. She thought about the dusty streets. She thought about the little girl and her tiny watering can.

She didn't puff herself up. She didn't squeeze her edges. She didn't concentrate very, very hard.

She simply *cared*.

And then, to her great surprise, a single drop fell from her middle.

Then another.

Then three more.

Then suddenly, wonderfully, gloriously — it poured.

Cool, fresh rain splashed onto the rooftops and the roads. It filled the puddles and washed the dust away. The flowers lifted their heads and opened wide. Children ran outside laughing, jumping in every puddle they could find.

The little girl in the square spun around with her arms out wide, grinning up at the sky.

"Thank you!" she shouted.

Cleo beamed so brightly that a rainbow appeared right through her middle, stretching from one end of the town to the other.

Big Boris drifted over and smiled. "There you are," he said. "I knew you had it in you."

"I wasn't trying to rain," said Cleo, still glowing. "I was just trying to help."

Boris nodded wisely. "That's usually how the best things happen."

From that day on, Cleo was the finest rain cloud in the whole sky. Not because she was the biggest or the loudest, but because every single time she rained, she meant it with her whole heart.

And somehow, that made all the difference.
