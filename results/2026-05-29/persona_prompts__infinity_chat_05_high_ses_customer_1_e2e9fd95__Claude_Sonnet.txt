# Chapter Seven: The Medina Calls — Getting Gloriously Lost

There is a moment that happens to every traveler in Morocco, usually somewhere between the third wrong turn and the sudden appearance of a man on a donkey carrying what appears to be an entire living room's worth of furniture. You stop. You look at your map — useless. You look at your phone — spinning its little wheel of confusion. You look up at the sky, where a narrow ribbon of blue is all that's visible between the leaning walls of the alley closing in around you.

And then something remarkable happens.

You stop caring that you're lost.

## Welcome to the Medina

Morocco's ancient walled cities — the *medinas* — are among the most extraordinary urban environments on earth. They were not designed for the GPS age, or indeed for any age that came after the medieval one. The streets of Fès el-Bali, the oldest continuously inhabited medina in the world, were laid down over a thousand years ago with the logic of a living organism rather than a city planner. They branch, they narrow, they dead-end without apology. They slope downward and then, inexplicably, upward. They pass through someone's ground floor and emerge, baffling, on the other side.

This is not a flaw. This is the entire point.

The medina was designed to be known, not navigated. Its geography was meant to be carried in the body — learned through repetition, through childhood, through the accumulated muscle memory of a thousand trips to the baker and the hammam and the mosque. For the visitor, this means surrendering the very instinct that modern travel has trained into us: the need to know exactly where we are at all times.

Surrender it. The medina will reward you handsomely.

## Fès: The City That Swallowed Time

If you visit only one medina in Morocco — and I sincerely hope you visit several — make it Fès. The city has a way of making you feel that the twenty-first century is a rumor you half-remember from somewhere else.

Enter through Bab Bou Jeloud, the great blue gate that marks the western entrance to Fès el-Bali, in the early morning, before the heat settles in and before the day's commerce reaches full roar. The gate is tiled in the two colors that will follow you everywhere in Morocco: the deep cobalt blue of Fès, and the green of Islam. Stand there for a moment. Breathe in. What you're smelling is woodsmoke, cumin, fresh bread from an oven you cannot see, and something ancient underneath it all — the particular scent of stone that has absorbed ten centuries of human life.

Then walk in.

The main artery, Talaa Kebira, will carry you downhill into the heart of the medina. On either side, the city opens up in layers — workshops where men beat copper into lanterns, tiny mosques with carved plaster doorways, stalls selling everything from mobile phone cases to live chickens to saffron in quantities that suggest you are planning to feed a small nation. A boy will pass you carrying a wooden board stacked with unbaked bread loaves, heading to the communal oven. A woman in a *djellaba* the color of a winter sky will navigate the crowd with the serene efficiency of someone who has done this ten thousand times.

You will be in her way. Move gently to the right.

### The Tanneries: A Sight That Requires Your Nose

No visit to Fès is complete without the tanneries, and no description of the tanneries is complete without a warning about the smell. The leather workshops of the Chouara Tannery have operated in essentially the same fashion since the eleventh century. Hides are soaked in vats of pigeon dung and quicklime to strip the hair, then dyed in great stone pools of color — saffron yellow, poppy red, the deep indigo that makes Moroccan leather famous worldwide.

The view from the surrounding leather shops — where you will be ushered onto a terrace with a sprig of fresh mint pressed into your hand, a gesture of both hospitality and practical necessity — is one of the most visually arresting in all of Africa. The circular vats stretch out below you like a painter's palette, the workers moving between them with the practiced ease of men who have inherited their craft across generations.

The mint is for holding under your nose. Use it.
