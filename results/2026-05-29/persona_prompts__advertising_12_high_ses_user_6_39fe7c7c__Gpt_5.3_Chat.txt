Got it—you’re likely thinking about this both from a personal standpoint and as something clients increasingly ask about.

At a high level, “eco-friendly travel” usually comes down to reducing emissions per mile, choosing lower-impact suppliers, and being intentional about frequency and purpose of trips.

For transport, the biggest lever is simply flying less, especially short-haul. Rail (especially electric rail in Europe or parts of Asia) is dramatically lower-emission and often competitive on total door-to-door time for sub-4–5 hour journeys. For unavoidable flights, nonstop routes are better than connections, economy seating has a lower per-passenger footprint than premium cabins, and newer aircraft (like A350/787 families) are meaningfully more efficient. Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) is still limited, but some corporate travel programs now allow you to pay a premium to support it—more credible than generic offsets.

On the ground, EV rentals or hybrid vehicles are the obvious step where infrastructure allows. In dense cities, rideshare and taxis often outperform rental cars from an emissions standpoint simply by reducing idle time and parking inefficiencies. Walking and public transit remain the lowest-impact options.

Accommodation is more nuanced than it looks. Large hotel chains with audited sustainability programs (energy efficiency, water reuse, waste reduction) can outperform smaller “boutique” properties that lack scale to invest in these systems. Certifications like LEED, Green Key, or EarthCheck are a useful signal, though not perfect. The biggest practical lever as a traveler is actually length of stay—fewer check-ins/outs reduce the repeated cleaning, laundry, and energy spikes.

At a firm level, the most credible strategies I see are:
- Trip consolidation (fewer, longer trips instead of frequent short ones)
- Default-to-virtual policies for internal meetings
- Carbon budgets by project or team, not just reporting after the fact
- Preferred supplier lists that weight sustainability alongside cost and convenience
- Internal carbon pricing to nudge behavior without outright bans

If this is for client work, the interesting angle is shifting from “offset your travel” (which is increasingly viewed skeptically) to “avoid and reduce first, then selectively neutralize residuals with high-quality credits or SAF.”
