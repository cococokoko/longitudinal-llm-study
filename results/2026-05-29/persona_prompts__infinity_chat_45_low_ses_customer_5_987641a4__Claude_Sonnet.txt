Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question with serious arguments on multiple sides.

## Main Positions

**Moral Objectivism** argues morality exists independently of opinions
- Some things seem wrong regardless of what anyone thinks (torturing innocents for fun)
- Moral progress seems real (abolishing slavery wasn't just a preference change)
- We argue about ethics as if there are right answers

**Moral Subjectivism/Relativism** argues morality depends on individuals or cultures
- Moral beliefs vary significantly across cultures and history
- We can't empirically test moral claims like scientific ones
- Disagreements often seem genuinely irresolvable

**Middle Ground positions** worth considering:
- **Constructivism** - morality is built through rational agreement
- **Error Theory** - moral claims aim at objectivity but are all technically false
- **Expressivism** - moral statements express attitudes rather than facts

## Honest Assessment

Strong points exist on multiple sides. I'd note:
- The *existence* of disagreement doesn't automatically prove subjectivity
- Some moral intuitions feel nearly universal
- But "objective" moral facts are philosophically hard to ground

**My honest take:** This remains genuinely contested among serious philosophers, and intellectual humility is warranted rather than strong confidence either way.
