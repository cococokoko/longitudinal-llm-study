How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a backyard vegetable garden is easier than it looks if you keep it simple and build up from there. Here’s a practical way to get going:

First, pick a good spot. Most vegetables need 6–8 hours of direct sunlight a day. Watch your yard for a day and notice where the sun hits the longest. Also make sure it’s a spot you can easily water and access—convenience matters more than you think.

Next, start small. A 4x4 or 4x8 foot area is plenty for a beginner. Big gardens sound exciting but get overwhelming fast.

Soil is everything. If your ground soil is decent, you can loosen it and mix in compost. If it’s rocky, clay-heavy, or poor, consider a raised bed and fill it with a mix of compost and garden soil. Healthy soil will make everything else easier.

Pick easy, beginner-friendly crops. Some reliable ones:
- Lettuce
- Radishes
- Green beans
- Zucchini
- Tomatoes (use stakes or cages)
- Herbs like basil or parsley

Try not to plant everything at once—start with a few crops you actually like to eat.

Decide seeds vs. starter plants. Seeds are cheaper and fine for things like beans, carrots, and lettuce. Starter plants are easier for tomatoes, peppers, and herbs.

Water consistently. Most gardens need about 1 inch of water per week. Deep watering a few times a week is better than light daily sprinkling.

Give plants space and don’t overcrowd. It’s tempting, but crowded plants compete for light and nutrients and get sick more easily.

Expect some trial and error. Pests, weird weather, and mistakes happen—it’s part of gardening, not failure.
