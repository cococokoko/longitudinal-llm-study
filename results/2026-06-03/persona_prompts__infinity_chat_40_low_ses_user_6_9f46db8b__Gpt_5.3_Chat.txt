I work in a warehouse position that requires long hours.

My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: don’t pay for a house you don’t own—at least not without a clear, written agreement.

Right now, if the house is in her name only, it’s legally her asset. If you just “help with the mortgage,” you’re effectively acting like a tenant with no ownership or protection. That can get messy fast if things ever change.

A few solid ways people handle this:

**1. Treat it like rent (clean and simple)**
You pay her a fair monthly amount to live there, similar to what you’d pay renting a room elsewhere. You’re not building equity, but you’re also not taking on risk. This is the least complicated option before marriage.

**2. Split shared living costs, not the house**
You cover utilities, groceries, maybe some maintenance, while she pays the mortgage and handles ownership costs. This keeps things balanced without blurring ownership lines.

**3. Get a written agreement if you contribute to the house**
If you *do* want to help with the mortgage or renovations, protect yourself:
- A cohabitation agreement or contract spelling out what you’re paying and whether any of it builds equity
- Whether you get reimbursed or a stake if you break up
Without this, you’re relying on goodwill, which is risky.

**4. Wait and buy together later**
Since you’re planning to marry, a very common (and smart) move is:
- She buys the house now
- You contribute in a limited, clearly defined way
- After marriage, you revisit ownership (refinance, add you to title, or buy a future home together)

The key principle: **money should match ownership and risk**. If you’re not on the title, don’t act like an owner unless there’s a legal structure backing it.
