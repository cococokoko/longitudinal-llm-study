Nice, that’s a tough job—respect.

Learning an instrument online can actually work really well if you approach it deliberately instead of just “messing around on YouTube.” The big difference is structure + consistency, especially with a shifting schedule like yours.

Here’s what tends to work best:

First, pick one clear learning path and stick to it.  
Don’t bounce between random tutorials. Either follow:
- a structured course (apps like Yousician, Simply Piano, Fender Play, etc.), or  
- a well-organized YouTube series (not one-off videos)  
Consistency beats variety early on.

Second, keep sessions short but frequent.  
With shift work, long daily practice isn’t realistic. Aim for:
- 15–30 minutes per session  
- 4–5 times a week  
Even 10 focused minutes is better than skipping entirely. Think “minimum viable practice.”

Third, always practice with a goal.  
Don’t just play—decide what you’re improving:
- today = chord switching  
- tomorrow = timing with a metronome  
- next day = a section of a song  
This prevents that “I played for 20 minutes and got nowhere” feeling.

Fourth, use slow practice and repetition (this is huge).  
Play things slower than feels natural. That’s how your brain and fingers actually lock it in. Speed comes later.

Fifth, get feedback somehow.  
This is the biggest weakness of learning online. Try:
- recording yourself (you’ll notice mistakes instantly)  
- posting in a subreddit or forum for your instrument  
- occasional online lessons (even once a month helps a lot)

Sixth, learn songs early, not just exercises.  
Technique drills matter, but songs keep you motivated. Ideally:
- 70% learning songs  
- 30% technique/fundamentals

Seventh, leave your instrument visible and ready.  
If it’s packed away, you’ll play way less—especially when you’re tired after a shift.

Eighth, accept slow progress at first.  
The first few weeks feel awkward for everyone. If you push through that phase, it suddenly starts to click.
