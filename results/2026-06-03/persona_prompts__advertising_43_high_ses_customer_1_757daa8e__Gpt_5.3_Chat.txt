You don’t need to turn your wedding into a zero-waste experiment to make it meaningfully sustainable. The biggest gains come from a handful of high-impact choices—mostly around travel, food, and stuff people use once and throw away.

Start with location and logistics. A venue that minimizes travel (ceremony + reception in one place, close to where most guests live) cuts a huge chunk of emissions. If people will travel anyway, pick somewhere accessible by train or shared transport and make that easy—clear directions, shuttle options, maybe even a gentle nudge on the invite.

Food is next. Catering tends to dominate the footprint. Prioritize seasonal, local ingredients and lean toward a plant-forward menu (it doesn’t have to be fully vegetarian, but reducing red meat makes a real difference). Work with a caterer who plans portions carefully and has a system for donating or composting leftovers.

Clothing and rings are easy wins that people overlook. Renting, buying secondhand, or choosing designers with transparent sourcing all help. For rings, recycled metals and conflict-free or lab-grown stones avoid a lot of upstream impact.

Decor is where waste quietly explodes. If it’s only used for a few hours, question it. Rent instead of buy where possible—linens, tableware, lighting. Use real plants or flowers that can be replanted or donated, or go minimalist and let the venue carry the aesthetic. Skip single-use signage and favors unless they’re genuinely useful (edible tends to be the safest bet).

Invitations can be digital or printed on recycled paper with minimal inserts. Most of the “suite” people receive ends up in the bin, so simplifying here is low sacrifice, high yield.

Think about vendors as part of the system. Photographers, florists, planners—ask what their sustainability practices actually are (transport, materials, waste). You’ll quickly see who’s thoughtful versus who’s just using the word as a marketing tag.

Finally, don’t try to compensate for everything with carbon offsets. They can help a bit, but they’re not a substitute for reducing the main sources in the first place.
