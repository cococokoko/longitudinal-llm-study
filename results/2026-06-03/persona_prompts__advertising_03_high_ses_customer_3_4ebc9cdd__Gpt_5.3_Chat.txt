Working from home tends to fail or flourish on structure. Most people don’t actually need more “tips”—they need a system that reduces decision fatigue and protects attention.

A few levers that reliably matter:

First, make the start of your day automatic. Decide in advance when and how you begin work, and remove choices. Same location, same first task, same sequence. The goal is to eliminate the daily negotiation with yourself. If you begin by checking email or news, you’ve already surrendered your highest-quality cognitive window.

Second, treat attention as your primary resource, not time. Two or three uninterrupted blocks of 60–90 minutes will outperform an entire day of fragmented work. This means aggressively controlling inputs: silence notifications, close tabs, and—if needed—use site blockers. It feels extreme, but it’s the digital equivalent of closing your office door.

Third, define what “done” means before you start. Vague tasks (“work on paper,” “prepare lecture”) invite drift. Concrete targets (“draft section 2 argument with citations,” “produce 10-slide lecture outline”) create momentum and a clear stopping point.

Fourth, separate “deep work” from “administrative gravity.” Email, meetings, and small requests expand to fill the day if you let them. Batch them into a fixed window—ideally later in the day when your cognitive energy is lower. You’re not being unresponsive; you’re being deliberate.

Fifth, engineer friction in the right places. Make it harder to procrastinate and easier to begin. Keep your workspace prepared, documents open, notes visible. Conversely, make distractions slightly inconvenient—log out of social media, keep your phone in another room, or at least out of reach.

Sixth, use a visible constraint to end the day. Work from home blurs boundaries, which paradoxically reduces both productivity and recovery. Decide when you stop, and have a small ritual that signals closure—write tomorrow’s first task, shut down your workspace, leave the room.

Finally, don’t ignore physical context. Light, posture, and movement have measurable effects on focus. Even short walks between work blocks can reset attention more effectively than pushing through fatigue.

If you want to refine this further, the interesting question isn’t “how do I work more,” but “when, specifically, do I produce my best thinking—and how do I protect that window?” That’s where most of the gains are.
