I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

What are the best strategies for investing in the stock market?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, the question isn’t really “how do I pick stocks,” it’s “how do I structure an investment system that compounds reliably without demanding attention or introducing hidden risks.”

The best strategies tend to be surprisingly simple, but executed with discipline and scale:

First, decide whether you actually want to generate alpha or just capture it.  
Most high-performing professionals are better off focusing on their primary income engine (which already has a high return on time) and treating public markets as a compounding vehicle. That usually means a core allocation to low-cost, diversified index funds (global equities, maybe some tilt to factors like value or quality if you believe in them). It’s not intellectually exciting, but it’s very hard to beat after fees and taxes.

Second, if you do want to pursue alpha, be honest about your edge.  
Edge usually comes from one of three places: informational (you know something others don’t), analytical (you interpret data better), or behavioral (you stay rational when others don’t). Most people overestimate all three. As a consultant, your realistic edge is probably behavioral and structural, not informational. That points toward strategies like:
- systematically buying during drawdowns  
- rebalancing when others won’t  
- holding concentrated positions only when you have true conviction and time horizon  

Third, use a “core–satellite” approach.  
Keep the majority (say 70–90%) in a low-cost, diversified core. Then allocate a smaller “satellite” portion to higher-conviction or higher-risk ideas—individual stocks, thematic bets, private markets, or even tactical plays. This scratches the intellectual itch without jeopardizing long-term compounding.

Fourth, think hard about taxes and friction.  
At higher income levels, after-tax return matters more than gross return. That means:
- minimizing turnover  
- using tax-advantaged accounts efficiently  
- harvesting losses when appropriate  
- being cautious with short-term trading or high-fee products  

Fifth, avoid the common traps that catch high-achievers.  
People in demanding careers often:
- overtrade because they’re used to being active problem-solvers  
- chase complexity (structured products, niche funds) that look sophisticated but don’t outperform  
- overconcentrate in familiar sectors (e.g., tech, consulting clients, or “what they understand”)  

Sixth, align your portfolio with your life constraints.  
Your real scarce resource is attention. If your strategy requires constant monitoring, it’s probably mis-specified. A good portfolio should survive long stretches of neglect without blowing up.

If you want something concrete, a very robust baseline looks like:
- globally diversified equity index exposure as the core  
- a modest allocation to bonds or alternatives depending on risk tolerance  
- periodic rebalancing (e.g., annually or threshold-based)  
- optional satellite sleeve for higher-conviction ideas
