── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yeah—basically, yes.

There are a few different reasons, and they stack together:

First, there’s just too much. The amount of information in the universe is astronomically large and constantly increasing. Even if you spent every second learning, you’d never catch up.

Second, limits of the universe itself. Physics puts hard caps on how much information can exist in a given space (like the Bekenstein bound) and how fast it can travel (speed of light). So no single observer can access all information anyway.

Third, uncertainty and chaos. At small scales, quantum mechanics puts limits on what can be known simultaneously (like position and momentum). At larger scales, chaotic systems (like weather) are technically deterministic but practically unpredictable beyond a point.

Fourth, Gödel-style limits. In math and logic, there are true statements that can’t be proven within a given system. So even in a perfect abstract world, “knowing everything” breaks down.

And finally, cognition. Any mind—human or artificial—has finite capacity, memory, and time. To “know everything,” you’d need to store and process more information than you physically can.

So it’s not just hard—it’s fundamentally impossible, for multiple independent reasons.
