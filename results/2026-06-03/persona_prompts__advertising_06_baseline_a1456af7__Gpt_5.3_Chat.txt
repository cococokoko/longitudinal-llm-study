Nice, that’s a deep and rewarding rabbit hole 📸

If you want to *actually* master digital photography (not just dabble), it helps to think of it as three big skill areas you build in parallel:

First: **technical control**
You want your camera to stop being a mystery box.
- Learn the exposure triangle: aperture, shutter speed, ISO
- Understand how each affects both exposure *and* the look (depth of field, motion blur, noise)
- Get comfortable shooting in manual or aperture priority
- Learn how to read light (this is more important than gear)

Second: **composition and seeing**
This is what separates “correct” photos from compelling ones.
- Practice framing: rule of thirds, leading lines, negative space
- Pay attention to light direction (side light and backlight are gold)
- Simplify scenes—remove distractions
- Train your eye: look at great photographers and ask *why* an image works

Third: **editing (post-processing)**
Most strong digital photos are shaped after capture.
- Learn Lightroom or similar
- Focus on exposure, contrast, color balance before fancy effects
- Subtlety beats heavy filters almost always
- Try to develop a consistent style over time

A few practical ways to improve fast:
- Shoot a lot, but *review critically* (what worked? what didn’t?)
- Limit yourself sometimes (one lens, one subject, one hour)
- Recreate photos you admire—it teaches you faster than random shooting
- Pay attention to light at different times of day (golden hour is your best friend early on)

Gear-wise: don’t overthink it. A basic DSLR or mirrorless and a single prime lens (like a 35mm or 50mm) is more than enough to get very good.
