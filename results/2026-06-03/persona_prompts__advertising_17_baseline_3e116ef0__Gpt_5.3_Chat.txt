── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good question—this is one of those areas where a few smart changes can noticeably cut bills and improve comfort.

Start with the “low effort, high impact” stuff:

- Seal air leaks. Drafts around windows, doors, attic hatches, and outlets can waste a lot of energy. Weatherstripping, caulk, and door sweeps are cheap and effective.
- Switch to LED lighting. If you still have incandescent or halogen bulbs, replacing them can cut lighting energy use by 70–90%.
- Use a programmable or smart thermostat. Automatically lowering heating or raising cooling when you’re asleep or away saves a lot over time.
- Adjust your water heater. Setting it to around 120°F (49°C) reduces energy use and lowers scald risk.

Then move to bigger wins:

- Improve insulation. Attic insulation is usually the best bang for buck. Walls and floors help too, but attic upgrades are often easiest and cheapest.
- Upgrade to efficient appliances. Look for ENERGY STAR models, especially for refrigerators, washers, and HVAC systems.
- Maintain your HVAC system. Clean filters regularly and service the system annually to keep it running efficiently.
- Consider a heat pump. If you’re replacing heating or cooling, modern heat pumps are very efficient and can handle both.

Behavior tweaks also matter more than people expect:

- Wash clothes in cold water and air-dry when possible.
- Run full loads in the dishwasher and laundry.
- Close blinds/curtains in summer to block heat, open them in winter to let sunlight in.

If you want to go further:

- Install solar panels (if your roof and budget allow).
- Get a home energy audit—many utilities offer them free or cheap, and they’ll point out the best upgrades for your specific home.

If you tell me your climate and whether you own or rent, I can narrow this down to the most cost-effective steps for your situation.
