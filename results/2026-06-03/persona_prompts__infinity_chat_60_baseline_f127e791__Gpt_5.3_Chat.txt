── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a super cool Lego machine. You put in one block, and it builds something step by step—click, click, click.

Now you want to know what it will look like after 100 clicks.

You might think: “Can I just *skip ahead* and see the final thing right away?”

But with some machines, you *can’t*. The only way to know what it looks like at step 100 is to watch (or run) all 100 steps.

That idea is called **computational irreducibility**.

It means:
Even if you’re really smart, you can’t take a shortcut.  
You have to let the process play out step by step to see what happens.

It’s like:
- Watching popcorn pop 🍿 — you can’t know exactly which kernel pops next without waiting  
- Or growing a plant 🌱 — you can’t jump to day 30 without going through days 1–29

Some things in the world are like that—they only reveal themselves by unfolding, one step at a time.
