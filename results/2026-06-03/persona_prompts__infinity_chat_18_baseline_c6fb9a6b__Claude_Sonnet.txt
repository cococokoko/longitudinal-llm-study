# Review: *The Last Frequency* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Okonkwo's debut feature is a quiet devastation. Set in a dying radio station in rural Montana, the film follows two estranged siblings who discover their late father left behind recordings that seem to predict events that haven't happened yet.

What could have been a cheap thriller instead becomes something far more patient and strange. Okonkwo resists every obvious plot turn, letting the mystery breathe alongside a genuinely moving portrait of grief. The performances are extraordinary — particularly Dominic Sessa as the skeptical younger brother, whose slow unraveling feels completely earned.

The cinematography leans into long, uncomfortable silences and wide winter landscapes that make the characters feel appropriately small against something they can't explain.

**The flaws are real.** The third act stumbles slightly, trading the film's careful ambiguity for answers that feel a little too tidy. And one subplot involving a local sheriff goes essentially nowhere.

But *The Last Frequency* lingers. It's the kind of film that makes you want to call someone you've been avoiding. In a year crowded with loud, exhausting spectacle, it arrives like a signal from somewhere quieter and more honest.

*Opens in limited release November 8th.*
