── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some key considerations:

## Arguments that complete knowledge is impossible

- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest any sufficiently complex formal system contains true statements that can't be proven within that system
- **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** places fundamental limits on simultaneously knowing certain physical properties
- **The knower problem** - knowing everything would include knowing your own mental states completely, which may be self-referentially problematic
- **Infinite information** - if reality contains infinite facts, a finite mind couldn't contain them all
- **The universe's scale** - practical limitations on information gathering

## Reasons to be cautious about strong claims

- "Impossible" is a strong claim - we should distinguish *practically* impossible from *logically* impossible
- Some arguments assume specific things about the nature of knowledge and reality
- We don't fully understand consciousness or what "knowing" fundamentally means

## Honest assessment

There are **strong reasons** to think complete omniscience faces genuine logical and physical barriers, not just practical ones. The mathematical and physical limits seem fairly robust.

But philosophers genuinely debate whether these arguments are conclusive, and some theological traditions argue omniscience is coherent.
