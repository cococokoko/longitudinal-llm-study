── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

Give me a tip for managing a team of coworkers.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Create “decision clarity” for your team.

In research groups, friction often comes from people not knowing who owns what or how decisions actually get made. Even very capable scientists stall or duplicate effort when this is fuzzy. A simple fix is to explicitly define, for each project: who is responsible for driving it, who needs to be consulted, and who has final say when there’s disagreement.

You don’t need a heavy framework—just make it visible. At the start of a project or meeting, state something like: “Alex is leading this analysis; Priya and I are advisors; I’ll make the final call if we’re split.” That one sentence can prevent weeks of drift or quiet tension.

It also frees your trainees: they know when they can move fast and when they need input, which is especially valuable in a setting where hierarchy and uncertainty can otherwise slow everything down.
