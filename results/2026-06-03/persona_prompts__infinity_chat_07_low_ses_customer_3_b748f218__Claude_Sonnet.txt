Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
That's a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are some key considerations:

## Arguments that complete knowledge is impossible

- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even mathematics has true statements that can't be proven within a system
- **Physical limits** - the universe may contain more information than any mind could process
- **The observer problem** - knowing something can change it (quantum mechanics)
- **Infinite regress** - knowing everything might require knowing the context of that knowledge, endlessly
- **Constantly changing reality** - new things come into existence continuously

## Arguments for more optimism about knowledge

- We can know things **completely within defined domains**
- Knowledge can be **practically sufficient** even without being total
- Some things may genuinely be **fully knowable**

## Honest assessment

Most philosophers and scientists would say **yes, complete omniscience is effectively impossible** for several reasons:
- The universe appears genuinely complex beyond any finite mind's capacity
- Some uncertainty seems **fundamental**, not just a gap we haven't filled yet

## Worth noting

The question itself is interesting because **claiming to know it's impossible** requires a kind of broad knowledge about the limits of knowledge.

It's a question that touches on epistemology, physics, and mathematics simultaneously.
