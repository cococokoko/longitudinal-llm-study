This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question worth thinking carefully about.

## Several angles worth considering:

**Practical arguments for impossibility:**
- The universe contains more information than any finite mind could store
- Knowledge is constantly being generated faster than any individual could absorb
- Some knowledge requires direct experience that can't all be accumulated by one person

**Deeper theoretical arguments:**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even formal systems can't be complete and consistent simultaneously
- **Heisenberg's uncertainty principle** places physical limits on simultaneous knowledge of certain properties
- **Epistemic limitations** - we may lack the cognitive architecture to grasp certain truths

**Complications worth noting:**
- "Everything" needs definition - everything *knowable*? Everything *existing*?
- There's a distinction between **knowing that** something exists versus knowing its content
- Some philosophers argue certain things are **structurally unknowable** (things-in-themselves, in Kant's framework)

**Honest counterpoints:**
- We should be careful not to assume current cognitive limitations are permanent
- "Impossible" is a strong claim requiring strong justification

**My honest assessment:** There are strong theoretical and practical reasons to think complete omniscience is impossible, but the question touches on genuinely unresolved philosophical problems.
