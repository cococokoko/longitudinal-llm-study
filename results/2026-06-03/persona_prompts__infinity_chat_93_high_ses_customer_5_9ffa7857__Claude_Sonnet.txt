# How Stress Affects the Body and Mind

Stress triggers a cascade of physiological and psychological responses that, while useful in short bursts, can become damaging when chronic.

## Physical Impact

When you encounter a stressor, your body activates the **fight-or-flight response**, releasing cortisol and adrenaline. Your heart rate increases, blood pressure rises, and muscles tense. In the short term, this sharpens performance. Over time, however, sustained cortisol elevation contributes to:

- Cardiovascular disease and hypertension
- Weakened immune function
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Digestive issues and inflammation
- Increased risk of metabolic disorders

## Mental and Cognitive Impact

Chronic stress directly impairs the brain. The prefrontal cortex — responsible for **decision-making, strategic thinking, and emotional regulation** — becomes less effective under prolonged stress. The amygdala, your threat-detection center, becomes hyperactive, making it harder to think clearly and respond rationally.

This matters significantly in high-stakes leadership environments. Executives under sustained pressure often experience reduced cognitive flexibility, diminished creativity, and impaired judgment — precisely when clear thinking is most critical.

## The Compounding Effect

Stress also disrupts sleep, which further degrades cognitive performance, creating a reinforcing cycle that compounds over time.

## Managing the Impact

The good news is that targeted interventions — structured recovery time, physical exercise, mindfulness practices, and clear boundaries between work and rest — demonstrably reduce cortisol levels and restore cognitive function.

Understanding stress as a **physiological and neurological phenomenon**, rather than simply a feeling, helps reframe recovery not as a luxury but as a strategic necessity for sustained high performance.
