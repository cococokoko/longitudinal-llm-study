A good way to think about meditation is not as “clearing your mind,” but as training attention and awareness. You’re practicing noticing what’s happening—breath, thoughts, sensations—without immediately getting pulled around by it.

Here’s a simple, workable entry point that avoids a lot of common beginner traps:

Start very small and very regular. Five minutes a day is enough to build the habit. Sit somewhere reasonably quiet, but it doesn’t have to be perfectly silent. Set a timer so you’re not checking the clock.

Use the breath as an anchor. Sit upright but not rigid, and bring your attention to the sensation of breathing—air at the nose, chest movement, or abdomen rising and falling. Pick one spot and stick with it.

Expect your mind to wander. It will, often within seconds. That’s not failure; that *is* the exercise. Each time you notice you’ve drifted into thinking, gently return to the breath. That “noticing and returning” is the core repetition that builds the skill.

Be precise but not forceful. You’re not trying to clamp down on thoughts. Let them arise and pass, but don’t follow them. If it helps, you can quietly label experiences—“thinking,” “hearing,” “planning”—and then return to the breath.

After a week or two, you can expand slightly:
- Increase to 10–15 minutes if it feels sustainable.
- Broaden awareness to include body sensations or sounds, not just breath.
- Occasionally try a “body scan,” moving attention slowly from head to toe.

Two things that tend to matter more than people expect:

First, consistency beats intensity. A daily 5–10 minute practice will change your baseline attention more than sporadic longer sessions.

Second, the effects are subtle but cumulative. You’re not aiming for a particular feeling during meditation. The payoff often shows up outside the session—more stability of attention, a bit more space before reacting, clearer thinking.

If you want structure, a short guided program (e.g., apps like Waking Up, Headspace, or UCLA’s free recordings) can help establish the basics, but it’s not strictly necessary.
