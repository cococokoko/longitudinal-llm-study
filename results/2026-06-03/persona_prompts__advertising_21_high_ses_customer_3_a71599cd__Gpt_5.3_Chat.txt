How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A good vegetable garden starts less with planting and more with choosing the right constraints—light, soil, and scale—and then working with them rather than against them.

First, look carefully at light. Most vegetables want 6–8 hours of direct sun. If your yard has only partial sun, you’re not excluded—you just shift your crop choices toward leafy greens, herbs, and perhaps peas. It’s worth observing for a few days where the sun actually falls; intuition is often misleading here.

Next, soil. People often rush past this, but it’s the quiet determinant of everything that follows. If you can, do a simple soil test (many university extensions offer inexpensive kits). In the meantime, assume improvement is needed: mix in compost generously. Think of compost less as “fertilizer” and more as building a stable ecosystem your plants can draw from.

On layout, resist the temptation to go large. A small, well-tended plot will outperform an ambitious, neglected one. Something like a few raised beds (or even containers, if space is tight) gives you control and reduces early frustration. Keep beds narrow enough that you can reach the center without stepping on the soil—compaction matters more than most beginners expect.

For crops, start with things that are forgiving and actually worth growing fresh: lettuce, radishes, zucchini, green beans, herbs, and perhaps tomatoes if you’re willing to stake them. These give quick feedback, which is pedagogically useful—you’ll see cause and effect within weeks rather than months.

Watering should be consistent rather than heroic. Deep, occasional watering encourages roots to grow down; frequent shallow watering does the opposite. Mulch (straw, shredded leaves) helps retain moisture and stabilizes soil temperature—small intervention, disproportionate payoff.

Finally, treat the first season as an experiment rather than a production goal. Keep a few notes—what you planted, when, and what happened. Gardening rewards iteration more than initial correctness.
