How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a backyard veggie garden is easier than it looks—you just want to set yourself up so the plants do most of the work.

First, pick a good spot. Most vegetables need at least 6–8 hours of direct sun per day. Watch your yard for a day if you’re unsure. Also make sure the area drains well (no standing water after rain) and is reasonably close to a water source.

Next, start small. A common mistake is going too big and getting overwhelmed. Even a single 4x8 ft bed can grow a lot of food. You can plant in the ground, raised beds, or even large containers—raised beds are especially beginner-friendly because you control the soil quality.

Soil matters more than anything. If you’re planting in the ground, loosen the soil and mix in compost. If you’re using a raised bed, fill it with a mix of topsoil, compost, and something for drainage (like coarse sand or perlite). Healthy soil = healthy plants.

Choose easy crops to begin with. Good beginner vegetables include:
- Lettuce, spinach, arugula (fast and forgiving)
- Radishes (super quick wins)
- Tomatoes (very rewarding, but need support)
- Zucchini (productive, almost too productive)
- Green beans (bush types are easiest)
- Herbs like basil, parsley, mint (mint should go in a container—it spreads aggressively)

Plant at the right time. This depends on your climate, but generally:
- Cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, peas) go in early spring or fall
- Warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, zucchini) go in after the last frost

Water consistently. Most gardens need about 1 inch of water per week. It’s better to water deeply a few times a week than lightly every day. Morning watering is best.

Expect some trial and error. Pests, weird weather, and mistakes are part of it. You don’t need perfection—you just need to learn as you go.
