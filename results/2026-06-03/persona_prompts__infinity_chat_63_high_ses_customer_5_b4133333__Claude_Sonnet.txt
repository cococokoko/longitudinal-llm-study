# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## The Business Scenario

Suppose your company's **quarterly revenue** (in millions) depends on two variables:

- **x** = marketing spend (millions $)
- **y** = engineering headcount (hundreds of engineers)

$$R(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + 5y^2$$

---

## Setting Up the Example

Let's evaluate at a **specific operating point**: **x = 4** ($4M marketing), **y = 3** (300 engineers)

$$R(4, 3) = 3(16) + 2(4)(3) + 5(9) = 48 + 24 + 45 = \$117M$$

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

**∂R/∂x** — *How much does revenue change per additional $1M in marketing, holding headcount fixed?*

$$\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y \quad \Rightarrow \quad 6(4) + 2(3) = \mathbf{30}$$

> Adding $1M in marketing yields ~**$30M more revenue** at this operating point

**∂R/∂y** — *How much does revenue change per 100 additional engineers, holding marketing fixed?*

$$\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} = 2x + 10y \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2(4) + 10(3) = \mathbf{38}$$

> Adding 100 engineers yields ~**$38M more revenue** at this operating point

---

## The Key Insight

| Decision | Marginal Return |
|----------|----------------|
| +$1M Marketing | +$30M revenue |
| +100 Engineers | +38M revenue |

**The partial derivative isolates the impact of one variable while treating everything else as constant** — exactly what you need when making resource allocation decisions with multiple levers.

> ⚠️ These are *local* estimates. The returns change as x and y change, which is why the operating point matters.
