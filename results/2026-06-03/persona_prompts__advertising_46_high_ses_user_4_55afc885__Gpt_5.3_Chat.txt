I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great question—and it’s nice to see this coming from someone who’s likely thinking about both evidence and practical implementation.

I’ll break this into three layers: high-quality evidence-based guidance, practical clinician-oriented resources, and then “real life” tools for actually doing it.

First, evidence-based and medically grounded resources:
- The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics position paper on vegetarian diets (regularly updated) is still one of the most rigorous summaries of nutritional adequacy, risks, and planning considerations.
- The EAT-Lancet Commission report is useful if you’re also thinking about population health and sustainability, though it’s broader than strictly “plant-based.”
- The Adventist Health Studies (AHS-2 in particular) and EPIC-Oxford datasets are worth revisiting if you want primary literature on outcomes.
- For cardiometabolic disease specifically, Dean Ornish and Caldwell Esselstyn’s interventional work is often cited, but I’d read it alongside more contemporary meta-analyses to contextualize effect sizes and adherence issues.

Clinician-oriented and translational resources:
- The American College of Lifestyle Medicine (ACLM) has very practical toolkits, including prescribing plant-based diets, billing considerations, and patient handouts.
- Kaiser Permanente’s “Plant-Based Diets: A Physician’s Guide” is concise and clinically usable.
- The Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM) offers CME courses and protocols; they’re more advocacy-forward, so it’s worth pairing with neutral sources, but their materials are very actionable.
- NutritionFacts.org (Michael Greger) is exhaustive and citation-heavy; it’s useful as a literature aggregator, though you’ll want to independently verify key claims given the strong framing.

For nutritional adequacy and common pitfalls (this is where many implementations fail):
- Key nutrients to actively manage: B12 (non-negotiable supplementation), iodine, iron (especially in premenopausal women), calcium, vitamin D, omega-3s (EPA/DHA—consider algae-based sources), and protein distribution in older adults.
- The NIH Office of Dietary Supplements and Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health both have solid, relatively unbiased nutrient-specific guidance.
- VeganHealth.org (Jack Norris, RD) is one of the more careful, detail-oriented sites on micronutrient planning.

For actually helping people adopt and sustain it:
- “How Not to Die” (Greger) and “Eat to Beat Disease” (Li) are popular entry points, but for a more clinically grounded tone, “The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners” (Shah) or similar RD-authored guides are often easier for patients.
- Forks Over Knives (website + meal planner) is very effective for behavior change, though it leans toward low-fat whole-food plant-based, which isn’t necessary for everyone.
- Apps like Cronometer are useful early on for both patients and researchers to sanity-check micronutrient intake patterns.

If you’re thinking as a research group leader, a few additional angles might be worth your time:
- Adherence and behavioral economics matter more than macronutrient ideology—interventions that allow some flexibility (e.g., “plant-forward” vs strictly vegan) often outperform in real-world settings.
- The heterogeneity within “plant-based” is huge; ultra-processed plant foods can attenuate or erase expected benefits, which is becoming clearer in newer cohort analyses.
- There’s still an evidence gap around long-term outcomes in diverse populations and in older adults with sarcopenia risk—protein quality and distribution become non-trivial there.
