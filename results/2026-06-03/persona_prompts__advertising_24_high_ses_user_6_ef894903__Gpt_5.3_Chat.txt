── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, this is less about “which account” and more about tax arbitrage, liquidity planning, and not over-concentrating in your firm. The usual advice (max your 401(k), etc.) is table stakes—you’re likely already doing that or should be.

Here’s how I’d think about it in layers:

First, exhaust tax-advantaged space  
You want to aggressively use anything that shelters growth or shifts taxation.

- 401(k): Max it, including catch-up if applicable. If your plan allows after-tax contributions + in-plan Roth conversion (mega backdoor Roth), that’s one of the highest-value moves available.
- Backdoor Roth IRA: Still worth doing annually unless income limits or pro-rata rules complicate it.
- HSA (if eligible): Treat it as a stealth retirement account—invest it, don’t spend it.
- Deferred compensation plan (NQDC): Many consulting partners have access. This can be powerful, but it’s not risk-free (you’re an unsecured creditor of the firm). Best used if:
  - You expect lower tax rates later, and
  - You’re not already overly exposed to your firm’s financial health.

Second, manage tax diversification  
You want money in three buckets: taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free.

- Tax-deferred (401k, NQDC): Good for high-income years.
- Tax-free (Roth): Extremely valuable later for flexibility and estate planning.
- Taxable brokerage: Underrated. Gives you liquidity, capital gains treatment, and flexibility before retirement age.

High earners often over-index on tax deferral and end up with very large future RMDs. That creates a different tax problem later.

Third, invest simply—but tax-efficiently  
At your income level, complexity rarely beats discipline.

- Broad, low-cost index exposure (global equities, some fixed income as you age).
- Use tax-efficient funds/ETFs in taxable accounts.
- Place less tax-efficient assets (like bonds, REITs) inside tax-deferred accounts.
- Tax-loss harvest consistently in taxable accounts.

Fourth, think about concentration risk (especially for partners)  
If your net worth is tied to:
- Your firm’s equity or profit distributions, and
- Your future earning power from that same firm,

you already have a lot of exposure to one “asset.”

So:
- Avoid over-investing in your firm if optional.
- Build a diversified portfolio outside it.
- Be cautious with deferred comp if it materially increases firm-specific risk.

Fifth, plan for uneven income and lifestyle creep  
Partner income can be high but volatile.

- Anchor your lifestyle below your “good year” income.
- Use windfall years to fund taxable investments or pre-fund future commitments.
- Consider smoothing strategies (e.g., deferral, donor-advised funds for charitable bunching).

Sixth, optimize around taxes at the margins  
This is where a lot of value hides:

- Roth conversions in lower-income years (e.g., post-retirement, pre-RMD).
- Donor-advised funds if you give to charity—especially in high-income years.
- Direct indexing or SMAs for tax-loss harvesting at scale.
- Municipal bonds if you’re in a very high tax bracket and need fixed income in taxable accounts.

Seventh, don’t ignore insurance and estate structure  
At your wealth trajectory:

- Umbrella insurance is essential.
- Basic estate plan (will, revocable trust, healthcare directives).
- Consider more advanced structures later (e.g., GRATs, SLATs) if your net worth continues to grow.
