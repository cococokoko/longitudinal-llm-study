── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Learning a new language effectively.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The biggest mistake people make is treating language learning like a school subject instead of a skill. You don’t “study” your way into fluency—you build it through use.

Here’s a way that actually works in practice:

Start with high-frequency basics  
Don’t try to learn everything. Focus on the most common words, phrases, and sentence patterns first. A few hundred high-use words get you surprisingly far.

Use the language daily (even badly)  
You need output, not just input. Speak, write, or think in the language every day. It will feel slow and awkward—that’s not failure, that’s the process working.

Comprehensible input beats random exposure  
Listening or reading works best when you understand most of it but still have to stretch a little. If you understand 90–95%, you’re in the sweet spot. If you understand nothing, you’re just drowning.

Spaced repetition for memory  
Use flashcards (like Anki) for vocabulary and phrases. The key isn’t cramming—it’s revisiting things just before you forget them.

Learn phrases, not just words  
Instead of memorizing isolated words, learn chunks like “I’m looking for…” or “Do you mind if…”. This helps you sound natural faster.

Speak early, but don’t obsess over perfection  
Waiting until you “feel ready” delays progress. You won’t feel ready. Start anyway. Mistakes are how your brain adjusts.

Immerse your environment  
Switch your phone language, watch shows, listen to music, follow social media in the language. Even passive exposure helps reinforce patterns.

Have a clear, personal reason  
“Because I should” won’t carry you. “I want to talk to X,” “move to Y,” or “understand Z” will.

If you want, tell me which language you’re learning and your goal (travel, fluency, conversation, etc.), and I can map out a more specific plan.
