# How Stress Affects the Body and Mind

Stress is a natural response designed to help us handle challenges, but when it becomes chronic, it can take a serious toll on both physical and mental health.

## Physical Effects

When the brain perceives a threat, it triggers the release of hormones like **cortisol and adrenaline**. These chemicals prepare the body for a "fight or flight" response — increasing heart rate, tensing muscles, and sharpening focus. While useful in short bursts, prolonged exposure to these hormones can cause:

- Headaches and muscle tension
- Digestive problems and weakened immunity
- Elevated blood pressure and increased risk of heart disease
- Disrupted sleep patterns and chronic fatigue

## Mental and Emotional Effects

Stress doesn't stop at the body. It significantly impacts how we think and feel. Chronic stress can lead to:

- **Anxiety and irritability**
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Memory problems
- Depression and emotional exhaustion

The brain's prefrontal cortex — responsible for rational thinking — becomes less effective under stress, while the amygdala, which drives emotional reactions, becomes overactive. This imbalance explains why stressed individuals often feel overwhelmed and reactive.

## Finding Balance

Recognizing stress early is essential. Simple strategies like **regular exercise, mindfulness, adequate sleep, and social connection** can significantly reduce its impact. Seeking professional support when stress feels unmanageable is equally important.

Understanding how stress works empowers us to respond to it more effectively, protecting both our physical health and mental well-being before long-term damage occurs.
