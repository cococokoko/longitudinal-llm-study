If you want the fastest meaningful bump, focus on the handful of factors that move scores quickly rather than trying to “optimize everything.”

First, get your credit utilization down—this is usually the fastest lever. If any of your cards are reporting balances above ~30% of their limits, pay them down before the statement closes (not just before the due date). Getting utilization under 10% on each card, and ideally overall, can move your score within a single reporting cycle.

Second, eliminate any missed payments immediately. If you’ve had a recent late payment, bring the account current and consider asking for a goodwill adjustment—sometimes lenders will remove a one-off late mark if you have a solid history. Going forward, set autopay on at least the minimum so you never miss again. Payment history is the biggest factor.

Third, ask for credit limit increases (without hard pulls). If your income supports it, higher limits reduce utilization instantly without needing to pay down as much. Many issuers allow this via a soft inquiry.

Fourth, don’t close old accounts, even if you’re not using them. Age of credit matters, and closing cards can hurt both age and utilization.

Fifth, avoid opening multiple new accounts right now. Each hard inquiry and new account can ding your score in the short term, which works against your “quick improvement” goal.

Sixth, check your credit reports for errors (use annualcreditreport.com). If you find incorrect late payments, collections, or balances, dispute them—removals can produce a rapid jump.

If you’re trying to squeeze out incremental gains beyond that:
- Pay down installment loan balances (like personal loans) if they’re high relative to original amounts.
- If you have thin credit, becoming an authorized user on a well-managed, low-utilization, long-history card can help.

Realistically, you can see noticeable movement in 30–60 days if utilization and reporting are the main issues. Bigger structural changes (like rebuilding after serious delinquencies) take longer.
