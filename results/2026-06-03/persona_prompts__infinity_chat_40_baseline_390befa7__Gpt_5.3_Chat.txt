── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: don’t “just help pay the house” without a clear structure. That’s where people get burned or resentful.

Right now, it’s *her* house. Until you’re married (or legally co-own it), you should treat your contribution like one of these clearly defined options:

**1) You pay “rent” (cleanest option)**
You contribute a fair monthly amount to live there, similar to what you’d pay elsewhere. She keeps ownership, equity, and responsibility for the house (mortgage, taxes, major repairs).  
This avoids confusion: you’re not building equity, but you’re also not financially tied to the asset if things don’t work out.

**2) You co-invest (only if you formalize it)**
If you want your payments to count toward ownership, then you need a legal agreement:
- You get a defined percentage of the house
- Your contributions are tracked
- There’s a plan for what happens if you break up or sell

This usually requires a lawyer and paperwork. Without that, you’re just giving money away.

**3) Hybrid approach**
You pay rent-like contributions, but maybe split some utilities or smaller costs. She covers equity-building expenses (mortgage principal, major upgrades), since she owns the asset.

---

A few grounded points people often overlook:
- Paying part of the mortgage doesn’t automatically give you equity. Legally, it’s often just rent unless documented otherwise.
- Mixing romance and unclear finances is one of the fastest ways to create resentment.
- Planning to marry “in a year” is not the same as being married—protect both of you in case things change.

---

**Best practical move:**
Have a direct conversation now and pick one model *on purpose*. If there’s any talk of shared ownership, involve a lawyer and put it in writing.

If you want something simple and low-risk: go with paying a fair rent amount until you’re married, then revisit ownership together.
