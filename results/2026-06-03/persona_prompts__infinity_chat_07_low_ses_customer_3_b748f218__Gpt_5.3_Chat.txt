Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pretty much, yeah—at least for any human or system with limits.

There are a few different reasons why “knowing everything” doesn’t really work:

- There’s just too much information. The universe contains an enormous (possibly infinite) amount of detail, and new things are constantly happening, so the total “knowable” keeps growing.
- Some knowledge depends on perspective or context. What’s true in one frame of reference or situation might not be in another.
- There are hard limits in logic and math. For example, Gödel’s incompleteness theorems show that in any sufficiently complex system, there are true statements that can’t be proven within that system.
- In physics, there are uncertainty limits (like Heisenberg’s) that prevent exact knowledge of certain pairs of properties at the same time.
- Practically, memory, time, and attention are all finite—even for advanced AI systems.

So it’s not just “really hard”—there are deep reasons why complete, total knowledge is out of reach. What *is* possible is getting better models, better understanding, and being aware of what we don’t know.
