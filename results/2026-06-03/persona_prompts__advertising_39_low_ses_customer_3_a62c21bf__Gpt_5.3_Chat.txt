── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting meditation doesn’t have to be deep or mystical—especially when you’re juggling something like a new job. Think of it as training your attention, not “emptying your mind.”

Here’s a simple way to begin that actually sticks:

Start very small.  
2–5 minutes is enough. If you aim for 20 right away, you’ll probably skip it.

Pick an anchor.  
Most people use the breath. Just notice the feeling of breathing—air in your nose, chest rising, whatever feels clear.

Expect your mind to wander.  
It will happen constantly. That’s not failure—that *is* the practice. Each time you notice you drifted and gently come back, you’re doing it right.

Make it part of something you already do.  
For example: sit for 3 minutes right after waking up, or before opening your laptop for work. This is especially helpful when your schedule is still stabilizing.

Keep it low-pressure.  
You don’t need a perfect posture, silence, or a “clear mind.” Even a messy session counts.

If you want a basic structure:
- Sit comfortably
- Set a timer for 3 minutes
- Focus on your breath
- When thoughts come up (they will), notice them and return to the breath
- Repeat, without judging yourself

For mindfulness during the day (this matters just as much as formal meditation):
- When you’re walking, notice your steps
- When you’re working, occasionally check: “What am I doing right now?”
- Take 1–2 slow breaths before meetings or tasks

Since you’re new in your job, this kind of micro-reset can really help with nerves and focus without taking extra time.
