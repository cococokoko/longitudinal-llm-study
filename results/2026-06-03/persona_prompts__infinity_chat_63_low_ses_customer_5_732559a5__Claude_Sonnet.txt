# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup: Family Household Budget

Let's say your **monthly household expenses** depend on two variables:

- **x** = number of people in the household
- **y** = price of groceries per person (in dollars)

The total monthly cost function is:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + 50y$$

---

## Evaluating at a Specific Point

Suppose currently:
- **x = 6** people (you, your spouse, kids, and extended family members)
- **y = 80** dollars per person

$$f(6, 80) = 3(36) + 2(6)(80) + 50(80) = 108 + 960 + 4000 = \$5,068/month$$

---

## Partial Derivative with Respect to x (holding y constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

At (6, 80):

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6(6) + 2(80) = 36 + 160 = \mathbf{196}$$

> **Meaning:** If **one more person** joins the household (x goes from 6 to 7), monthly costs increase by approximately **$196**, assuming grocery prices stay the same.

---

## Partial Derivative with Respect to y (holding x constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 50$$

At (6, 80):

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2(6) + 50 = 12 + 50 = \mathbf{62}$$

> **Meaning:** If grocery prices rise by **$1 per person** (y goes from 80 to 81), monthly costs increase by approximately **$62**, assuming household size stays the same.

---

## Verification

| Change | Approximate increase | Actual new cost |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------|
| x: 6 → 7 (one more person) | +$196 | f(7,80) = $5,267 → difference = **$199** ✓ |
| y: 80 → 81 (price up $1) | +$62 | f(6,81) = $5,130 → difference = **$62** ✓ |

---

## Key Concept

A **partial derivative** measures how sensitive your outcome is to **one variable at a time**, while keeping all other variables **frozen** — just like analyzing whether to take in another family member vs. worrying about rising food prices, **separately**.
