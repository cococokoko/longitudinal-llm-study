## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually **widely accepted across the political spectrum** as a legitimate principle. The interesting debates are about implementation, not the core idea.

## Legitimate economic concerns about specific implementations

- **Measurement problems**: defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult and creates administrative/legal complexity
- **Wage compression** can reduce incentives for skill development
- **Compliance costs** fall heavily on smaller employers
- **Unintended consequences** like reduced hiring of protected groups (Becker's discrimination research)
- **Price controls generally** create distortions when set incorrectly

## Where economists actually disagree

- Whether **market competition** adequately corrects discrimination without mandates
- How much observed wage gaps reflect discrimination vs. other factors
- Whether **transparency requirements** work better than mandates

The empirical evidence on gender/race pay gaps shows **real discrimination components** that markets don't fully self-correct.
