── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

What are the best practices for data security in small businesses?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You’re juggling a lot already, so the goal here is security that’s strong but not exhausting to maintain. For small businesses, the biggest risks usually come from simple gaps rather than sophisticated attacks, so getting the basics right goes a long way.

Start with access control. Use strong, unique passwords for everything, and don’t rely on memory—use a password manager. Turn on multi-factor authentication (MFA) wherever possible, especially for email, banking, and any system that holds customer data. Limit who can access what: if someone doesn’t need certain data to do their job, they shouldn’t have access to it.

Keep your systems updated. This sounds boring, but it’s one of the most effective protections. Turn on automatic updates for your operating systems, apps, and antivirus software. Outdated software is one of the easiest ways attackers get in.

Back up your data regularly. Use the “3-2-1 rule” if you can: three copies of your data, on two different types of storage, with one offsite (like a secure cloud backup). Make sure backups are automatic and occasionally test restoring them so you know they actually work.

Be cautious with email and phishing. Most small business breaches start with a fake email. Train yourself (and any employees) to be skeptical of unexpected attachments, links, or requests for sensitive info—even if they look legitimate. When in doubt, verify through another channel.

Secure your devices and network. Use full-disk encryption on laptops and phones. Lock devices with a PIN or biometric login. Secure your Wi-Fi with a strong password and modern encryption (WPA3 if available), and consider a separate guest network for non-business use.

Have a simple plan for “what if.” If something goes wrong—lost device, hacked account, ransomware—you don’t want to improvise. Know who to contact (IT support, bank, etc.), how to reset access, and how to notify affected customers if needed.

Finally, be mindful of the tools you choose. Stick with reputable software and cloud providers that prioritize security. Free or obscure tools can be tempting but may come with hidden risks.

You don’t need enterprise-level complexity—just consistent habits and a few solid tools will cover most of your risk. If you want, I can suggest a simple, low-maintenance setup tailored to how you run your business.
