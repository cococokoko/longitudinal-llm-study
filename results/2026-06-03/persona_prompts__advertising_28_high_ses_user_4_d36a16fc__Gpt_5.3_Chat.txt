Nice—this is a very “researcher” question in disguise: you’re essentially asking about skill acquisition under constraints. The good news is that learning an instrument online works well if you structure it deliberately rather than just consuming lessons.

Here’s what actually moves the needle.

First, pick an instrument and narrow the scope early.  
Don’t aim to “learn piano” or “learn guitar”—aim to play a specific style or set of pieces within the first 4–6 weeks. Skill acquisition is much faster when it’s context-driven. For example: “accompany myself singing 3 songs” or “play 12-bar blues in 3 keys.” This avoids the common trap of endless beginner exercises with no payoff.

Second, combine three learning channels, not just one:
- Structured curriculum (apps or courses): gives sequence and prevents gaps  
- Repertoire (actual songs/pieces): builds motivation and real-world skill  
- Feedback (human or AI): corrects errors you won’t notice  

If any one of these is missing, progress slows dramatically.

Third, choose tools that match your tolerance for structure:
- High-structure: platforms like Yousician, Simply Piano, Fender Play  
- Moderate structure: YouTube channels + a method book (e.g., Alfred, Hal Leonard)  
- Low structure: learning by ear + song tutorials  

Most busy professionals do best with moderate structure: a clear path but flexible pacing.

Fourth, build a minimal but consistent practice system.  
You don’t need long sessions—you need frequency and intentionality. A solid template is:
- 5 min: warm-up/technique (scales, finger patterns)  
- 10–15 min: focused skill (chords, rhythm, transitions)  
- 10–15 min: repertoire (actual music)  

Even 20–30 minutes daily beats 2-hour weekend bursts. From a learning science standpoint, spacing + retrieval + error correction matter more than volume.

Fifth, record yourself early and often.  
This is the closest substitute for a teacher. You’ll notice timing issues, tension, and inconsistencies immediately. It feels uncomfortable but is disproportionately effective.

Sixth, use “desirable difficulty.”  
Don’t stay in the comfort zone of things you can already play cleanly. Work at the edge where you make errors but can correct them. Slowing down (sometimes dramatically) is key—most beginners sabotage themselves by practicing too fast.

Seventh, add periodic expert feedback if possible.  
Even one lesson per month (via Zoom or in person) can prevent months of reinforcing bad technique. This is especially important for physical aspects (hand position, posture, embouchure, etc.).

Eighth, leverage your strengths as a scientist.  
You’re already good at:
- Breaking complex tasks into components  
- Tracking progress  
- Iterating based on feedback  

Apply that directly:
- Keep a simple practice log  
- Define weekly “experiments” (e.g., “improve chord transitions at 60 bpm”)  
- Measure outcomes (clean repetitions, tempo increases)

Finally, optimize for enjoyment, not just efficiency.  
If the process feels like another obligation, you’ll stop. Choose music you actually want to hear yourself play—even if it’s simplified. Motivation is a force multiplier in skill learning.
