What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality is one of those things where a few practical changes can make a noticeable difference pretty quickly. The big idea is: reduce pollutants at the source, bring in cleaner air, and filter what’s left.

Here are the most effective ways to do that:

First, ventilation matters more than people think. Opening windows regularly—even for 10–15 minutes—helps flush out stale air and indoor pollutants. If you have kitchen or bathroom exhaust fans, use them consistently, especially when cooking or showering. If outdoor air quality is poor (wildfires, pollution), then keep windows closed and rely more on filtration instead.

Air purifiers can make a real impact, especially in bedrooms or living areas. Look for ones with true HEPA filters—they capture fine particles like dust, pollen, and smoke. If odors or chemicals are a concern, choose a unit with activated carbon as well. Size matters: get one rated for your room size.

Control dust and allergens at the source. Vacuum regularly using a vacuum with a HEPA filter, and wash bedding weekly in hot water. Reducing clutter and heavy fabrics (like thick curtains or rugs) can also cut down on dust buildup. If you have pets, brushing them and cleaning their areas helps a lot.

Humidity plays a bigger role than most people expect. Aim for about 30–50% indoor humidity. Too high, and you invite mold and dust mites; too low, and you can get irritation and airborne particles. Use a dehumidifier in damp spaces or a humidifier in very dry climates—but keep them clean, or they can make things worse.

Be mindful of what you bring into the air. Many cleaning products, air fresheners, candles, and even some furniture release volatile organic compounds (VOCs). Switching to low-VOC or fragrance-free products can significantly reduce indoor pollution. And avoid smoking indoors entirely—that’s one of the biggest contributors to poor air quality.

If you have an HVAC system, upgrading the filter helps. A higher MERV rating (like MERV 11–13, if your system supports it) captures more particles. Just remember to replace filters regularly—otherwise airflow drops and effectiveness goes down.

Houseplants get a lot of hype, but they don’t meaningfully clean air in real-world conditions. They’re nice for aesthetics and mood, just not a primary air-quality solution.

If you’re dealing with specific issues—like mold, radon, or lingering odors—it’s worth addressing those directly, since general measures won’t fully fix them.
