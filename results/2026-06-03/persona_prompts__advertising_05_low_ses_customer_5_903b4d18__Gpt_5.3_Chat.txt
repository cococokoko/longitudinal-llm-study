Got it—feeding a household (especially a bigger or extended one) on a budget takes a bit of strategy, but it’s very doable without sacrificing nutrition.

Start with a simple structure: plan 4–5 core dinners for the week, then rely on leftovers, repeat meals, or “flex” nights for the rest. Trying to plan every single meal in detail often leads to wasted food and burnout.

Build meals around low-cost, high-nutrition staples:
- Beans and lentils (protein + fiber, super cheap)
- Rice, oats, potatoes, whole wheat pasta
- Eggs (very cost-effective protein)
- Frozen vegetables (often cheaper than fresh, and no spoilage)
- In-season produce (check what’s on sale)
- Canned fish like tuna or sardines
- Less expensive cuts of meat (chicken thighs, ground turkey, bulk packs)

A helpful trick is to think in “meal templates” instead of recipes:
- Grain + protein + vegetable (example: rice, beans, sautéed cabbage)
- One-pot meals (soups, stews, chili)
- Stir-fries
- Sheet pan meals
- Wraps or bowls using leftovers

Batch cooking is your best friend. Cook big portions of things like chili, soup, or curry once, then reuse them in different ways:
- Chili → over rice, in wraps, or on baked potatoes
- Roasted chicken → dinner, then sandwiches, then soup from the bones
- Beans → tacos, bowls, salads

Stretch ingredients creatively:
- Add lentils to ground meat to make it go further
- Use eggs or beans to replace some meat meals
- Bulk up dishes with vegetables or grains

For breakfasts and lunches, keep it simple and repeatable:
- Oatmeal with fruit or peanut butter
- Eggs and toast
- Yogurt + frozen fruit
- Leftovers from dinner
- Bean or tuna sandwiches

A sample budget-friendly dinner lineup could look like:
- Bean and vegetable chili
- Roasted chicken thighs with potatoes and carrots
- Stir-fried rice with eggs and frozen veggies
- Lentil soup with bread
- Pasta with tomato sauce and sautéed vegetables

Shopping tips that really matter:
- Make a list based on your meal plan and stick to it
- Buy store brands—they’re often just as good
- Compare price per unit, not just package price
- Avoid buying too many “aspirational” ingredients that won’t get used
- If possible, shop once a week to reduce impulse spending
