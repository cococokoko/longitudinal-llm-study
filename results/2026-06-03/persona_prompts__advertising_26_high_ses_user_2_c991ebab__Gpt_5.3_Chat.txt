── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best practices for data security in small businesses?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Even small businesses sit on data that’s valuable to someone—client info, payroll, contracts, IP—so the goal is to make yourself a harder target than the next firm without turning operations into a fortress. The most effective practices tend to be the ones that are consistently applied, not the fanciest.

Start with access control. Limit who can see or edit what, based on role, and revisit permissions regularly—especially after staff changes. Require strong, unique passwords and enforce multi-factor authentication everywhere it’s available (email, cloud storage, remote access, accounting systems). MFA alone stops a huge percentage of opportunistic attacks.

Keep systems updated. Many breaches exploit known vulnerabilities with available patches. Turn on automatic updates for operating systems, software, and firmware where possible, and have someone accountable for checking anything that can’t auto-update.

Encrypt sensitive data, both at rest and in transit. That means full-disk encryption on laptops and phones, secure configurations for cloud storage, and avoiding sending sensitive information over unencrypted email unless it’s protected (e.g., secure portals or encrypted attachments).

Backups are your safety net. Maintain regular, automated backups of critical data, and keep at least one copy offline or otherwise isolated (to protect against ransomware). Test restoration periodically—backups that can’t be restored aren’t backups.

Human behavior is often the weakest link, so basic training matters. Staff should know how to spot phishing attempts, avoid suspicious downloads, and verify unusual requests (especially anything involving payments or sensitive documents). This doesn’t need to be elaborate, but it should be repeated and updated.

Use reputable security tools: endpoint protection (modern antivirus/EDR), firewalls, and, if budget allows, some level of centralized monitoring or managed security service. For a small business, outsourcing this to a managed IT/security provider is often more realistic than building it in-house.

Have a simple incident response plan. If something goes wrong, people should know who to contact, how to isolate affected systems, and when to involve legal counsel, clients, or regulators. For a law firm, this ties directly into confidentiality obligations and breach notification requirements.

Vendor risk is easy to overlook. If you rely on cloud providers, document management systems, or billing platforms, make sure they follow reasonable security practices. Review their certifications, security features, and contractual commitments around data protection.

Finally, align with a recognized framework, even informally. Something like the NIST Cybersecurity Framework or CIS Critical Security Controls gives you a checklist to avoid blind spots without overengineering.

For a law firm specifically, it’s also worth ensuring your practices line up with professional responsibility obligations around safeguarding client information—many bar associations have issued guidance that maps pretty closely to the measures above.
