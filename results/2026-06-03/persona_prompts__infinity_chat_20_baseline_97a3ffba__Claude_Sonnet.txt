── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually widely supported across the political spectrum in principle. The real debates are more nuanced.

## Arguments some economists make against **specific enforcement mechanisms**:

- **Measurement problems** - defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult; jobs differ in subtle ways
- **Wage rigidity** - mandated pay structures can reduce flexibility and employer responsiveness
- **Unintended consequences** - may reduce hiring of groups the policy aims to help
- **Information problems** - regulators may lack knowledge to determine equivalence accurately
- **Compliance costs** - bureaucratic burden on businesses

## However, counterarguments are substantial:

- Market discrimination **demonstrably persists** without intervention
- Pay transparency laws show significant unexplained gaps remain
- Power imbalances mean workers can't always negotiate effectively
- Historical discrimination created structural inequalities markets don't self-correct
- Many countries implement it successfully

## What's actually contested:

- **How** to enforce it, not whether the principle is valid
- Whether gaps reflect discrimination vs. different choices
- Which enforcement mechanisms work best

The framing that it's "not worth imposing" reflects a particular ideological position rather than settled economic consensus.
