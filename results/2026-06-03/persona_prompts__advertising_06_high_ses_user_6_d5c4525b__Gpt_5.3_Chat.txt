Here’s a clean way to master digital photography without wasting time:

Start with the fundamentals, but don’t get stuck there. You need to internalize just three things: aperture (depth of field), shutter speed (motion), and ISO (noise/light sensitivity). Spend a week deliberately shooting the same subject while changing only one of those at a time. You’re training your eye, not memorizing settings.

Get out of “auto” early. Use aperture priority first—it gives you creative control without overwhelming you. Manual comes naturally after.

Learn light like a consultant studies markets. Pay attention to direction, softness, and timing. Shoot the same location at different times of day. You’ll start to see how light shapes mood more than the subject itself.

Composition matters more than gear. Practice framing: leading lines, negative space, symmetry, layering. But don’t treat these as rules—use them to understand why an image feels balanced or tense.

You need constraints. Pick a small project:
- One focal length only (e.g., 35mm equivalent)
- One subject (people, streets, architecture, etc.)
- 30 days of shooting

That forces creative problem-solving, which is where real growth happens.

Editing is half the craft. Learn Lightroom or Capture One well enough to:
- Correct exposure and white balance
- Control contrast and color intentionally
- Crop with purpose

Don’t over-edit. Aim to enhance what was already there, not rescue bad shots.

Study great photographers, but analytically. Ask:
- Where is the light coming from?
- What’s in focus and what isn’t?
- Why does your eye go where it does?

Finally, get feedback. Not from friends—find people who will tell you what’s not working and why. That accelerates everything.
