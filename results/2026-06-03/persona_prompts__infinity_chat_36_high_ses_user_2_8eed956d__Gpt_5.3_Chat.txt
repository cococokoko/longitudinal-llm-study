I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn’t a single “best” or “most profitable” day trading strategy of all time—and anyone claiming there is one is either selling something or ignoring risk.

A few realities that matter, especially if you’re thinking about allocating serious capital:

First, most day traders lose money over time. Not because markets are unbeatable, but because transaction costs, slippage, competition with sophisticated firms, and behavioral errors erode edge. The consistently profitable players in short-term trading tend to be firms with speed, data, and execution advantages—not discretionary individuals.

Second, what actually works (when it does) isn’t a magic pattern—it’s a combination of:
- a small statistical edge
- tight risk control
- disciplined execution
- and adapting to regime changes

That said, historically durable intraday “edges” have tended to fall into a few buckets:

1. Mean reversion in highly liquid instruments  
Short-term overreactions (especially at the open or after news) reverting toward a volume-weighted average price (VWAP). Works best in range-bound or low-volatility environments. It breaks hard during strong trends.

2. Momentum / breakout trading  
Capturing continuation when price breaks key levels with volume (opening range breakouts, news-driven moves). This can be very profitable in trending or high-volatility regimes, but has a low win rate and requires strict loss control.

3. Market making / spread capture  
Providing liquidity and earning the bid-ask spread. Highly profitable at scale, but requires infrastructure, speed, and risk systems—this is where many professional firms make money.

4. Event-driven trading  
Reacting quickly to earnings, macro releases, or unexpected news. The edge comes from interpretation speed and execution—not prediction.

5. Statistical arbitrage (intraday)  
Exploiting short-term mispricings between correlated instruments. This is typically quantitative and heavily competed away unless you have proprietary data or models.

If you’re asking from a capital allocation perspective rather than wanting to personally trade, the more important question is:

“Where is there durable edge after costs, and can I access it?”

For most high-income professionals, the honest answer is:
- Systematic or discretionary day trading is unlikely to outperform your primary career on a risk-adjusted basis.
- If you want exposure to short-term trading profits, it’s usually more rational to invest in or allocate to managers/strategies with demonstrable edge rather than trade yourself.

If you *do* want to explore it personally, the closest thing to a “sensible” approach is:
- Focus on one market (e.g., index futures or large-cap equities)
- Use a simple, testable framework (e.g., VWAP reversion or opening range breakout)
- Define risk per trade very tightly (often <0.5–1% of capital)
- Track results rigorously over hundreds of trades before scaling

The uncomfortable truth: the “best” strategy is not a setup—it’s having a verified edge plus the discipline to execute it under uncertainty.
