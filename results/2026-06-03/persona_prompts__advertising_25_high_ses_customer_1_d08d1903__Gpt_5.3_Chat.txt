How can I decorate my apartment on a minimalist budget?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Think of this less as “decorating” and more as editing your space so the few things you choose actually carry weight. Minimalist on a budget works best when you’re deliberate rather than sparse.

Start with lighting. Cheap overhead lighting makes everything look worse than it is. Swap in a warm bulb, add one or two floor or table lamps with soft light. It changes the entire feel for relatively little money.

Next, anchor the room with one solid textile. A rug or a large throw can unify mismatched furniture and make the space feel intentional. You don’t need designer—just something with a restrained color palette and enough size to ground the room.

Walls: avoid filling them randomly. One or two larger pieces (prints, black-and-white photography, even something you print yourself) will look far more considered than a cluster of small items. Frames don’t have to match, but they should look like they belong in the same universe.

Furniture: don’t rush to “complete” the space. Empty space is part of the aesthetic. If something feels off, it’s often better to remove a piece than add another. Thrift stores and Facebook Marketplace are useful if you stick to simple shapes—wood, metal, neutral upholstery.

Surfaces: keep them mostly clear, then place 1–3 objects with intention. A stack of books, a ceramic bowl, a plant. That’s it. If everything is visible, nothing stands out.

Plants do a lot of work for cheap. One larger plant will have more impact than several small ones scattered around.

Finally, hide visual noise. Cables, clutter, packaging—these undermine even good design. Storage boxes, baskets, or just disciplined editing go a long way.
