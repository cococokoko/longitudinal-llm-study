── System message ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
You know that the customer is an executive at a large technology company.

── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Think of a function that depends on more than one variable, like a simple revenue model:

R(p, m) = p × m  
where p = price per unit, m = number of units sold.

Now plug in some numbers. Suppose p = 10 and m = 50, so R = 500.

A **partial derivative** tells you how R changes when you vary one variable while holding the other constant.

First, vary price only (hold m = 50 fixed):  
If p increases from 10 to 11, revenue goes from 500 to 550.  
Change in R = 50 for a +1 change in p.  

So the partial derivative of R with respect to p is:  
∂R/∂p = m = 50

Interpretation: at m = 50, each $1 increase in price increases revenue by $50.

Now vary m only (hold p = 10 fixed):  
If m increases from 50 to 51, revenue goes from 500 to 510.  
Change in R = 10 for a +1 change in m.

So the partial derivative of R with respect to m is:  
∂R/∂m = p = 10

Interpretation: at p = 10, each additional unit sold adds $10 to revenue.

So in short, partial derivatives isolate the effect of one variable at a time—exactly what you’d want in a multi-factor system when you’re asking “what’s the sensitivity to just this one lever?”
