For someone in your situation (steady work, probably limited free time and energy), the “best” strategy isn’t about picking hot stocks—it’s about keeping things simple, consistent, and low-stress.

Here’s what actually works in real life:

First, automate everything  
If you have long hours, you don’t want to be thinking about the market every day. Set up automatic contributions from your paycheck into an investment account (401k, IRA, or brokerage). This turns investing into a habit instead of a decision.

Second, stick to broad index funds  
Instead of trying to guess winners, buy funds that track the whole market, like:
- S&P 500 index funds (tracks top U.S. companies)
- Total stock market funds (even broader)
- Maybe a small international fund for diversification

These are boring—but boring wins long-term. Most professional stock pickers don’t beat them consistently.

Third, invest consistently (even when the market looks scary)  
Prices will go up and down. The key is to keep investing regularly (this is called dollar-cost averaging). When the market drops, you’re actually buying at a discount.

Fourth, take advantage of tax-advantaged accounts  
If available:
- 401(k), especially if your employer matches (that’s free money)
- IRA (Roth IRA is great if you expect your income to grow over time)

These accounts let your money grow faster because of tax benefits.

Fifth, don’t overtrade  
Checking stocks constantly or trying to time the market usually leads to worse results. A good rule: if you feel the urge to “do something,” do nothing.

Sixth, build a safety buffer first  
Before investing heavily, make sure you have an emergency fund (3–6 months of expenses). Warehouse work can be physically demanding, so having a cushion matters.

Seventh, increase contributions over time  
Whenever you get a raise or overtime, try to increase how much you invest. Even small increases make a big difference over years.

If you want a simple “set-it-and-forget-it” setup:
- 70–90% in a total market or S&P 500 index fund  
- 10–30% in an international index fund  
Adjust based on your comfort with risk.
