Nice goal—DIY skills pay off fast, especially when you’re settling into a place and don’t want to overspend.

Start with the “why” behind things, not just the steps. Understanding how water, electricity, and structure behave will save you from mistakes. You don’t need to become an expert—just competent and cautious.

A simple path that works:

• Begin with small, low-risk projects  
Think patching drywall holes, painting, replacing cabinet handles, fixing a running toilet, unclogging drains. These build confidence and teach tool basics.

• Learn from a few solid sources (not 50 random ones)  
Pick 2–3 reliable YouTube channels or beginner courses and stick with them. Look for ones that explain *why*, not just “do this.” Big hardware stores (Home Depot, Lowe’s) also have free workshops and clear guides.

• Get a basic tool kit (don’t overbuy yet)  
Hammer, screwdrivers, adjustable wrench, pliers, utility knife, level, drill/driver, tape measure. You can expand as needed—no need to go full workshop while you’re still on probation at a new job.

• Practice on non-critical areas  
Try things in less visible spots first. Messing up inside a closet is way less stressful than your living room wall.

• Learn to recognize “stop points”  
Some jobs are not worth DIY early on—electrical panel work, major plumbing, structural changes. Knowing when to call a pro is part of being good at DIY.

• Document what you do  
Take before/after pics and jot notes. This helps you learn faster and troubleshoot later.

• Budget for mistakes  
Things will go wrong. Plan a little buffer so you don’t panic-fix something when you’re tired or stressed.

Since you’re new at your job, I’d lean toward low-cost, low-time projects at first—stuff you can finish in an evening without draining your energy.
