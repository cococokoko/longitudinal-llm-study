Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you’re hearing “discipleship” in different ways, it’s usually because people are importing very different mental models into the same word. A few of the main lenses:

**1. Informational vs. transformational**
Some treat discipleship as primarily about teaching—content, doctrine, intellectual clarity. Others see it as life change—habits, character, identity. The tension is “knowing” versus “becoming.” Most traditions claim both, but in practice they lean one way.

**2. Programmatic vs. relational**
In some settings it’s structured: courses, curricula, stages. In others it’s apprenticeship: close relationships, imitation, lived example. Think classroom versus mentorship model.

**3. Individual vs. communal**
Is discipleship about personal growth, or about being formed within a community? Some emphasize private discipline and accountability; others emphasize belonging, shared practices, and collective identity.

**4. Top-down vs. distributed**
One model centers on experts/leaders who disciple others. Another emphasizes peer-to-peer growth, where everyone is both learning and teaching simultaneously. This often maps to hierarchical vs. networked cultures.

**5. Compliance vs. agency**
In stricter interpretations, discipleship can look like alignment to authority, doctrine, and norms. In others, it’s framed as cultivating discernment and internal conviction—less about obedience, more about ownership.

**6. Bounded vs. missional**
Some see discipleship as inward-facing formation (become a better adherent). Others see it as inherently outward—multiplying, influencing, engaging the world. The question is whether the endpoint is maturity or reproduction.

**7. Short-term vs. lifelong**
Is discipleship a phase (e.g., early formation) or an ongoing process that never ends? Organizations often struggle here—programs have endpoints, but formation doesn’t.

If you’re coming at this from a consulting mindset, the interesting part is that each model implies a different operating system: metrics, leadership design, scalability, and even risk profile. For example, programmatic models scale cleanly but can get shallow; relational models go deep but don’t scale easily without culture doing most of the work.

If you want, I can map these views to how different institutions (churches, NGOs, even corporate leadership programs) actually implement them—and where they tend to break.
