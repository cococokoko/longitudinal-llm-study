── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the future of work in an increasingly automated economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question worth thinking through carefully rather than just optimistically or pessimistically.

## What We Actually Know

**Historical pattern:** Previous automation waves displaced specific jobs but created new ones overall. Agricultural and manufacturing employment collapsed while service employment grew.

**Current situation is different in some ways:**
- AI can now affect cognitive work, not just physical/routine tasks
- The pace of change may exceed workers' ability to adapt
- Benefits have concentrated rather than distributed broadly

## Competing Honest Perspectives

**Optimistic case:**
- New job categories will emerge we can't currently imagine
- Productivity gains could fund shorter work weeks or universal income
- Humans retain advantages in creativity, judgment, and relationship work

**Skeptical case:**
- This time the technology is more general-purpose
- Transition costs fall heavily on specific workers who may not recover
- Historical job creation isn't guaranteed to repeat

## Real Tensions Worth Acknowledging

- Retraining programs have **mixed track records**
- Geographic concentration of new jobs creates winners and losers
- The timeline matters enormously - gradual vs. rapid displacement
- Policy responses lag technological change significantly

## What Seems Reasonably Clear

- **Adaptation will be uneven** across demographics and regions
- Skills emphasizing judgment, creativity, and interpersonal work are more durable
- Policy choices will significantly shape outcomes
