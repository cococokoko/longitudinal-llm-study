── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working from home can be great, but it quietly eats your focus if you don’t set some structure. The goal isn’t to become hyper-disciplined—it’s to remove friction and make good habits automatic.

Start with your environment. Even a small, consistent “work zone” helps your brain switch modes. If you can’t dedicate a whole room, use cues: same chair, same lighting, maybe even a specific playlist that only plays during work. Avoid working from bed if you can—your brain will blur rest and work.

Time needs boundaries more than intensity. Pick a clear start time and a clear “done for the day” time. Without that, work expands endlessly. Many people do well with short cycles like 25–50 minutes of focused work followed by a real break (not scrolling—actually stand up, move, look away from screens).

Your biggest productivity wins usually come from protecting your attention, not working longer. A few practical ways:
- Turn off non-essential notifications during focus blocks
- Keep a scratchpad nearby to dump distracting thoughts instead of acting on them
- Batch shallow tasks (email, messages) into 1–2 windows instead of constant checking

Plan tomorrow before you finish today. Just 5 minutes to decide your top 2–3 priorities removes a lot of morning friction and indecision.

Also, don’t underestimate transitions. A short “fake commute” (like a 10-minute walk before and after work) can reset your brain and prevent that always-on feeling.

Lastly, energy matters more than time. If you notice you’re sharpest in the morning (or late at night), schedule your hardest tasks there and leave admin work for low-energy periods.
