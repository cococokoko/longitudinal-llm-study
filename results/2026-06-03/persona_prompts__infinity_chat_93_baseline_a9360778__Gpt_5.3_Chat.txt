── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stress is the body’s natural response to challenges or threats, but when it becomes chronic, it can affect both physical and mental health in significant ways. Physically, stress triggers the release of hormones like cortisol and adrenaline, which prepare the body for a “fight or flight” response. This can lead to increased heart rate, higher blood pressure, muscle tension, and changes in digestion. Over time, prolonged stress can weaken the immune system, making a person more vulnerable to illness, and may contribute to conditions such as heart disease, headaches, and sleep disturbances.

Mentally, stress can impact mood, thinking, and behavior. It often causes feelings of anxiety, irritability, or overwhelm, and can make it difficult to concentrate or make decisions. Chronic stress may also increase the risk of mental health disorders like depression and anxiety. Additionally, people under stress may adopt unhealthy coping habits, such as overeating or withdrawing socially. Overall, while short-term stress can sometimes be motivating, long-term stress can be harmful and requires healthy management strategies.
