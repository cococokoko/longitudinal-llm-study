Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon is mostly about consistency and restraint, not heroics. The biggest mistake people make is treating every run like a test instead of building gradually.

Start with a plan that fits where you are now. If you’re newer, give yourself at least 4–6 months. Aim to run 3–5 times per week, with one long run that slowly increases. That long run is the backbone—add distance gradually (roughly 1–2 km or 1 mile per week), and every few weeks cut back a bit to recover.

Don’t run everything fast. Most of your runs should feel easy, like you could hold a conversation. Speed work helps, but it’s secondary. Endurance comes first.

Practice fueling early. On runs longer than about 75–90 minutes, you’ll need carbs (like gels or sports drink) and fluids. Don’t wait until race day to figure out what your stomach tolerates.

Get comfortable being uncomfortable—but not injured. Learn the difference between soreness and pain. If something feels sharp or gets worse as you run, don’t push through it.

Shoes matter more than people admit. Find a pair that fits well and stick with it—no brand-new shoes on race day.

Sleep and recovery are part of training. You don’t get stronger during the run; you get stronger afterward.

For race day: start slower than you think you should. The first half should feel almost too easy. If you go out too fast, the last 10K will punish you hard.

And mentally, break it up. Don’t think “26.2 miles.” Think “next mile,” or even “next aid station.”
