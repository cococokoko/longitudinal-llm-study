── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cooking a great steak is mostly about controlling three variables: heat, moisture, and time. Everything else is supporting detail.

Start with the meat. Thickness matters more than cut for technique—aim for at least 1–1.5 inches so you can build a crust without overcooking the center. Ribeye is forgiving (fat = buffer), strip is a bit leaner, filet is delicate and easy to overdo.

Salt early or late, but not in between. Either salt at least 40–60 minutes before cooking (so it reabsorbs) or right before it hits the pan. Pat it dry—surface moisture kills browning.

Heat is your main tool. You want a ripping hot pan (cast iron or steel). Add a high-smoke-point fat (grapeseed, avocado). When the steak hits, don’t move it for the first minute or two—let the Maillard reaction do its thing. Flip once a deep crust forms. If it sticks, it’s not ready to flip.

For thickness >1 inch, use a two-phase cook:
- Sear hard on both sides
- Then finish in a 180–200°C (350–400°F) oven, or reduce heat and baste in the pan

Basting is where you level up: add butter, crushed garlic, and a sprig of thyme or rosemary. Tilt the pan and spoon the hot butter over the steak repeatedly. This improves heat transfer and flavor.

Temperature targets (pull 3–5°C below because of carryover):
- Rare: 50°C / 122°F
- Medium-rare: 54–56°C / 129–133°F
- Medium: 58–60°C / 136–140°F

Use a thermometer. Guessing is unnecessary in 2026.

Rest it 5–10 minutes. This isn’t about “sealing juices” (that’s a myth), it’s about pressure equalization so the fluid doesn’t flood out when you cut.

Slice against the grain where applicable, finish with flaky salt.

If you want absolute control, reverse sear is worth learning: bring the steak up slowly in a low oven (~95–120°C / 200–250°F) until near target temp, then finish with a hard sear. More even edge-to-edge doneness, less gray band.

Common mistakes:
- Pan not hot enough → gray, steamed meat
- Overcrowding → drops temperature
- Constant flipping too early → no crust
- No thermometer → inconsistent doneness

Once you can reliably hit a crust + target internal temp, you’ve basically mastered it. The rest is preference and nuance.
