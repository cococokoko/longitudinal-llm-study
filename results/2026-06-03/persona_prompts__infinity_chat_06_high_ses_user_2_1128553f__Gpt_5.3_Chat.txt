── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
That’s a deceptively hard question, especially at your level. By the time you’re a senior attorney, you’re usually very good at figuring out what’s *expected*—from clients, partners, courts—but that skill can crowd out knowing what’s actually *yours*.

A useful way to approach this is to separate three signals that often get tangled:

First, pay attention to energy, not just competence. You likely perform well across a wide range of work, but where do you feel pulled in versus drained? Not “what am I good at,” but “what leaves me with more mental bandwidth at the end of the day than it should?” Over a few weeks, notice which matters you find yourself thinking about voluntarily.

Second, look at your avoidance. What decisions have you been postponing, even though you have enough information to act? Avoidance is often a clue—not always fear, sometimes misalignment. If you imagine making the “expected” choice in those areas, do you feel relief, or a kind of quiet resistance?

Third, strip away audience and inertia. If no one in your firm would know your decision for a year—no reputational cost, no immediate consequences—what would you choose about your practice, your role, or your trajectory? This helps isolate preference from signaling.

It can also help to run small, low-risk experiments instead of trying to extract a fully formed answer. Shift a slice of your practice toward something you’re curious about. Take on (or decline) a type of matter and watch your reaction. You’re gathering data on yourself, not making a final declaration.

One more angle that’s often overlooked at your stage: distinguish between wanting a different *feeling* and wanting a different *structure*. For example, “I want more freedom” might actually mean fewer urgent client demands, or it might mean a different kind of intellectual problem. Those lead to very different changes.
