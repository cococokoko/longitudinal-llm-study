A feature I think would genuinely move the needle is a **passive cognitive drift detector**—something that quietly tracks subtle changes in behavior over time and flags early signs of cognitive decline.

Instead of relying on explicit “tests,” it would learn a user’s baseline patterns: how quickly they respond to notifications, their typical movement routines, speech cadence during calls, even variability in sleep-wake timing. Small deviations—like increasing hesitation in responses, missed habitual actions (e.g., forgetting a usual walk), or growing irregularity in daily structure—can precede clinically obvious impairment.

When the system detects a meaningful shift, it doesn’t alarm the user directly. Instead, it could:
- Prompt gentle, non-intrusive check-ins (“Want to review today’s plan?”)
- Offer optional micro-assessments framed as casual interactions
- Notify a designated caregiver or clinician only if thresholds are crossed

From a clinical standpoint, this could complement periodic cognitive screening with continuous, real-world data—arguably more sensitive to early decline than episodic testing.

The key is restraint: it should feel like a supportive companion, not a diagnostic device. Privacy, consent, and false-positive control would be critical design constraints.
