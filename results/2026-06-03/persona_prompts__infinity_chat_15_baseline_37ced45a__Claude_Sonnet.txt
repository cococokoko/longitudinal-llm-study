── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Power asymmetries**
- Wealthy states have greater leverage in negotiations, institutions, and coercive capacity
- Economic dependence creates political vulnerability for poorer states
- This can produce genuine cooperation or exploitation depending on circumstances

**Institutional design**
- Bretton Woods institutions (IMF, World Bank) reflect founding power distributions
- Voting structures often favor wealthy contributors
- Though poorer states have gained some voice over time - the picture isn't static

## Competing Interpretations Worth Considering

**Liberal view**: Trade interdependence reduces conflict and creates mutual interests despite inequality

**Dependency/structuralist view**: Global economic structures systematically disadvantage developing nations, perpetuating inequality

**Realist view**: Economic inequality is primarily a tool of state power competition

**Each captures something real** while missing important counterexamples

## Genuine Tensions and Complications

- Some previously poor states (South Korea, China) dramatically improved positions, challenging purely structural explanations
- Economic inequality doesn't straightforwardly predict conflict - relationships are messier
- Domestic politics within states often matter more than international structures
- Climate negotiations demonstrate how inequality creates genuine coordination problems

## What's Actually Contested

- Whether current institutions primarily help or harm developing nations
- Whether inequality is self-perpetuating or addressable through existing frameworks

What specific dimension interests you most? The mechanisms differ significantly across trade, security, climate, or migration contexts.
