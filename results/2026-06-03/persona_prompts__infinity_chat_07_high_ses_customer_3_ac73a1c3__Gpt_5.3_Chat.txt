── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If “everything” means all true facts, then yes—there are strong reasons to think complete knowledge is impossible, not just impractical.

Some limits are physical. Any knower has finite time, energy, and storage capacity. The universe appears to contain an enormous (possibly unbounded) amount of information, and new information keeps coming into being as systems evolve. Even in principle, a finite system can’t encode all of that.

Some limits are logical. There are true statements that cannot be proven within a given formal system (Gödel). If “knowing” requires proof or justification, then no single, consistent system captures all truths about even basic arithmetic, let alone the world.

Some limits are computational. Many well-posed questions are undecidable (the halting problem), and many others are intractable in practice. So even with perfect rules, there are truths no procedure can always deliver.

Some limits are epistemic. Quantum theory constrains simultaneous knowledge of certain pairs of quantities; chaotic dynamics makes long-term prediction effectively impossible; and any observer is embedded in the system they’re trying to know, which creates self-reference issues.

You can try to soften “everything” (e.g., all truths up to some complexity bound, or all truths expressible in a given language), and then the project becomes more coherent—but still not complete in an absolute sense.

So the interesting question isn’t whether total knowledge is achievable (it likely isn’t), but how far structured inquiry can push reliable, intersubjective knowledge under these constraints—and what kinds of limits are merely practical versus principled.
