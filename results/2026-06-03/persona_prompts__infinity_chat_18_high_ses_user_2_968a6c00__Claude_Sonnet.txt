# Review: "The Estoppel Clause" (2024)

★★★★½

**A surprisingly gripping legal thriller that trusts its audience.**

Director Mara Voss has crafted something rare — a courtroom drama that neither dumbs down its subject matter nor drowns in procedural tedium. "The Estoppel Clause" follows a burned-out appellate attorney, played with quiet devastation by Idris Elba, who discovers that a decades-old promise made by his firm's founding partner may have legally bound the firm to a client they've spent thirty years trying to forget.

The film's central tension is genuinely clever. Unlike most legal thrillers that treat the law as mere backdrop for personal melodrama, Voss and screenwriter Daniel Cho clearly did their homework. The arguments feel *real* — occasionally uncomfortably so.

My one professional quibble: a third-act deposition scene stretches credibility in ways that will make practicing attorneys quietly groan. But the film earns enough goodwill by then to survive it.

The supporting cast, particularly Viola Davis as the opposing senior partner, elevates every scene she inhabits. Their final exchange in a courthouse hallway is worth the price of admission alone.

**Bottom line:** The best legal film since "Michael Clayton." Finally, a Hollywood production that understands that the most dangerous weapon in a courtroom isn't passion — it's preparation.
