I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Good indoor air quality usually comes down to controlling three things: what’s coming in, what’s generated inside, and how well you remove or dilute pollutants. The most effective improvements are surprisingly practical:

Start with ventilation. Increasing the amount of outdoor air is one of the highest-impact steps. If your HVAC system allows, increase fresh air intake and ensure it’s balanced properly. In smaller settings, even opening windows strategically for cross-ventilation can help, though it’s less reliable in polluted or humid climates.

Upgrade filtration. Use high-efficiency filters in your HVAC system—MERV 13 or higher if the system can handle it. That alone can significantly reduce fine particles, allergens, and some pathogens. In offices or rooms with higher occupancy, adding portable HEPA air purifiers is often worth it, especially in conference rooms or enclosed offices.

Control moisture. Humidity that’s too high encourages mold and dust mites; too low can irritate airways. Aim for roughly 40–60% relative humidity. Fix leaks promptly and consider dehumidifiers in damp areas.

Reduce indoor pollution sources. This is often overlooked but critical. Limit or eliminate:
- Scented candles, air fresheners, and incense  
- Harsh cleaning products (switch to low-VOC options)  
- Off-gassing from new furniture or carpets (ventilate heavily after installation)  
- Printers or copiers in poorly ventilated spaces  

Maintain your HVAC system. Regular servicing, filter changes, and duct inspections prevent buildup and ensure proper airflow. A poorly maintained system can circulate contaminants instead of removing them.

Monitor air quality. A good indoor air quality monitor (tracking PM2.5, CO2, humidity, and sometimes VOCs) gives you real-time feedback and helps you spot issues early—like inadequate ventilation in meeting rooms.

Be mindful of occupancy and layout. Overcrowded spaces drive up CO2 and airborne contaminants. If you manage office space, redistributing high-density areas or improving airflow in conference rooms can make a noticeable difference.
