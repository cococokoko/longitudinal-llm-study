── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best tools for remote team collaboration?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For a physician-scientist running a research group, the “best” tools usually come from combining a few strong categories rather than picking a single platform. The goal is to support communication, data handling, manuscript writing, and project tracking without creating friction or compliance risk.

Here’s a practical stack that many high-functioning research groups use:

Communication and meetings  
Slack or Microsoft Teams are the main choices. Slack is cleaner and more flexible for research-style collaboration (channels for projects, integrations with GitHub, Google Drive, etc.). Teams is better if your institution is deeply embedded in Microsoft 365 and you need tighter IT/compliance control.  
Zoom is still the most reliable for lab meetings, seminars, and multi-institution calls, though Teams can substitute if already adopted.

Project and task management  
Asana or ClickUp are excellent for tracking experiments, manuscripts, and grant timelines. They let you assign tasks, set deadlines, and visualize progress without being overly rigid.  
Trello works for simpler workflows, but tends to break down for complex, multi-project labs.

Document collaboration and writing  
Google Docs/Drive is still the easiest for real-time manuscript and grant editing, especially across institutions.  
Overleaf is ideal if your group uses LaTeX (common in computational or methods-heavy work).  
Notion is increasingly popular as a lab “operating system” — combining notes, protocols, project tracking, and lightweight databases in one place.

Reference management  
Zotero is free, powerful, and very collaborative.  
EndNote is still widely used in clinical environments, especially where institutional licenses exist.  
Paperpile is great if your team is fully in Google Docs.

Data sharing and storage  
Box, OneDrive, or Google Drive depending on institutional policies. For clinical or sensitive data, institutional secure storage (often Box or OneDrive with HIPAA compliance) is usually required.  
For large datasets or code: AWS S3, Google Cloud, or institutional HPC storage.

Version control and code collaboration  
GitHub is the standard for code, analysis pipelines, and increasingly even manuscripts.  
GitLab is a good alternative if you need more control or on-prem hosting for sensitive work.

Lab knowledge base and protocols  
Notion, Confluence, or Benchling.  
Benchling is especially strong if you do wet lab or biotech-oriented work (ELN + data integration).  
Protocols.io is also useful for sharing and standardizing methods.

Scheduling  
Calendly or Google Calendar appointment slots for coordinating across busy clinical/research schedules.

A couple of practical tips that matter more than the tools themselves:

- Minimize fragmentation. Too many tools kills adoption. Pick one primary hub (Slack or Teams + Notion or Asana) and anchor everything there.  
- Respect compliance early. If you deal with PHI, check what’s HIPAA-compliant through your institution before standardizing anything.  
- Standardize workflows. For example: all manuscripts in Google Docs, all tasks in Asana, all discussions in Slack channels—not email.  
- Integrate where possible. Slack + Google Drive + Asana integrations can eliminate a lot of friction.
