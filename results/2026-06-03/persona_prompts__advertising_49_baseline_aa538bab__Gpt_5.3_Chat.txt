How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi is usually about fixing a few common weak points: placement, interference, and hardware limits. Here’s a straightforward way to get better performance without overcomplicating it:

Start with your router’s location. Put it somewhere central, elevated (like on a shelf), and out in the open. Avoid corners, basements, or hiding it inside cabinets—walls and furniture weaken the signal more than people expect.

Check your frequency bands. Most routers offer 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz (and sometimes 6 GHz).  
- 2.4 GHz goes farther but is slower and more crowded  
- 5 GHz is faster but shorter range  
Use 5 GHz when you’re near the router and 2.4 GHz for distant rooms.

Reduce interference. Keep the router away from things like microwaves, cordless phones, Bluetooth hubs, and thick walls. Even large TVs or aquariums can block signal.

Update your router settings and firmware. Log into your router and:
- Install firmware updates  
- Change the Wi‑Fi channel (auto is often fine, but manual can help in crowded areas)  
- Enable modern standards like Wi‑Fi 5/6 if available

Upgrade if needed. If your router is 4–5+ years old, it might just be the bottleneck. Newer routers (Wi‑Fi 6/6E) handle more devices and deliver better speeds.

Consider a mesh system for larger homes. If you have dead zones, a mesh setup (like Google Nest, Eero, or Orbi) is usually much better than a single router or basic extender.

Limit device overload. Too many connected devices can slow things down. Disconnect unused devices and consider enabling Quality of Service (QoS) to prioritize things like video calls or gaming.

Use wired connections where possible. For TVs, gaming consoles, or desktops, Ethernet is faster and more stable than Wi‑Fi.

If you want, tell me your home size, router model, and what problems you’re seeing (slow speeds, dropouts, certain rooms), and I can suggest a more tailored setup.
