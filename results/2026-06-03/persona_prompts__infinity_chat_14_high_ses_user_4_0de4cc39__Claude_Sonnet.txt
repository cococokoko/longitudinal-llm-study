── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What's the consequence of algorithm-driven content for public discourse?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question with several dimensions worth thinking through carefully.

## Core Mechanisms

**Engagement optimization** tends to favor:
- Emotionally activating content (outrage, fear, excitement)
- Confirmation of existing beliefs
- Simple narratives over complex ones
- Novelty over accuracy

## Documented Consequences

**For information quality:**
- Misinformation spreads faster than corrections (Vosoughi et al., MIT study)
- Health misinformation particularly problematic - directly relevant to your field
- Scientific nuance gets systematically disadvantaged

**For discourse structure:**
- Filter bubbles reduce exposure to challenging perspectives
- Polarization amplification (though evidence is more mixed than popular accounts suggest)
- Epistemic fragmentation - people operating from different factual premises

## Where Evidence Is Actually Weaker

- Direct causal links to political polarization are contested
- Individual effects vary considerably
- Platform architecture differences matter significantly

## Specific Relevance to Medical Research Communication

- Preliminary findings get amplified before replication
- Peer review process invisible to public
- Competing interests in health information are obscured
- Your credibility as a scientist competes poorly against emotionally compelling anecdotes

## Worth Considering

The mechanisms aren't purely technological - they interact with **pre-existing cognitive tendencies** and **institutional trust deficits**.

What specific aspect concerns you most - health misinformation, research communication, or broader epistemic effects?
