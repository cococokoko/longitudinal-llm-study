Effective ways to reduce household energy use.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You can cut household energy use quite a bit without making your home feel like a cave. The biggest wins usually come from a mix of quick habits and a few targeted upgrades.

Start with heating and cooling since that’s the largest chunk. Set your thermostat a few degrees lower in winter and higher in summer, and use a programmable or smart thermostat so it adjusts automatically when you’re asleep or out. Sealing drafts around doors, windows, and vents also makes a huge difference—cheap weatherstripping can save more than you’d expect. If your insulation is weak, improving attic or wall insulation is one of the highest-impact upgrades.

Lighting is an easy win. Switch everything to LED bulbs—they use a fraction of the energy and last much longer. Also, turn off lights in empty rooms; it sounds obvious, but it adds up.

Appliances matter more than people think. Wash clothes in cold water, run full loads, and air-dry when possible. Dishwashers are usually efficient, but only run them when full and use eco mode. If you’re replacing appliances, look for high-efficiency models—they pay off over time.

“Phantom” energy (devices drawing power when off) is another quiet drain. Use power strips to cut power to TVs, consoles, and office setups when not in use, or get smart plugs that shut things off automatically.

Hot water is another area to trim. Lower your water heater temperature slightly (around 120°F / 49°C is usually enough), fix leaks, and consider low-flow showerheads. Shorter showers help more than people expect.

If you want to go further, consider things like:
- Installing a smart thermostat
- Upgrading to a heat pump for heating/cooling
- Adding solar panels if it’s viable where you live
