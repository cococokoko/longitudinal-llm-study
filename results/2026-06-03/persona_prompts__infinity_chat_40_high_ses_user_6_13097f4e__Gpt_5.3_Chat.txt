── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You’ve got a few clean ways to handle this, but the “best” one depends on what you’re optimizing for: fairness, simplicity, or long-term alignment as a couple.

First, don’t casually pay for a house you don’t legally own. That’s the one setup that tends to create resentment or risk later, even in strong relationships.

From there, the main options:

**1. She buys it, you pay “rent”**
- She owns the home fully.
- You contribute a monthly amount (often below market rent or roughly half of carrying costs).
- Clean, simple, and low-risk.
- You’re not building equity, but you’re also not exposed if things don’t work out.

**2. You co-invest properly**
- You both contribute to the purchase (down payment and/or mortgage).
- You formalize ownership percentages and responsibilities in writing.
- This can be 50/50 or proportional to what each of you puts in.
- Requires legal structure (cohabitation or property agreement), but aligns money with ownership.

**3. You stay out of it for now**
- She buys the house entirely on her own.
- You don’t contribute meaningfully to the asset (maybe just split living expenses).
- If/when you marry, you can revisit ownership or treat it as her premarital asset.

**4. You help, but structure it explicitly**
- If you want to contribute without owning, it should be clearly defined (e.g., rent, or a documented loan, or a gift).
- Avoid “fuzzy” contributions like paying part of the mortgage without clarity—those are the ones that blow up later.

A couple of practical points people often miss:
- This is as much a legal decision as a relationship one. A short conversation with a lawyer is worth it.
- If marriage is likely within a year, keeping things simple now (option 1 or 3) and revisiting post-marriage often reduces friction.
- Unequal income matters. If you earn much more, “equal dollars” may not feel fair; if you contribute more, that should show up in ownership or be consciously treated as support, not an implied stake.

If I had to give a default: let her buy it, you pay a fair, clearly defined rent, and revisit ownership once you’re married. It’s clean, protects both of you, and avoids mixing a major asset into a pre-marriage gray zone.
