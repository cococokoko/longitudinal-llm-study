── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best platforms for freelance writers to find work?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you’re asking from the perspective of hiring freelance writers (which is what it sounds like), the “best” platforms depend on how much vetting you want to do yourself versus how curated you want the talent pool to be.

For a law firm—where accuracy, tone, and subject-matter familiarity really matter—I’d lean toward higher-signal platforms rather than open marketplaces.

Here are the main buckets, with practical pros/cons:

**High-quality, curated talent (best starting point)**
- **Contently** – Strong for experienced writers, including those with legal and financial backgrounds. Expensive, but you get vetted professionals and managed workflows.
- **ClearVoice** – Similar to Contently, slightly more flexible. Good for ongoing content needs (articles, thought leadership).
- **nDash** – Solid middle ground: you can post assignments or receive pitches. Many writers have niche expertise, including legal.

**Professional marketplaces (more control, more vetting required)**
- **Upwork** – Huge pool, including many legal writers and former attorneys. You’ll need to screen carefully, but you can find excellent people at reasonable rates.
- **Contra** – Cleaner, more modern alternative to Upwork with a growing base of experienced freelancers. Less saturated, often higher quality on average.
- **Fiverr Pro** – Only the “Pro” tier is worth considering for legal content. Still requires diligence, but quicker for smaller projects.

**Niche and writing-focused platforms**
- **Reedsy** – Originally for publishing, but has very strong editorial and writing talent. Better for long-form, polished work (white papers, guides).
- **Superpath Jobs / community** – Not a platform per se, but a high-quality content marketing community. Good place to post roles or find referrals.
- **LinkedIn** – Honestly one of the most effective channels for legal writers. Searching for “legal content writer” or posting a role often yields better candidates than marketplaces.

**Legal-specific angle (often overlooked)**
- Many of the best “freelance writers” for legal content are:
  - Former practicing attorneys
  - Law clerks or academics
  - Legal journalists  
  You’ll often find them more reliably via LinkedIn or referrals than gig platforms.

**A practical approach that works well**
If you want quality without wasting time:
- Start with LinkedIn + a curated platform (like nDash or ClearVoice)
- Ask for 2–3 published legal writing samples (not generic blog posts)
- Give a short paid test assignment (e.g., a 600-word client alert)

If you want, I can tailor recommendations depending on what you need—client alerts, SEO blog content, thought leadership, or something more technical like white papers or briefs.
