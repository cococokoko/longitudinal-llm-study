What are the safest ways to travel during a pandemic?

The safest approach is to reduce exposure as much as possible, but if you do need to travel, you can lower your risk quite a bit with some practical steps.

Start with planning. Check current infection levels and any public health guidance for both your destination and the places you’ll pass through. Avoid traveling if you’re sick or have been recently exposed. If testing is recommended, do it close to departure.

How you travel matters. Private transport (your own car) is generally the lowest risk since you control the environment. If you’re flying or using trains/buses, pick options that are less crowded, travel during off-peak times, and choose airlines or operators with good ventilation and cleaning practices.

Masking and air quality make a big difference. A well-fitted, high-quality mask (like an N95/KN95 or similar) reduces both your risk of catching and spreading illness, especially in crowded indoor settings like airports, stations, or cabins. Good ventilation helps too—on planes, overhead air vents can improve airflow; in cars, crack windows when possible.

Hand hygiene still matters. Wash your hands regularly or use sanitizer, especially after touching shared surfaces like security bins, door handles, or seat areas. Try to avoid touching your face.

Keep some distance where you can. It’s not always possible, but spacing out in lines, waiting areas, or boarding queues reduces risk.

Food and stops are another factor. Eating in crowded indoor spaces increases exposure because masks come off. If you can, opt for takeout or eat in less crowded, well-ventilated areas.

Vaccination (if available for the disease in question) is one of the most effective ways to reduce severe illness, so staying up to date is important.

Finally, have a backup plan. Policies and conditions can change quickly during a pandemic, so be prepared for delays, cancellations, or new requirements.
