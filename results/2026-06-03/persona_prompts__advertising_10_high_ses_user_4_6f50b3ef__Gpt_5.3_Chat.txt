Nice—this is very doable if you approach it like a long-term research project instead of a hobby you squeeze in.

The biggest shift: treat language as a *skill under active use*, not a subject to “learn.” That means high-frequency exposure + deliberate practice + feedback loops.

Here’s a structure that tends to work well for people with your kind of schedule and cognitive style:

First, pick a narrow operational goal early  
Don’t start with “be fluent.” Start with something like:
- hold a 10‑minute conversation with a patient  
- present a 5‑slide research summary  
- read abstracts in your field without translation  

That constraint sharpens everything else.

Second, build a daily minimum viable routine (30–60 min)  
Consistency beats intensity. A solid split:
- 10–15 min: spaced repetition (Anki or similar)  
  Focus on high-frequency words + domain-specific vocabulary (your field gives you an advantage here)
- 10–15 min: input (listening/reading slightly above your level)  
  Podcasts, graded readers, or even medical content once you’re ready
- 10–20 min: active production  
  Speaking (ideally) or writing short passages

If you only have 20 minutes on a bad day, still do *something*. Gaps kill momentum.

Third, prioritize speaking earlier than feels comfortable  
Most high achievers over-index on comprehension and delay speaking too long. That slows real progress.

You want:
- 2–3 sessions/week with a tutor or language partner (even 20–30 min)  
- immediate correction, not just passive conversation  

Think of this like lab feedback, not performance.

Fourth, exploit your domain expertise  
You already have a huge semantic network in medicine/science. Leverage it:
- learn vocabulary tied to concepts you already understand  
- read papers or abstracts in the target language earlier than typical learners  
- rehearse explaining your own research in the new language

This dramatically accelerates retention because you’re mapping onto existing knowledge.

Fifth, use “comprehensible overload” strategically  
You’ll progress fastest when input is:
- mostly understandable (70–90%)  
- but still stretching you  

Too easy = stagnation  
Too hard = burnout

Sixth, measure progress like a scientist  
Track:
- hours of exposure  
- number of conversations  
- vocabulary retention (Anki stats are useful here)  
- functional milestones (e.g., “can explain my last paper in 3 minutes”)

Adjust based on data, not vibes.

Seventh, accept the plateau phase  
Around intermediate level, progress *feels* like it stops. It hasn’t—you’re just refining. The fix is usually:
- more speaking  
- more varied input  
- less perfectionism
