Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cooking a great steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control. Heat, timing, and letting the meat speak for itself.

Start with the steak itself. Thicker cuts (ribeye, strip, filet) are more forgiving than thin ones. Look for good marbling—that white fat running through the meat is flavor. Take it out of the fridge 30–60 minutes before cooking so it cooks evenly.

Salt it generously. Not a light sprinkle—really coat it. You can do this right before cooking, or even better, 1–24 hours ahead (leave it uncovered in the fridge for a dry brine). Pepper is optional before cooking; it can burn in high heat, so some people add it after.

Heat is everything. Get your pan or grill very hot—like “almost smoking” hot. Cast iron is ideal because it holds heat well. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like avocado or canola), just enough to coat.

Sear the steak without moving it for a couple minutes. You’re building that deep brown crust. Flip once. If it sticks, it’s not ready yet. After flipping, you can add butter, garlic, and herbs (like thyme), then tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak.

Use temperature, not guesswork:
- Rare: ~120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: ~130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: ~140–145°F (60–63°C)

Pull the steak about 5°F (3°C) before your target—it’ll keep cooking as it rests.

Rest it. 5–10 minutes. This keeps the juices from running out when you cut it.

A few common mistakes to avoid:
- Cooking straight from the fridge (uneven results)
- Not enough salt
- Weak heat (you’ll steam instead of sear)
- Flipping constantly (you lose crust)
- Skipping the rest

Once you’ve got the basics down, you can play with techniques like reverse searing (low oven first, then a hard sear) or finishing over charcoal for smoky flavor.
