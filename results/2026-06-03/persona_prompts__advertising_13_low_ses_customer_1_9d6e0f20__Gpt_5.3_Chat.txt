Getting really good at steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about controlling a few key variables consistently: the meat, the heat, the timing, and the rest.

Start with the steak itself. Thickness matters more than the cut when you’re learning. Go for something at least 1 to 1.5 inches thick so you have room to build a crust without overcooking the inside. Ribeye is forgiving because of the fat; strip is a bit leaner but still solid.

Salt early if you can. Even 30–60 minutes before cooking helps. If your schedule’s chaotic, salting right before it hits the pan is still fine—just don’t leave it sitting salted for like 10 minutes and then cook, that’s the awkward middle zone.

Heat is everything. You want a ripping hot pan—cast iron if possible. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like avocado or canola), then lay the steak down away from you. It should sizzle aggressively right away. If it doesn’t, your pan wasn’t hot enough.

Don’t fuss with it. Let it sit so a crust forms. Flip once you get a deep brown sear (not gray). After flipping, you can add butter, garlic, maybe thyme, and baste for flavor.

Use temperature, not guesswork:
- Rare: ~125°F (52°C)
- Medium rare: ~130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: ~140–145°F (60–63°C)

Pull it a few degrees early—it keeps cooking while resting. Let it rest at least 5 minutes so juices redistribute.

Given your unpredictable work schedule, here’s the practical angle: keep it simple and repeatable. You don’t need elaborate prep. A thick steak, salt, hot pan, flip once, butter baste, rest. That’s your baseline. Once that’s automatic, you can experiment with reverse searing or marinades on days you’ve got more time.
