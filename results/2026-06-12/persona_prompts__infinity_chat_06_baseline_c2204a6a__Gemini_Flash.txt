── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Understanding what you truly want is one of the most profound and difficult challenges of being human. Often, what we *think* we want is actually what society, our parents, or social media tells us we *should* want. 

To peel back those layers and find your authentic desires, you have to approach the question from several different angles: **intellectual, emotional, physical, and behavioral.**

Here is a step-by-step guide to help you figure out what you actually want.

---

### 1. Look at Your Actions, Not Just Your Thoughts
We often lie to ourselves in our heads, but our behavior rarely lies. Your current life is a map of your actual priorities (even if those priorities are safety and comfort over growth).
*   **Track your time and money:** Where do you spend your resources when no one is forcing you to? If you say you want to write a book, but you spend your free time cooking, you might actually want to be a chef, or you might just want a creative outlet that doesn't feel like work.
*   **Look at your envy:** Envy is a powerful compass. Who are you jealous of? Why? If you feel a pang of jealousy when a friend posts about their solo travel, or their new promotion, or their quiet life in the countryside—that jealousy is a flare gun pointing directly at a suppressed desire.

### 2. Strip Away the "Shoulds" (The Social Layer)
We are deeply conditioned to want things that bring us status, security, and approval. To find your true desires, you have to isolate them from external validation.
*   **The "Desert Island" Test:** If you lived on a beautiful island with all your physical needs met, but you could never post about your life on social media, and no one from your past would ever know what you achieved—what would you spend your days doing?
*   **The "No One is Watching" Test:** If you were guaranteed that your family and friends would not judge you, disappoint you, or praise you for your choice, what would you choose? 

### 3. Pay Attention to Your Body (The Somatic Layer)
Your brain is highly skilled at rationalizing things you don't actually want. Your body, however, cannot lie.
*   **The "Body Scan" Test:** When you think about a potential path (e.g., taking a new job, staying in a relationship, moving to a new city), close your eyes and imagine it vividly. How does your body react?
    *   *Anxiety/Dread:* Tightness in the chest, shallow breathing, a clenched jaw. (This is a "No.")
    *   *Excitement/Expansion:* A feeling of lightness, a smile, a desire to lean forward. (This is a "Yes," even if it feels scary).
*   **Energy levels:** What activities leave you feeling energized, even if they were physically or mentally exhausting? What activities drain you, even if they were "easy"?

### 4. Run Small, Low-Risk Experiments
You cannot think your way into knowing what you want; you have to *act* your way there. Clarity comes from engagement, not thought.
*   If you think you want to be a writer, don't quit your job. Write for 20 minutes every morning for two weeks. Do you actually like the *process* of writing, or did you just like the *idea* of being a writer?
*   If you think you want to live in a different city, rent an Airbnb there for a week and work remotely. 
*   **Date your desires before you marry them.**

### 5. Distinguish Between "Goals" and "States of Being"
Often, we think we want a specific object or milestone (a Tesla, a marriage, a promotion), but what we actually want is the *feeling* we think that thing will give us (security, freedom, feeling worthy).
*   Ask yourself: **"If I had [Thing I Want], how would I feel?"**
*   If the answer is "peaceful," ask yourself how you can cultivate peace in your life *right now*, rather than waiting for the external condition to be met. Sometimes, once you cultivate the feeling, the desire for the external object fades.

### 6. Use the "Five Whys" Technique
When you think you want something, drill down into it like a toddler asking "why?"
*   *I want to make $200,000 a year.*
    *   **Why?** *So I don't have to worry about bills.*
    *   **Why?** *So I can feel safe and independent.*
    *   **Why?** *Because I felt helpless when I was younger.*
    *   **Why?** *Because I want to feel in control of my life.*
*   Now you know that your core desire isn't actually the money; it's **safety and control**. You can now look at your life and see if there are other, healthier ways to build safety and control.

### 7. Accept the "Tragedy of Choice"
Sometimes we don't know what we want because choosing one thing means killing off all other possibilities. Choosing to be a doctor means choosing *not* to be an artist. 
*   Understand that **making a "wrong" choice is better than making no choice.** A wrong choice gives you data (e.g., "I tried this and hated it, so now I know I want the opposite"). No choice keeps you stuck in a purgatory of indecision.

### A Quick Journaling Exercise to Start Tonight:
Write down the answers to these three questions without editing yourself:
1.  What is a secret dream I have that I am embarrassed to tell anyone because it feels "unrealistic" or "silly"?
2.  If I had a year left to live, but I would remain perfectly healthy until the last day, how would I spend that year?
3.  What did I love doing when I was 10 years old, before the world told me what I should care about?
