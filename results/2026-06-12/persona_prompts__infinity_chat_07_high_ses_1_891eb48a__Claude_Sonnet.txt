This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question worth thinking carefully about.

## Several strong reasons to think yes, complete knowledge is impossible:

**Logical/mathematical limits**
- Gödel's incompleteness theorems suggest any sufficiently complex formal system contains true statements it cannot prove
- The halting problem demonstrates fundamental computational limits

**Physical limits**
- The observable universe has finite information content
- Quantum mechanics suggests some properties are fundamentally indeterminate, not just unknown
- The speed of light limits what information can reach any observer

**Practical limits**
- Knowing everything would require more storage capacity than exists in the universe
- Some knowledge may require experiencing things that are mutually exclusive

**Self-referential problems**
- A complete knower would need to know their own knowledge completely, creating potential infinite regress

## Reasons for some caution about this conclusion:

- We should distinguish between **in principle** and **in practice** impossibility
- "Everything" needs careful definition - everything about what?
- Some limits (like quantum indeterminacy) are debated among physicists
- We might be wrong about what the actual limits are

## My honest assessment:

The evidence is fairly strong that **complete knowledge is impossible even in principle**, not just practically. The convergence of mathematical, physical, and logical arguments is compelling.
