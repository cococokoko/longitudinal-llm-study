How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job—especially while on probation—is a high-pressure, high-stimulus environment. It is completely normal to feel a bit lost, overwhelmed, or uncertain about what you actually want right now. When you are in "survival and adaptation mode," your brain is so busy processing new names, systems, and expectations that your deeper desires get pushed to the background.

To figure out what you want (both in your career and your broader life), you need to transition from **reactive mode** (just trying to survive probation) to **reflective mode**. 

Here is a structured guide to help you understand what you want, tailored to where you are in your life right now.

---

### Phase 1: Clear the "Survival" Noise First
Before you can think about your 5-year plan, you need to stabilize your nervous system. 
*   **Acknowledge the probation pressure:** Probation creates a constant, low-grade anxiety of "Am I doing enough?" This anxiety distorts your thinking. Remind yourself: *My job right now is just to learn and show up, not to have my entire life figured out.*
*   **Separate "Now" from "Forever":** You do not need to decide if this new job is your forever career. You just need to decide what you want *from* it right now (e.g., financial stability, a new skill on your resume, or a foot in the door).

### Phase 2: Pay Attention to Your "Clues"
You rarely figure out what you want by staring at a blank wall. You figure it out by looking at the data your daily life is already giving you. Start tracking three things:

1.  **Energy Givers vs. Energy Drainers:**
    *   Keep a small notebook or a note on your phone. 
    *   At the end of each workday, write down one task that made you feel energized or focused (even a little bit), and one task that felt like pulling teeth.
    *   *Why this matters:* What we "want" is often closely tied to what gives us energy. If you love organizing the team spreadsheet but hate client calls, that is a massive clue about your preferred working style.
2.  **The "Jealousy" Test:**
    *   Pay attention to who you feel envious of at your new company or in your social circle. 
    *   Jealousy is a map. If you feel jealous of the person who gets to work remotely, you want freedom. If you feel jealous of the person who just got promoted to manager, you want leadership and status. 
3.  **The "Sunday Scaries" Root Cause:**
    *   If you dread Monday, ask yourself *exactly* why. Is it the fear of making a mistake (probation anxiety)? Is it a specific person? Or is it the actual work? Pinpointing the source of your discomfort tells you what you want to *avoid*, which is the first step to finding what you want to *approach*.

### Phase 3: Define Your "Core Values"
We often don't know what we want because we are chasing what society, our parents, or our peers want. To find *your* truth, you need to know your values.
*   **Pick your top 3 values right now.** (Examples: Security, Creativity, Autonomy, High Income, Work-Life Balance, Status, Helping Others).
*   **Apply them to your current situation:** If your top value is *Security*, then surviving probation and getting that permanent contract is your primary "want" right now. If your top value is *Creativity*, and your new job is very rigid, you may want to look for creative outlets outside of work for now.

### Phase 4: Use the "Process of Elimination"
If you don't know what you *do* want, start with what you *don't* want. It is much easier to identify.
*   "I don't want to work 60 hours a week."
*   "I don't want to be in a role where I have to pitch to clients."
*   "I don't want a long commute."
*   **Flip these negatives into positives:** "I want a strict 40-hour work week, an internal-facing role, and a hybrid or remote setup."

### Phase 5: Give Yourself Permission to "Draft"
You do not need a final, permanent answer. Treat your desires like a draft.
*   **Ask: "What do I want for the next 6 months?"** 
    *   *Example:* "For the next 6 months, I want to pass probation, build a good relationship with my manager, and save $3,000."
*   This takes the pressure off. You aren't signing a contract with your future; you are just setting a direction for the next season of your life.

### Summary Checklist for This Week:
1.  **Get through probation:** Focus on doing your job well enough to secure your position. Security brings clarity.
2.  **Observe:** Write down one thing you liked and one thing you disliked about your workdays.
3.  **Rest:** Do not try to solve your life's purpose on a Sunday night when you are exhausted. Give your brain quiet time to process.
