── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on how you and your girlfriend want to balance finances, risk, and ownership.

A few key points:

- If she is buying the house solely in her name, and you are not on the deed or mortgage, then payments you make toward the mortgage generally build her equity, not yours.
- If you move in and live there, it's reasonable to contribute something toward housing costs because you're receiving the benefit of living there.
- The question is how that contribution should be structured.

Common approaches:

1. Pay rent
- Treat the situation like a landlord-tenant arrangement.
- You pay a fixed monthly amount that is less than or comparable to market rent.
- She covers the mortgage and ownership costs.
- This is often the cleanest option before marriage because ownership and payments are clearly separated.

2. Split household expenses, but not the mortgage
- She pays the mortgage, property taxes, major repairs, and homeowner costs.
- You contribute to utilities, groceries, internet, and similar expenses.
- This avoids the feeling that you're funding an asset you don't own.

3. Contribute to the mortgage and gain ownership
- If you want to help pay for the house as an owner, you should discuss being added to the title and potentially the mortgage, or create a legal agreement defining your share.
- This usually requires legal and financial planning.
- Don't assume that paying part of the mortgage automatically gives you ownership rights.

4. Wait until marriage to combine housing finances
- Many couples keep ownership separate until they're married.
- After marriage, they reassess whether to combine assets, refinance, add a spouse to the deed, or handle finances jointly.

Since you're planning to marry in about a year, a practical middle-ground is often:
- She buys the house in her name.
- You contribute a reasonable amount as rent or toward shared living expenses.
- After marriage, revisit ownership, equity, and long-term financial planning together.

Before making a decision, have a direct conversation about:
- Who owns the house.
- Who pays for repairs, taxes, and insurance.
- What happens if the relationship ends before marriage.
- Whether either of you expects contributions to create ownership rights.

One important question: Will you be moving into the house immediately after she buys it, and will the house be solely in her name? The answer affects what arrangement makes the most sense.
