── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For a physician-scientist leading a research group, "what do I want?" is often harder to answer than it sounds because multiple identities and incentive systems are operating simultaneously:

- What advances science
- What helps patients
- What advances your career
- What supports your trainees
- What your institution rewards
- What your family and personal life require
- What genuinely interests and energizes you

These can overlap, but they are not the same thing.

One useful distinction is between preferences, values, and goals.

- Preferences: what you enjoy doing day-to-day.
  - Writing papers?
  - Seeing patients?
  - Building teams?
  - Analyzing data?
  - Teaching?
- Values: what outcomes matter to you regardless of recognition.
  - Discovery?
  - Service?
  - Excellence?
  - Mentorship?
  - Independence?
  - Impact on patients?
- Goals: concrete states you aim to reach.
  - Department chair.
  - Landmark clinical trial.
  - A thriving lab.
  - More time with family.
  - Financial independence.

Many accomplished people pursue goals that no longer reflect their values or preferences because the goals were adopted from the surrounding culture.

A practical approach is to examine your behavior rather than your self-description.

Ask:

- When during the past year have I felt most alive, engaged, or absorbed?
- What tasks do I repeatedly volunteer for?
- What work would I continue doing if promotion and prestige were removed?
- Which achievements felt surprisingly empty after reaching them?
- What frustrations recur in my life? They often point toward blocked values.
- If I were unable to increase my reputation from this point onward, how would I spend my professional time?

Another useful method is to project yourself forward.

Imagine you are 80 years old reviewing your career.

What would make you think:
- "I used my abilities well."
- "I contributed something that mattered."
- "I treated people properly."
- "I spent my time wisely."

The answers often reveal deeper wants than near-term ambitions.

For leaders, there is also a distinction between wanting a role and wanting the activities associated with that role.

For example:
- Wanting to be a renowned principal investigator is different from wanting to spend years managing people, fundraising, reviewing grants, and setting strategy.
- Wanting to lead a large research center is different from wanting the daily work of leadership.

Many people discover they desire the status attached to a role more than the actual work involved.

One exercise I find particularly revealing is to write three lists:

1. What I want.
2. What I think I should want.
3. What others want from me.

Keep the lists separate. Do not reconcile them immediately.

The tension between those lists often contains the answer.

Finally, it may help to treat this as an empirical question rather than a philosophical one. As a scientist, you are trained to observe data. Your emotional reactions, recurring interests, envy, admiration, and persistent dissatisfaction are data. Over months, patterns emerge. The goal is not to invent what you want but to notice what your life already indicates you want.
