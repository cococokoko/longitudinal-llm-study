── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The relationship with a mother is often a child’s first blueprint for how the world works, how relationships function, and how they view themselves. When a mother is emotionally unavailable, hypercritical, neglectful, or inconsistent, it leaves deep psychological imprints. 

In psychology, this is often referred to as the **"mother wound."** Because children cannot process that a parent’s lack of love is the parent's own limitation, they internalize it, believing, *"I am not loved because I am not lovable."*

Here are the common signs in adulthood that suggest you may not have received enough love, warmth, or emotional safety from your mother as a child:

### 1. Deep-Seated Self-Doubt and Low Self-Esteem
*   **The "Inner Critic":** You have a harsh, relentless inner voice that criticizes everything you do. This voice is often an internalization of a critical or cold mother.
*   **Imposter Syndrome:** Even when you succeed, you feel like a fraud. You struggle to internalize praise because, deep down, you feel fundamentally flawed or unworthy.
*   **Difficulty Making Decisions:** Because your feelings and choices were not validated in childhood, you struggle to trust your own judgment and constantly seek external validation.

### 2. Relationship and Attachment Struggles
The way you bonded with your mother shapes your **attachment style** in adulthood:
*   **Anxious Attachment (Fear of Abandonment):** You may become clingy, needy, or hyper-vigilant in romantic relationships. You constantly worry that your partner will leave you, and you require constant reassurance.
*   **Avoidant Attachment (Fear of Intimacy):** You keep people at a distance. You might believe that relying on others is dangerous (because your primary caregiver wasn't reliable), so you adopt a fiercely independent "I don't need anyone" attitude.
*   **Attraction to Toxic Dynamics:** You may unconsciously seek out partners who are emotionally unavailable, critical, or distant because that dynamic feels "familiar" and "normal" to your nervous system.

### 3. Chronic People-Pleasing and Lack of Boundaries
*   **Earning Love:** If you had to behave perfectly or perform to get your mother’s attention, you may believe love is conditional. In adulthood, you might overextend yourself, suppress your own needs, and say "yes" to everyone to avoid rejection.
*   **Boundary Issues:** You find it incredibly difficult to set boundaries or say "no" because you fear that setting a boundary will cause people to abandon or dislike you.

### 4. Emotional Dysregulation and Numbness
*   **Difficulty Identifying Emotions (Alexithymia):** If your mother ignored, dismissed, or punished your emotions (e.g., saying "stop crying or I'll give you something to cry about"), you may have learned to disconnect from your feelings. As an adult, you might feel numb or struggle to name what you are feeling.
*   **Extreme Sensitivity to Rejection:** A minor slight, a change in someone's tone, or a delayed text message can trigger a massive emotional spiral or a feeling of panic.

### 5. Hyper-Responsibility or "Parentification"
*   If your mother was emotionally immature, struggling with addiction, or mentally ill, you may have had to step up and care for her or your siblings. 
*   As an adult, you feel responsible for everyone else’s happiness and comfort. You feel guilty when you relax, practice self-care, or focus on your own needs.

### 6. A Persistent Feeling of Emptiness or "Homesickness"
*   You may carry a chronic, vague sense of emptiness, loneliness, or grief that you can't quite explain. 
*   This is often a longing for the maternal warmth and safety you never received. It can feel like a physical ache or a sense of being "homesick" for a place you’ve never actually been.

### 7. Difficulty Accepting Love and Kindness
*   When people show you genuine love, care, or compliments, you might feel deeply uncomfortable, suspicious, or waiting for "the other shoe to drop." 
*   Because unconditional love was not modeled for you, genuine kindness can feel unfamiliar and therefore unsafe.

---

### The Path to Healing
Recognizing these signs is not about dwelling on blame, but about understanding the origin of your pain so you can heal. The brain is neuroplastic, meaning it can change and heal at any age. 

*   **Reparenting:** This is the process of giving yourself the love, validation, patience, and boundaries that your mother could not provide.
*   **Therapy:** Working with a trauma-informed therapist (especially those specializing in attachment, EMDR, or Inner Child work) can help you process the grief of the childhood you deserved but didn't get.
*   **Grieving:** Healing requires allowing yourself to grieve the mother you wished you had, rather than continuing to try to change or earn the love of the mother you actually have.
