── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital, your personal brand can be valuable for several reasons: patient education, professional reputation, academic visibility, trainee recruitment, public trust, and opportunities for speaking, research collaboration, and media engagement.

A strong physician brand is usually built around credibility, consistency, and a clearly defined niche rather than self-promotion alone.

Key steps:

• Define your positioning
  - What do you want to be known for?
  - Examples:
    - Complex spine surgery
    - Brain tumor surgery
    - Neurosurgical education
    - Innovation and technology in surgery
    - Physician leadership
    - Academic medicine and mentoring
  - Choose 2–3 core themes and stay consistent.

• Identify your audience
  - Patients and families
  - Medical students
  - Residents and fellows
  - Referring physicians
  - Media and healthcare leaders
  - Industry and research collaborators
  - Different audiences may require different platforms.

• Choose platforms strategically
  - LinkedIn:
    - Often the highest-value platform for academic physicians.
    - Share publications, insights, leadership perspectives, conference takeaways.
  - X:
    - Strong medical and academic community.
    - Useful for discussing research, conferences, and education.
  - Instagram:
    - Effective for visual education, operating room insights (while protecting privacy), and humanizing your work.
  - YouTube:
    - Excellent for long-form patient education and professional teaching.
  - Avoid trying to dominate every platform initially.

• Create content pillars
  - Patient education
    - Common neurosurgical conditions
    - Surgical myths and misconceptions
    - Recovery expectations
  - Academic expertise
    - Research findings
    - New techniques
    - Conference summaries
  - Teaching and mentorship
    - Training advice
    - Case-based learning
    - Resident education
  - Leadership and healthcare systems
    - Quality improvement
    - Innovation
    - Healthcare policy
  - Personal perspective
    - Career journey
    - Work-life integration
    - Lessons learned in medicine

• Build trust
  - Explain complex topics simply.
  - Cite evidence when discussing medical topics.
  - Avoid sensationalism.
  - Acknowledge uncertainty when appropriate.
  - Never discuss identifiable patient details.

• Develop a recognizable voice
  - Clear and educational.
  - Calm and authoritative.
  - Curious and evidence-based.
  - Avoid sounding like marketing copy.

• Create a sustainable publishing schedule
  - For example:
    - 3–4 LinkedIn posts per week
    - 2–3 X posts per day
    - 1 educational video per week
  - Consistency matters more than volume.

• Leverage your academic work
  - Turn one publication into:
    - A LinkedIn post
    - An X thread
    - A short video
    - A conference presentation summary
  - Repurpose rather than constantly creating from scratch.

• Showcase outcomes carefully
  - Focus on education and process.
  - Avoid implying guaranteed results.
  - Follow institutional policies and local regulations regarding testimonials and outcomes.

• Engage with others
  - Comment on colleagues' work.
  - Highlight trainees' achievements.
  - Participate in specialty discussions.
  - Social media growth often comes more from engagement than posting alone.

Potential positioning example:

"Academic neurosurgeon focused on brain and spine surgery, surgical innovation, and training the next generation of neurosurgeons. Translating complex neuroscience into practical insights for patients, physicians, and trainees."

A practical goal for the first year would be:
- Establish a professional presence on LinkedIn and X.
- Publish 2–4 educational posts weekly.
- Share research and conference insights consistently.
- Build a reputation as a trusted educator rather than aiming primarily for follower count.

If your goal is patient acquisition, academic influence, media visibility, or becoming a nationally recognized thought leader, the branding strategy should be tailored differently.
