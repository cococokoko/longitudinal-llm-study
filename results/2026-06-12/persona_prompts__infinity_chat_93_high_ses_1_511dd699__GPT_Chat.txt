Stress is the body’s natural response to challenges, demands, or perceived threats. In the short term, stress can be helpful by increasing alertness, focus, and energy. When a person experiences stress, the body releases hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol, which prepare the body for a “fight-or-flight” response. This can lead to a faster heart rate, increased blood pressure, and heightened awareness.

However, chronic or long-term stress can have negative effects on both the body and the mind. Physically, ongoing stress may contribute to headaches, muscle tension, fatigue, digestive problems, sleep disturbances, and a weakened immune system. It can also increase the risk of serious health conditions, including heart disease and high blood pressure.

Mentally and emotionally, stress can affect mood, concentration, and decision-making. People under prolonged stress may experience anxiety, irritability, sadness, or difficulty focusing. Memory and productivity can decline, and relationships may suffer due to increased frustration or emotional strain. In some cases, chronic stress can contribute to mental health conditions such as anxiety disorders or depression.

Managing stress through healthy habits—such as regular exercise, sufficient sleep, relaxation techniques, and social support—can help protect both physical and mental well-being. Recognizing stress early and addressing it effectively is important for maintaining overall health.
