There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, widespread use would typically reduce or eliminate its edge over time. Markets change, and strategies that work well in one period can perform poorly in another.

That said, some of the most successful day-trading approaches historically have shared a few characteristics:

- They exploit a specific, measurable market inefficiency.
- They have a positive expected value over many trades.
- They use strict risk management.
- They are executed consistently and with discipline.

Examples of widely used day-trading strategies:

- Trend following / momentum trading
  - Buy strong stocks or assets moving upward and sell weak ones moving downward.
  - Historically one of the most persistent market effects across many asset classes.

- Opening range breakout
  - Trade breakouts above or below the high/low established shortly after the market opens.
  - Common among intraday traders because volatility is often highest early in the session.

- Mean reversion
  - Buy short-term oversold conditions and sell short-term overbought conditions.
  - Often works best in range-bound markets.

- Market-making and statistical arbitrage
  - Used by professional firms and quantitative traders.
  - Can be highly profitable but typically requires sophisticated technology, data, and execution.

- VWAP-based trading
  - Trades around the Volume Weighted Average Price, which many institutions monitor.
  - Often used to identify intraday trends and reversals.

If the question is which strategy has likely generated the most aggregate profits in history, the answer is probably not a retail day-trading method at all. High-frequency market making, statistical arbitrage, and quantitative trend-following strategies run by major firms have likely produced far greater total profits than any simple discretionary day-trading setup.

For individual traders, the biggest determinant of long-term profitability is often risk management rather than the specific entry signal. A mediocre strategy with strong risk controls can outperform a great strategy with poor risk controls.
