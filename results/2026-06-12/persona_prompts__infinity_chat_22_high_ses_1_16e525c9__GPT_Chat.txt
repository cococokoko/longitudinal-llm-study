In Christianity, "discipleship" generally means following Jesus and growing as his disciple, but different traditions emphasize different aspects of what that looks like.

Some major views include:

- Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus.
  - Discipleship involves Bible study, prayer, evangelism, obedience, and spiritual growth.
  - Often focuses on mentoring relationships and small-group accountability.

- Reformed view
  - Stresses discipleship as a lifelong process of sanctification through God's grace.
  - Places strong emphasis on Scripture, preaching, and participation in the church community.
  - Often highlights God's sovereignty in spiritual growth.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as following Christ through faith, participation in the sacraments, prayer, and works of charity.
  - Emphasizes the role of the Church, tradition, and the example of the saints.
  - Views discipleship as a journey toward holiness.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on "theosis" or becoming increasingly united with God.
  - Emphasizes prayer, worship, fasting, spiritual disciplines, and participation in the sacramental life of the Church.
  - Often stresses spiritual formation under guidance from experienced believers.

- Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Highlights personal holiness and social holiness.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual disciplines as well as service to others and concern for justice.
  - Encourages ongoing growth in love of God and neighbor.

- Anabaptist view
  - Strongly emphasizes living according to Jesus' teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Often focuses on community, simplicity, peacemaking, and practical obedience.
  - Discipleship is seen as visible and transformative in daily life.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, evangelism, and personal transformation.
  - Often stresses active ministry and spiritual experience.

- Liberation theology perspectives
  - Focus on following Jesus through solidarity with the poor and marginalized.
  - Discipleship involves social action, justice, and confronting oppression.
  - Often interprets Christian faith through social and economic realities.

Across these traditions, common themes include:
- Learning from Jesus.
- Growing in faith and character.
- Participating in a community of believers.
- Serving others.
- Living according to Christian teachings.

The main differences tend to be in how spiritual growth occurs, the role of the church and sacraments, the importance of social action, and the balance between personal faith and communal practice.
