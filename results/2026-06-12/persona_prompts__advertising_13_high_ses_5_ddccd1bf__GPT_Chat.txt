Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few key variables: the cut, temperature, seasoning, and technique.

1. Start with the right cut
- Ribeye: Rich, well-marbled, forgiving.
- New York strip: Beefy flavor with moderate marbling.
- Filet mignon: Extremely tender, leaner.
- Sirloin: Good balance of flavor and value.

2. Choose a thick steak
- Aim for 1–1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick.
- Thicker steaks are easier to cook evenly without overcooking.

3. Season properly
- Salt generously at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before placing in the pan.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.
- A simple salt-and-pepper approach lets the beef shine.

4. Bring the surface dry
- Pat the steak dry with paper towels.
- A dry surface promotes better browning and crust formation.

5. Use high heat for the sear
- Preheat a heavy skillet (cast iron is ideal) until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.
- Sear until a deep brown crust forms.

6. Cook to temperature, not time
Use an instant-read thermometer:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Many steak enthusiasts prefer medium-rare for the best balance of tenderness and flavor.

7. Finish with butter and aromatics (optional)
- Add butter, garlic cloves, and herbs such as thyme or rosemary during the final minutes.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest before slicing
- Rest 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps juices redistribute throughout the meat.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain when serving.
- This shortens muscle fibers and improves tenderness.

Common mistakes to avoid
- Cooking cold, wet steaks.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak before a crust develops.
- Relying solely on cooking times.
- Skipping the resting period.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.

Practice with the same cut repeatedly and track temperatures. Consistency comes from learning how your equipment behaves and using a thermometer until doneness becomes intuitive.
