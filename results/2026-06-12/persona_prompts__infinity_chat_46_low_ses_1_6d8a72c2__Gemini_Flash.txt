Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, a **linear graph** is most commonly referred to as a **Path Graph** (usually denoted as $P_n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices). 

It is a graph where the vertices are connected in a single, straight line with no branches, loops, or cycles. 

Here is a simple example of a path graph with 4 vertices ($P_4$):

### Visual Representation:

```text
(A) --------- (B) --------- (C) --------- (D)
```

### Formal Definition of this Example:
*   **Vertices (Nodes):** $V = \{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges (Connections):** $E = \{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$

### Key Properties of a Linear/Path Graph:
1.  **End Vertices:** The two vertices at the very ends (in this case, $A$ and $D$) have a **degree of 1** (they only have one connection).
2.  **Internal Vertices:** All other vertices in the middle (in this case, $B$ and $C$) have a **degree of 2** (they have exactly two connections).
3.  **Number of Edges:** A path graph with $n$ vertices will always have exactly $n - 1$ edges. (Our example has 4 vertices and 3 edges).
4.  **No Cycles:** You cannot start at any vertex and follow the edges to get back to where you started without retracing your steps.

### Real-World Analogy:
Think of a **one-way bus route** with 4 stops. You start at Stop A, go to Stop B, then Stop C, and finally arrive at the end of the line, Stop D. You cannot take a shortcut, and the route doesn't loop back to the start.
