── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities shape international relations in many ways, influencing power, cooperation, conflict, and development. Differences in wealth, resources, and economic influence affect how countries interact with one another and how global institutions function.

Key impacts include:

- Distribution of power:
  - Wealthier countries often have greater political and military influence.
  - Economic strength allows states to shape international rules, trade agreements, and financial systems.
  - Major economies frequently have more influence in organizations such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank, and G7.

- Trade relationships:
  - Richer countries often export high-value goods and services, while poorer countries may rely on exporting raw materials or low-cost labor.
  - Unequal trade relationships can reinforce existing economic disparities.
  - Access to global markets can create opportunities for growth, but benefits are not always distributed evenly.

- Development assistance and aid:
  - Wealthier nations provide loans, grants, and technical assistance to developing countries.
  - Aid can support infrastructure, education, and public health.
  - At the same time, aid relationships may create political influence or dependency concerns.

- Debt and financial dependence:
  - Many lower-income countries rely on external borrowing to finance development.
  - Debt burdens can affect domestic policy choices and international negotiations.
  - Creditors, including governments and international institutions, may gain leverage through lending arrangements.

- Migration:
  - Economic disparities encourage migration from poorer regions to wealthier ones in search of employment and better living standards.
  - Migration can strengthen economic ties through remittances but may also generate political tensions over immigration policies.

- Access to technology and innovation:
  - Wealthier countries generally have greater access to advanced technologies, research capabilities, and digital infrastructure.
  - Technological advantages can increase economic competitiveness and geopolitical influence.
  - The "digital divide" can widen existing inequalities between countries.

- International cooperation and conflict:
  - Economic inequality can contribute to instability, especially where poverty, unemployment, and weak institutions are severe.
  - Competition over resources, investment, and development opportunities can influence diplomatic relations.
  - Shared concerns about inequality can also encourage cooperation through development programs and international agreements.

- Climate change and global governance:
  - Wealthier countries have historically contributed more to greenhouse gas emissions, while many poorer countries are more vulnerable to climate impacts.
  - Disputes over climate finance, adaptation funding, and responsibility for emissions are major issues in international negotiations.
  - Economic inequalities shape debates about fairness and burden-sharing in global governance.

Examples:
- China's rise has altered global power balances by increasing its economic influence through trade, investment, and initiatives such as the Belt and Road Initiative.
- Negotiations within the World Trade Organization often reflect tensions between developed and developing countries over market access, agricultural subsidies, and intellectual property rights.
- Climate conferences frequently involve debates between wealthier and poorer nations regarding funding and responsibility.

Overall, global economic inequalities are a central factor in international relations. They influence which countries hold power, how international rules are created, patterns of cooperation and conflict, and efforts to address shared global challenges.
