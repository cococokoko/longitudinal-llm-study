This is a substantive question worth thinking through carefully rather than just offering platitudes.

## Key Mechanisms Worth Considering

**Economic power translates directly to political leverage**
- Creditor nations gain influence over debtor nations through debt conditions
- Large markets create dependency relationships (China's Belt and Road illustrates this clearly)
- Technology gaps create strategic vulnerabilities that shape alliance decisions

**Where the evidence is fairly strong**
- Wealthier nations dominate international institutions (IMF voting weights, UN Security Council composition)
- Trade dependency genuinely constrains foreign policy options for smaller economies
- Resource wealth creates both power and instability simultaneously

**Where analysis gets more complicated**
- Economic interdependence sometimes *reduces* conflict (trading partners have incentives for stability) but sometimes creates leverage for coercion
- Development aid relationships are genuinely mixed - sometimes building goodwill, sometimes creating resentment
- Middle-income countries increasingly challenge simple rich/poor frameworks

## Honest Tensions in the Analysis

- Whether inequality primarily causes conflict or whether conflict primarily causes inequality is genuinely contested
- "Economic coercion" vs "legitimate market relationships" is often politically framed rather than analytically clear
- Multinational corporations complicate state-centric analysis significantly

## What I'd Push Back On

Simplistic narratives - both "inequality drives all conflict" and "free markets solve inequality automatically" - tend to obscure more than they reveal.

What specific dimension interests you most - trade relationships, institutional power, development dynamics?
