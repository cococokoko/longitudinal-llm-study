Different views on discipleship.

“Discipleship” generally means following and learning from a teacher, but different Christian traditions emphasize different aspects of what that looks like in practice. Here are some major perspectives:

- Evangelical Protestant view
  - Discipleship is often understood as a personal commitment to follow Jesus.
  - Emphasizes Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and spiritual growth.
  - Frequently involves mentoring relationships, small groups, or one-on-one guidance.

- Reformed view
  - Focuses on discipleship as lifelong growth in holiness under God's grace.
  - Stresses the importance of sound doctrine, preaching, and participation in the church community.
  - Often highlights God's sovereignty in the believer's spiritual development.

- Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Emphasizes sanctification and growing in love for God and neighbor.
  - Encourages accountability groups, spiritual disciplines, and acts of service.
  - Sees discipleship as an ongoing process of becoming more Christlike.

- Roman Catholic view
  - Understands discipleship as participation in the life of Christ through the Church.
  - Emphasizes the sacraments, prayer, service, and spiritual formation.
  - Often includes guidance from clergy, religious orders, or spiritual directors.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Views discipleship as a journey of transformation and union with God (theosis).
  - Emphasizes worship, prayer, fasting, and participation in the sacramental life of the Church.
  - Focuses strongly on spiritual formation within the historic Christian tradition.

- Anabaptist view
  - Stresses obedience to Jesus' teachings in daily life.
  - Often emphasizes simplicity, community, peacemaking, and practical faithfulness.
  - Discipleship is frequently seen as visible, lived-out commitment rather than primarily doctrinal agreement.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Includes prayer, spiritual gifts, evangelism, and personal transformation.
  - Often focuses on active participation in ministry and spiritual experience.

- Liberation theology perspective
  - Views discipleship as following Jesus through solidarity with the poor and oppressed.
  - Emphasizes justice, social action, and transformation of society alongside personal faith.

Across these traditions, common themes include:
- Following Jesus.
- Learning and growing spiritually.
- Participating in a faith community.
- Serving others.
- Developing a life shaped by Christian teachings.

The main differences tend to concern how spiritual growth happens, the role of the church and sacraments, the importance of doctrine versus practice, and the relationship between personal faith and social action.
