── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to get started with meditation and mindfulness?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Getting started with meditation and mindfulness is simpler than it often appears. The key is consistency rather than trying to achieve a particular mental state.

A beginner-friendly approach:

• Start small
  - Begin with 3–5 minutes per day.
  - Choose a regular time, such as after waking up or before bed.

• Find a comfortable position
  - Sit in a chair, on a cushion, or anywhere you can remain alert and reasonably comfortable.
  - You don't need a special posture.

• Focus on your breath
  - Notice the sensation of breathing in and out.
  - You can pay attention to the air moving through your nose, the rise and fall of your chest, or your abdomen.

• Expect your mind to wander
  - This is normal and happens to everyone.
  - When you notice you've been thinking, gently return your attention to your breath.
  - The act of returning attention is a core part of the practice.

• Gradually increase duration
  - After a week or two, try 5–10 minutes.
  - Increase only if it feels sustainable.

Mindfulness throughout the day:

• Pay full attention to one routine activity:
  - Drinking tea or coffee
  - Walking
  - Washing dishes
  - Brushing your teeth

• Notice:
  - Physical sensations
  - Sounds
  - Smells
  - Thoughts and emotions as they arise

• Practice observing without immediately judging experiences as good or bad.

A simple 5-minute meditation:

1. Sit comfortably and set a timer for 5 minutes.
2. Take a few natural breaths.
3. Notice the feeling of breathing.
4. When thoughts, emotions, or sensations pull your attention away, acknowledge them briefly.
5. Return attention to the breath.
6. Repeat until the timer ends.

Helpful resources:

• Apps: Waking Up, Headspace, Calm, Insight Timer
• Books:
  - "Wherever You Go, There You Are" by Jon Kabat-Zinn
  - "The Mind Illuminated" by Culadasa
  - "Mindfulness in Plain English" by Bhante Gunaratana

A common misconception is that meditation is about stopping thoughts. It's more accurate to think of it as training awareness: noticing what's happening in your mind and learning to redirect attention intentionally. Over time, many people find that this improves focus, emotional regulation, and their ability to respond thoughtfully rather than react automatically.
