How to get started with meditation and mindfulness?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Getting started with meditation and mindfulness is simpler than many people expect. The goal isn't to stop thinking—it's to practice paying attention and gently returning your attention when it wanders.

A beginner-friendly approach:

• Start small
  - Begin with 3–5 minutes a day.
  - Consistency matters more than duration.
  - Pick a regular time, such as before a shift, during a break, or before bed.

• Try a basic breathing meditation
  1. Sit comfortably.
  2. Notice your breathing without changing it.
  3. Focus on the sensation of air moving in and out, or the rise and fall of your chest.
  4. When your mind wanders (which it will), gently bring your attention back to your breath.
  5. Repeat until your timer ends.

• Practice mindfulness during daily activities
  - While washing your hands, notice the temperature of the water and the sensation on your skin.
  - While walking, pay attention to the feeling of your feet contacting the ground.
  - While eating, notice flavors, textures, and smells.
  - During a busy hospital shift, you can take a few mindful breaths between tasks.

• Use guided meditations if helpful
  - Many people find guided audio easier than meditating in silence.
  - Apps and websites such as Headspace, Calm, Insight Timer, UCLA Mindful, and Smiling Mind offer beginner-friendly options.

• Be realistic about expectations
  - Meditation can help improve awareness, focus, and stress management over time.
  - Some sessions will feel calm; others may feel restless.
  - A wandering mind is normal and part of the practice.

A simple 1-minute mindfulness exercise:
• Stop what you're doing.
• Notice 5 things you can see.
• Notice 4 things you can feel.
• Notice 3 things you can hear.
• Notice 2 things you can smell.
• Notice 1 thing you can taste.

If you work in a healthcare setting and have limited time, even 30–60 seconds of focused breathing between patients or tasks can be a meaningful mindfulness practice.
