── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If your home Wi‑Fi is slow, unreliable, or has dead zones, these steps usually provide the biggest improvements:

- Place the router centrally
  - Put it in an open area, not inside a cabinet.
  - Elevate it on a shelf or table.
  - Keep it away from large metal objects, thick walls, microwaves, and cordless phone bases.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band
  - 5 GHz (or 6 GHz if available) offers higher speeds but shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz provides better range but is often more congested.
  - If your router supports band steering, enable it.

- Update router firmware
  - Manufacturers regularly release updates that improve performance, stability, and security.

- Change Wi‑Fi channels
  - In crowded neighborhoods, neighboring networks can cause interference.
  - Use a Wi‑Fi analyzer app to identify less congested channels, especially on 2.4 GHz.

- Upgrade security settings
  - Use WPA3 if supported (or WPA2‑AES if not).
  - Avoid older standards like WEP or WPA.

- Reduce network congestion
  - Disconnect devices you no longer use.
  - Schedule large downloads during off-hours if possible.
  - Enable Quality of Service (QoS) features if your router supports them.

- Upgrade old equipment
  - If your router is more than 5–7 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can provide significant improvements.
  - Make sure your devices also support newer Wi‑Fi standards.

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system
  - For large homes or homes with multiple floors, mesh systems often provide better coverage than a single router.

- Use wired connections where possible
  - Connect stationary devices such as desktop PCs, gaming consoles, or streaming boxes via Ethernet for maximum performance.

- Check your internet plan
  - Sometimes the bottleneck is the internet connection itself rather than the Wi‑Fi network.
  - Run a speed test near the router and compare it to the speeds you're paying for.
