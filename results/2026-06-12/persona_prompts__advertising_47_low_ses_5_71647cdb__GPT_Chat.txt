If you're preparing to run a marathon, consistency matters more than any single workout. Since many people balancing family and household responsibilities have limited training time, a realistic plan is often the most sustainable one.

Some key tips:

- Build mileage gradually
  - Increase your weekly distance slowly (many runners use a guideline of no more than about 10% per week).
  - Include a weekly long run that gradually increases over several months.

- Follow a structured training plan
  - Most marathon plans last 12–20 weeks.
  - Aim for a mix of easy runs, long runs, and occasional speed or tempo workouts.

- Don't skip easy days
  - Easy runs help build endurance while reducing injury risk.
  - Avoid turning every run into a hard effort.

- Practice your race-day nutrition
  - Test gels, sports drinks, or other fuel during long runs.
  - Many runners consume carbohydrates regularly during runs lasting longer than 90 minutes.

- Hydrate appropriately
  - Drink according to thirst and conditions.
  - Practice your hydration strategy during training rather than trying something new on race day.

- Prioritize recovery
  - Sleep is a major part of training adaptation.
  - Consider stretching, mobility work, or light cross-training if they help you feel better.

- Wear tested gear
  - Use shoes and clothing you've already trained in.
  - Avoid new shoes, socks, or nutrition products on race day.

- Learn your pacing
  - Start slower than feels necessary.
  - Many marathon runners lose time by going out too fast in the first half.

- Strength train when possible
  - Even 1–2 short sessions per week can help support running form and reduce injury risk.

- Respect warning signs
  - Persistent pain, worsening injuries, dizziness, or unusual symptoms should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

Race-week tips:
- Reduce training volume (taper).
- Get adequate sleep.
- Eat familiar foods.
- Prepare logistics early so race morning is less stressful.
