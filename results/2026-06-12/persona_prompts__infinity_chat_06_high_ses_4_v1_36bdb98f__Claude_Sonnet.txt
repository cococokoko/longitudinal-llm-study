── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting question, and the fact that you're asking it suggests some self-awareness worth building on.

## Why This Is Hard for Physician-Scientists Specifically

You likely operate under **layered obligations** - to patients, trainees, funders, institutions, collaborators - making it genuinely difficult to separate what *you* want from what others expect of you. High achievers in medicine often develop sophisticated systems for meeting external demands while losing touch with internal ones.

## Some Honest Frameworks

**Distinguish between wanting and performing wanting**
- What would you pursue if no one would know about it or evaluate it?
- What do you defend when challenged, versus what do you quietly feel relieved to abandon?

**Notice your attention, not your stated priorities**
- What do you actually read when you have free time?
- What problems do you find yourself thinking about without being paid to?

**Take your frustrations seriously**
- Persistent frustration often signals a violated value or unmet want
- What bothers you about your field that others seem to accept?

**Separate the question into domains**
- Research direction
- Career structure
- Daily work experience
- Relationships and mentorship
- Life outside the lab

These can have very different answers.

## A Practical Suggestion

Write for 20 minutes answering: *"If my current lab dissolved tomorrow and I could rebuild anything, what would I actually build?"*
