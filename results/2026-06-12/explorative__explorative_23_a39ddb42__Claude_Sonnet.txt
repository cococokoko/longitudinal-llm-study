# The January Reset

The notification had been sitting in Maya's inbox since December 27th.

*"Your annual subscription to ProjectFlow Pro is renewing in 7 days. Thank you for being a valued member."*

She hadn't opened it. She hadn't opened most things since December 27th.

---

It was January 2nd, and Maya was sitting cross-legged on her bed surrounded by what she had decided to call her *system*. Three notebooks — one coral, one black, one with a motivational quote on the cover that she found increasingly aggressive — two highlighters that still worked, one that definitely didn't, and her laptop, which had forty-three browser tabs open across two windows.

She had been freelancing for three years. Graphic design, mostly. Some brand consulting when clients weren't sure what they actually wanted, which was most of the time. She was good at it. She knew she was good at it. The problem was everything that surrounded the actual work — the invoices, the follow-ups, the project timelines, the tax receipts she photographed and then could never find, the clients who emailed at 11pm and expected a response by 8am.

Every January, she told herself this would be the year she got organised.

She had the notebooks to prove it.

---

She started, as she always started, with a YouTube video.

*"How I Run My Entire Freelance Business as a SOLOPRENEUR (2024 System That Actually Works)"*

The woman in the video had a ring light, a colour-coded Notion dashboard, and the calm, unhurried energy of someone who had never once sent an invoice to the wrong client. Maya watched twenty-two minutes of it before realising she had been taking notes in the aggressive motivational notebook without absorbing a single word.

She closed the tab.

She opened four more.

---

By 11am, she had:

- Created a new folder on her desktop called **ADMIN 2025**
- Created a second folder called **ADMIN 2025 ACTUAL**
- Downloaded a free invoice template that was slightly uglier than the one she already used
- Written *January Goals* at the top of the coral notebook in her best handwriting
- Stared at the words *January Goals* for six minutes
- Made coffee
- Let the coffee go cold

The problem, she understood, was not that she lacked systems. She had systems. She had a system for tracking client hours that lived in a spreadsheet she updated roughly every three weeks in a panic. She had a system for invoicing that involved a combination of the ugly template, a notes app, and memory, which was not technically a system. She had a folder called *Receipts 2024* that contained seventeen screenshots, a photo of a parking ticket, and something she couldn't identify.

The problem was that her systems didn't talk to each other. They existed in separate corners of her life like estranged relatives at a wedding — present, technically functional, refusing to make eye contact.

---

She called her friend Dom at noon. Dom was a freelance copywriter and the only person Maya knew who seemed to have genuinely figured it out, or at least gave a convincing performance of having figured it out.

"I'm trying to get organised," Maya said.

"Again?"

"It's a new year."

"It's the second of January."

"That's when you're supposed to do it."

She heard Dom exhale slowly, the sound of someone choosing their words carefully. "Okay. What have you done so far?"

"I made some folders."

"Right."

"And I watched most of a video."

"Maya."

"I know."

"You don't need more folders," Dom said. "You need to pick one thing. Just one. What's the thing that causes you the most stress?"

Maya didn't have to think about it. "Invoices. Chasing invoices. I hate it. I feel like I'm begging people to pay me for work I already did and it makes me want to—"

"Okay, stop. Just do that. Sort out your invoicing. One thing. Don't touch anything else."

"But I also need to sort out my—"

"One thing, Maya."

---
