── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no simple, strong correlation between intelligence and compassion in humans.

What research suggests:

- Intelligence and compassion are different traits. A person can be highly intelligent and very compassionate, highly intelligent and not very compassionate, or vice versa.
- Studies on cognitive ability (IQ, reasoning, problem-solving) generally find only weak relationships with traits such as empathy, kindness, or prosocial behavior.
- Some research finds that people with higher cognitive ability may be somewhat better at understanding another person's perspective ("cognitive empathy"), but that does not necessarily mean they feel more concern for others ("emotional empathy" or compassion).
- Compassion is influenced by many factors beyond intelligence, including personality, upbringing, culture, life experiences, emotional regulation, moral values, and social environment.
- Emotional intelligence—the ability to recognize and manage emotions in oneself and others—is often more closely related to compassionate behavior than traditional measures of intelligence are.

One way to think about it is that intelligence can help someone understand suffering and identify ways to help, but it does not automatically create the motivation to care. Compassion involves both recognizing another person's situation and being moved to respond with concern.

So, if there is a correlation between intelligence and compassion, it appears to be modest and indirect rather than strong or universal.
