Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding a few key principles: selecting the right cut, seasoning properly, controlling heat, and cooking to the desired doneness.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, and forgiving.
- New York strip: Firm texture with a balance of tenderness and flavor.
- Filet mignon: Extremely tender but milder in flavor.
- Sirloin: Leaner and more economical.
- T-bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip steak and tenderloin.

2. Start with quality meat
- Look for good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- USDA Choice or Prime grades generally provide better results.
- Thicker steaks (1–1.5 inches / 2.5–4 cm) are easier to cook evenly.

3. Season simply
- Salt generously at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before it goes in the pan.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.
- A simple seasoning of salt and pepper is often enough for a quality steak.

4. Bring the steak close to room temperature
- Let it sit out for about 20–30 minutes before cooking.
- Pat the surface dry with paper towels for better browning.

5. Use high heat for a great crust
- Cast-iron skillets are excellent for searing.
- Heat the pan until it's very hot before adding oil.
- Use a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.

6. Cook to temperature, not time
Use an instant-read thermometer:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Remove the steak about 5°F (3°C) below the target temperature because it will continue cooking while resting.

7. Try the butter-basting technique
- After searing both sides, reduce heat slightly.
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs such as thyme or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest before slicing
- Let the steak rest for 5–10 minutes.
- This helps retain juices and improves texture.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain when serving.
- This shortens muscle fibers and makes the steak feel more tender.

Common mistakes
- Cooking cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Moving the steak too often during searing.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Skipping the resting period.
- Relying solely on cooking time instead of a thermometer.

A simple steakhouse method:
1. Pat a 1.5-inch ribeye dry and season with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a cast-iron skillet until very hot.
3. Add a small amount of high-smoke-point oil.
4. Sear 2–3 minutes per side.
5. Add butter, garlic, and thyme; baste for 1–2 minutes.
6. Cook until the internal temperature reaches 130°F (54°C) for medium-rare.
7. Rest 5–10 minutes and serve.

The biggest upgrade most home cooks can make is using an instant-read thermometer. It removes guesswork and consistently delivers the doneness you want.
