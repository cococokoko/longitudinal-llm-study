Imagine you have a very long line of dominoes set up in a special pattern.

You ask, "If I knock over the first domino, what will the millionth domino be doing?"

Sometimes that's easy. For example, if every domino just knocks over the next one, you can quickly figure it out.

But some domino setups are so complicated that there isn't a shortcut. The only way to know what the millionth domino is doing is to start at the beginning and watch each domino fall, one after another, until you get there.

That's the idea of computational irreducibility:

- Some systems follow simple rules.
- But even if the rules are simple, the results can become very complicated.
- For certain systems, there is no faster way to know what happens later.
- You have to let the process run step by step.

A simple way to say it is:

"Some things can only be understood by watching them happen."

It's like asking what a story's last page says. For some stories, you can't skip ahead by using a clever trick—you have to read the story as it unfolds.
