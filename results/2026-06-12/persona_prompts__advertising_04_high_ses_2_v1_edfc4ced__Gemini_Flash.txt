I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on taking this step. As a senior attorney, you already possess a massive advantage: you understand risk mitigation, contract enforcement, and regulatory compliance better than 99% of new entrepreneurs. 

However, the transition from **advising** businesses to **operating** one requires a shift in mindset. In law, we are trained to find every possible risk and eliminate it. In entrepreneurship, some level of risk is necessary for speed and growth. The goal is *calculated* risk, not zero risk.

Here is a strategic roadmap tailored for a senior attorney launching a small online business, focusing on structure, efficiency, and balancing your current practice.

---

### Phase 1: The "Day Job" & Ethical Considerations
Before launching, you must clear the runway within your current firm.
*   **Partnership Agreement / Employment Contract:** Review your firm’s partnership agreement or employment contract. Look for non-compete clauses, outside business activity (OBA) restrictions, and intellectual property (IP) assignments (ensuring the firm doesn't own what you build).
*   **Conflicts of Interest:** Ensure your online business does not conflict with the firm’s current or prospective clients. 
*   **Time & Resources:** Establish a strict boundary. Do not use firm resources (lexis/nexis, firm laptops, paralegals, or firm email) for your side venture. 

### Phase 2: Entity Selection & Asset Protection
You know this drill, but it’s worth treating yourself as your own most important client.
*   **The Entity:** Set up a single-member LLC (or an S-Corp if you expect high initial revenues) to shield your personal assets (including your law firm equity/draws) from the liabilities of the online business.
*   **IP Protection:** Register your domain names, social media handles, and file for trademarks early. If you are creating digital products (courses, templates, software), ensure your terms of service clearly state your ownership of the copyright.
*   **Insurance:** Do not rely solely on the LLC. Get a robust General Liability and Cyber Liability policy (crucial for online businesses handling customer data).

### Phase 3: Defining the Business Model
Online businesses generally fall into four categories. Choose the one that best fits your available time:
1.  **E-commerce (Physical Goods):** High overhead, supply chain management, but highly scalable. (e.g., Shopify store).
2.  **Digital Products (E-books, Courses, Templates):** High profit margins, low maintenance once created. *Excellent for leveraging your legal expertise (e.g., selling industry-specific contract templates).*
3.  **SaaS (Software as a Service):** High upfront cost/development, but highly scalable with recurring revenue.
4.  **Productized Services/Consulting:** High hourly rate, but ties your time to your income. 

### Phase 4: The Tech Stack (Keep it Lean)
Do not over-engineer your website or systems. Start with "No-Code" or "Low-Code" tools to test the market.
*   **Website/Storefront:** Shopify (for physical products) or Squarespace/WordPress (for content/services).
*   **Payment Processor:** Stripe or PayPal.
*   **Email Marketing:** ConvertKit or Klaviyo.
*   **Operations/CRM:** Notion or Airtable.

### Phase 5: The "Founder's Dilemma" (Time Management)
As a senior attorney, your billable hour is highly valuable. You cannot afford to spend 5 hours fixing a website bug or designing social media graphics.
*   **Outsource Early:** Use platforms like Upwork, Fiverr, or specialized agencies to handle web development, graphic design, and basic administrative tasks. 
*   **Automate:** Use Zapier or Make.com to connect your apps (e.g., automatically adding a Shopify buyer to your email list).
*   **The 5-Hour Rule:** Dedicate a non-negotiable block of time each week (e.g., Saturday mornings from 7 AM to noon) to work *on* the business, rather than trying to squeeze it into 15-minute increments between client calls.

3.  **Build a Minimum Viable Product (MVP):** What is the simplest, cheapest version of your product/service you can sell to prove people actually want it?
