### Thesis Idea 1: "The Autonomous Sphere of Care: Gorz vs. Wages for Housework"
* **The Core Question:** How does Gorz’s distinction between "autonomous activity" and "heteronomous work" redefine the Marxist-feminist debate on social reproduction?
* **The Argument:** Traditional Marxist-feminist frameworks (like Silvia Federici’s "Wages for Housework") argue for the commodification or state-compensation of care work to make it visible. Gorz, however, argued vehemently against professionalizing or commodifying life’s intimate spheres. He believed that turning care, parenting, and mutual aid into paid "jobs" colonizes the "lifeworld" with market logic. 
* **Your Contribution:** You can argue for a **"Gorzian Care Ethics."** Instead of demanding wages for care, we should demand the *reduction of work time* so that care can exist entirely in the "autonomous sphere"—free from both market valuation and state surveillance. This reframes parenting not as "unpaid labor" to be compensated, but as the ultimate form of "non-work" (autonomous self-production) that must be protected from the market.

### Thesis Idea 2: "The 'Non-Worker' in the Age of the Platform Economy: Gorz’s *Farewell to the Working Class* Revisited"
* **The Core Question:** Does the rise of the gig economy, remote work, and algorithmic management validate Gorz’s controversial thesis in *Farewell to the Working Class*?
* **The Argument:** In 1980, Gorz argued that the traditional proletariat was being replaced by a "non-class of non-workers"—people who do not identify with their jobs, have no job security, and whose work is fragmented. Today, gig workers, freelance creatives, and remote workers fit this description perfectly. 
* **Your Contribution:** Traditional Marxists often try to "re-proletarianize" these workers (organizing them into traditional unions). You can argue that Gorz offers a better path: **embracing the "non-worker" identity.** Instead of fighting for "good jobs," the goal of the platform precariat should be the decoupling of survival from employment (via a Universal Basic Income, which Gorz championed) to allow for a "multi-active" life.

### Thesis Idea 3: "Eco-Socialism and the 'Right to be Lazy': Gorz, Degrowth, and the Politics of Time"
* **The Core Question:** How does Gorz’s ecological critique of "productivism" provide a psychological and existential foundation for the modern Degrowth movement?
* **The Argument:** Many ecological Marxist theories focus on macro-economics (metabolic rift, resource extraction). Gorz was unique because he connected ecology to *existentialism*. He argued that we consume useless commodities because we are starved of *time* and *autonomy*. We buy convenience (fast food, outsourced care) because wage labor exhausts us.
* **Your Contribution:** Develop a theory of **"Temporal Ecology."** Argue that the ecological crisis cannot be solved by green technology, but by a radical reduction in working hours. By reclaiming time, we reclaim the ability to produce for ourselves (cooking, repairing, caring, playing), which inherently reduces our ecological footprint. You can use your experience of balancing childcare/work as a micro-case study of how "time poverty" forces ecological unsustainability.

### Thesis Idea 4: "The Existentialist Marxist: Reconciling Sartre and Marx through Gorzian Autonomy"
* **The Core Question:** How does Gorz’s early existentialist work (like *La Morale de l'histoire*) inform his later ecological and economic theories?
* **The Argument:** Gorz was a student of Jean-Paul Sartre. While many Marxists abandoned Sartre’s radical focus on individual freedom, Gorz integrated it. For Gorz, the goal of socialism is not just the collective ownership of the means of production, but the maximization of individual *existential freedom*—the freedom to define oneself outside of one's economic utility.
* **Your Contribution:** This would be a highly theoretical thesis. You would argue that Gorz solves the "structure vs. agency" problem in Marxism. By using existentialism, Gorz ensures that Marxist theory does not become authoritarian or purely technocratic. Socialism, in a Gorzian sense, is the infrastructure required for individual existential authenticity.
