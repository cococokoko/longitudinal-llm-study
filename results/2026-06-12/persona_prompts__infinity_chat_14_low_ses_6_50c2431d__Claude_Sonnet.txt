── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question worth thinking through carefully.

## Key Documented Effects

**Concerning patterns:**
- Engagement optimization tends to favor emotionally provocative content
- Filter bubbles can limit exposure to challenging perspectives
- Outrage and conflict often generate more interaction than nuanced discussion
- Misinformation can spread faster than corrections

**But worth noting complications:**
- Research on filter bubbles is actually more mixed than popular narratives suggest
- People actively seek confirming information regardless of algorithms
- Algorithms also expose people to *unexpected* content sometimes
- Traditional media had its own distortion effects

## Genuine Tensions

- Personalization improves user experience but may fragment shared reality
- Engagement metrics aren't perfectly aligned with informational quality
- Platform incentives don't necessarily match democratic health
- Scale makes effects harder to study rigorously

## What's Actually Contested

- How much algorithms *cause* polarization vs. reflect existing divisions
- Whether exposure to opposing views actually reduces polarization (some research suggests it increases it)
- What "healthy" public discourse even looks like as a standard

## Honest Assessment

The effects are **real but probably more complex** than either "algorithms are destroying democracy" or "this is just neutral technology."
