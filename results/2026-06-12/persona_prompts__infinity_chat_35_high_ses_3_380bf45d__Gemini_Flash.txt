Osaka is a city of profound historical gravity (as the merchant capital of the Edo period) and vibrant, democratic energy (as Japan’s culinary kitchen).

### **Day 1: The Historical Axis & The Neon Corridor**
*Focus: Feudal power, merchant wealth, and the post-war economic boom.*

*   **Morning: Osaka Castle (Ōsaka-jō) & The Historical Museum**
    *   **8:30 AM – 11:30 AM:** Begin your exploration at Osaka Castle. While the main keep is a concrete reconstruction (rebuilt in 1931), the monumental stone walls, the Otemon Gate, and the surrounding moats are authentic 17th-century marvels of Edo-period engineering. 
    *   *Academic Insight:* Pay close attention to the massive "octopus stone" (Tako-ishi) near the Sakura-mon Gate. It reflects the competitive display of wealth by the daimyo (feudal lords) who were forced to contribute to the reconstruction.
    *   **Alternative/Extension:** If you prefer authentic artifacts to reconstructions, visit the **Osaka Museum of History** located just across the street. Its top floors offer a brilliant architectural scale model of the ancient Naniwa Palace.

*   **Lunch: Kuromon Ichiba Market**
    *   **12:00 PM – 1:30 PM:** Take the Tanimachi Subway Line to Tanimachi 9-chrome, transfer to the Sennichimae Line, and exit at Nippombashi. Walk through Kuromon Market. While tourist-heavy today, this market has been the "Kitchen of Osaka" since the Meiji era. Sample fresh sashimi, grilled wagyu skewers, or grilled scallops.

*   **Afternoon: Shitenno-ji Temple & Shinsekai**
    *   **2:00 PM – 4:30 PM:** Head south to **Shitenno-ji**, founded in 593 AD by Prince Shotoku. It is one of Japan’s oldest officially administered Buddhist temples. Although rebuilt over the centuries due to fire and war, the layout (the *Shitenno-ji-shiki*) remains a pristine example of 6th-century continental design.
    *   **4:30 PM – 6:00 PM:** Walk west to **Shinsekai** ("New World"). Developed in 1912 with a design inspired by Paris and New York's Coney Island, it became a symbol of early 20th-century Japanese modernity. Today, it is a nostalgic, slightly gritty relic of the Showa era. Stop for a snack of *Kushikatsu* (deep-fried skewered meat and vegetables)—remember the golden rule: *no double-dipping in the communal sauce*.

*   **Evening: Dotonbori & Shinsaibashi**
    *   **6:30 PM onward:** Walk or take the subway north to Namba. As night falls, experience the sensory overload of **Dotonbori**. This canal district was the theater heartland of Osaka during the Edo period; today, it is a monument to consumerism and gastronomy. 
    *   *Dinner:* Seek out *Takoyaki* (octopus balls) or *Okonomiyaki* (savory cabbage pancakes). For a quieter evening, slip into **Hozenji Yokocho**, a narrow, stone-paved alleyway that preserves the atmosphere of pre-war Osaka, and pay respects to the moss-covered Fudo Myoo statue at Hozenji Temple.

### **Day 2: Modernity, Height, and the Port City**
*Focus: Post-modern architecture, maritime trade, and contemporary urban culture.*

*   **Morning: Umeda Sky Building & Nakazaki-cho**
    *   **9:00 AM – 11:00 AM:** Start in the northern hub of Umeda. Visit the **Umeda Sky Building**, designed by Hiroshi Hara. Its "Floating Garden Observatory" is a brilliant architectural feat—two 40-story towers connected by a circular bridge with an open-air atrium. It offers an unparalleled view of the Yodo River and the city's sprawling geography.
    *   **11:00 AM – 12:30 PM:** Walk east to **Nakazaki-cho**. This quiet neighborhood miraculously survived the air raids of World War II. Today, its traditional *nagaya* (wooden row houses) have been converted into bohemian cafes, art galleries, and vintage clothing shops. It offers a stark, peaceful contrast to the steel of Umeda.

*   **Lunch: Umeda Underground**
    *   **12:30 PM – 1:30 PM:** Return to Umeda and navigate the subterranean labyrinth of "Umeda Chika." Osaka’s underground malls are cities unto themselves. Find a traditional *udon* or *tonkatsu* spot frequented by local salarymen.

*   **Afternoon: Osaka Bay Area (Tempozan & Kaiyukan)**
    *   **2:00 PM – 5:30 PM:** Take the Chuo Subway Line to Osakako Station. Osaka’s identity is fundamentally tied to its port. Visit the **Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan**, one of the world's largest public aquariums. The exhibit design is brilliant: you begin at the top floor and spiral downward around a massive central tank representing the Pacific Ocean's Ring of Fire.

*   **Evening: Amerikamura & Orange Street**
    *   **6:30 PM onward:** Head back to the Shinsaibashi area. Explore **Amerikamura** (the epicenter of Osaka's youth culture and vintage fashion since the 1970s) and **Orange Street** (Tachibana-dori), a former furniture-maker district that is now a sophisticated strip of boutiques, cafes, and design shops. 
    *   *Dinner:* Enjoy a modern Izakaya experience in the Nishi-Shinsaibashi or Horie districts.

### **Day 3: The Merchant Legacy & Departure**
*Focus: Traditional commerce, spiritual preservation, and seamless transit.*

*To ensure a stress-free departure, you must tailor this morning based on your departure airport. I will assume you are flying out of **Kansai International Airport (KIX)**, which requires a 45-to-50-minute train ride from central Osaka.*

*   **Morning: Sumiyoshi Taisha Shrine**
    *   **9:00 AM – 11:00 AM:** Check out of your hotel and leave your luggage with the concierge (or utilize the coin lockers at Namba Station, which is highly recommended as it is your transit hub to the airport).
    *   Take the Nankai Main Line from Namba to Sumiyoshi-Taisha Station. **Sumiyoshi Taisha** is one of Japan’s oldest Shinto shrines, founded in the 3rd century, before the introduction of Buddhism. It is built in the *Sumiyoshi-zukuri* architectural style, which is purely indigenous to Japan (characterized by straight roofs, unlike the curved roofs influenced by Chinese architecture).
    *   *Highlight:* Walk across the iconic, steeply arched Sorihashi (Taiko) Bridge, which reflects beautifully in the water below.

*   **Lunch & Last-Minute Shopping: Sennichimae Doguyasuji Shopping Street**
    *   **11:30 AM – 1:30 PM:** Return to Namba. Walk through **Doguyasuji**, a 150-meter-long shopping arcade dedicated entirely to kitchenware and restaurant supplies. This is where Osaka’s professional chefs buy their knives, copper tamagoyaki pans, and the famous wax food models (*shampuru*). It is the perfect place to buy high-quality, authentic souvenirs.
    *   *Lunch:* Grab a quick, comforting bowl of *Kitsune Udon* (udon topped with sweet fried tofu, which originated in Osaka) at a local noodle shop.

*   **Early Afternoon: Den Den Town or Namba Parks**
    *   **1:30 PM – 3:30 PM:** Depending on your interests:
        *   *For the pop-culture enthusiast:* Walk through **Den Den Town** (Nipponbashi), Osaka’s answer to Tokyo’s Akihabara, specializing in electronics, anime, and gaming.
        *   *For the architecture enthusiast:* Visit **Namba Parks**, a massive shopping complex designed by Jon Jerde. It features a highly acclaimed rooftop garden that slopes upward over eight floors, integrating nature into a dense urban canyon.
