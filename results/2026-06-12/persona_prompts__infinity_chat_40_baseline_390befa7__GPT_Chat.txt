── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on what you're contributing, what legal rights you'll have, and what the two of you agree is equitable.

A useful principle is: your financial contributions should roughly match the benefits and ownership you're receiving.

Some common options:

- Option 1: Pay rent, but don't contribute to ownership
  - Your girlfriend buys the house in her name.
  - You live there and pay a reasonable amount toward housing costs, similar to rent.
  - She builds the equity because she's taking the ownership risk and making the investment.
  - This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.

- Option 2: Split household expenses, but not the mortgage principal
  - You contribute to utilities, groceries, maintenance, and perhaps part of the interest, taxes, or insurance.
  - She pays the mortgage principal that creates equity.
  - This can feel fair if you don't have ownership rights.

- Option 3: Buy into the house
  - If you're contributing significantly to the mortgage and want ownership, you can become a co-owner.
  - This should be documented legally.
  - The ownership percentages should reflect each person's contribution.

- Option 4: Wait until marriage and reassess
  - If marriage is likely within a year, some couples keep the house solely in the buyer's name until after marriage.
  - Then they decide whether to combine finances, refinance, add a spouse to the title, or maintain separate ownership.

Questions that matter:
- Will your name be on the title?
- Will your name be on the mortgage?
- Are you helping with the down payment?
- If you break up before marriage, what happens to the money you contributed?
- If you marry, do you expect finances to be combined or partially separate?

One thing many people regret is contributing substantial money toward a partner's house without any written agreement about ownership. If you're paying thousands toward a down payment, renovations, or mortgage principal, it's worth documenting exactly what those payments mean.
