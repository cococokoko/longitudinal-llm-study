My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here because it depends on your finances, your relationship, and what risks each of you is taking. The key is to separate the emotional question ("we're building a life together") from the legal and financial question ("who owns what if things don't go as planned?").

A few common approaches:

- She buys the house in her name, and you pay "rent."
  - If she's the sole owner and is taking on the mortgage, down payment, maintenance risk, and ownership benefits, this is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.
  - You contribute a mutually agreed monthly amount that is lower than or comparable to what you'd pay to live elsewhere.
  - This avoids ambiguity about ownership.

- You contribute to household expenses but not the mortgage.
  - For example, she pays the mortgage, and you cover utilities, groceries, internet, or other shared costs.
  - This can feel simpler if you don't want to create any expectation of equity.

- You buy into ownership.
  - If you're contributing substantially toward the down payment or mortgage and want ownership rights, talk to a real-estate attorney about formalizing your share.
  - This requires documentation and can become complicated, but it aligns contribution and ownership.

- Wait until marriage to combine housing finances.
  - If marriage is likely within a year, some couples intentionally keep the house as the purchasing partner's separate asset until they're married, then revisit finances afterward.

Questions to discuss together:
- Who is providing the down payment?
- Who is responsible for repairs, taxes, insurance, and major maintenance?
- If you contribute monthly, do either of you expect that to create ownership rights?
- If you broke up before marriage, what would each of you think was fair?
- After marriage, do you plan to merge finances or keep some assets separate?

In many situations like yours, a practical middle ground is:
- She purchases and owns the house.
- You pay a reasonable amount toward living there (rent or shared expenses).
- Both of you explicitly agree that your payments do not create ownership unless you later decide otherwise.
- After marriage, revisit the arrangement and decide whether to combine finances or formally add you to the property's ownership.
