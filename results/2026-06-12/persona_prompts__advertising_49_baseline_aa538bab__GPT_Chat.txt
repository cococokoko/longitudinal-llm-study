How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what's causing the problem, but these steps usually have the biggest impact:

- Place the router centrally
  - Put it in an open area, not inside a cabinet.
  - Elevate it on a shelf or table.
  - Keep it away from large metal objects, TVs, microwaves, and cordless phone bases.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available
  - 5 GHz and 6 GHz offer higher speeds and less interference than 2.4 GHz.
  - 2.4 GHz reaches farther but is often more crowded.

- Update router firmware
  - Manufacturers regularly release updates that improve performance, security, and stability.

- Optimize Wi‑Fi channels
  - In crowded neighborhoods or apartments, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Use your router's automatic channel selection or a Wi‑Fi analyzer app to identify less congested channels.

- Upgrade older equipment
  - If your router is several years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can provide better coverage and performance.
  - Ensure your devices also support newer Wi‑Fi standards where possible.

- Use a mesh system for larger homes
  - Mesh Wi‑Fi systems are often more effective than traditional range extenders.
  - Place mesh nodes where they still receive a strong signal from the main unit.

- Connect stationary devices with Ethernet
  - PCs, game consoles, TVs, and streaming boxes perform best on wired connections.
  - This also reduces wireless congestion.

- Reduce interference
  - Move Wi‑Fi equipment away from Bluetooth devices, baby monitors, wireless cameras, and microwave ovens when possible.

- Check your internet plan
  - Sometimes the issue is the internet connection itself rather than Wi‑Fi.
  - Run a speed test near the router and compare it to the speed you're paying for.

- Restart equipment occasionally
  - Rebooting the modem and router can resolve temporary issues.
