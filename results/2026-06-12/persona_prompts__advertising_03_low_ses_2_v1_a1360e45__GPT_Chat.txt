I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

Improving productivity while working from home.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working from home as a single parent often means managing two full-time responsibilities at once. Productivity strategies tend to work best when they account for interruptions rather than assuming uninterrupted focus.

Some approaches that can help:

- Use a "must-do" list instead of a full task list.
  - Identify the 1–3 most important work tasks for the day.
  - If those get done, the day is still a success even if other items move to tomorrow.

- Work in focused blocks.
  - Use periods when your child is occupied, napping, at school, or engaged in an activity for your highest-concentration work.
  - Reserve lower-focus tasks (email, scheduling, administrative work) for times when interruptions are more likely.

- Create a predictable daily rhythm.
  - Even a simple routine can reduce decision fatigue.
  - For example: morning planning, focused work block, lunch, meetings, afternoon admin tasks.

- Communicate boundaries clearly.
  - Let coworkers know your typical availability and response times.
  - Use calendar blocks to show when you're unavailable or doing focused work.

- Prepare independent activities in advance.
  - Age-appropriate activities, books, puzzles, or educational apps can help create short windows for concentrated work.
  - Rotating activities can help maintain interest.

- Reduce task-switching.
  - Group similar tasks together, such as answering emails at specific times rather than continuously throughout the day.

- Keep a capture system.
  - When interrupted, jot down the next step of what you were doing.
  - This makes it easier to resume work quickly afterward.

- Build buffer time into your schedule.
  - Children get sick, routines change, and unexpected needs arise.
  - Scheduling at 70–80% of your theoretical capacity often leads to more realistic productivity.

- Consider flexible work arrangements if available.
  - Adjusted hours, asynchronous communication, or compressed schedules can sometimes fit better with caregiving responsibilities.

- Protect your energy.
  - Adequate sleep, short breaks, and realistic expectations often improve productivity more than trying to work longer hours.

A useful mindset is to measure productivity by meaningful progress on priorities rather than by uninterrupted hours worked. For many single parents, consistency and adaptability are more sustainable than trying to replicate a traditional office schedule at home.
