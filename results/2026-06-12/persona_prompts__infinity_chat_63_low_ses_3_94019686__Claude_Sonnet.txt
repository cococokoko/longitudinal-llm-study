# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## The Function

Let's use a simple function of two variables:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^2$$

This could represent, for example, a **company's revenue** where x = units of Product A and y = units of Product B.

---

## Finding the Partial Derivatives

### Partial derivative with respect to x (treat y as constant):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

### Partial derivative with respect to y (treat x as constant):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 2y$$

---

## Numerical Illustration

Let's evaluate at the point **(x = 3, y = 4)**

**First, find f(3, 4):**
$$f(3,4) = 3(3)^2 + 2(3)(4) + (4)^2 = 27 + 24 + 16 = \mathbf{67}$$

---

### ∂f/∂x at (3, 4):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(3,4)} = 6(3) + 2(4) = 18 + 8 = \mathbf{26}$$

> **Meaning:** If x increases by 1 unit (while y stays fixed at 4), f increases by approximately **26 units**

**Verification:**
$$f(4, 4) = 3(16) + 2(4)(4) + 16 = 48 + 32 + 16 = 96$$
$$96 - 67 = \mathbf{29} \approx 26 ✓$$ *(close, with small difference due to nonlinearity)*

---

### ∂f/∂y at (3, 4):
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(3,4)} = 2(3) + 2(4) = 6 + 8 = \mathbf{14}$$

> **Meaning:** If y increases by 1 unit (while x stays fixed at 3), f increases by approximately **14 units**

**Verification:**
$$f(3, 5) = 3(9) + 2(3)(5) + 25 = 27 + 30 + 25 = 82$$
$$82 - 67 = \mathbf{15} \approx 14 ✓$$

## Key Concept

> A **partial derivative** measures the rate of change of a function with respect to **one variable at a time**, while all other variables are **held constant**.
