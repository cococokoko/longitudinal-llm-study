── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background actually makes it worth thinking about carefully.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, practically impossible

- **Volume alone**: Human knowledge doubles roughly every few years across all fields
- **Even within neurosurgery**, keeping current with literature is a recognized professional challenge
- **Specialization exists precisely because** comprehensive mastery requires narrowing scope

## But the question deserves more precision

**What counts as "knowing"?**
- Memorizing facts vs. understanding principles
- Procedural knowledge vs. declarative knowledge
- Knowing *that* vs. knowing *how*

**"Everything" about what?**
- All human knowledge? Almost certainly impossible
- Everything relevant to your specialty? Debatable but practically very difficult
- Everything about a single patient case? Arguably more achievable

## Philosophically interesting complications

- Some knowledge may be **fundamentally unknowable** (certain quantum states, subjective experience)
- **Known unknowns vs. unknown unknowns** - you can't know what you don't know you're missing
- Gödel's incompleteness theorems suggest even formal systems have inherent limits

## Practically relevant to your work

The honest recognition that **you cannot know everything** is arguably what makes good physicians - knowing the **limits of your knowledge** and when to consult, refer, or say "I don't know."
