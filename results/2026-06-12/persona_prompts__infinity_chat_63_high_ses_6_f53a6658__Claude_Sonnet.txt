# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## The Function

Consider a simple **revenue function** for a business:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^2$$

where:
- **x** = units of Product A sold
- **y** = units of Product B sold

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### Partial Derivative with respect to x
*"How does revenue change as we sell more of Product A, holding Product B constant?"*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

### Partial Derivative with respect to y
*"How does revenue change as we sell more of Product B, holding Product A constant?"*

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 2y$$

---

## Numerical Illustration

**Suppose x = 4 and y = 3**

| Calculation | Value |
|---|---|
| f(4, 3) = 3(16) + 2(4)(3) + 9 | **= 81** |
| ∂f/∂x at (4,3) = 6(4) + 2(3) | **= 30** |
| ∂f/∂y at (4,3) = 2(4) + 2(3) | **= 14** |

---

## Verification (Intuitive Check)

**Increase x by a small amount (Δx = 0.01), hold y = 3 fixed:**

$$f(4.01, 3) = 3(4.01)^2 + 2(4.01)(3) + 9 = 81.3003$$

$$\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{81.3003 - 81}{0.01} ≈ \textbf{30.03} \approx 30 ✓$$

---

## Key Insight

> **∂f/∂x = 30** means: selling **one more unit of Product A** (while keeping Product B sales fixed at 3) increases revenue by approximately **30 units**

> **∂f/∂y = 14** means: selling **one more unit of Product B** (while keeping Product A sales fixed at 4) increases revenue by approximately **14 units**

This tells management that **at this operating point**, expanding Product A sales is **more than twice as impactful** as expanding Product B sales.
