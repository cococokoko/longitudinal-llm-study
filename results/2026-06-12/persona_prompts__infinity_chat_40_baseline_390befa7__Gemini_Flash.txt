── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of the most common—and potentially minefield-laden—financial situations couples face. Because you are not yet married, the law treats you as two separate individuals, but your emotional commitment means you want to act as a team. 

To find the "best" solution, you have to separate the **legal/equity** side of homeownership from the **living expenses** side. 

Here is a breakdown of how to look at this, the options available to you, and how to protect both of your interests.

---

### The Golden Rule of Pre-Marital Real Estate
**If your name is not on the deed, you should not be paying the mortgage.** 

If you pay toward the mortgage of a house you do not legally own, you are essentially giving your girlfriend a tax-free gift or paying "rent" with zero return on investment. If you break up, she keeps the house and all the equity you helped build, and you leave with nothing. Conversely, if you pay for the mortgage and expect a share of the house without being on the deed, it creates massive legal gray areas.

---

### Option 1: The "Tenant" Model (Safest before marriage)
Since she is buying the house now, she is the sole owner. You move in as a tenant, but with "partner" perks.

*   **How it works:** She pays the mortgage, property taxes, and home insurance (the costs of *ownership*). You pay her a set monthly amount of "rent" plus half the utilities. 
*   **How to calculate "rent":** Do not just pay half her mortgage. Instead, look at market rent for a similar room/house in your area and pay a fraction of that (e.g., 30% to 50% of market rent), or pay an amount proportional to your income.
*   **Why it’s fair:** She gets help with her living costs, but she bears the risk of homeownership. You get a place to live that is likely cheaper than renting your own apartment, allowing you to save money. 
*   **The Rule:** If the water heater breaks or the roof leaks, **she pays for it.** You do not pay for renovations or repairs on a house you do not own.

### Option 2: The "Co-Buyer" Model (Delay the purchase)
If you plan to marry in a year, the most financially integrated option is to wait to buy.

*   **How it works:** She pauses the home search. You get married, pool your savings, and buy a home *together* as a married couple.
*   **Why it’s fair:** You both start with equal footing, equal ownership, and a joint mortgage. 

### Option 3: The "Buy-In" Model (Post-Marriage)
She buys the house now. You move in and pay "rent" (Option 1). Once you get married, you legally "buy in" to the house.

*   **How it works:** After the wedding, you refinance the mortgage to put both of your names on it, or draft a legal agreement. You contribute a lump sum (perhaps what you saved while paying low rent) to pay down the principal, or you start paying 50% of the mortgage and expenses.
*   **Why it’s fair:** It transitions the asset from "hers" to "ours" once the legal protection of marriage is in place.

---

### How to Decide: Ask Yourselves These Questions

To find the exact right formula, sit down with your girlfriend and discuss these four things:

1.  **What is her motivation for buying now?** Is she buying because she wants *her own* investment, or is she buying this as your future family home? If it's the latter, you should have a say in which house she buys.
2.  **What is the income disparity?** If she makes $150k and you make $50k, a 50/50 split of expenses is unfair. If you make similar incomes, a 50/50 split of *living expenses* (not mortgage equity) makes sense.
3.  **What happens if you break up?** It is unromantic, but essential. If you put $20,000 into her down payment today and you break up in 10 months, how do you get that money back? (If you do contribute to the down payment, you *must* have a real estate lawyer draw up a **Cohabitation Agreement**).
4.  **How does she feel about it?** Some buyers feel strongly about "I bought this, it is mine." Others want a partner to share the load immediately. 

### The Best Solution (Recommended Path)

1.  **For the next year (Before Marriage):** Treat her as the landlord and yourself as the tenant. Pay a reasonable, agreed-upon "rent" that is lower than market rate. Do not pay for repairs, renovations, or the down payment. Save the money you are sparing on rent.
2.  **After the Wedding:** Revisit the home. Have a lawyer add your name to the deed. At that point, combine your finances, use the savings you accumulated over the year to make a large payment on the principal or renovate, and begin paying the mortgage together as equal partners.
