### 1. The Institutional Champion: High-Frequency Trading (HFT) & Market Making
By a wide margin, the most profitable day trading strategy in human history is **institutional market making powered by High-Frequency Trading (HFT) algorithms.**

*   **How it works:** Firms like Virtu Financial or Citadel Securities act as market makers. They continuously post bids and offers for securities. They do not hold positions overnight. Instead, they capture the "bid-ask spread" millions of times a day, holding assets for fractions of a second.
*   **The Profitability:** To illustrate its dominance, Virtu Financial famously disclosed in its 2014 IPO prospectus that **it had only one losing trading day in a span of 1,238 days.** 
*   **Why you cannot do it:** This strategy requires billions of dollars in infrastructure, co-location (placing servers inside the stock exchange buildings), and direct market access. It is legally and technologically out of reach for retail traders.

### 2. The Quantitative King: Statistical Arbitrage (StatArb)
Developed in the 1980s by Morgan Stanley and perfected by hedge funds like Renaissance Technologies (specifically their Medallion Fund, which averaged ~66% gross annual returns from 1988 to 2018), Statistical Arbitrage is the gold standard of quantitative trading.

*   **How it works:** It uses complex mathematical models to identify temporary pricing inefficiencies between related financial instruments (e.g., two highly correlated stocks, or a stock and its index). The algorithm buys the undervalued asset and shorts the overvalued asset, expecting them to revert to their historical correlation.
*   **Why it works:** It relies on mathematical probability and massive diversification across thousands of trades a day, rather than "guessing" the direction of the market.

### 3. The Best Retail Day Trading Strategies (Historically Consistent)
For individual traders who do not have supercomputers, the "best" strategies are those that are simple, highly disciplined, and exploit human psychology. Historically, two strategies stand out:

#### A. Momentum / Trend Following (The "Ride the Wave" Strategy)
*   **The Concept:** "The trend is your friend." Instead of trying to predict reversals, momentum traders look for stocks that are already moving heavily in one direction on high volume (often driven by news, earnings, or macroeconomic data) and ride that wave for a few minutes or hours.
*   **Why it’s profitable:** It aligns with the herd mentality of the market. When a stock breaks out of a key technical level, automated algorithms and retail traders pile in, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.

#### B. Mean Reversion (The "Rubber Band" Strategy)
*   **The Concept:** This is the opposite of momentum. It assumes that asset prices are like rubber bands—if they are stretched too far in one direction (overbought or oversold), they will eventually snap back to their average price.
*   **How it’s traded:** Traders use indicators like the Relative Strength Index (RSI) or Bollinger Bands to identify extreme price extensions and trade the reversal.

### The Legal & Practical Reality: Why Strategy is Only 20% of the Equation
In my profession, I have represented clients who have lost their life savings to "guaranteed" trading strategies, and I have represented successful fund managers. The difference between those who succeed and those who fail is rarely the *strategy* itself. It comes down to three pillars:

1.  **Risk Management (The Golden Rule):** The most profitable traders in the world are actually masters of *losing*. They use strict stop-losses. A trader with a 40% win rate can be highly profitable if their average win is three times larger than their average loss. Conversely, a trader with a 90% win rate can go bankrupt on a single bad trade if they do not manage risk.
2.  **Psychological Discipline:** Human emotion (greed and fear) is the enemy of trading. The "best" strategy is useless if a trader panics and closes a position too early, or holds onto a losing trade hoping it will recover.
3.  **Capitalization and Leverage:** Under-capitalized traders are forced to use excessive leverage to make meaningful profits, which almost always leads to ruin.

If you are looking for the **most profitable strategy of all time**, it is **Quantitative Market Making / Statistical Arbitrage**—but this is a game played by billionaires with supercomputers.
