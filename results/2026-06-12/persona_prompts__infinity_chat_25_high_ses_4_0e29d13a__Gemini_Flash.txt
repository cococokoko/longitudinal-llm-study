How to say a formal thank you for your time in the subject line of an email?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, I receive a high volume of emails daily. The subject lines that stand out—and get opened quickly—are those that are **professional, specific, and concise**. 

To make a "thank you for your time" subject line effective, it is best to pair the gratitude with the **context** of your meeting. This helps the recipient immediately recall who you are and why you are writing.

Here are several formal options, categorized by the scenario:

### 1. After an Interview (Residency, Fellowship, Faculty, or Lab Position)
These should be highly professional and include your name for easy reference.
*   **Thank you for your time: [Your Name] – [Position Title]**
    *   *Example:* Thank you for your time: Dr. Jane Smith – Cardiology Fellowship
*   **Appreciation for your time / [Your Name] – [Position Title]**
*   **Thank you for the opportunity to interview – [Your Name]**

### 2. After a Scientific Collaboration or Research Meeting
If you met to discuss a project, grant, or paper, keep it focused on the topic.
*   **Thank you for your time today / [Project Name or Topic]**
    *   *Example:* Thank you for your time today / R01 Grant Proposal
*   **Follow-up and thank you: [Topic]**
*   **Appreciation for your time regarding [Topic]**

### 3. After a Mentorship, Advice, or Informational Meeting
When someone has generously given you their time for career guidance or scientific advice.
*   **Thank you for your time and guidance today**
*   **With sincere appreciation for your time and advice**
*   **Thank you for your time / Follow-up from [Your Name]**

### 4. Short & Classic (Best for general professional meetings)
If you want to keep it brief and traditional.
*   **Thank you for your time today**
*   **Sincere thanks for your time**
*   **In appreciation of your time**

### Pro-Tip from a Principal Investigator:
Avoid overly vague subject lines like just "Thank you" or "Following up." Busy clinicians and scientists appreciate knowing exactly what the email is about before they click. Adding the **date** or **topic** (e.g., *"Thank you for your time / Oncology Panel follow-up"*) is always appreciated.
