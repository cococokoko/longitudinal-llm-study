### Project Title: *The Lotus Leaf Mimic: Developing Low-Cost, Bio-Based Hydrophobic Coatings for Mekong Delta Flood Protection*

#### 1. The Real-World Vietnam Context (The "Why")
The Mekong Delta and coastal cities like Ho Chi Minh City and Da Nang face severe, worsening seasonal flooding due to rising sea levels and urbanization. Traditional building materials (like concrete, brick, and local bamboo/wood) absorb water, leading to structural degradation, mold, and rot. Commercial waterproof sealants are chemical-heavy, expensive, and inaccessible to low-income rural families.

#### 2. The Scientific Concept (The "How")
This project utilizes **biomimicry**—specifically, the **"Lotus Effect"** (superhydrophobicity). The lotus flower (*Nelumbo nucifera*), deeply symbolic in Vietnam, has leaves that naturally repel water due to microscopic wax nanostructures. 

Your goal is to extract natural waxes from local plant waste (like lotus leaves, banana leaves, or rice husks) and synthesize a cheap, non-toxic, hydrophobic (water-repelling) spray coating to protect local building materials.

#### 3. Materials & Affordability (Estimated Cost: <$20 USD)
*   **Source Material:** Discarded lotus leaves or banana leaves (easily sourced for free/cheap from local markets or ponds in Vietnam).
*   **Solvent:** Isopropyl alcohol (rubbing alcohol) or ethanol (cheaply available at pharmacies).
*   **Substrates to Test:** Local building materials—bamboo, cheap brick, concrete blocks, and compressed earth blocks.
*   **Testing Equipment:** A smartphone camera (for contact angle measurement), a dropper, and a homemade scale.

---

### Step-by-Step Experimental Design

#### Phase A: Extraction of the Bio-Wax
1.  **Collect and Dry:** Collect discarded lotus or banana leaves. Wash and dry them thoroughly.
2.  **Solvent Extraction:** Steep the leaves in isopropyl alcohol for 24–48 hours to dissolve the natural epicuticular waxes. 
3.  **Concentration:** Filter out the leaves. Let the alcohol partially evaporate in a well-ventilated area until you are left with a concentrated, waxy liquid solution.

#### Phase B: Application and Testing
1.  **The Control vs. Variable:** Prepare samples of bamboo, brick, and wood. Leave half untreated (control) and coat the other half with your bio-wax solution (variable).
2.  **Water Contact Angle Measurement (The "Wow" Science):** 
    *   Place a drop of water on the treated and untreated surfaces.
    *   Take a close-up, level photo using a smartphone.
    *   Use free, open-source software (like *ImageJ* or mobile contact-angle apps) to measure the angle of the water droplet. A contact angle $>90^\circ$ is hydrophobic; $>150^\circ$ is superhydrophobic (like a lotus leaf).
3.  **Water Absorption Test:** Weigh the materials dry. Submerge them in water for 24 hours. Weigh them again. Calculate the percentage of water absorbed. (The treated bamboo/brick should absorb significantly less water).
4.  **Durability Test (Simulating Vietnam's Monsoon):** Expose the treated materials to simulated heavy rain (using a showerhead or hose) and UV light (sunlight) for several days, then re-test the hydrophobicity to see how long the coating lasts.

### Why This Project Will Win Awards

As a judge, here is what I would look for, and why this project hits every mark:

*   **High Intellectual Merit:** It touches on chemistry (extraction, solvents), physics (surface tension, contact angles), and materials science (porosity, degradation).
*   **Social Impact:** It addresses a critical issue in Vietnam (flooding, housing durability for low-income families in the Mekong Delta) using a culturally significant plant (Lotus).
*   **Frugal Innovation:** It takes agricultural waste (leaves) and turns it into a high-value protective coating.
*   **Quantitative Data:** Judges hate purely descriptive projects. This project allows you to generate beautiful graphs showing *Contact Angle vs. Extraction Time* or *Water Absorption % over Time*.

### Professor's Tip for the Presentation:
When presenting, do not just show graphs. Bring a live demonstration. Have a piece of treated bamboo and untreated bamboo on your table. Use a dropper to show the judges how water instantly beads up and rolls off your treated bamboo, while soaking into the untreated one. That visual "aha!" moment is what secures first place.
