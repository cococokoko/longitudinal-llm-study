── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support or permit the death penalty typically rely on several major arguments. The strength of these arguments depends on one's ethical, legal, and empirical assumptions.

Common arguments in favor include:

- Retributive justice
  - Some believe that certain crimes, especially premeditated murder or mass murder, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - This view holds that justice requires punishment to match the gravity of the offense, independent of deterrence or rehabilitation.

- Moral accountability
  - Supporters may argue that individuals who knowingly commit extreme crimes can forfeit their right to life through their actions.
  - In this framework, capital punishment recognizes the offender's agency and responsibility.

- Deterrence
  - Some argue that the possibility of execution discourages potential offenders from committing the most serious crimes.
  - Although empirical evidence on deterrence is heavily debated, deterrence remains a central rationale for many supporters.

- Incapacitation and public safety
  - Execution permanently prevents the offender from committing future crimes.
  - Advocates sometimes point to cases involving prison violence, escapes, or the possibility that a convicted murderer could kill again.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Some believe capital punishment provides a sense of justice or closure to victims' relatives.
  - The argument is not necessarily that it heals grief, but that it demonstrates society's recognition of the severity of the harm done.

- Social condemnation
  - The death penalty can be viewed as society's strongest expression of moral condemnation.
  - Supporters may argue that abolishing it risks diminishing the seriousness with which society treats certain atrocities.

- Democratic legitimacy
  - In some jurisdictions, supporters argue that if citizens, through democratic processes, choose to retain capital punishment, the law reflects the community's moral judgment.
  - This argument focuses on self-governance rather than the morality of the punishment itself.

- Cost arguments (in theory)
  - Some proponents contend that lifelong incarceration is expensive and that execution could reduce long-term costs.
  - In practice, however, many studies have found that capital cases often become more expensive than life imprisonment because of extensive legal proceedings and appeals.

It is worth noting that each of these arguments has substantial counterarguments. For example, critics point to the risk of executing innocent people, concerns about unequal application, questions about deterrence, and ethical objections to state-sanctioned killing. The debate persists because reasonable people disagree about justice, punishment, and the role of the state.
