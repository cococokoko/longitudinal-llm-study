Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a single parent, I ask myself this question almost every single day—usually right around the time my kid asks me something like, *"Why is the sky blue, how do airplanes stay up, and what are we having for dinner?"* all in the span of thirty seconds while I’m trying to send a work email. 

The short answer is: **Yes, it is absolutely, scientifically, and practically impossible to know everything.** 

But honestly? That is actually a wonderful thing. Here is how I look at it, both from a logical standpoint and a "surviving daily life" standpoint:

### 1. The "Cup of Water vs. the Ocean" Problem
Even if you had a brain that worked like a supercomputer, the sheer volume of information in the universe is infinite. Every second, new data is being created. New stars are forming, new scientific discoveries are being made, and somewhere, a toddler is inventing a new word. Our brains are like a very nice, sturdy bucket, but the universe is an infinite ocean. You can't fit the ocean into a bucket. 

### 2. The "Jack of All Trades" Reality
In my daily life, I have to know a little bit of everything. I have to be a amateur chef, a basic medic (for scraped knees), a negotiator (for bedtime), a budget analyst, and a professional in my actual job. But because I have to split my focus, I can't be a world-class expert in *all* of those things. If I spent all my time mastering quantum physics, I’d probably burn the mac and cheese. We have to choose what to focus on.

### 3. The Beauty of "I Don't Know"
There is a huge relief in admitting we can't know everything. 
* **It keeps us curious:** If we knew everything, life would be incredibly boring. There would be nothing to discover, no mysteries to solve, and no surprises.
* **It teaches humility:** One of the best things I can say to my child is, *"I don't know the answer to that, but let's look it up together."* It shows them that learning is a lifelong journey, not a test you finish and get a grade on.
* **It connects us:** Because no single person can know everything, we *need* each other. I rely on doctors to know medicine, mechanics to know cars, and teachers to know how to explain long division. Our collective knowledge is what makes humanity work.

So, don't stress about not knowing it all. Nobody does—not even the people who look like they have it completely together. We are all just figuring it out as we go, one question at a time!
