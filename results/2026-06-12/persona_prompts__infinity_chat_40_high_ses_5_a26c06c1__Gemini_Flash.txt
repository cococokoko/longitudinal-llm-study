── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
First of all, congratulations on the upcoming marriage and the new house. This is an exciting milestone, but it is also one of the most common friction points for couples. 

As an executive, I look at complex situations like this through three lenses: **Risk Mitigation, Equity (Fairness), and Strategic Alignment (your shared future).** 

Here is a framework to help you and your girlfriend find the "best" solution, broken down by how you should think about it, the legal realities, and the practical options.

---

### Phase 1: The Core Principle (The "Why")
Before looking at numbers, you must agree on a philosophy. 
* **Is this *her* house, or is this *our* house?** 
* If she is buying it solely in her name, with her down payment, before you are married, it is legally **her asset**. 
* If you pay for a house that you do not own, you are essentially giving her a tax-free gift or paying "rent" with no equity. If the relationship ends, you lose that capital. 
* Conversely, if you pay nothing but live there for free, she is subsidizing your lifestyle while taking on 100% of the financial risk.

**The Golden Rule:** Your financial contribution should match your legal ownership and your long-term security.

---

### Phase 2: The 4 Best Solutions

Here are the four most viable paths forward, ranging from conservative to fully integrated.

#### Option 1: The "Market Rent" Model (Safest/Most Common)
* **How it works:** She buys the house on her own (down payment and mortgage in her name). You move in and pay her a monthly "rent" or contribution to household expenses (utilities, groceries, and a portion of the mortgage).
* **What is fair:** Your "rent" should be below market rate, and it should **never** go toward major capital improvements (like a new roof or renovation)—she pays for those as the sole owner. 
* **Pros:** Clean, simple, protects her asset and protects you from over-investing in something you don't own.
* **Cons:** You aren't building equity yet.

#### Option 2: The "Joint Purchase" Model (Delayed or Immediate)
* **How it works:** You put your name on the deed and the mortgage. 
    * *Option A (Immediate):* You buy it together now. You both contribute to the down payment and are co-owners.
    * *Option B (Post-Marriage):* She buys it now. After you marry, you "buy in." You pay her half of the equity she has put in, and refinance the mortgage to put both of your names on the deed.
* **Pros:** True partnership. You both build equity.
* **Cons:** Doing this *before* marriage is legally risky. If you break up, dividing a co-owned house without a divorce court is a legal nightmare.

#### Option 3: The "Proportional Expense" Model
* **How it works:** You calculate your contributions based on income. If you make $150k and she makes $100k, you pay 60% of the joint living expenses (utilities, food, lifestyle), while she pays the actual mortgage (building her equity). 
* **Pros:** Feels fair day-to-day; accounts for income disparity.
* **Cons:** Requires detailed budgeting.

---

### Phase 3: The Executive Action Plan

To resolve this professionally and romantically, I recommend taking these steps:

1. **Have the "Pre-Nup" Conversation Now:** Since you plan to marry in a year, you need to talk about how pre-marital assets will be treated. If she buys this house now, is it off-limits in a future divorce, or does it become joint property upon marriage? 
2. **Consult a Real Estate Attorney:** Do not rely on handshake agreements. If you decide to contribute to the down payment or mortgage *before* marriage, have an attorney draft a **Cohabitation Agreement**. This document outlines who gets what if the relationship ends before the wedding.
3. **Run the Numbers:** Sit down with a spreadsheet. 
    * What is the monthly mortgage?
    * What would it cost you to rent a similar place alone?
    * What is a fair "roommate rate" that allows her to cover her mortgage while allowing you to save money for your future wedding or joint investments?

### The Bottom Line
It is **not fair** for you to pay for the mortgage or down payment of a house you do not legally own. 

The best solution for a couple marrying in a year is **Option 1 (The Rent Model)** for the next 12 months. Pay her a fair, agreed-upon monthly amount to cover living expenses, and keep your savings in a high-yield account. Once you are married, merge your finances and legally add your name to the deed, or use your saved cash to pay down the principal together.
