── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth thinking through carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## Actually, the premise deserves scrutiny

**Arguments people make against mandating equal pay:**
- Enforcement is administratively complex
- "Equal work" is genuinely difficult to define objectively
- May create unintended consequences (employers avoiding hiring certain groups)
- Market competition theoretically should eliminate pay discrimination over time
- Wage differences may reflect legitimate factors (experience, negotiation, hours)

## But strong counterarguments exist:

**The market-corrects-itself argument has weaknesses:**
- Labor markets have significant information asymmetries
- Discrimination can persist for decades without correction
- Power imbalances affect negotiation outcomes
- Historical exclusion creates compounding disadvantages

**The economic case FOR equal pay:**
- Reduces misallocation of human capital
- Increases workforce participation
- Reduces poverty
- Evidence suggests discrimination imposes real economic costs

## Honest assessment

The question frames equal pay opposition as obviously correct, but **economists actually disagree significantly** on this. Many mainstream economists support equal pay protections while debating implementation details.

The strongest legitimate concerns are about **measurement and enforcement complexity**, not whether equal pay is a worthwhile goal.
