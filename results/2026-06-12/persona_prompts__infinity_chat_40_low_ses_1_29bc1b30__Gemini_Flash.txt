── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common and stressful situation, especially when you are working a demanding job with an unpredictable schedule and fluctuating income. 

Because you are not married yet, and because you have different financial situations, the "best" solution is one that **protects both of you legally** and **feels fair to both of you emotionally.**

Here is a breakdown of how to look at this, the options available to you, and how to protect yourself.

---

### 1. The Golden Rule: Do not pay the mortgage if your name is not on the deed.
If your girlfriend is buying the house in her name only, **you should not be paying the mortgage.** 

If you pay the mortgage and you two break up before getting married, she keeps the house and all the equity, and you get nothing back. Legally, you would have just been paying her mortgage for her. 

### 2. The "Fair" Solutions (Before Marriage)
Since you are moving in before you are married, here are the three best ways to handle expenses:

#### Option A: The "Market Rent" Model (Highly Recommended)
Instead of paying the mortgage, you pay her a set amount of "rent" each month. 
* **How it works:** You agree on a flat, affordable monthly fee that is lower than or equal to what you would pay to rent a room elsewhere. 
* **Why it’s fair:** She gets help with her living costs, and you get a place to live. If you break up, you were just a tenant—you don't owe her anything, and she doesn't owe you any equity.
* **Your Fast-Food Schedule Factor:** Since your hours might vary, agree on a flat rate that you can afford even on a "low-hour" week, or agree that you pay a set percentage of your income (e.g., 25% of whatever you made that month).

#### Option B: The "Split the Bills" Model
You do not pay for the house itself, but you cover other living expenses.
* **How it works:** She pays 100% of the mortgage, property taxes, and home insurance (the "house" costs). You pay for the utilities (internet, electricity, water) and buy the groceries. 
* **Why it’s fair:** You are contributing to the household daily, but you are not putting money into an asset you don't own. 

#### Option C: Sweat Equity (With Caution)
Since you work a demanding job, your time is valuable. 
* **How it works:** You pay a very low rent, but you agree to do the heavy lifting around the house (mowing the lawn, painting, minor repairs, deep cleaning). 
* **Why it’s fair:** It saves her money on hiring handymen, and respects your hard work, while keeping your financial output low.

---

### 3. What happens when you get married?
Once you get married, the conversation changes. At that point, you can discuss:
* **Refinancing:** Adding your name to the mortgage and deed.
* **Joint Finances:** Putting your incomes into one pot and paying the mortgage together. 

*Note: In many states/countries, if you marry and then help pay the mortgage, you may become entitled to a portion of the home's increase in value during the marriage, but this varies wildly by location. Talk to a local professional before assuming this.*

---

### How to have the conversation with her:
Since you plan to marry her, you need to be able to talk about money openly. Here is a script you can use to start the conversation:

> *"I am so excited for you to buy this house, and I’m so excited for our future together. Since we aren't married yet and my work hours can be unpredictable, I want to make sure we handle the finances in a way that is fair to both of us. Since the house will be in your name, I don't think it makes sense for me to pay the mortgage directly. Instead, how would you feel if I paid a set amount of 'rent' or took over the utility bills and groceries?"*

### Summary Checklist for You:
1. **Do not** put money toward the down payment.
2. **Do not** pay the mortgage directly unless your name is on the deed.
3. **Do** pay a fair, agreed-upon amount for "rent" or utilities that fits your fast-food income.
4. **Do** get any agreement in writing (even a simple text message or email) so there are no misunderstandings later.
