Congratulations on the new job! Navigating the probation period can be a busy time, so let’s keep this explanation highly practical, intuitive, and easy to digest.

To understand a **partial derivative**, think of it as measuring how a change in **just one specific factor** affects your overall result, while keeping all other factors completely constant.

Here is a real-world numerical example using a scenario you might relate to right now: **calculating your monthly take-home pay.**

---

### The Scenario: Your Take-Home Pay

Let’s say your monthly take-home pay ($P$) depends on two variables:
1. $H$: The number of **hours** you work per month.
2. $R$: Your hourly **rate** of pay.

To make it realistic, let's assume you also pay a flat tax/benefit deduction of $500. 

The formula for your take-home pay is:
$$P(H, R) = (H \times R) - 500$$

Currently, your baseline situation is:
*   You work **$H = 160$ hours** per month.
*   Your rate is **$R = \$30$ per hour**.

Your current take-home pay is:
$$P = (160 \times 30) - 500 = \$4,300$$

---

### 1. The Partial Derivative with respect to Hours ($\frac{\partial P}{\partial H}$)

This asks: **"If my hourly rate stays exactly the same, how much does my pay change for every additional hour I work?"**

To find this, we treat the rate ($R$) as a constant ($30$) and the deduction ($500$) as a constant. 

*   **Mathematical Calculation:**
    If we take the derivative of $P = (H \times R) - 500$ with respect to $H$ (treating $R$ as a constant number):
    $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial H} = R$$
    Since $R = 30$, then:
    $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial H} = 30$$

*   **Numerical Proof:**
    Let’s increase your hours by just **1 hour** (from 160 to 161), keeping your rate strictly at \$30.
    $$New\ Pay = (161 \times 30) - 500 = \$4,330$$
    The change in pay is:
    $$\$4,330 - \$4,300 = \$30$$
    
    **The partial derivative is $30$.** It tells you that at this moment, for every 1-unit increase in hours, your pay increases by \$30.

---

### 2. The Partial Derivative with respect to Rate ($\frac{\partial P}{\partial R}$)

Now, let's look at the other side. Once you pass your probation, you might get a raise. 

This asks: **"If my hours stay exactly the same (160 hours), how much does my pay change for every \$1 increase in my hourly rate?"**

To find this, we treat the hours ($H$) as a constant ($160$).

*   **Mathematical Calculation:**
    If we take the derivative of $P = (H \times R) - 500$ with respect to $R$ (treating $H$ as a constant number):
    $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = H$$
    Since $H = 160$, then:
    $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = 160$$

*   **Numerical Proof:**
    Let’s increase your hourly rate by **\$1** (from \$30 to \$31), keeping your hours strictly at 160.
    $$New\ Pay = (160 \times 31) - 500 = \$4,460$$
    The change in pay is:
    $$\$4,460 - \$4,300 = \$160$$

    **The partial derivative is $160$.** It tells you that at this moment, a \$1 increase in your hourly rate increases your monthly pay by \$160.

---

### Summary

In calculus, we write these "rates of change" using the curly $\partial$ symbol:

*   $\frac{\partial P}{\partial H} = 30$ (How sensitive your pay is to **hours**).
*   $\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = 160$ (How sensitive your pay is to your **rate**).

By isolating one variable at a time, partial derivatives allow you to see exactly which lever has the biggest impact on your final result. (In this case, getting a raise has a much larger impact on your monthly income than working one extra hour!)
