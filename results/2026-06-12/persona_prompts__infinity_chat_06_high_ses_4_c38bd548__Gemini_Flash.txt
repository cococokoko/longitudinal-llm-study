As a physician-scientist, I often approach complex, abstract questions by looking for the underlying "physiology" of the problem. 

When you ask, *"How do I understand what I want?"* you are facing a classic system failure of the human brain: **a conflict between different processing networks.** 

In medicine, if a patient has vague, systemic symptoms, we don't guess; we run a diagnostic workup. To understand what you truly want, you need to run a diagnostic workup on your own mind. Here is a framework to help you dissect your desires, separating the "noise" from the "signal."

---

### Phase 1: The Differential Diagnosis (Why is it hard to know?)
Before you can find the answer, you must understand why it is currently hidden. In psychology and neuroscience, there are three main "pathologies" of desire:

1.  **Mimetic Desire (The "Infection"):** Coined by philosopher René Girard, this is the phenomenon where we want things simply because we see others wanting them. You think you want a certain career, lifestyle, or status, but you have actually just "caught" that desire from your environment.
2.  **The Amygdala Hijack (The "Inflammation"):** When you are chronically stressed, burnt out, or anxious, your brain operates in survival mode. In survival mode, you cannot access deep desires; your brain only wants *relief* (sleep, escape, distraction). You might mistake the desire for "numbness" as a desire for "nothing."
3.  **The "Should" Suppression (The "Autoimmune Response"):** Your prefrontal cortex is highly trained in societal rules. Often, your true desires are actively suppressed by your own brain because they violate what you think you *should* want.

---

### Phase 2: The Diagnostic Tests
To bypass these cognitive biases, you need to gather objective data on yourself. Try these three diagnostic exercises:

#### 1. The "Jealousy Audit" (Tracking the Signal)
We are taught that jealousy is a negative emotion, but in diagnostics, it is a highly specific biomarker. 
*   **The Test:** For the next two weeks, pay close attention to when you feel a pang of envy, jealousy, or resentment. 
*   **The Analysis:** Do not judge the feeling. Write down exactly *who* you were jealous of and *why*. Are you jealous of a colleague’s promotion, or are you actually jealous of their free time? Are you jealous of someone’s wealth, or their creative freedom? Jealousy is a map of your unfulfilled desires.

#### 2. The "Via Negativa" (Elimination Diet)
Sometimes it is biologically easier to identify what is toxic than what is nourishing. 
*   **The Test:** Instead of asking "What do I want?", ask: **"What do I absolutely know I *do not* want anymore?"** 
*   **The Analysis:** Write down the activities, people, environments, and obligations that drain your energy. By systematically eliminating or minimizing these (the *via negativa*), you clear the cognitive space required for your true desires to surface.

#### 3. The "Somatic Marker" Test (Listening to the Body)
As a physician, I know the body often registers truth before the conscious mind does. This is based on neuroscientist Antonio Damasio’s "Somatic Marker Hypothesis."
*   **The Test:** Sit quietly and visualize a potential path (e.g., taking a new job, moving to a new city, staying exactly where you are). 
*   **The Analysis:** Don't think about the logistics. Pay attention to your physiology. Does your chest tighten? Do your shoulders drop? Do you feel a sense of expansion or constriction? Your autonomic nervous system is constantly sending signals about what you actually want; you just have to learn to read the instrument panel.

---

### Phase 3: The Treatment Plan (Action over Analysis)
In science, we do not formulate a theory and assume it is correct; we run an experiment. 

The biggest mistake people make when trying to figure out what they want is trying to **think** their way to the answer. You cannot think your way into knowing what you want; you must **act** your way there.

*   **Formulate a Hypothesis:** "I think I want to write a book," or "I think I want to transition into a leadership role."
*   **Run a Low-Risk Experiment:** Do not quit your job or make a massive life change. Run a "micro-trial." If you think you want to be a writer, write for 30 minutes every morning for two weeks. If you think you want to lead, volunteer to manage a small project.
*   **Analyze the Data:** Did the reality of the experiment match the fantasy? Did it energize you, or did it drain you? 

### Summary
Understanding what you want is not an act of creation; it is an act of **excavation**. 

Strip away the mimetic desires of others, quiet the survival-mode noise of your brain, pay attention to your body's somatic signals, and run small, real-world experiments. The data you gather will point the way.
