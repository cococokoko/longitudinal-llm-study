What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, it would attract capital until the opportunity was largely competed away. Markets change over time, and strategies that work well in one environment can perform poorly in another.

That said, some of the most successful categories of day trading strategies historically include:

- Trend-following / momentum
  - Buy assets showing strong upward momentum and sell assets showing weakness.
  - Often uses breakouts, moving averages, or volume confirmation.
  - Many professional traders and quantitative funds have built successful systems around momentum.

- Mean reversion
  - Assumes short-term price moves often overshoot and then revert toward an average.
  - Common in stocks, ETFs, and market-making strategies.
  - Requires strict risk controls because trends can persist longer than expected.

- Opening range breakout (ORB)
  - Uses the high and low established shortly after the market opens.
  - Traders enter when price breaks out of that range with volume.
  - Popular because much of the day's volatility often occurs near the open.

- Market making
  - Profits from the bid-ask spread by continuously buying and selling.
  - Historically one of the most reliable trading businesses, but now dominated by firms with sophisticated technology and low-latency infrastructure.

- Statistical arbitrage
  - Uses mathematical models to identify temporary pricing inefficiencies.
  - Includes pairs trading and other quantitative approaches.
  - Has been highly profitable for some hedge funds but is difficult for individual traders to execute competitively.

For an individual retail trader, a simple momentum strategy with disciplined risk management is often more realistic than complex arbitrage methods. For example:
- Trade only highly liquid stocks or futures.
- Look for unusually high volume.
- Enter on a confirmed breakout.
- Risk a small fixed percentage of capital per trade (many traders use 0.25%–1%).
- Use predefined stop losses.
- Focus on maintaining a favorable reward-to-risk ratio.

One important reality: most academic studies and broker disclosures indicate that a majority of retail day traders lose money over time. The biggest determinants of long-term results are usually:
- Risk management
- Position sizing
- Transaction costs
- Emotional discipline
- Consistent execution

If your goal is maximizing expected wealth rather than specifically day trading, broad long-term investing has historically outperformed the vast majority of active traders.
