── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job while still on probation can make it especially hard to know what you want, because you're adapting to a new environment and may feel pressure to prove yourself. Sometimes the question isn't "What do I want?" but "How can I tell what's genuinely important to me versus what I'm reacting to right now?"

A few approaches can help:

- Separate short-term feelings from longer-term preferences.
  - Ask yourself: "What specifically am I enjoying or disliking?"
  - Is it the work itself, the people, the pace, the uncertainty, the learning curve, or something else?
  - New jobs often feel uncomfortable simply because they're new.

- Pay attention to your energy.
  - Over a few weeks, note which tasks leave you feeling engaged, curious, or satisfied.
  - Also note which tasks consistently drain you.
  - Patterns are often more informative than strong feelings on a single day.

- Think in terms of values rather than decisions.
  - What matters most to you right now?
    - Learning?
    - Stability?
    - Income?
    - Creativity?
    - Autonomy?
    - Recognition?
    - Work-life balance?
  - Ranking these can clarify what you want from a job and from your life more broadly.

- Imagine different futures.
  - If nothing changed and you stayed in this role for a year, how would you feel?
  - If you became very successful in this role, would that be appealing?
  - If another opportunity appeared tomorrow, what would make you seriously consider it?

- Distinguish fear from desire.
  - Sometimes "I want to leave" actually means "I'm afraid I'm not performing well."
  - Sometimes "I want to stay" means "I'm afraid of change."
  - Asking "What would I choose if I weren't worried about probation?" can be revealing.

- Give yourself permission not to know yet.
  - Understanding what you want often emerges through experience rather than introspection alone.
  - If you've only recently started, collecting information about yourself and the role may be more useful than trying to reach a final conclusion immediately.

You might also try answering these questions:
- What made you accept this job?
- What has surprised you since starting?
- What are you currently struggling with most?
- When you ask "How do I understand what I want?", are you thinking about this job specifically, or about your life and career more generally?
