── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you occupy one of the most intellectually demanding, highly pressured, and structurally complex roles in academia and medicine. You are constantly straddling two distinct worlds: the immediate, high-stakes, human-centered world of clinical medicine, and the slow, uncertain, highly competitive world of scientific discovery. 

When you ask, *"How do I understand what I want?"* it is often because the external demands of these two worlds (grants, clinic templates, publications, tenure, administrative duties) have drowned out your internal compass. 

To find clarity, you must approach this question with the same rigor you apply to your research, but with a different set of tools. Here is a framework designed specifically for a physician-scientist to systematically uncover what they actually want.

---

### Phase 1: The Diagnostic (Data Collection)
Before you can make a hypothesis about what you want, you need to collect objective data on your current state. For two weeks, track your energy and emotions, not just your time.

*   **The Energy Audit:** Create a simple spreadsheet. At the end of each day, list your major activities and rate them on a scale of -5 (drained/dreaded) to +5 (energized/excited). 
    *   *Clinical:* Inpatient rounds, outpatient clinic, charting, patient interactions.
    *   *Research:* Writing grants, analyzing data, mentoring trainees, lab meetings, writing papers.
    *   *Administrative:* Committee meetings, compliance, emails.
*   **Identify the "Flow" States:** Look for moments of "flow"—where you lost track of time because you were so deeply engaged. Was it while explaining a complex diagnosis to a patient? Was it while looking at a raw data set with a postdoc? Was it while writing the discussion section of a paper?
*   **The "Resentment" Marker:** Pay close attention to what makes you resentful. Resentment is a powerful diagnostic tool. If you resent clinic because it takes you away from the lab, or if you resent grant writing because it takes you away from patients, your subconscious is telling you where your true affinity lies.

### Phase 2: Deconstruct the "Physician-Scientist" Identity
The "triple threat" model (clinician, researcher, educator/leader) is a myth that leads to burnout. You need to deconstruct which parts of this identity are *yours* and which were *implanted* by your mentors, institution, or ego.

Ask yourself these hard questions:
*   **The Funding Question:** If you had a permanent, unrestricted $10 million endowment for your lab, would you still see patients? If yes, how many half-days a week? (If the answer is zero, you want to be a full-time scientist. If the answer is "yes, but only 1 day," you want clinical work as a source of inspiration, not a primary duty).
*   **The Clinical Question:** If you were told you could never run another gel, write another grant, or manage a lab, but could be the absolute best clinician in your department with a lightened administrative load, would you feel relieved or devastated?
*   **The Legacy Question:** When you retire, what do you want to be remembered for? 
    *   *Option A:* "They discovered the mechanism of [X] and changed how we understand the disease."
    *   *Option B:* "They were an incredible doctor who saved my life and trained the next generation."
    *   *Option C:* "They built a world-class department and enabled dozens of others to succeed."

### Phase 3: Analyze the "Friction Points"
Often, we think we don't know what we want, but we actually do—we are just afraid of the friction required to get it. 

*   **Is it the *work* or the *system*?** Do you actually dislike research, or do you just hate the endless cycle of NIH grant rejections and institutional bureaucracy? Do you dislike clinical medicine, or do you hate the Electronic Health Record (EHR) and RVU targets?
*   **The "Who" vs. "What" Friction:** As a lab head, you are now a CEO, HR manager, and fundraiser. Do you actually want to *do* science, or do you want to *manage* science? If you miss the bench/data analysis and hate managing people, you may want a smaller, leaner lab, or a transition to a role (like industry or a research institute) where you can be a senior individual contributor.

### Phase 4: Formulate Your Hypotheses
Based on your data, write down 3 distinct "Future Scenarios" for yourself. Do not worry about feasibility yet.

*   **Scenario A (The Status Quo Optimized):** You keep your current split (e.g., 80/20 or 60/40), but you ruthlessly outsource or eliminate the tasks you hate. What does that look like?
*   **Scenario B (The Pivot):** You shift heavily in one direction. You become a 90% clinician who does collaborative clinical trials, OR you close your wet lab and transition to 90% computational research with minimal clinic.
*   **Scenario C (The Alternative):** You leave academia. You move to biotech/pharma, venture capital, or a foundation where you can apply your dual background without the academic grind.

### Phase 5: Run "Micro-Experiments"
Do not make a massive career pivot based on a feeling. Run low-risk experiments to test your hypotheses.

*   **To test wanting more research time:** Block off one full "sacred" day a week with zero clinical or administrative interruptions. See if your productivity and happiness spike, or if you struggle to fill the time.
*   **To test wanting to leave academia:** Reach out to three former physician-scientists now in industry or biotech. Ask them for a 15-minute informational interview. Ask them: *"What do you miss? What do you not miss?"*
*   **To test wanting less clinical time:** Talk to your division chief about a temporary clinical reduction (even 10%) for the next 6 months to focus on a specific grant, and see how your mental state shifts.

### A Final Thought for the Physician-Scientist
You have spent your entire life clearing hurdles that others set for you: getting into medical school, matching into residency/fellowship, securing your first K-award, getting your first R01. You are highly conditioned to want **achievement** rather than **fulfillment**.

To understand what you want, you must stop asking, *"What is the next milestone I should achieve?"* and start asking, *"What kind of Tuesday do I actually want to live, week after week, for the next ten years?"*
