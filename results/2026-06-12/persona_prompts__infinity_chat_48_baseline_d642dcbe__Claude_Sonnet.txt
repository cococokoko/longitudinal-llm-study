The Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) witnessed remarkable economic development that transformed China into one of the ancient world's most prosperous civilizations. The early Han emperors adopted a policy of light taxation and minimal government interference, allowing the agricultural sector to recover from the devastation of the preceding Qin Dynasty and the civil wars that followed it. As the population grew and agricultural output increased, the government gradually introduced more active economic policies, including state control over key industries such as salt and iron production, which generated significant revenue for the imperial treasury. The Silk Road, established during the reign of Emperor Wu (141–87 BCE), opened lucrative trade routes connecting China to Central Asia, the Middle East, and eventually Rome, facilitating the exchange of silk, ceramics, and other Chinese goods for gold, glass, and exotic products from the West. Domestically, improvements in technology, including the invention of the iron plow and the collar harness for horses, greatly enhanced agricultural productivity, while advances in textile production and metallurgy strengthened manufacturing. A standardized currency system, centered on the bronze wuzhu coin, helped unify markets across the vast empire and encouraged commercial activity. Together, these developments made the Han Dynasty a period of extraordinary economic vitality that laid the foundation for China's enduring prosperity in subsequent centuries.
