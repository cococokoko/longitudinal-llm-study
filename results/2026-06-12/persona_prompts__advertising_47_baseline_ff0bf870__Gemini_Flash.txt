── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon is a monumental achievement that requires physical endurance, mental toughness, and smart preparation. To help you succeed, here is a comprehensive guide broken down into four phases: **Training, The Week Before, Race Day, and Post-Race.**

---

### Phase 1: Training (Months 1–4)
The foundation of your marathon is built during your training cycle. 

*   **Respect the 10% Rule:** Never increase your weekly mileage or the length of your weekly long run by more than 10% from the previous week. This is the golden rule for preventing overuse injuries (like shin splints or runner's knee).
*   **Prioritize the "Long Run":** Your weekly long run is the most important workout. It teaches your body to burn fat for fuel, builds mental stamina, and strengthens your muscles and joints. You don’t need to run 26.2 miles in training; peaking at one or two **18 to 20-mile runs** is sufficient.
*   **Practice Your Fueling:** Use your long runs as dress rehearsals. Experiment with energy gels, chews, or sports drinks to see what your stomach tolerates. *Rule of thumb:* Consume 30–60 grams of carbohydrates per hour for runs lasting longer than 75 minutes.
*   **Don't Skip Strength Training:** Lift weights or do bodyweight exercises (squats, lunges, planks) 1–2 times a week. Strong glutes, hips, and core muscles keep your running form from collapsing when you get tired.
*   **Respect the Taper:** Three weeks before the race, reduce your mileage by 20–30%, then 50%, then 75% in the final week. This allows your muscles to fully repair and store maximum glycogen. You will feel twitchy and anxious—this is normal and means the taper is working!

---

### Phase 2: The Week Before the Race
This week is all about resting, hydrating, and preparing your logistics.

*   **Carbo-Loading (Done Right):** You do not need to eat a mountain of pasta the night before the race (this will just make you feel bloated and sluggish). Instead, slightly increase your carbohydrate intake **2 to 3 days before** the race. Focus on easy-to-digest carbs like rice, oatmeal, potatoes, and bananas.
*   **Hydrate Early:** Hydration is a multi-day process. Drink plenty of water and electrolytes in the 3 days leading up to the race. If your urine is pale yellow, you are good to go.
*   **Nothing New on Race Day:** This is the ultimate runner's commandment. Do not wear new shoes, new socks, or a new shirt. Do not eat a new breakfast or use a gel you haven't tested in training. 
*   **Cut Your Toenails:** Clip them short a few days before the race to prevent them from bruising or falling off from constant friction against the front of your shoe.
*   **Study the Course Map:** Know where the hills are, where the water stations are located, and where your family/friends plan to cheer for you.

---

### Phase 3: Race Day
The big day is here. Stay calm and execute your plan.

*   **Arrive Early:** Aim to get to the start village 60 to 90 minutes before your wave starts. This gives you time for long bathroom lines, gear drop-off, and a light warm-up.
*   **Prevent Chafing:** Apply Vaseline, Body Glide, or Squirrel's Nut Butter to areas prone to friction: thighs, armpits, sports bra lines, and nipples (for men, band-aids over the nipples are a lifesaver).
*   **Start Slow (The "Negative Split" Goal):** The energy at the start line is electric, and adrenaline will make you want to sprint. **Don't.** Run the first 3–5 miles about 10–15 seconds *slower* than your target marathon pace. Every second you save by going too fast in the first half will cost you minutes in the second half.
*   **Break the Race into Segments:** Thinking about 26.2 miles can be overwhelming. Break it down:
    *   *Miles 1–10:* A controlled, easy cruise.
    *   *Miles 10–20:* The "working" phase. Stay locked into your rhythm.
    *   *Miles 20–26.2:* The mental game. This is where the real race begins.
*   **Walk the Water Stations:** You don't have to run through water stations. Walking for 10–15 seconds allows you to actually drink the water/sports drink rather than splashing it on your face, and gives your heart rate a tiny break.
*   **Embrace the "Wall":** Around mile 20, your body’s glycogen stores will deplete, and your brain will tell you to stop. Expect this. When it happens, dedicate each of the remaining miles to someone you love, focus on your breathing, and repeat a mantra (e.g., *"I am strong, I am trained"*).

---

### Phase 4: Post-Race Recovery
You crossed the finish line! Now, take care of your body.

*   **Keep Walking:** Do not sit down immediately. Walk for at least 10–15 minutes to allow your heart rate to drop gradually and prevent your muscles from seizing up.
*   **Get Warm:** Your body temperature will drop rapidly once you stop running. Put on the foil space blanket provided by the race, or change into dry clothes immediately.
*   **Refuel Quickly:** Within 30 minutes of finishing, consume a mix of carbohydrates and protein (chocolate milk, a banana, or a recovery shake) to kickstart muscle repair.
*   **Avoid Hot Baths:** Do not take a hot bath on race day; it can increase inflammation. Opt for a cool shower or an ice bath if you can brave it. Save the hot tub/sauna for 48 hours later.
*   **Take a Week Off:** Give yourself at least 7 to 10 days of complete rest or very light walking before you attempt to run again. Your body has just gone through major physical trauma and needs time to heal.
