As a neurosurgeon, I witness daily how stress is not merely a psychological state, but a profound, systemic disruption of human biology. 

When a patient experiences chronic stress, the brain’s amygdala triggers a cascade, signaling the hypothalamus to activate the sympathetic nervous system. This releases a flood of hormones, primarily cortisol and adrenaline. In the short term, this "fight-or-flight" response is evolutionary and adaptive. However, sustained activation is highly destructive. 

Physically, chronic cortisol elevation leads to systemic inflammation, arterial constriction, and elevated blood pressure, significantly increasing the risk of stroke and cardiovascular disease. In my field, we also see how stress impairs the immune system, delaying post-operative wound healing and exacerbating neuro-inflammatory pathways. 

Mentally, the toll is equally severe. High levels of cortisol actively damage the hippocampus—the brain's center for learning and memory—and cause atrophy in the prefrontal cortex, which governs executive function, decision-making, and emotional regulation. This manifests clinically as cognitive fatigue, memory deficits, anxiety, and depression. 

Ultimately, stress remodels the brain's architecture and strains the body's vital systems. Managing it is not a lifestyle luxury; it is a clinical necessity for neurological and systemic health.
