Working shifts as a patient care aide at the hospital, I see firsthand how stress isn't just a feeling—it is a physical and mental state that takes a heavy toll on the entire system. 

Physically, stress triggers the body’s "fight or flight" response, releasing a flood of hormones like cortisol and adrenaline. In the short term, this spikes your heart rate and tightens your muscles—something I feel during a hectic shift when call lights are buzzing. Over time, however, chronic stress wreaks havoc. It weakens the immune system, making you more susceptible to illnesses, elevates blood pressure, and disrupts digestion. Many of my patients—and coworkers—struggle with chronic tension headaches, muscle pain, and profound fatigue because their bodies never get the chance to fully reset.

Mentally, the impact is just as severe. Stress clouds cognitive function, making it incredibly difficult to focus, make decisions, or retain information. It drains your emotional reserves, leading to irritability, anxiety, and a sense of overwhelm. Sleep is often the first casualty; a stressed mind refuses to quiet down at night, leading to insomnia. This creates a vicious cycle, as sleep deprivation only heightens anxiety and lowers your ability to cope the next day. 

Whether you are managing a busy hospital ward or navigating daily life, recognizing these signs is crucial. Stress isn't just in your head; it is a full-body experience that requires active rest and recovery to heal.
