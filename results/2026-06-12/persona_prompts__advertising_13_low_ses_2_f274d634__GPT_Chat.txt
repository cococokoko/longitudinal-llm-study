── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak comes down to a few key principles: choosing the right cut, controlling temperature, and timing the cook correctly.

1. Start with a good cut
- Ribeye: rich and flavorful due to higher fat content.
- New York strip: balanced flavor and texture.
- Filet mignon: very tender, milder flavor.
- Sirloin: leaner and often more budget-friendly.

2. Bring the steak closer to room temperature
- Take it out of the refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking if possible.
- Pat it dry with paper towels for a better crust.

3. Season simply
- Generously season with salt.
- Add black pepper before or after cooking, depending on preference.
- A little garlic powder can complement the flavor.

4. Use high heat
- A cast-iron skillet is excellent for developing a crust.
- Heat the pan until very hot before adding the steak.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado or canola oil.

5. Cook by temperature, not just time
Target internal temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

An instant-read thermometer is one of the best tools for consistency.

6. Baste for extra flavor
- During the last minute or two, add butter, garlic, and herbs such as thyme or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak.

7. Rest before slicing
- Let the steak rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps retain juices.

8. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain to maximize tenderness.

For a busy schedule, a simple weeknight method is:
- Season steak.
- Sear 3–4 minutes per side in a hot skillet.
- Check temperature.
- Rest while preparing a quick side such as roasted vegetables or a salad.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Cooking a cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Moving it constantly instead of allowing a crust to form.
- Skipping the resting period.
- Relying solely on cooking times rather than internal temperature.
