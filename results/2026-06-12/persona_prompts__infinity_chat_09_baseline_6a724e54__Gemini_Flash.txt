── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is there life on other planets?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is: **We do not know yet, but most scientists believe it is highly likely.**

To date, we have not found definitive, universally accepted evidence of life beyond Earth. However, the search is one of the most active and exciting areas of modern science. 

Here is a breakdown of where we stand, why scientists are optimistic, and where we are looking.

---

### 1. The Numbers Game (Why it is likely)
The primary reason scientists believe life exists elsewhere is sheer mathematics. 
* **Trillions of planets:** There are an estimated 100 billion to 400 billion stars in our Milky Way galaxy alone, and almost all of them are believed to have planets. 
* **Trillions of galaxies:** There are an estimated 2 trillion galaxies in the observable universe.
* This means there are more planets in the universe than there are grains of sand on all of Earth’s beaches. Even if the conditions for life are incredibly rare, the sheer number of opportunities makes it highly improbable that Earth is the only place where life sparked.

### 2. Where We Are Looking (Our Solar System)
We don't have to look to other star systems to find potential life. There are several candidates in our own cosmic backyard:
* **Mars:** Mars was once warm, wet, and had a thick atmosphere. NASA’s Perseverance and Curiosity rovers are currently searching for signs of ancient microbial life (fossils) in dried-up lakebeds.
* **Europa (Jupiter’s moon) & Enceladus (Saturn’s moon):** These icy moons have liquid water oceans hidden beneath their frozen crusts. Enceladus even shoots geysers of water, organic molecules, and silica into space, suggesting hydrothermal vents on its ocean floor—similar to where life may have started on Earth.
* **Titan (Saturn’s moon):** Titan has a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane and ethane. If life exists there, it would be "life as we don't know it," using a completely different chemistry than Earth life.

### 3. Where We Are Looking (Exoplanets)
Planets orbiting other stars are called **exoplanets**. 
* Using telescopes like the **James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)**, astronomers are analyzing the atmospheres of planets in the "Goldilocks Zone" (the region around a star where it is neither too hot nor too cold for liquid water to exist).
* Scientists are looking for **biosignatures**—gases like oxygen, ozone, methane, and carbon dioxide in a planet's atmosphere that, when found together, strongly suggest the presence of living organisms.

### 4. The Search for Intelligent Life (SETI)
The **Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence (SETI)** uses massive radio telescopes to scan the skies for artificial signals (like radio waves or laser pulses) sent by advanced civilizations. So far, we have heard only silence, a puzzle known as the **Fermi Paradox** ("If life is so likely, where is everybody?").

### Summary
While we have **zero proof** today, we are closer than ever to finding an answer. Many astrobiologists believe we may find evidence of microbial life (bacteria or single-celled organisms) on Mars or the icy moons of the outer solar system within the next few decades.
