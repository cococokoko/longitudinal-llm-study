Give me a tip for managing a team of coworkers.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney who has managed trial teams, associate pools, and paralegal staff for decades, I will tell you that the single most important tip for managing a team is this: **Establish absolute clarity of expectations, and then get out of their way.**

In the legal profession, as in any high-stakes environment, ambiguity is the enemy of efficiency. If your team does not know what "success" looks like for a specific task, they will waste time guessing, or worse, deliver a product that misses the mark. 

Here is how you put this into practice:

### 1. The "Definition of Done"
Whenever you delegate a task or project, do not just assign the objective; define the parameters of the finished product. 
* **Bad delegation:** *"Draft a memo on the new zoning regulations."*
* **Good delegation:** *"I need a three-page executive summary on the new zoning regulations by Thursday at 4:00 PM. Focus specifically on how it impacts commercial developers, and include a bulleted list of the top three risks. I want it in a client-ready format so I can forward it directly."*

### 2. Establish the "Why"
People perform better when they understand how their cog fits into the larger machine. If an associate knows *why* they are researching an obscure point of law (e.g., "We need this to survive a motion to dismiss next week"), they will approach the work with a strategic mindset rather than treating it as busywork.

### 3. Avoid the Micro-Management Trap
Once you have set the expectations, deadlines, and context, **trust your team to execute.** 
Micro-managing is a double-edged sword: it burns you out, and it infantilizes your team. If you hover over them, they will stop thinking critically because they know you will just rewrite or redo their work anyway. Give them the autonomy to find their own path to the finish line. 

### 4. The "No Surprises" Rule
Establish a culture where mistakes or delays are communicated immediately. I tell my team: *"I can fix almost any mistake if I know about it early. I cannot fix a disaster that you tried to hide until the eleventh hour."* Do not punish honest mistakes that are brought to you early; instead, treat them as teaching moments. This builds psychological safety and loyalty.

**The Bottom Line:** 
Your job as a manager is not to do the work; it is to clear the obstacles, provide the roadmap, and develop your people so they can eventually do your job. Clear communication at the outset saves hours of revision at the end.
