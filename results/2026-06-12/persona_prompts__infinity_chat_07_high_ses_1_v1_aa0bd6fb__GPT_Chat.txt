── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes—if by "everything" you mean all true facts, all relevant knowledge, and all future discoveries, it is effectively impossible for any individual human to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The volume of knowledge vastly exceeds any individual's capacity. Even within neurosurgery, no single person can master every subfield in complete detail, let alone all of medicine.
- Knowledge continually expands. New findings are generated faster than any one person can fully assimilate them.
- Some knowledge is inaccessible at a given time. There are truths not yet discovered, observations not yet made, and events that have not yet occurred.
- Human cognition has limits in memory, attention, processing speed, and lifespan.
- Certain domains may have inherent limits on knowability. In mathematics, logic, and physics there are results suggesting that some truths cannot be derived within particular systems or that there are limits to prediction and measurement.

A more interesting question is what "knowing everything" means. If you define it as possessing every fact, then the answer is no. If you define it as having sufficient understanding to navigate any question effectively, then expertise becomes less about storing all knowledge and more about:
- Knowing foundational principles.
- Knowing how to find reliable information.
- Knowing how to evaluate evidence.
- Knowing the limits of one's current understanding.

In academic medicine, many of the most accomplished physicians are distinguished not by omniscience but by an unusually accurate sense of what they know, what they do not know, and how to close the gap.
