Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about noticing patterns in your reactions, choices, and experiences over time.

A few approaches that can help:

- Pay attention to energy, not just enjoyment.
  - Ask yourself: What activities leave me feeling more engaged, curious, or alive afterward?
  - What conversations do I keep returning to?
  - What problems do I naturally spend time thinking about?

- Separate "want" from "should."
  - Many people carry goals inherited from family, culture, peers, or past versions of themselves.
  - Try listing:
    - Things I genuinely want.
    - Things I think I should want.
  - Notice where the lists differ.

- Look at your behavior rather than your ideals.
  - What do you actually spend time, money, and attention on?
  - Actions often reveal priorities more accurately than intentions.

- Use experiments instead of introspection alone.
  - You can think about a path for years and still not know whether you want it.
  - Try small, low-risk tests:
    - Take a class.
    - Volunteer.
    - Shadow someone.
    - Start a project.
  - Experience provides information that reflection alone cannot.

- Notice recurring envy.
  - Envy is uncomfortable, but it can be informative.
  - When you envy someone, ask:
    - What exactly do I want that they have?
    - Their status? Freedom? Skill? Relationships? Impact?
  - Often the underlying desire is different from the surface object.

- Imagine multiple futures.
  - Instead of asking, "What do I want?" ask:
    - Which future seems most meaningful?
    - Which future would I regret not exploring?
    - Which sacrifices am I willing to make?

- Accept that wanting can be unclear.
  - People often expect certainty before acting.
  - In reality, clarity frequently follows action rather than preceding it.

If you're feeling especially stuck, it can help to answer three questions:
- What am I curious about?
- What am I avoiding?
- What would I pursue if success and others' approval were not factors?
