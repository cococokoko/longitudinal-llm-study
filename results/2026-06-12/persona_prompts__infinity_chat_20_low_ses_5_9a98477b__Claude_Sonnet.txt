## Arguments some economists make against mandated equal pay policies:

- **Wage differences may reflect unmeasured productivity differences** that are hard to observe
- **Compliance costs** for businesses could reduce hiring
- **Market competition** theoretically already penalizes employers who underpay productive workers
- **Measurement problems** - defining "equal work" is genuinely complex

## However, significant counterarguments exist:

- **Market failures are well-documented** - discrimination persists even in competitive markets
- **Information asymmetries** allow wage gaps to persist without correction
- **Existing equal pay laws** haven't eliminated documented gaps, suggesting markets don't self-correct
- **Economic evidence** suggests closing wage gaps increases overall economic output
- **Monopsony power** means workers often can't simply move to better-paying employers

## What the evidence actually shows:

- Unexplained wage gaps persist even after controlling for occupation, hours, and experience
- Countries with stronger equal pay enforcement don't show the predicted economic harms
- The premise that equal pay mandates harm economies isn't strongly supported empirically
