Healthy meal planning on a budget is largely about prioritizing nutrient-dense staples, reducing food waste, and building meals around versatile ingredients.

Key principles
- Plan meals before shopping.
- Buy staples in bulk when practical.
- Favor seasonal produce and store brands.
- Use frozen fruits and vegetables as cost-effective alternatives to fresh.
- Choose affordable protein sources such as beans, lentils, eggs, canned fish, tofu, and chicken thighs.
- Cook larger batches and use leftovers intentionally.
- Minimize food waste by repurposing ingredients across multiple meals.

Budget-friendly grocery categories
- Proteins:
  - Eggs
  - Dried or canned beans
  - Lentils
  - Tofu
  - Canned tuna or salmon
  - Greek yogurt
  - Chicken thighs
- Grains and starches:
  - Oats
  - Brown rice
  - Whole-wheat pasta
  - Potatoes
  - Whole-grain bread
- Vegetables:
  - Frozen mixed vegetables
  - Carrots
  - Onions
  - Cabbage
  - Broccoli
  - Spinach
- Fruits:
  - Bananas
  - Apples
  - Seasonal fruit
  - Frozen berries
- Healthy fats:
  - Peanut butter
  - Olive oil
  - Nuts and seeds (purchased in larger packages if economical)

Example 5-day meal plan

Day 1
- Breakfast: Oatmeal with banana and peanut butter
- Lunch: Lentil soup with whole-grain bread
- Dinner: Roasted chicken thighs, brown rice, and broccoli

Day 2
- Breakfast: Greek yogurt with oats and fruit
- Lunch: Leftover chicken and rice bowl with vegetables
- Dinner: Bean and vegetable chili

Day 3
- Breakfast: Scrambled eggs and whole-grain toast
- Lunch: Chili leftovers
- Dinner: Stir-fried tofu, frozen vegetables, and rice

Day 4
- Breakfast: Oatmeal with frozen berries
- Lunch: Tuna salad sandwich with carrots
- Dinner: Whole-wheat pasta with tomato sauce and vegetables

Day 5
- Breakfast: Greek yogurt and fruit
- Lunch: Leftover pasta and side salad
- Dinner: Bean tacos with cabbage slaw

Ways to reduce costs further
- Compare unit prices rather than package prices.
- Build meals around weekly grocery promotions.
- Cook beans from dry when time allows.
- Freeze extra portions for future meals.
- Limit highly processed snacks and convenience foods.
- Keep a short list of low-cost meals you can rotate regularly.

For a busy executive schedule, consider a weekly 60–90 minute meal-prep session focused on preparing:
- A protein (e.g., chicken, tofu, or beans)
- A grain (e.g., rice or quinoa)
- Chopped vegetables
- Two simple sauces or dressings

This approach makes it easier to assemble healthy meals throughout the week while controlling both costs and time.
