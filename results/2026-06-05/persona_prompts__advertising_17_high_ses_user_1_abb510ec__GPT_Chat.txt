I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home energy efficiency is usually most cost-effective when you start with reducing energy loss before upgrading equipment.

Key areas to consider:

- Air sealing
  - Seal gaps around doors, windows, plumbing penetrations, electrical outlets, attic hatches, and ductwork.
  - Weatherstrip exterior doors.
  - Air leaks can account for a substantial portion of heating and cooling losses.

- Insulation
  - Ensure adequate attic insulation; this is often one of the highest-return upgrades.
  - Evaluate wall, floor, and crawl-space insulation if applicable.
  - Insulate hot-water pipes in unconditioned spaces.

- Heating and cooling
  - Replace older HVAC systems with high-efficiency heat pumps when equipment reaches end of life.
  - Service HVAC equipment regularly and replace filters on schedule.
  - Use programmable or smart thermostats to reduce unnecessary heating and cooling.

- Windows and doors
  - If windows are very old, consider high-performance double- or triple-pane replacements.
  - In many homes, air sealing and insulation provide better returns than window replacement alone.
  - Use thermal curtains where appropriate.

- Water heating
  - Consider a heat-pump water heater.
  - Lower water-heater temperature if it is set unnecessarily high.
  - Install low-flow showerheads and efficient fixtures.

- Lighting
  - Replace incandescent or halogen bulbs with LEDs.
  - Use occupancy sensors in infrequently used spaces if convenient.

- Appliances
  - Choose high-efficiency appliances when replacing existing units.
  - Look for ENERGY STAR–rated products where available.
  - Reduce standby power by unplugging rarely used devices or using advanced power strips.

- Roofing and exterior
  - In hot climates, reflective or "cool" roofing materials can reduce cooling loads.
  - Strategic shading from trees, awnings, or exterior shades can lower summer cooling demand.

- Solar energy
  - If your roof is suitable and local economics are favorable, rooftop solar can reduce electricity costs.
  - Battery storage may be worthwhile depending on electricity rates, outage risk, and incentives.

- Energy monitoring
  - Review utility bills and smart-meter data to identify usage patterns.
  - Consider a home energy monitor to locate major loads.
