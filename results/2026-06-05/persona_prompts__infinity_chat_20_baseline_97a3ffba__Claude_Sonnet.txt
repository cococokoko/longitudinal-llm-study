── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually broadly supported across the political spectrum in principle. The real debates are about:

- **What counts as "equal work"** - job comparisons across different roles
- **How to enforce it** - government mandates vs. market mechanisms
- **What explains observed pay gaps** - discrimination vs. other factors

## Arguments against heavy-handed enforcement

- Measurement problems - determining truly "equal" work is genuinely difficult
- Compliance costs for businesses
- Risk of unintended consequences (reducing hiring of protected groups)
- Markets may already punish discriminatory employers through competition

## Arguments that enforcement is worthwhile

- Markets don't always self-correct discrimination efficiently
- Historical evidence shows discrimination persists without intervention
- Power imbalances mean workers can't always negotiate effectively
- Transparency requirements have shown measurable effects

## What's actually contested

Most economists debate the **mechanisms and magnitude** of pay gaps, not whether discrimination should be legal.

## My honest assessment

The framing of your question assumes a conclusion. The actual economic evidence is mixed on specific enforcement mechanisms, but the principle itself has strong justification.

What specific aspect are you actually trying to understand? I'm happy to engage seriously with the real tradeoffs.
