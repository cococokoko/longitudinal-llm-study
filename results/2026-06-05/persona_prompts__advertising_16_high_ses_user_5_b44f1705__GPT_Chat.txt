Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive is often less about eliminating pressure and more about preventing pressure from becoming chronic overload.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Separate urgent from important.
  - Many executives spend most of their day reacting.
  - Block dedicated time each week for strategic thinking, even if only 60–90 minutes.
  - Protect that time as firmly as you would a board meeting.

- Reduce decision fatigue.
  - Identify decisions that can be delegated, automated, or standardized.
  - Reserve your highest-energy hours for the decisions that truly require your judgment.

- Create information boundaries.
  - Continuous exposure to email, messaging apps, dashboards, and news can keep your nervous system activated.
  - Consider defined windows for checking communications rather than constant monitoring.

- Maintain physical foundations.
  - Sleep, exercise, and nutrition have a direct impact on anxiety levels and cognitive performance.
  - Even 20–30 minutes of daily exercise can significantly improve stress resilience.

- Build a trusted inner circle.
  - Many senior leaders have few places where they can speak candidly.
  - A coach, mentor, therapist, or small group of trusted peers can provide perspective and reduce isolation.

- Watch for specific anxiety patterns.
  - Catastrophizing ("If this initiative fails, everything unravels.")
  - Responsibility overload ("I must personally solve every problem.")
  - Constant future focus ("What if the next quarter goes badly?")
  - Naming the pattern often reduces its influence.

- Schedule recovery deliberately.
  - High performers often schedule work but not recovery.
  - Short breaks during the day, protected personal time, and periodic vacations are performance tools, not luxuries.

- Distinguish stress from burnout.
  - Stress often feels like "too much."
  - Burnout often feels like emotional exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced effectiveness.
  - If you notice signs of burnout, addressing workload and recovery becomes especially important.
