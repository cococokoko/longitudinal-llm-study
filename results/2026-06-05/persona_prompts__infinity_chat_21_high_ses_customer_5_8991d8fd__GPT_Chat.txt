People who support the death penalty typically do so for a few major reasons. Even among supporters, the emphasis varies—some focus on justice, others on public safety, and others on broader social considerations.

Some of the strongest arguments made in favor of the death penalty are:

- Retributive justice
  - Supporters argue that certain crimes, especially premeditated murder or mass killings, are so severe that the offender deserves the highest possible punishment.
  - This view is based on the principle of proportionality: the punishment should fit the crime.
  - For some, executing a person who intentionally took innocent lives is seen as a just response rather than an act primarily aimed at deterrence.

- Permanent incapacitation
  - Execution guarantees that the offender can never commit another crime, escape, orchestrate violence from prison, or be released in the future.
  - Advocates argue that this provides the maximum level of protection to society.

- Deterrence
  - Some supporters believe that the possibility of execution discourages at least some potential offenders from committing the most serious crimes.
  - The empirical evidence on deterrence is heavily debated, but the argument remains influential because even a small deterrent effect could, in theory, save lives.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Some argue that the death penalty recognizes the gravity of the harm done to victims and their families.
  - They contend that the strongest crimes warrant the strongest societal condemnation.
  - Certain families of victims report feeling that a death sentence delivers a sense of closure or justice, although many others do not.

- Moral accountability
  - Supporters often argue that adults who knowingly commit extreme crimes should be held fully accountable for their actions.
  - In this view, imposing the highest penalty reflects respect for moral agency and responsibility.

- Social and symbolic condemnation
  - The death penalty can be viewed as a public statement that certain acts are beyond the limits of what society can tolerate.
  - Advocates argue that it communicates the value society places on innocent human life by imposing the harshest sanction for its intentional destruction.

- Historical and legal tradition
  - Some supporters point to longstanding legal traditions in many countries and cultures that have treated execution as an appropriate punishment for the most serious offenses.
  - They argue that democratic societies have the authority to determine that certain crimes merit capital punishment.

It is worth noting that each of these arguments has corresponding counterarguments. For example, opponents often cite the risk of executing innocent people, questions about deterrence, ethical objections, racial or socioeconomic disparities, and the possibility of life imprisonment without parole as an alternative. The debate is ultimately a combination of moral, legal, practical, and empirical considerations.
