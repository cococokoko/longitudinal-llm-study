── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you're starting a plant-based diet, it helps to combine a few reliable resources: practical nutrition guidance, simple recipes, and meal-planning tools.

Good evidence-based nutrition resources:
- Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM) — pcrm.org
  - Offers beginner guides, meal plans, recipes, and nutrition information focused on plant-based eating.
- NutritionFacts.org
  - Created by Dr. Michael Greger. Reviews nutrition research and provides free videos, articles, and practical tips.
- Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health Nutrition Source
  - Provides balanced information on healthy eating patterns, including plant-forward diets.

Books for beginners:
- "How Not to Die" by Michael Greger, MD
  - Explains the research behind plant-based eating and includes practical recommendations.
- "Plant-Based on a Budget" by Toni Okamoto
  - Focuses on affordable, accessible meals.
- "The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners" by Gabriel Miller
  - A straightforward introduction with meal plans and recipes.

Recipe websites:
- Minimalist Baker
  - Many simple plant-based recipes with limited ingredients.
- Forks Over Knives
  - Extensive collection of whole-food, plant-based recipes.
- Rainbow Plant Life
  - Flavorful recipes and helpful cooking tutorials.
- Love & Lemons
  - Vegetable-focused recipes suitable for beginners.

Apps and meal-planning tools:
- Forks Meal Planner
  - Structured meal plans and grocery lists.
- Mealime
  - Customizable meal planning with many plant-based options.
- Cronometer
  - Useful for tracking nutrient intake, especially when transitioning diets.

Key nutrition topics to learn early:
- Protein sources: beans, lentils, tofu, tempeh, edamame, seitan, nuts, seeds.
- Vitamin B12: usually requires supplementation on a fully plant-based diet.
- Iron: found in legumes, tofu, whole grains, and leafy greens; vitamin C helps absorption.
- Calcium: fortified plant milks, tofu made with calcium sulfate, leafy greens.
- Omega-3 fats: flaxseeds, chia seeds, walnuts, or algae-based supplements.
- Vitamin D: may require supplementation depending on sun exposure and location.

A simple way to start:
- Replace one meal a day with a plant-based option.
- Build meals around:
  - A protein source (beans, tofu, lentils)
  - Whole grains (brown rice, oats, quinoa)
  - Vegetables
  - Healthy fats (nuts, seeds, avocado)
- Keep a few easy staples on hand, such as canned beans, frozen vegetables, oats, tofu, and whole-grain pasta.
