# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose your **hourly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **x** = number of hours worked per shift
- **y** = hourly wage rate ($/hr)

The total pay function is:

$$f(x, y) = x^2 + 3xy + 2y$$

---

## Finding the Partial Derivatives

### ∂f/∂x (rate of change with respect to hours, holding wage constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + 3y$$

### ∂f/∂y (rate of change with respect to wage, holding hours constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x + 2$$

---

## Numerical Calculation

Suppose you work **x = 8 hours** at **y = $20/hr**:

| Partial Derivative | Calculation | Result |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x | 2(8) + 3(20) | **76** |
| ∂f/∂y | 3(8) + 2 | **26** |

---

## Interpretation

- **∂f/∂x = 76** → If you work **one extra hour** (holding wage at $20), your total pay increases by approximately **$76**
- **∂f/∂y = 26** → If your **wage increases by $1** (holding hours at 8), your total pay increases by approximately **$26**

> **Key idea:** A partial derivative measures how much the output changes when you adjust *one variable at a time*, keeping everything else fixed — just like asking *"what happens to my paycheck if only my hours change?"*
