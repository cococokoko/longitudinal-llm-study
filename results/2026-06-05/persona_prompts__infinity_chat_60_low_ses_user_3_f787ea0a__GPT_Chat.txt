── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a row of dominoes set up in a very special pattern.

You ask, “What will the 1,000th domino look like after they all fall?”

For some things, you can use a clever trick and figure out the answer right away.

But for other things, there is no shortcut. The only way to know what the 1,000th domino will look like is to knock over the first domino and watch what happens, one step at a time, until you get to the 1,000th.

That's the basic idea of computational irreducibility:

- Some processes can't be predicted with a faster calculation.
- To know what happens later, you have to let the process actually unfold.
- Even a very smart computer can't skip ahead and instantly know the result.

A simple analogy:

- If I ask, "What is 2 + 2?" you can answer immediately.
- If I ask, "What will this complicated game look like after a million moves?" there may be no shortcut. You might have to play all million moves to find out.

Computational irreducibility is the idea that some systems are like that game: the future can't be compressed into an easier calculation. The shortest way to find out what happens is to actually do all the steps.
