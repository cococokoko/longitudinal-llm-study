Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to four areas: router placement, equipment quality, configuration, and reducing interference.

Here are the highest-impact steps:

1. Optimize router placement
- Put the router in a central location in the home.
- Place it out in the open, not inside a cabinet or closet.
- Elevate it on a shelf or table.
- Keep it away from large metal objects, TVs, microwaves, and cordless phone bases.

2. Use modern Wi‑Fi hardware
- If your router is more than 4–5 years old, upgrading can make a significant difference.
- Look for Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E routers; Wi‑Fi 7 is the newest generation if you want maximum performance and future-proofing.

3. Upgrade to a mesh system for larger homes
- For homes with multiple floors or more than ~2,000 sq ft (185 sq m), a mesh Wi‑Fi system often performs better than a single router.
- Place mesh nodes where the signal is still reasonably strong, not at the edge of coverage.

4. Use the right frequency band
- 5 GHz: Faster speeds, shorter range.
- 2.4 GHz: Longer range, slower speeds.
- 6 GHz (if supported): Highest performance with less congestion, but shorter range.
- Allow devices to use the fastest band they can reliably connect to.

5. Reduce wireless interference
- Change Wi‑Fi channels if neighboring networks are crowded.
- Separate Wi‑Fi devices from Bluetooth-heavy equipment when possible.
- Avoid placing routers near microwaves and baby monitors.

6. Update firmware
- Keep router firmware current for performance, stability, and security improvements.
- Enable automatic updates if available.

7. Use Ethernet where practical
- Connect TVs, gaming consoles, desktop PCs, and workstations via Ethernet.
- Every wired device reduces congestion on Wi‑Fi.

8. Check your internet plan versus Wi‑Fi performance
- Run a speed test near the router and then in problem areas.
- If speeds are poor even next to the router, the internet connection or router may be the bottleneck.
- If speeds are good near the router but poor elsewhere, coverage is the issue.

9. Secure the network
- Use WPA3 security if supported.
- Remove unknown devices from the network.
- A crowded network can affect performance.

10. Consider advanced options for demanding use cases
- For home offices, video conferencing, gaming, or large homes, enterprise-grade access points (e.g., Ubiquiti UniFi, Aruba Instant On, TP-Link Omada) can provide more reliable coverage than consumer routers.
