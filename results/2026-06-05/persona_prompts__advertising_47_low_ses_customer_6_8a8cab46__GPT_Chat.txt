Training for a marathon is as much about consistency and recovery as it is about running. Since you work long hours in a warehouse, managing fatigue and recovery will be especially important.

Some key tips:

- Build mileage gradually
  - Increase your weekly running distance slowly, often by no more than about 10% per week.
  - Most runs should feel comfortable and conversational.

- Follow a structured plan
  - Marathon plans typically last 12–20 weeks.
  - Include:
    - Easy runs
    - One weekly long run
    - A speed or tempo workout
    - Rest or recovery days

- Prioritize the long run
  - This is the most marathon-specific workout.
  - Gradually extend your longest run over time, often reaching 16–22 miles (26–35 km) depending on the plan.

- Don't run every workout hard
  - Many new runners go too fast too often.
  - Easy runs help build endurance while reducing injury risk.

- Practice fueling
  - For long runs, experiment with gels, sports drinks, or other carbohydrate sources.
  - Never try new nutrition for the first time on race day.

- Hydrate consistently
  - Pay attention to fluid intake, especially if your warehouse environment is warm or physically demanding.
  - Replace fluids and electrolytes after long runs and demanding work shifts.

- Take recovery seriously
  - Sleep is one of the biggest performance enhancers available.
  - If a particularly demanding workday leaves you exhausted, it may be smarter to shorten or modify a workout than force it.

- Strength train
  - Two short sessions per week can help improve running economy and reduce injury risk.
  - Focus on core, glutes, hips, calves, and hamstrings.

- Invest in good shoes
  - Choose running shoes that fit well and are comfortable.
  - Replace them periodically as they wear out.

- Practice race-day strategy
  - Learn your target pace.
  - Avoid starting too fast; many marathon runners lose significant time in the second half by going out aggressively.

- Taper before the race
  - Reduce training volume during the final 2–3 weeks while maintaining some intensity.
  - Arrive at the start line rested rather than exhausted.
