── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here because it depends on your finances, your relationship, and how ownership will work. The key is to separate three different issues:

- Who owns the house?
- Who pays the mortgage and other housing costs?
- What happens if you break up before marriage or divorce later?

If your girlfriend is buying the house in her name alone before you're married, the simplest arrangement is often:

- She remains the sole owner.
- You contribute a mutually agreed amount toward living expenses (similar to rent).
- You do not contribute toward building equity unless you are receiving ownership rights in return.

The reason many people choose this is that if she owns the asset and benefits from its appreciation, it can feel unbalanced for you to make large mortgage or down-payment contributions without acquiring any ownership interest.

Some common options:

1. Pay rent to her
- She owns the house.
- You pay an agreed monthly amount comparable to what you'd pay elsewhere.
- She handles the mortgage as the owner.
- This is usually the cleanest arrangement legally and financially.

2. Split housing costs but not equity-building payments
- You split utilities, maintenance, insurance, groceries, etc.
- She pays the mortgage principal.
- This keeps ownership and equity aligned.

3. Contribute to the mortgage and receive ownership
- If you're making substantial contributions, consider becoming a co-owner.
- Ownership percentages can reflect each person's contributions.
- This should be documented by a real estate attorney.

4. Wait until marriage and then revisit
- Keep the house as her premarital asset.
- After marriage, decide whether to combine finances, add you to the title, refinance jointly, or keep it separate.

The biggest mistake is often contributing significant money toward a down payment, mortgage principal, renovations, or major repairs without a written agreement about ownership. People frequently assume marriage is coming soon and never document anything; if circumstances change, that can create conflict.

A few questions that would help narrow this down:
- Will the house be purchased entirely in her name?
- Do you plan to move in immediately?
- Would you be contributing to the down payment?
- Are your incomes roughly similar, or is one of you earning much more?
- Are you in the U.S. or another country? Property laws vary significantly.
