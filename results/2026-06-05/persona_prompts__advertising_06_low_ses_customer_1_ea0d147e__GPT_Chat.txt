If your goal is mastering digital photography, a structured approach can help, especially if you're balancing a full-time fast-food job with an unpredictable schedule.

A practical roadmap:

1. Learn the fundamentals
- Exposure triangle:
  - Aperture (depth of field)
  - Shutter speed (motion blur/freeze)
  - ISO (sensor sensitivity/noise)
- Focus modes and composition basics
- White balance and lighting

2. Practice intentionally
- Spend 15–30 minutes whenever you have free time rather than waiting for long study sessions.
- Choose one skill per week:
  - Week 1: Aperture
  - Week 2: Shutter speed
  - Week 3: ISO
  - Week 4: Composition

3. Master composition
- Rule of thirds
- Leading lines
- Framing
- Symmetry
- Negative space
- Learn when to follow and break these guidelines.

4. Understand light
- Photograph during different times of day.
- Study:
  - Golden hour
  - Blue hour
  - Harsh midday light
  - Indoor lighting
- Light often matters more than camera gear.

5. Learn editing
- Start with Lightroom, Capture One, Darktable, or another editor.
- Focus on:
  - Exposure
  - Contrast
  - Color correction
  - Cropping
- Avoid relying on heavy filters.

6. Analyze great photographs
- Study work from photographers you admire.
- Ask:
  - Where is the light coming from?
  - Why does the composition work?
  - What emotion is being conveyed?

7. Build projects
Instead of random photos, try:
- Street photography
- Food photography
- Portraits of friends
- Local architecture
- A 30-day photo challenge

8. Get feedback
- Join photography communities online.
- Share images and ask for specific critiques.
- Compare your current work to your own older work rather than only to professionals.

Suggested weekly schedule for a busy, unpredictable work routine:
- 2–3 short learning sessions (15–20 minutes each)
- 1 photo walk or shooting session (30–60 minutes when available)
- 1 editing session (20–30 minutes)
- Review your best photos at the end of the week

Recommended beginner resources:
- YouTube: Simon d'Entremont, Nigel Danson, Tony & Chelsea Northrup
- Books:
  - Understanding Exposure by Bryan Peterson
  - The Photographer's Eye by Michael Freeman
