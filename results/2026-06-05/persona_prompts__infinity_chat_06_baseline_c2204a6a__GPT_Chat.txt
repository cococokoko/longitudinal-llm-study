── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experience over time.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice envy and admiration.
  - When you feel jealous of someone, ask: "What exactly am I jealous of?"
  - Often it's not the person themselves but something they have—freedom, recognition, creativity, stability, intimacy, adventure.

- Pay attention to energy.
  - What activities leave you feeling engaged, absorbed, or satisfied?
  - What kinds of conversations do you naturally want more of?
  - Energy isn't everything, but it's a useful signal.

- Separate wants from expectations.
  - Ask yourself:
    - "If nobody judged me, what would I choose?"
    - "If I didn't have to impress anyone, what would matter most?"
  - Sometimes we pursue goals because they're expected, not because we genuinely want them.

- Look at recurring themes.
  - What have you wanted repeatedly over months or years?
  - Temporary impulses come and go; deeper desires tend to return.

- Experiment instead of overthinking.
  - Many people try to think their way into certainty.
  - Often you learn what you want by trying things:
    - Take the class.
    - Go on the date.
    - Apply for the role.
    - Visit the place.
  - Information from experience is usually more reliable than speculation.

- Distinguish the goal from the feeling.
  - Sometimes what we want is not the thing itself but the experience we imagine it will create.
  - For example, wanting a promotion may really be wanting competence, respect, or financial security.

You can also try a simple exercise:

Write answers to these prompts as quickly as possible, without editing yourself:

- I feel most alive when...
- I wish I had more time for...
- I keep thinking about...
- If I could spend the next year learning one thing, it would be...
- At age 80, I would regret not...
- The part of my life that feels most neglected is...

Then read your answers and look for patterns rather than a single definitive answer.
