── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. For any human being, it is impossible to know everything.

There are several independent reasons:

- Finite cognitive capacity: The amount of information in the universe appears vast, and a human brain has finite storage, attention, and processing ability.

- Finite lifespan: Even if knowledge were perfectly organized, there is more information available than a person could learn during a lifetime.

- Knowledge is continuously expanding: New observations, discoveries, technologies, and historical events are constantly being generated. The total body of human knowledge is not static.

- Limits of access: Some facts may be inaccessible in practice because they are lost, undiscovered, or impossible to observe.

- Fundamental limits in mathematics and logic: Results such as Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that within sufficiently powerful formal systems there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system. This does not directly prove that humans cannot know everything, but it demonstrates deep limits on complete formal knowledge.

- Fundamental limits in physics: Depending on the interpretation of current theories, there may be aspects of reality that are inherently uncertain, unobservable, or unknowable.

There is also a distinction between "knowing all facts" and "understanding everything." Even if someone could memorize every currently known fact, complete understanding of all domains and all relationships among those facts would be another challenge entirely.

As a neurosurgeon, you may see a practical version of this principle every day: expertise grows by increasing specialization. Modern medicine became more powerful precisely because no individual can master the entirety of medicine, biology, physics, engineering, statistics, ethics, and every related field at once.

So while it may not be impossible to know an extraordinary amount, omniscience appears unattainable for humans.
