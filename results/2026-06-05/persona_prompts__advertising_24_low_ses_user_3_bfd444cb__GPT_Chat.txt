── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The best retirement-saving strategy depends on your country, tax situation, age, income, and whether your employer offers a retirement plan. However, a few principles are broadly useful:

- Start as early as possible.
  - Time is one of the most powerful factors in retirement investing because of compound growth.
  - Even relatively small contributions can grow substantially over decades.

- Take advantage of employer-sponsored retirement plans.
  - If your employer offers a retirement plan with matching contributions, contributing enough to receive the full match is often a high-priority step.
  - Employer matches can effectively provide an immediate return on your contributions.

- Use tax-advantaged accounts when available.
  - Many countries offer retirement accounts with tax benefits, such as tax deductions on contributions, tax-deferred growth, or tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
  - These benefits can significantly increase long-term savings.

- Invest rather than keeping retirement savings entirely in cash.
  - For long-term goals like retirement, diversified investments (such as broad stock and bond index funds) have historically provided higher returns than cash savings, though they involve risk and market fluctuations.

- Keep investment costs low.
  - Fees can reduce long-term returns.
  - Low-cost index funds are a popular option for many retirement savers.

- Increase contributions over time.
  - Consider raising your savings rate when you receive pay increases or bonuses.
  - Automating contributions can make this easier.

- Maintain an emergency fund separately.
  - Having accessible savings for unexpected expenses may help you avoid withdrawing retirement funds early, which can trigger taxes, penalties, or lost growth.

Since you mentioned that you're new and still on probation:
- Check whether you're already eligible for any employer retirement plan, as eligibility rules can vary.
- If you're not yet eligible, you can often begin saving through other tax-advantaged or investment accounts and later adjust once employer benefits become available.
- It may also be sensible to maintain enough cash reserves to cover several months of expenses while you're establishing yourself in the new role.
