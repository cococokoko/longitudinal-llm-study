# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a patient's **intracranial pressure (ICP)** modeled as a function of two variables:

$$P(V, R) = \frac{V \cdot R}{4}$$

where:
- **V** = cerebrospinal fluid volume (mL)
- **R** = cerebrovascular resistance (mmHg·min/mL)

*(This is a simplified illustrative model, not a clinical formula)*

---

## Specific Point of Evaluation

Let **V = 10 mL** and **R = 8 mmHg·min/mL**, giving:

$$P(10, 8) = \frac{10 \times 8}{4} = 20 \text{ mmHg}$$

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### ∂P/∂V (holding R constant at 8)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = \frac{R}{4} = \frac{8}{4} = \boxed{2 \text{ mmHg/mL}}$$

> **Interpretation:** If CSF volume increases by **1 mL** while resistance stays fixed, ICP rises by approximately **2 mmHg**

### ∂P/∂R (holding V constant at 10)

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial R} = \frac{V}{4} = \frac{10}{4} = \boxed{2.5 \text{ mmHg per unit R}}$$

> **Interpretation:** If resistance increases by **1 unit** while volume stays fixed, ICP rises by approximately **2.5 mmHg**

---

## The Key Concept

| What changes | What's frozen | Rate of change |
|---|---|---|
| Volume (V) | Resistance (R = 8) | 2 mmHg/mL |
| Resistance (R) | Volume (V = 10) | 2.5 mmHg/unit |

The partial derivative isolates **one variable's contribution** to the change — exactly like asking *"if only this factor changes, how does the outcome respond?"* — which mirrors clinical reasoning when you adjust one parameter while monitoring others.
