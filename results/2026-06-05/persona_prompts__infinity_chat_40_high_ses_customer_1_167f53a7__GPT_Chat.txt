── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on balancing three things:

- The financial reality of who owns the house and who is building equity.
- Your plans to marry.
- The health of the relationship and your comfort with risk.

A useful way to think about it is:

If she buys the house before marriage and the house is solely in her name:
- The down payment, mortgage principal, appreciation, and equity generally belong to her.
- If you move in and contribute money, you should be clear about whether that money is:
  - Rent/living expenses, or
  - An investment that gives you some ownership interest.

Many couples in this situation choose one of these approaches:

1. Pay rent-like contributions
- You contribute a reasonable amount toward housing costs, often less than half the total ownership costs.
- You do not acquire ownership rights.
- She remains responsible for major ownership expenses, taxes, capital improvements, and the risks and rewards of ownership.
- This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.

2. Split living expenses but not the mortgage principal
- You contribute toward utilities, groceries, maintenance, and perhaps a portion of interest or occupancy costs.
- She pays the mortgage principal because that builds her equity.
- This avoids the feeling that you're buying part of an asset you don't own.

3. Create a formal ownership arrangement
- If you want your payments to build equity, get a written legal agreement.
- This could involve being added to the title, purchasing a percentage interest, or having a contract that tracks contributions.
- Do not rely on informal understandings when real estate is involved.

4. Revisit after marriage
- If you're planning to marry in about a year, you may decide to keep ownership separate until then.
- After marriage, you can decide whether to combine finances, refinance jointly, or keep the property separate.

What I would generally avoid:
- Paying substantial amounts toward the mortgage principal for a year or more while having no written ownership rights and assuming you'll "sort it out later."
- Vague verbal agreements about equity.

A practical conversation might sound like:
"The house is yours, and I want to contribute fairly to our living costs. Since I'm not an owner yet, should we treat my contribution as rent and shared expenses? If we want my payments to create equity, I'd prefer that we put that in writing."

A few questions would help narrow this down:
- Will the house be titled only in her name?
- Will you be living there full-time?
- Are you planning to combine finances after marriage?
- How much would your contribution be relative to the mortgage and your incomes?
