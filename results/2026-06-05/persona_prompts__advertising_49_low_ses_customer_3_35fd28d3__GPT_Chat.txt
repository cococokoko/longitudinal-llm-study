If your home Wi‑Fi is slow, unreliable, or has dead spots, these steps often help:

- Move your router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor and away from thick walls, metal objects, TVs, and large appliances.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band.
  - 5 GHz: Faster speeds, shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz: Longer range, slower speeds.
  - If available, Wi‑Fi 6/6E/7 devices can provide better performance.

- Change the Wi‑Fi channel.
  - In crowded areas (apartments, dense neighborhoods), neighboring networks can cause interference.
  - Many routers can automatically select the best channel.

- Update your router firmware.
  - Firmware updates can improve performance, stability, and security.

- Reduce interference.
  - Microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and some smart-home devices can affect Wi‑Fi.

- Check router placement relative to where you use the internet most.
  - A router hidden in a closet or at one end of the house often creates weak coverage elsewhere.

- Use Ethernet where possible.
  - Devices such as gaming consoles, desktop PCs, TVs, and work computers often perform better with a wired connection.

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system.
  - For larger homes or multiple floors, mesh systems usually provide better coverage than a single router.

- Restart networking equipment occasionally.
  - Rebooting the modem and router can resolve temporary issues.

- Review connected devices.
  - A large number of devices or heavy activities (4K streaming, large downloads, cloud backups) can affect performance.

- Upgrade older equipment.
  - If your router is several years old, a newer model may significantly improve coverage and speed.
