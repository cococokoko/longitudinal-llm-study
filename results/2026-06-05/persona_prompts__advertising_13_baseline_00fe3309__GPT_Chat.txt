Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak comes down to understanding a few key principles: choosing the right cut, controlling temperature, seasoning properly, and resting the meat after cooking.

1. Choose the Right Cut
Popular steak cuts include:
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, and forgiving.
- New York Strip: Firm texture with a good balance of tenderness and flavor.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender but milder in flavor.
- Sirloin: Leaner and more affordable.
- T-Bone/Porterhouse: Combines strip steak and tenderloin in one cut.

For beginners, ribeye or New York strip are often the easiest to cook well.

2. Start with Quality Meat
- Look for good marbling (thin streaks of fat throughout the meat).
- Steaks 1 to 1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick are ideal.
- If possible, choose USDA Choice or Prime grades (or equivalent in your country).

3. Bring the Steak Close to Room Temperature
- Remove the steak from the refrigerator 30–60 minutes before cooking.
- Pat it dry thoroughly with paper towels. A dry surface promotes better browning.

4. Season Generously
- Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper are often all you need.
- Salt just before cooking or up to several hours in advance for deeper seasoning.

5. Use High Heat
For pan-searing:
- Use a heavy skillet, preferably cast iron.
- Heat the pan until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, grapeseed).
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.

6. Monitor Internal Temperature
Use an instant-read thermometer for accuracy.

Target temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
- Well-done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Remove the steak about 5°F (3°C) before your target temperature because it will continue cooking while resting.

7. Baste for Extra Flavor
Near the end of cooking:
- Add butter, garlic, and fresh herbs such as thyme or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Rest the Steak
- Let it rest for 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps the juices redistribute throughout the meat.

9. Slice Properly
- Cut against the grain when serving to maximize tenderness.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Cooking cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Not drying the surface before searing.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak before a crust develops.
- Guessing doneness instead of using a thermometer.
- Skipping the resting period.

A simple beginner-friendly method:
- Heat a cast-iron skillet until very hot.
- Season a 1-inch ribeye generously with salt and pepper.
- Sear 3–4 minutes per side.
- Add butter, garlic, and thyme during the last minute.
- Cook to 130°F (54°C) for medium-rare.
- Rest for 5–10 minutes before serving.

Once you consistently hit your target temperature, steak cooking becomes far more repeatable and reliable than relying on cooking times alone.
