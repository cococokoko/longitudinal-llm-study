How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what's causing the problem: weak signal, slow speeds, interference, or too many devices. Here are the most effective steps, roughly in order of impact:

- Move your router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large objects.
  - Place it away from microwaves, cordless phone bases, and other electronics.

- Restart and update your equipment.
  - Reboot the router occasionally.
  - Install the latest router firmware updates.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available.
  - Faster speeds and less congestion than 2.4 GHz.
  - Shorter range, so it's best for nearby devices.

- Optimize Wi‑Fi channels.
  - In crowded areas, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Many routers can automatically select the best channel.
  - For 2.4 GHz, channels 1, 6, and 11 are typically recommended.

- Upgrade old hardware.
  - If your router is more than 5–7 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router may provide significantly better performance.
  - Ensure your modem is also appropriate for your internet plan.

- Add coverage if signal is weak in certain rooms.
  - A mesh Wi‑Fi system is often the best solution for larger homes.
  - Wi‑Fi extenders can help but may reduce performance compared with mesh systems.

- Connect high-bandwidth devices with Ethernet.
  - Gaming consoles, desktop PCs, TVs, and workstations benefit from wired connections.
  - This also frees Wi‑Fi capacity for other devices.

- Reduce network congestion.
  - Disconnect unused devices.
  - Schedule large downloads for off-peak times if needed.

- Check your internet speed.
  - Run a speed test near the router and in problem areas.
  - If speeds are slow even next to the router, the issue may be with your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi.

- Secure your network.
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 security.
  - Set a strong password to prevent unauthorized usage.
