I'm 60 years old and haven't achieved anything. What have I missed out on?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
That depends on what you mean by "achieved anything."

If by achievement you mean public markers—career success, wealth, recognition, major accomplishments—then there may be things you hoped for that didn't happen. It's reasonable to feel grief about unrealized ambitions, especially at 60 when people often take stock of their lives.

But if you're asking what you've objectively "missed out on," there's no single answer. People who achieve one set of things usually miss others:

- The person who built a company may have missed years with family.
- The person who traveled widely may have missed putting down roots.
- The person who pursued prestige may have missed a quieter life.
- The person who chose security may have missed risk and adventure.

No one gets all possible lives.

Another question is whether you've truly achieved nothing. Many people discount things that don't fit their original definition of success:
- Relationships maintained over decades.
- People helped.
- Skills learned.
- Hardships endured.
- Responsibilities carried.
- Moments of joy, curiosity, love, or service.

Those may not make headlines, but they are real parts of a life.

At 60, there's also a difference between "I haven't achieved what I wanted" and "it's too late to do anything meaningful." The first may be true; the second often isn't. Many people begin significant work, creative projects, careers, relationships, or contributions in their 60s and beyond.

A useful question might be: When you say you haven't achieved anything, what specific achievements do you feel are missing? The answer to that will reveal much more than your age does.
