── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background makes it particularly relevant.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, it's impossible to know everything

**Practical reasons:**
- The volume of human knowledge already exceeds any individual's capacity
- Knowledge grows faster than any person can absorb it
- Even in neurosurgery specifically, the literature expands continuously

**More fundamental reasons:**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even formal systems can't capture all truths within themselves
- **Physical limits** - the brain has finite storage and processing capacity
- **The observer problem** - knowing everything would include knowing the complete state of the system doing the knowing, which creates logical paradoxes
- **Unknown unknowns** - we can't fully map what we don't know

## Worth being honest about the nuances though

- "Everything" needs defining - everything about *what*?
- Some domains may be **completable** in principle
- The question of whether reality itself is fully knowable is genuinely contested philosophically

Neurosurgery sits at an interesting intersection - you likely appreciate both:
- How much **functional knowledge** is practically achievable
- How much **fundamental uncertainty** remains about consciousness, neural mechanisms, etc.
