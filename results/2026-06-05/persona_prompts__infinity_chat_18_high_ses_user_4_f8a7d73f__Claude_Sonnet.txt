# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Sundqvist has crafted something genuinely rare — a film that treats scientific failure as emotionally equivalent to grief.

Tilda Swinton plays Elena Voss, a molecular biologist whose decade-long pursuit of a prion therapy collapses in the third act of a Phase II trial. What follows isn't a redemption arc. It's something quieter and more honest: a woman learning to sit inside a result she didn't want.

The film's greatest achievement is its refusal to dramatize the science incorrectly for the audience's comfort. Lab sequences feel lived-in rather than theatrical. A scene where Voss walks a junior postdoc through a failed Western blot — speaking softly, not performing disappointment — is the most emotionally devastating moment in any film this year.

The supporting cast, particularly Riz Ahmed as a biostatistician who saw the signal problems early and said nothing, adds genuine moral complexity. Nobody is a villain. Everyone made reasonable choices. The outcome was still bad.

Some critics have called the pacing slow. They are wrong. It is *accurate*.

The final shot — Voss submitting the negative results for publication anyway — will mean different things to different audiences. For anyone who has run a research group, it will feel like a standing ovation and a funeral simultaneously.

**Required viewing.**
