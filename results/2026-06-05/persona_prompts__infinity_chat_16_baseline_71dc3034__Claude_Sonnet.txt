── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the future of work in an increasingly automated economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely important question worth thinking through carefully rather than just offering reassurance or alarm.

## What We Actually Know

**Historical patterns suggest:**
- Previous automation waves displaced specific jobs but created new categories
- Productivity gains eventually created more employment overall
- Transition periods caused real hardship even when outcomes improved long-term

**Current situation differs in some ways:**
- AI can now affect cognitive work, not just physical/routine tasks
- Speed of change may outpace adaptation capacity
- Some economists genuinely disagree about net job creation this time

## Competing Honest Perspectives

**More optimistic view:**
- New industries and job categories will emerge
- Humans retain advantages in creativity, judgment, relationship-building
- Productivity gains could fund shorter work weeks or better conditions

**More concerning view:**
- Benefits may concentrate among capital owners
- Transition costs fall disproportionately on vulnerable workers
- Some displaced workers may never find equivalent employment

## What Seems Reasonably Clear

- **Skill adaptability** matters more than specific credentials
- **Policy choices** will significantly determine outcomes - this isn't purely inevitable
- **Some jobs** (care work, complex trades, creative work) are more durable than others
- **Distribution of gains** is a political question, not just an economic one

## The Honest Bottom Line

Nobody reliably knows the outcome. The future of work depends heavily on **choices we make** about education, labor policy, and wealth distribution - not just technology.
