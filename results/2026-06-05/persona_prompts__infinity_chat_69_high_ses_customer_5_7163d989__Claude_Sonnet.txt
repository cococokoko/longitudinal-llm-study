## Analysis vs. Design

**Analysis** answers *"What does the system need to do?"* — understanding requirements, problems, and constraints.

**Design** answers *"How will the system do it?"* — defining architecture, components, and solutions.

---

## Can You Design Without Analysis?

**Technically yes, practically no.**

Skipping analysis means you're designing against **assumptions rather than validated requirements**. This leads to:

- Building the **wrong thing** efficiently
- Costly rework when reality diverges from assumptions
- Solutions optimized for the **wrong problem**

---

## The Executive Reality

In fast-moving tech environments, teams often compress or parallelize analysis and design — which is acceptable **if assumptions are explicit and validated quickly** (e.g., agile iterations).

What's **never** acceptable is treating assumptions as facts. The most expensive engineering failures at scale typically trace back not to poor design, but to **skipped or shallow analysis** — solving a well-understood problem that turned out to be the wrong one.

> *"There is nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all."* — Drucker
