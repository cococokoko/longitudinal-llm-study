── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes—at least as far as current knowledge suggests, it is impossible for any finite being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The universe may contain an enormous or even infinite amount of information. If reality is infinite in extent or complexity, then "everything" would not fit into a finite mind.
- Knowledge appears to keep growing. New discoveries, events, and facts continually arise. Even if someone knew every fact at one moment, new facts could emerge afterward.
- There are physical limits on information storage and processing. A brain, computer, or any physical system has finite capacity.
- Some things may be fundamentally unknowable. For example, in mathematics, Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that any sufficiently powerful formal system contains true statements that cannot be proven within that system. In physics, quantum mechanics places limits on what can be simultaneously known about certain properties.
- Self-reference creates difficulties. A system trying to contain a complete account of itself can run into logical paradoxes and limits.

That said, the answer depends on what "everything" means.

- If "everything" means all true facts about reality, most philosophers and scientists would say no finite being can know everything.
- If "everything" means all facts within a small, well-defined domain, then complete knowledge may be possible in principle.
- Some religious or philosophical traditions posit an omniscient being that knows everything, but that is a theological claim rather than an established scientific fact.

So the strongest conclusion we can draw is that complete knowledge appears impossible for humans and likely for any finite intelligence, while absolute omniscience remains a philosophical or theological idea rather than something we know to be achievable.
