── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on your finances, your relationship, and how much risk each of you is willing to take.

A few key points:

- If she buys the house before you're married and the mortgage/title are only in her name, it's generally her house legally.
- If you move in and contribute money, you should be clear about what those payments represent:
  - Rent (you are helping cover housing costs as a resident).
  - Shared household expenses (utilities, groceries, etc.).
  - Contributions toward ownership/equity.

The biggest mistake is making substantial payments toward a house without a clear agreement about whether you're building any ownership interest.

Some common approaches:

- Pay rent and shared expenses
  - She owns the house and takes on the ownership risks and rewards.
  - You pay an agreed-upon amount comparable to rent.
  - This is often the simplest approach before marriage.

- Split expenses but not the mortgage principal
  - You help with utilities, groceries, maintenance, and other living costs.
  - She handles the mortgage and ownership costs.
  - This avoids confusion about equity.

- Create a written agreement
  - If you are contributing significantly toward the mortgage, down payment, renovations, or major repairs, consider a written agreement that explains what happens if you break up, marry, or sell the house.

- Wait until marriage to combine ownership
  - After you're married, you can decide whether to add you to the title, refinance together, or buy a different home jointly.

Given that you expect to marry in about a year, a practical middle-ground is often:
- She buys the house in her name.
- You contribute a fair monthly amount to live there (similar to rent and shared expenses).
- Keep finances transparent.
- Revisit ownership after marriage when you're making a longer-term legal commitment.

One additional factor: since you work long hours in a warehouse position, make sure any contribution leaves you with enough savings and emergency funds. Avoid putting so much money into a house you don't legally own that you weaken your own financial security.

A few questions would help narrow this down:
- Will your name be on the title or mortgage?
- Are you being asked to help with the down payment?
- What are your respective incomes?
- Would your monthly contribution be similar to local rent, or much higher?
