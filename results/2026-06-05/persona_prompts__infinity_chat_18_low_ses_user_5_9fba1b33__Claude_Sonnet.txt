# Movie Review: "The Invisible Pillar" (2024)

★★★★½

Director Maya Okonkwo's debut feature finally gives a face to the person every family has but rarely acknowledges — the one who holds everything together without a title or a paycheck.

The film follows Renata, a woman quietly managing her aging parents, her sister's kids during a custody battle, and her own household, all while her contributions are treated as simply *expected*.

What makes this film remarkable is its **refusal to be a tragedy**. Renata isn't a martyr. She's funny, occasionally resentful, deeply loving, and genuinely complicated. The screenplay trusts her to be all of those things simultaneously.

The kitchen table scenes alone deserve awards consideration — ordinary conversations loaded with the weight of everything *unsaid*.

**What works:** The film never asks you to pity Renata. It asks you to *see* her.

**What could be stronger:** A subplot involving her brother feels underdeveloped.

**The lasting impression:** Walking out, I found myself thinking about everyone in my life who quietly keeps things running — and whether I've ever actually *thanked* them.
