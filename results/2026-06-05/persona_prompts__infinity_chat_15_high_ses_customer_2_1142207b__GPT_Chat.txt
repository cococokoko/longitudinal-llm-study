Global economic inequalities shape international relations in several important ways, influencing power, diplomacy, security, trade, and development.

• Distribution of power and influence
  - Wealthier countries generally have greater military capabilities, stronger diplomatic networks, and more influence in international institutions.
  - Economic strength allows states to shape global rules on trade, finance, technology, and investment.

• Trade relationships
  - Differences in economic development often create unequal trade relationships, where developing countries export raw materials while wealthier countries export higher-value manufactured goods and services.
  - These patterns can reinforce existing inequalities and affect bargaining power in trade negotiations.

• Foreign aid and development policy
  - Wealthier nations and international organizations provide loans, grants, and technical assistance to poorer countries.
  - While aid can support development, it can also create political influence for donor countries and raise concerns about dependency or conditionality.

• Debt and financial dependence
  - Developing countries may rely on external borrowing to finance growth and public services.
  - Debt obligations can affect domestic policy choices and create leverage for creditors, including states, international financial institutions, and private investors.

• Migration flows
  - Economic disparities encourage migration from lower-income regions to higher-income countries in search of better opportunities.
  - Migration can become a significant diplomatic issue involving labor markets, border policies, refugee protections, and remittances.

• Security and conflict
  - Severe economic inequality, both within and between countries, can contribute to instability, political unrest, and conflict.
  - Economic grievances may influence civil wars, regional tensions, and international interventions.

• Influence within international institutions
  - Organizations such as the International Monetary Fund, World Bank, and World Trade Organization often reflect the economic weight of major economies.
  - Developing countries have frequently argued for greater representation and influence in global governance.

• Competition for resources and investment
  - Wealthier countries and multinational corporations often compete for access to strategic resources, markets, and supply chains in developing regions.
  - This can shape alliances, infrastructure projects, and geopolitical rivalries.

• South-South cooperation and emerging powers
  - Rising economies such as China, India, Brazil, and others have altered traditional patterns of influence.
  - New development banks, trade agreements, and investment initiatives provide alternatives to institutions historically dominated by Western powers.

• Global challenges
  - Economic inequality affects how countries respond to issues such as climate change, pandemics, and technological transformation.
  - Lower-income countries often argue that wealthier nations should bear a larger share of costs because of their greater resources and historical contributions to global problems.

In summary, global economic inequalities are not merely differences in wealth; they shape the distribution of power, the structure of international institutions, patterns of cooperation and conflict, and the ability of states to pursue their interests on the global stage.
