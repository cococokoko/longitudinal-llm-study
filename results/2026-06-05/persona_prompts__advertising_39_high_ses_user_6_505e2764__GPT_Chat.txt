I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

How to get started with meditation and mindfulness?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given your role, it's worth approaching meditation and mindfulness as practical skills rather than as spiritual practices or relaxation techniques. The core benefit is training attention: the ability to notice where your mind is, redirect it intentionally, and respond rather than react.

A simple way to start:

1. Begin with 5–10 minutes per day
- Sit comfortably.
- Set a timer.
- Close your eyes or soften your gaze.
- Bring attention to the sensation of breathing.
- When your mind wanders (it will), notice it and gently return attention to the breath.
- The repetition of noticing and returning is the practice.

2. Focus on consistency over duration
- Five minutes every weekday is more valuable than one 45-minute session each month.
- Tie it to an existing habit, such as:
  - Before opening email.
  - After your morning coffee.
  - Before leaving the office.

3. Treat distractions as part of the exercise
Many high-performing professionals assume they're "bad at meditation" because their minds are busy. In reality:
- Mind wanders → notice it → return attention.
- That cycle is the mental equivalent of a repetition in the gym.

4. Add informal mindfulness during the day
You don't need to be sitting on a cushion to practice.
Examples:
- Before entering a client meeting, take three conscious breaths.
- During a conversation, focus entirely on the speaker instead of planning your response.
- While walking between meetings, pay attention to physical sensations and surroundings instead of reaching for your phone.

5. Use guided apps if helpful
Popular options include:
- Waking Up
- Headspace
- Calm
- Ten Percent Happier

Many busy executives find guided instruction easier than starting alone.

6. Expect benefits gradually
Research suggests regular practice can improve:
- Attention and concentration
- Emotional regulation
- Stress management
- Self-awareness
- Interpersonal effectiveness

The changes are usually subtle at first. People often notice that they recover from distractions, pressure, or difficult interactions more quickly.

A practical executive-focused routine:
- Morning: 10 minutes of breath-focused meditation.
- During the day: three "pause points" (before a major meeting, before responding to a difficult email, before leaving work).
- Evening: 2 minutes reflecting on where attention was pulled away and where you responded skillfully.

If you're highly analytical, it can help to think of meditation as attention training and mindfulness as applying that trained attention in real-world situations. The goal is not to stop thinking. The goal is to become more aware of where your attention is and more intentional about where you place it.
