There is no single day trading strategy that can honestly be called "the best and most profitable of all time."

If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, it would eventually become crowded, market conditions would change, and its edge would likely diminish.

What the most successful professional day traders generally have in common is not a specific setup but a combination of:

- A measurable statistical edge
- Strict risk management
- Consistent execution
- Careful position sizing
- The ability to adapt to changing market conditions

Some of the most widely used profitable day-trading approaches include:

- Trend-following breakout strategies
  - Buy when price breaks above a well-defined range with strong volume.
  - Cut losses quickly if the breakout fails.
  - Works best in strongly trending markets.

- Momentum trading
  - Focus on stocks, futures, or other assets showing unusually strong movement.
  - Enter in the direction of momentum and exit quickly.
  - Common among professional intraday traders.

- Mean reversion
  - Trade temporary overextensions back toward an average price.
  - Often uses indicators such as VWAP or moving averages.
  - Can work well in range-bound markets.

- Opening range breakout
  - Use the high and low established during the first minutes of trading.
  - Enter when price breaks that range.
  - Popular because it is rule-based and easy to test.

- Market-making/scalping
  - Capture small bid-ask spreads repeatedly.
  - Requires very low costs, fast execution, and significant experience.

If your goal is maximizing long-term wealth while supporting family members and managing many responsibilities, it is worth knowing that historically the most reliable wealth-building approach has generally been:
- Consistent saving
- Diversified long-term investing
- Avoiding excessive leverage
- Letting compound growth work over years

Most retail day traders do not outperform a simple long-term index investing strategy after costs and taxes.

If you are interested in day trading anyway, a practical approach is:
1. Choose one market (stocks, futures, forex, etc.).
2. Learn one simple strategy.
3. Backtest it on several years of data.
4. Risk no more than 0.5%–1% of account equity per trade.
5. Track every trade in a journal.
6. Scale up only after demonstrating profitability over hundreds of trades.
