── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If your goal is to improve your credit score as quickly as possible, focus on the factors that can change within one or two reporting cycles:

- Lower your credit utilization
  - This is often the fastest way to see a score increase.
  - Pay down revolving credit card balances, especially cards that are above 30% utilization.
  - For the best scoring impact, aim for under 10% utilization overall and on individual cards if feasible.
  - You can also make multiple payments during the month so lower balances are reported to the credit bureaus.

- Check for reporting errors
  - Obtain your credit reports from all three major bureaus.
  - Dispute any inaccurate late payments, incorrect balances, accounts that do not belong to you, or duplicate entries.
  - Correcting an error can sometimes produce a significant score improvement.

- Make every payment on time
  - Payment history is a major scoring factor.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders to avoid missing any due dates.
  - If you are currently behind, bringing accounts current can help, although the historical late payment may still affect your score.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If approved without a hard inquiry (or if the issuer tells you the impact is minimal), a higher limit can reduce utilization immediately.
  - Avoid increasing spending simply because the limit is higher.

- Become an authorized user
  - If a trusted family member or spouse has a long-standing account with low utilization and excellent payment history, being added as an authorized user may help, depending on the scoring model and lender.

- Avoid applying for multiple new accounts
  - New applications can generate hard inquiries and reduce the average age of accounts.
  - Unless a specific strategy requires it, avoid opening unnecessary credit lines while trying to maximize your score.

- Pay off collection accounts when appropriate
  - The impact varies by scoring model.
  - Some newer scoring models ignore certain paid collections, while others still consider them.
  - If collections are present, evaluate the likely scoring effect before paying solely for score improvement.

Typical timelines:
- Utilization changes: often reflected within 30–60 days, sometimes faster.
- Error corrections: several weeks to a few months.
- Late payments and other negative marks: improvement is gradual over time, though their impact generally lessens as they age.
