Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few core variables: the cut, temperature control, seasoning, and resting.

Key principles

• Start with a good cut
  - Ribeye: rich marbling, forgiving, excellent for high-heat cooking.
  - Strip steak (New York strip): balanced tenderness and beef flavor.
  - Tenderloin/filet: extremely tender but less intensely flavored.
  - Porterhouse/T-bone: combines strip and tenderloin sections.

• Thickness matters
  - Steaks around 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) are easier to cook precisely than thin steaks.
  - Thicker steaks allow a better contrast between a browned crust and a properly cooked interior.

• Dry the surface
  - Pat the steak dry before cooking.
  - Surface moisture inhibits browning because energy is spent evaporating water rather than driving the Maillard reaction.

Seasoning

• Salt generously.
  - Salting 40 minutes to 24 hours in advance (dry brining) improves flavor and promotes a drier surface.
  - Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking, depending on preference.

Cooking methods

• Pan-searing
  - Use a heavy skillet, ideally cast iron or carbon steel.
  - Preheat until very hot.
  - Add a high-smoke-point oil.
  - Sear both sides until a deep brown crust forms.

• Reverse sear
  - Particularly effective for thick steaks.
  - Cook gently in a low oven (around 225–275°F / 107–135°C) until near target temperature.
  - Finish with a brief, intense sear in a hot pan.
  - Produces highly even doneness.

• Grilling
  - Create two heat zones: one hot, one cooler.
  - Sear over high heat, then finish over lower heat if needed.

Internal temperatures

Approximate pull temperatures (before resting):
• Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
• Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
• Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
• Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

A fast digital thermometer is one of the most valuable tools in steak preparation.

Butter basting

• After initial searing, reduce heat slightly.
• Add butter, garlic, thyme, or rosemary.
• Tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the steak repeatedly.
• This enhances flavor and promotes even surface cooking.

Resting

• Rest the steak for roughly 5–10 minutes after cooking.
• Resting allows temperature gradients within the meat to equilibrate and reduces excessive juice loss when sliced.

Common mistakes

• Cooking directly from a wet surface.
• Using a pan that is not sufficiently hot.
• Overcrowding the cooking surface.
• Relying solely on cooking time instead of temperature.
• Cutting into the steak immediately after cooking.

A useful way to think about steak cookery is similar to many procedural disciplines: the objective is controlled heat transfer. Browning develops flavor at the surface, while careful management of internal temperature determines texture and doneness. Once you can consistently control those two processes, steak preparation becomes highly reproducible.
