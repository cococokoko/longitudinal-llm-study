I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, but the relationship is modest, depends heavily on how both constructs are defined, and is not strong enough to support simple claims such as "more intelligent people are more compassionate."

A few distinctions matter:

- Intelligence is usually measured through general cognitive ability (g), IQ tests, reasoning ability, working memory, and related constructs.
- Compassion can refer to empathic concern, prosocial motivation, altruistic behavior, willingness to help, or broader moral concern.
- Empathy itself is often divided into:
  - Cognitive empathy: understanding another person's mental state.
  - Affective empathy: sharing or resonating with another person's emotions.

What the literature generally suggests:

- Intelligence shows a small positive association with cognitive empathy. People with higher cognitive ability are, on average, somewhat better at understanding others' perspectives and mental states.
- Intelligence has a weak and inconsistent relationship with affective empathy or compassionate concern. Being good at understanding another person's feelings does not necessarily mean caring more about them.
- Some studies find that higher intelligence is associated with greater prosocial behavior, cooperation, or concern for broader social welfare, but these effects are typically small and mediated by factors such as education, personality, socioeconomic environment, and culture.
- Personality traits, especially agreeableness, compassion, and honesty-humility, are generally much stronger predictors of compassionate behavior than intelligence.
- Exceptionally intelligent individuals can be highly compassionate, indifferent, exploitative, or anything in between. Cognitive ability does not determine moral character.

From a neuroscience perspective:

- The neural systems supporting reasoning and executive function (often associated with frontoparietal networks) overlap only partially with systems involved in empathy, social cognition, and emotional processing (including medial prefrontal, temporoparietal, anterior insular, and limbic structures).
- Damage or dysfunction can dissociate these capacities. One can observe individuals with preserved intelligence but impaired empathy, and vice versa.

If your question is whether there is a measurable population-level correlation, the best summary is:
- Intelligence and compassion are positively correlated in some studies, especially when compassion is operationalized as perspective-taking or prosocial reasoning.
- The correlation is generally small.
- Personality, upbringing, social norms, and moral development contribute far more to compassionate behavior than intelligence alone.
