── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no objectively "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

The highest-return trading approaches in history have generally shared a few characteristics:

- They exploited a specific market inefficiency.
- They worked for a period of time, sometimes spectacularly.
- They attracted competition.
- Their edge diminished or disappeared.

Examples include:

- Early statistical arbitrage (1980s–1990s)
  - Firms such as Renaissance Technologies and others identified short-term pricing anomalies across large numbers of securities.
  - Historically among the most profitable trading approaches ever developed.
  - Not realistically replicable by an individual trader today.

- High-frequency market making
  - Capturing bid-ask spreads while managing inventory risk.
  - Extremely profitable for some firms with technological advantages.
  - Requires infrastructure, capital, and expertise beyond ordinary day traders.

- Momentum trading
  - Buying assets that are already moving strongly upward and shorting those moving strongly downward.
  - One of the most persistent and well-documented market anomalies.
  - Can be implemented intraday, but risk management is critical because reversals can be severe.

- News and event-driven trading
  - Trading around earnings releases, economic data, mergers, or other catalysts.
  - Can generate large returns when information is processed faster or more accurately than the market.
  - Increasingly competitive due to algorithmic trading.

For a discretionary human day trader today, the closest thing to a durable edge is often:

- Trading highly liquid instruments.
- Focusing on momentum following a clear catalyst (earnings, guidance, economic news, sector news).
- Strict risk management.
- Taking many small gains while avoiding large losses.
- Maintaining statistical discipline and tracking performance.

A useful way to think about profitability is:

Expected Profit = Win Rate × Average Win − Loss Rate × Average Loss

Many successful traders have:
- Win rates below 50%.
- Large average winners relative to average losers.
- Strong risk controls.

The strategies with the greatest historical profits were generally quantitative, systematic, and executed at institutional scale. The strategies most heavily marketed to retail traders are rarely the ones that generated the largest fortunes.
