── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given your background, it may help to approach cooking as a skill-acquisition and systems-design problem rather than as a culinary hobby.

The goal is not to become a great cook. The goal is to reliably produce healthy, enjoyable meals with minimal cognitive load and time.

A practical approach:

• Learn a small number of high-yield techniques
  - Roasting vegetables
  - Pan-searing proteins
  - Making large salads
  - Stir-frying
  - Cooking grains (rice, quinoa, farro)
  - Simple soups

Mastering these 5–6 techniques covers a large percentage of healthy home cooking.

• Use the "protein + vegetable + carbohydrate" framework
  - Protein: fish, chicken, tofu, beans, lentils, eggs
  - Vegetables: fresh or frozen
  - Carbohydrate: potatoes, rice, whole grains, pasta

For example:
  - Salmon + roasted broccoli + potatoes
  - Tofu stir-fry + rice
  - Chicken + salad + quinoa

This reduces decision fatigue.

• Build a repertoire of 10 meals
  - Instead of learning hundreds of recipes, learn 10 healthy meals you can prepare in 15–30 minutes.
  - Repeat them until they become automatic.

Examples:
  - Vegetable omelet
  - Sheet-pan chicken and vegetables
  - Lentil soup
  - Salmon with roasted vegetables
  - Turkey chili
  - Tofu stir-fry
  - Greek salad with chickpeas
  - Shrimp and vegetables over rice
  - Bean tacos
  - Whole-grain pasta with vegetables and protein

• Leverage modern conveniences
  - Frozen vegetables are nutritionally excellent and save time.
  - Pre-washed greens reduce friction.
  - Canned beans are highly useful.
  - Rotisserie chicken can be a healthy component of meals.
  - Rice cookers, pressure cookers, and air fryers can significantly reduce active cooking time.

• Batch-prep ingredients, not necessarily meals
  - Cook a large batch of grains once or twice weekly.
  - Roast several trays of vegetables.
  - Prepare proteins in bulk.
  - Store separately and mix throughout the week.

Many people find this more sustainable than preparing identical meals.

• Apply deliberate practice
  - Pick one technique each week.
  - Cook the same dish multiple times.
  - Make small adjustments and observe results.

This is similar to developing procedural skills in medicine: repetition with feedback is more effective than constantly trying new tasks.

• Learn from high-quality educational sources
  - J. Kenji López-Alt (technique-focused)
  - America's Test Kitchen
  - New York Times Cooking
  - Serious Eats
  - Budget Bytes (efficient, practical meals)

Focus on understanding principles rather than memorizing recipes.

• Track a few key metrics
  - Active cooking time
  - Cost per meal
  - Protein content
  - Vegetable servings
  - Personal enjoyment

Optimization becomes easier when outcomes are measurable.

A particularly efficient strategy for a busy physician-scientist is to dedicate one weekend hour to preparing staple ingredients and then assemble 10–15 minute meals during the workweek. This often provides most of the health benefits of home cooking without requiring substantial daily effort.
