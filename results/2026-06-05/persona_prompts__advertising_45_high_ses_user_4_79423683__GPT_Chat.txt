── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many of the skills that transfer well to DIY: structured learning, troubleshooting, risk assessment, and comfort with technical information. The main challenge is developing practical experience and judgment.

A good approach is to treat home repair as a combination of theory, observation, and progressively more complex practice.

1. Learn the systems of a house

Start by understanding the major categories:

- Structure: framing, foundations, roofs
- Plumbing: supply lines, drains, fixtures
- Electrical: circuits, breakers, outlets, lighting
- HVAC: heating, cooling, ventilation
- Interior finishes: drywall, paint, flooring, trim
- Exterior: siding, windows, doors, drainage

Recommended resources:
- "The Visual Handbook of Building and Remodeling" by Charlie Wing
- "Renovation" by Michael Litchfield
- Family Handyman
- This Old House (website and YouTube)
- Fine Homebuilding

Focus first on understanding how things work before learning repairs.

2. Build a core tool set

Begin with:
- Tape measure
- Utility knife
- Screwdriver set
- Drill/driver
- Hammer
- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Stud finder
- Level
- Flashlight or headlamp

Avoid buying specialized tools until a project requires them.

3. Start with low-risk projects

Good beginner projects:
- Replacing faucets and showerheads
- Installing shelves
- Patching drywall holes
- Caulking bathrooms and kitchens
- Replacing light fixtures (after learning electrical safety)
- Painting rooms
- Replacing door hardware

Each project teaches transferable skills.

4. Learn through project-based YouTube channels

Particularly useful:
- This Old House
- Home Renovision DIY
- Essential Craftsman
- See Jane Drill
- HouseImprovements

Watch multiple videos on the same task because techniques vary.

5. Understand risk categories

DIY is not all-or-nothing.

Usually reasonable for homeowners:
- Painting
- Drywall repair
- Basic carpentry
- Fixture replacement
- Landscaping
- Simple plumbing repairs

Use greater caution with:
- Major electrical work
- Structural modifications
- Roofing
- Gas lines
- Foundation work

In many jurisdictions permits and licensed professionals are required for some work.

6. Practice inspection and diagnosis

One of the most valuable skills is learning to identify:
- Water intrusion
- Drainage problems
- Electrical hazards
- Structural movement
- Poor workmanship

Whenever you visit homes, clinics, offices, or laboratories, observe how systems are built and maintained. Experienced DIYers often spend more time diagnosing than repairing.

7. Volunteer for real projects

Hands-on experience accelerates learning.

Options include:
- Habitat for Humanity
- Community repair projects
- Helping friends with renovations
- Workshops at hardware stores

Working alongside skilled tradespeople can compress years of learning into months.

8. Keep a project notebook

For each project:
- Problem
- Diagnosis
- Materials used
- Cost
- Time required
- What worked
- What you would do differently

This mirrors a scientific workflow and builds a personal knowledge base.

9. Learn to estimate difficulty before starting

Before attempting any project, ask:
- What happens if I make a mistake?
- Is the consequence cosmetic, expensive, or dangerous?
- Do I understand the underlying system?
- Do I have a way to reverse an error?
- Is there a permit requirement?

This decision framework prevents many costly mistakes.

10. Consider a structured curriculum

If you want a more systematic education:
- Community-college courses in carpentry, electrical, plumbing, or home maintenance
- Adult education workshops
- Maker spaces with woodworking classes

Many physician-scientists enjoy woodworking because it develops measurement, tool handling, planning, and material intuition that transfer directly to home improvement.

A practical one-year goal would be:
- Read one foundational home-construction book.
- Complete 10 small household projects.
- Learn basic electrical and plumbing safety.
- Build one simple woodworking project.
- Assist on one larger renovation project.

By the end of that process, you'll usually have enough confidence and judgment to handle a substantial fraction of routine home repairs yourself while knowing when a professional is the better choice.
