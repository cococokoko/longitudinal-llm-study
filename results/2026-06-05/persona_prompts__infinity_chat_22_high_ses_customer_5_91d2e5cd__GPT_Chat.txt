Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and learning to live according to his teachings.

Some major perspectives include:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus and ongoing spiritual growth.
  - Discipleship often involves Bible study, prayer, mentorship, evangelism, and participation in a church community.
  - Focuses on helping believers become more like Christ and make new disciples.

• Roman Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as a lifelong journey of following Christ within the Church.
  - Strong emphasis on the sacraments, prayer, moral formation, service, and participation in the life of the Church.
  - Saints are often viewed as models of mature discipleship.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Understands discipleship as a process of transformation and union with God (often called theosis or deification).
  - Emphasizes worship, spiritual disciplines, prayer, fasting, and participation in the sacramental life of the Church.
  - Spiritual guidance from experienced clergy or monastics can play an important role.

• Mainline Protestant view
  - Often emphasizes both personal faith and social responsibility.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual formation, worship, service, justice, and engagement with society.
  - May place significant focus on living out Christian values in public life.

• Anabaptist view (e.g., Mennonite traditions)
  - Stresses obedience to Jesus’ teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Discipleship is demonstrated through community, peacemaking, simplicity, and ethical living.
  - Following Christ is often seen as requiring visible lifestyle commitments.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in the believer’s life.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual growth, prayer, worship, evangelism, and the exercise of spiritual gifts.
  - Personal transformation and active ministry are often central themes.

• Liberation Theology perspective
  - Common in some Latin American and other contexts.
  - Focuses on following Jesus through solidarity with the poor and oppressed.
  - Discipleship includes addressing social injustice and working for human dignity.

• Radical discipleship movements
  - Emphasize costly commitment to Jesus, sometimes involving intentional community, simplicity, and sacrificial service.
  - Often draw on passages where Jesus calls followers to leave behind competing loyalties.

Key areas of disagreement or emphasis include:
• Whether discipleship is primarily about personal spiritual growth, participation in the Church, social action, or some combination.
• The role of sacraments and church authority.
• The relationship between faith and works.
• The extent to which discipleship requires countercultural or sacrificial living.
• The balance between evangelism and social engagement.

Despite these differences, most Christian traditions view discipleship as more than belief alone; it involves a process of learning, transformation, and faithful practice.
