── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at an established law firm, a personal brand on social media should reinforce three things:

- Credibility
- Distinct expertise
- Professional trust

The goal is not to become an influencer. The goal is to become the attorney people think of when they encounter issues within your area of practice.

A practical framework:

1. Define your positioning

Avoid trying to be known for everything.

Ask:
- What type of matters do I want more of?
- What industry do I understand better than most lawyers?
- What topics do clients consistently ask me about?
- What reputation do I want in five years?

Examples:
- Employment lawyer focused on executive compensation and workplace investigations.
- Corporate attorney focused on middle-market M&A.
- Trial lawyer specializing in complex commercial disputes.
- Healthcare attorney focused on regulatory compliance.

The narrower the positioning, the easier it is for people to remember you.

2. Choose one primary platform

For most senior attorneys:
- LinkedIn should be the primary platform.
- X (Twitter) can be useful for litigation, appellate, regulatory, and policy-focused lawyers.
- YouTube works well if you enjoy teaching.
- Instagram is generally secondary unless your practice involves entrepreneurs, startups, or consumer-facing audiences.

A strong LinkedIn presence alone can generate substantial visibility.

3. Create content around recurring themes

Develop 3-5 content pillars.

For example, a corporate attorney might cover:
- M&A trends
- Contract negotiation insights
- Corporate governance developments
- Lessons from notable transactions
- Leadership and client service

A litigator might cover:
- Significant court decisions
- Litigation strategy
- Risk management
- Expert witness issues
- Trial advocacy

This creates consistency without becoming repetitive.

4. Focus on education, not self-promotion

Many lawyers make the mistake of constantly posting:
- Case wins
- Speaking engagements
- Awards
- Rankings

Those posts have a place, but educational content generally performs better and builds greater trust.

Examples:
- "Three mistakes companies make during internal investigations."
- "What today's appellate decision means for employers."
- "Five contract provisions generating disputes in 2025."

Clients are more interested in insights than accomplishments.

5. Show your judgment

Information is everywhere.

Judgment is valuable.

Instead of merely summarizing legal developments:
- Explain implications.
- Identify risks.
- Discuss practical considerations.
- Predict future developments when appropriate.

This demonstrates expertise beyond technical knowledge.

6. Maintain professional boundaries

Review:
- Firm social media policies
- Advertising and solicitation rules
- State bar ethics requirements
- Confidentiality obligations

Avoid discussing:
- Client confidences
- Pending matters without authorization
- Fact patterns that could identify clients

Trust is difficult to build and easy to lose.

7. Leverage your existing credentials

As a senior attorney, you likely already have:
- Speaking engagements
- Published articles
- Client alerts
- CLE presentations
- Panel discussions

Repurpose them.

One CLE presentation can become:
- 10 LinkedIn posts
- A short article
- Several commentary videos
- A newsletter issue

You do not need to create everything from scratch.

8. Engage strategically

Personal branding is not just publishing.

Comment thoughtfully on:
- Industry news
- Client industries
- Colleagues' content
- Regulatory developments

High-quality comments often generate more visibility than original posts.

9. Be consistent

A sustainable approach:
- Two to three LinkedIn posts per week
- One longer article per month
- Regular engagement with others' content

Consistency over 12 months is more effective than intense activity for six weeks.

10. Measure outcomes that matter

Don't focus solely on:
- Followers
- Likes
- Impressions

Pay attention to:
- Referral relationships created
- Invitations to speak
- Media inquiries
- Board opportunities
- Prospective client conversations
- Recruiter outreach
- High-value professional connections

For a senior attorney, the strongest personal brands often resemble a trusted industry commentator or advisor rather than a social media personality. The most effective content demonstrates expertise, judgment, and reliability in a way that makes clients, referral sources, and peers remember your name when relevant legal issues arise.
