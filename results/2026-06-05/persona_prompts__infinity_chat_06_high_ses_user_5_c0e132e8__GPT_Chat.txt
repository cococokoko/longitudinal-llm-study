── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At an executive level, "understanding what you want" is often harder than making decisions. Many senior leaders become exceptionally skilled at pursuing goals, solving problems, and meeting expectations, but spend relatively little time examining whether the goals themselves are truly their own.

A useful distinction is:

- What do I want to achieve?
- What do I want to experience?
- Who do I want to become?

Many executives spend almost all their attention on the first question.

Some approaches that can help:

- Observe your recurring choices.
  - Ignore what you say you want for a moment.
  - Look at your calendar, spending, attention, and sacrifices over the last 12–24 months.
  - What have you consistently chosen when there was a tradeoff?
  - Your behavior often reveals desires that your conscious narrative obscures.

- Separate desire from obligation.
  - Make three lists:
    - Things I genuinely want.
    - Things I want because others expect them.
    - Things I pursue because they once mattered and I haven't reevaluated them.
  - Many people discover that a significant portion of their effort is devoted to the second and third categories.

- Pay attention to envy.
  - Envy is often an informative signal.
  - When you feel jealous of someone, ask: "What specifically do I wish I had?"
  - It may not be their status. It may be their freedom, influence, creativity, family life, pace, or sense of purpose.

- Imagine success has already occurred.
  - Suppose every conventional marker of success is secured: compensation, prestige, recognition, financial security.
  - What problem would you work on then?
  - How would you spend a Tuesday?
  - What would remain important?

- Distinguish goals from values.
  - Goals are outcomes.
  - Values are qualities of living.
  - Examples:
    - Goal: become CEO.
    - Value: build consequential things.
    - Goal: accumulate wealth.
    - Value: autonomy.
  - Goals can change. Values tend to be more stable.

- Examine moments of aliveness.
  - Over the past year, when did you feel most energized?
  - When did time pass quickly?
  - What conversations did you keep thinking about afterward?
  - Desire often announces itself through energy before it becomes a coherent idea.

- Consider the cost of wanting.
  - Every desire has a price.
  - Don't ask only "Do I want this?"
  - Ask "Do I want the life required to obtain and maintain this?"
  - Many ambitions become less appealing when their ongoing costs are made explicit.

One question I have found particularly revealing for senior leaders is:

"If nobody would ever know what I chose—not my board, peers, family, industry, or biography—what would I do?"

That question removes status rewards from the equation and often exposes preferences that have been buried beneath expectations.

To understand what you want, treat yourself less as a strategist and more as an observer. Instead of deciding immediately, gather evidence from your behavior, emotions, attention, and imagination. Desire often emerges from patterns that are already present rather than from a single act of introspection.
