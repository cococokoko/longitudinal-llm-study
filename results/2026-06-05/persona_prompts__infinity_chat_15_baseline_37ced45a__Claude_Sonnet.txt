── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately influence international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO)
- This shapes rules that often reflect their interests, sometimes constraining poorer nations' policy options
- Dollar dominance gives the US leverage others lack

**Dependency relationships**
- Trade relationships can create genuine mutual benefit but also vulnerability
- Debt relationships create political leverage, as seen with both Western creditors and Chinese infrastructure lending
- Resource-dependent economies face particular exposure to external pressure

## Genuine Tensions in the Analysis

**Where scholars disagree:**
- Whether inequality primarily causes conflict or cooperation (both have evidence)
- Whether development aid helps or creates dependency
- Whether rising powers like China represent system reform or replacement
- How much inequality actually explains versus other factors like ideology or geography

## Practical Consequences

- Poorer nations have less capacity to resist sanctions or coercion
- Climate negotiations repeatedly fracture along wealth lines
- Migration pressure partly reflects economic disparities
- Security alliances often involve implicit economic bargains

## Worth Being Honest About

The relationship isn't simply "rich exploits poor" - some poor nations successfully leverage their strategic position, and some wealthy nations face significant constraints.

What specific dimension interests you most - trade, institutions, security, or something else?
