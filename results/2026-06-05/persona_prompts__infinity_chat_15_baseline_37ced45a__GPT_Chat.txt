── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities shape international relations in many ways because differences in wealth, resources, and economic power influence how states interact, cooperate, and compete.

Some of the main effects include:

• Power and influence
  - Wealthier countries often have greater influence in international organizations such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank, and World Trade Organization (WTO).
  - Economic strength can be used to shape global rules, standards, and policies that affect trade, finance, and development.

• Trade relationships
  - Many developing countries depend heavily on exporting raw materials or low-cost manufactured goods, while wealthier countries often control higher-value industries and technologies.
  - This can create unequal trade relationships in which poorer countries receive a smaller share of global economic benefits.

• Development and aid
  - Rich countries and international institutions provide loans, grants, and development assistance to poorer countries.
  - Aid can support economic growth and public services, but it may also create dependencies or give donor countries political influence.

• Debt and financial dependence
  - Many lower-income countries carry significant external debt.
  - Debt obligations can limit policy choices and increase reliance on international lenders, affecting domestic and foreign policy decisions.

• Migration
  - Economic disparities encourage migration from poorer regions to wealthier ones in search of jobs and better living conditions.
  - Migration influences diplomatic relations, labor markets, border policies, and debates over immigration.

• Conflict and instability
  - Severe economic inequality between and within countries can contribute to social unrest, political instability, and competition over resources.
  - Economic hardship may increase the risk of conflict, which can have regional and global consequences.

• Global governance and representation
  - Developing countries often argue that international institutions do not adequately reflect their interests.
  - Calls for reform frequently focus on giving emerging and lower-income countries greater representation in decision-making bodies.

• Geopolitical competition
  - Major powers use investment, trade agreements, infrastructure projects, and financial assistance to strengthen their international influence.
  - For example, competition over development financing and access to markets can become a tool of geopolitical strategy.

• Climate change and sustainability
  - Economic inequalities affect negotiations on climate policy.
  - Developing countries often argue that wealthier nations, having contributed more to historical emissions, should bear a larger share of the costs of mitigation and adaptation.

Overall, global economic inequalities create asymmetries in power, opportunities, and influence. These inequalities shape diplomatic relations, trade patterns, international institutions, migration flows, and global efforts to address shared challenges.
