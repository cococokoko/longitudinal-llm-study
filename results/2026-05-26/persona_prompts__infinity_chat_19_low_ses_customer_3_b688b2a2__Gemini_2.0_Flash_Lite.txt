**Top Recommendation (Beginner-Friendly & Practical):**

1.  **"Introduction to Algorithms" (CLRS) by Thomas H. Cormen, Charles E. Leiserson, Ronald L. Rivest, and Clifford Stein (often called "CLRS" or "The Big Book")**

    *   **Why it's good:** This is the gold standard. It's comprehensive, well-structured, and covers a vast range of algorithms and data structures. It's a great reference.
    *   **Considerations:** It's a large book. You don't need to read it cover-to-cover. Focus on the core topics (see below). It can be dense at times, so you might need to supplement with online resources.
    *   **Focus Areas (for your situation):**
        *   **Part I: Foundations:** (Chapters 1-5) Asymptotic notation (Big O, Big Omega, Big Theta), basic data structures (arrays, linked lists, stacks, queues), and recursion.
        *   **Part II: Sorting and Order Statistics:** (Chapters 6-9) Heapsort, Quicksort, Merge sort, and related concepts.
        *   **Part III: Data Structures:** (Chapters 10-12) Hash tables, binary search trees, and red-black trees (if time permits).
        *   **Part IV: Graph Algorithms:** (Chapters 22-25) Breadth-first search (BFS), Depth-first search (DFS), topological sort, minimum spanning trees (Prim's and Kruskal's), and shortest paths (Dijkstra's and Bellman-Ford).

**Alternative/Supplementary Books (More Focused or Easier to Digest):**

2.  **"Algorithms" by Robert Sedgewick and Kevin Wayne**

    *   **Why it's good:** This book is known for its clear explanations, practical examples, and focus on implementation. It's available in Java and Python versions. It's a great choice if you prefer a more hands-on approach.
    *   **Considerations:** It might not be as mathematically rigorous as CLRS, but it's excellent for understanding the core concepts and how to implement them.
    *   **Focus Areas:** Similar to CLRS, but with a stronger emphasis on implementation.

3.  **"Algorithm Design Manual" by Steven S. Skiena**

    *   **Why it's good:** This book is known for its practical approach and focus on problem-solving. It includes a "war stories" section with real-world examples. It's a good choice if you want to learn how to apply algorithms to solve problems.
    *   **Considerations:** It's less theoretical than CLRS, but it's still a valuable resource.
    *   **Focus Areas:** Algorithm design techniques (divide and conquer, dynamic programming, greedy algorithms), graph algorithms, and NP-completeness.
