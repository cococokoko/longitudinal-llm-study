**Objective Morality:**

*   **Core Idea:** Moral truths exist independently of individual opinions or cultural norms. They are universal and unchanging. Think of it like the laws of physics – they are true regardless of what anyone believes.
*   **Arguments for:**
    *   **Common Moral Ground:** Many people across different cultures share fundamental moral intuitions (e.g., the wrongness of murder, the importance of honesty). This suggests a deeper, objective basis.
    *   **Moral Progress:** We often talk about moral progress (e.g., the abolition of slavery, the expansion of human rights). This implies a standard to which we are progressing, a standard that is not simply based on subjective preference.
    *   **Foundation for Justice:** Objective morality provides a basis for legal systems and concepts of justice. If morality is purely subjective, it's difficult to argue that one person's actions are truly "wrong" if they don't align with someone else's feelings.
*   **Challenges:**
    *   **Identifying Objective Truths:** Where do these objective moral truths come from? Is it a divine source, reason, or something else? This is a major point of contention.
    *   **Cultural Differences:** The existence of diverse moral codes across cultures seems to contradict the idea of universal truths.
    *   **Proving Objectivity:** It's difficult, if not impossible, to empirically prove the existence of objective moral truths.

**Subjective Morality:**

*   **Core Idea:** Moral judgments are based on individual feelings, beliefs, and experiences. Morality is relative to the individual or the culture.
*   **Arguments for:**
    *   **Diversity of Opinions:** People have vastly different moral views, and these views often seem to be shaped by their upbringing, culture, and personal experiences.
    *   **Evolutionary Perspective:** Morality may have evolved to promote social cohesion and survival, rather than reflecting objective truths.
    *   **Tolerance and Respect:** Subjectivism can promote tolerance and understanding, as it acknowledges that different people may have different valid moral perspectives.
*   **Challenges:**
    *   **Moral Relativism:** If all moral views are equally valid, it becomes difficult to condemn actions like genocide or torture.
    *   **Lack of Moral Progress:** If morality is purely subjective, it's hard to explain how we can make moral progress.
    *   **Social Chaos:** Without some shared moral framework, it could be difficult to maintain a functioning society.

**A Middle Ground (Possible Perspectives):**

*   **Moral Realism:** This is a nuanced view that suggests that moral truths exist, but they may be complex and difficult to fully grasp.
*   **Moral Constructivism:** This view suggests that moral truths are constructed through social processes, such as rational deliberation and agreement.
*   **Virtue Ethics:** This approach focuses on developing virtuous character traits, rather than adhering to strict rules. It emphasizes the importance of context and judgment.
