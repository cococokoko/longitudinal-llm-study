**1. Focus on the Biggest Impact First (and Fastest):**

*   **Pay Down Credit Card Debt (High Priority):** This is the single biggest factor impacting your score.
    *   **Prioritize High-Interest Cards:** Focus on paying down the cards with the highest interest rates first. This saves you money in the long run.
    *   **Small, Consistent Payments:** Even small, consistent payments above the minimum can make a difference. Aim to pay more than the minimum whenever possible.
    *   **Balance Transfer (If Possible):** If you have good credit (even if not *great*), consider a balance transfer to a card with a 0% introductory APR. This can give you breathing room to pay down the debt without accruing interest. **Important:** Be aware of balance transfer fees (usually 3-5% of the transferred amount) and the interest rate after the introductory period.
    *   **Debt Management Plan (If Needed):** If you're overwhelmed, consider a non-profit credit counseling agency. They can help you create a debt management plan, which may involve lower interest rates and a structured repayment schedule. This can negatively impact your credit score initially, but can improve it over time.

*   **On-Time Payments (Critical):** Payment history is the most significant factor in your credit score.
    *   **Set Up Automatic Payments:** This is the easiest way to ensure you never miss a payment.
    *   **Calendar Reminders:** If you can't automate, set up multiple reminders (phone, email, etc.) a few days *before* the due date.
    *   **Prioritize Bills:** If you're struggling, prioritize paying credit card bills, mortgage/rent, and utilities on time. Late payments on these can severely damage your score.

**2. Strategies for Moderate Impact (and Speed):**

*   **Keep Credit Utilization Low:** This is the percentage of your available credit that you're using.
    *   **Aim for Under 30%:** Ideally, keep your credit utilization below 30% on each card and overall. The lower, the better.
    *   **Example:** If you have a credit card with a $1,000 limit, try to keep your balance below $300.
    *   **Request a Credit Limit Increase:** If you're consistently using a high percentage of your credit, consider requesting a credit limit increase from your card issuer. This can lower your utilization ratio without you spending more. Be aware that this can sometimes trigger a hard inquiry on your credit report, which can slightly lower your score temporarily.
    *   **Make Mid-Cycle Payments:** If you can't pay down the balance significantly, making a payment before your statement closing date can lower your reported utilization.

*   **Become an Authorized User (If Possible):** If a trusted family member has a credit card in good standing, ask to be added as an authorized user. Their positive payment history can be added to your credit report. This is a quick way to build credit, but it depends on the other person's responsible credit behavior.

**3. Strategies That Take Longer (But Are Still Important):**

*   **Check Your Credit Report Regularly:**
    *   **AnnualCreditReport.com:** Get a free copy of your credit report from each of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) annually.
    *   **Look for Errors:** Dispute any errors you find (incorrect accounts, inaccurate balances, etc.). Errors can negatively impact your score.
    *   **Monitor for Fraud:** Check for any unauthorized accounts or activity.

*   **Avoid Opening New Credit Accounts (Unless Necessary):** Opening multiple new accounts in a short period can lower your score. Only open new accounts if you truly need them.

*   **Build a Positive Credit History:**
    *   **Secured Credit Card:** If you have no credit or bad credit, a secured credit card can be a good option. You provide a security deposit, which becomes your credit limit. Use it responsibly and pay on time.
    *   **Credit Builder Loan:** These loans are designed to help you build credit. You make payments on the loan, and the lender reports your payment history to the credit bureaus.
