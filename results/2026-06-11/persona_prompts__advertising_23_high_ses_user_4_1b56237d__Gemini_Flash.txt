── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, your professional life is defined by high cognitive load, unpredictable schedules, and a need for efficiency. To succeed in the kitchen, you should approach cooking not as an art or a chore, but as a **system to be optimized**. 

By applying the principles of clinical workflow efficiency and experimental design to your kitchen, you can prepare healthy, delicious meals in under 15 minutes of active time.

Here is your protocol for high-efficiency, healthy cooking.

---

### Phase 1: System Design (The "Lab" Setup)

Just as a well-organized lab reduces experimental error and protocol time, a structured kitchen minimizes friction.

1. **The "Mise en Place" Workflow:**
   * **The Trash Bowl:** Keep a large bowl on your counter while prepping. Toss all scraps (onion skins, vegetable ends) into it. This eliminates constant trips to the trash can and keeps your workspace clear.
   * **Sharp Knives:** A dull knife is slow and dangerous. Invest in one high-quality 8-inch chef’s knife and keep it sharp. It reduces prep time by half.
2. **The "Cold Chain" (Smart Grocery Strategy):**
   * **Pre-prepped aromatics:** Buy frozen minced garlic, frozen ginger cubes, and chopped frozen onions. They retain their nutritional value and eliminate 70% of prep work.
   * **Pre-washed greens:** Keep tubs of baby spinach, arugula, or kale on hand. They require zero washing or chopping and can be thrown into almost any dish.

---

### Phase 2: The "Modular" Cooking Framework

Do not cook recipes; cook **modules**. A healthy meal consists of three macronutrient components. If you keep these prepped or quick-to-cook, you can assemble a meal in 5 minutes.

$$\text{Meal} = \text{Protein} + \text{Fiber (Vegetable)} + \text{Complex Carbohydrate} + \text{Flavor Vehicle}$$

#### 1. Proteins (The Rate-Limiting Step)
* **Ultra-Fast (0–5 mins):** Canned wild salmon/sardines, eggs/egg whites, organic tofu (cubed and pan-seared), or rotisserie chicken (skin removed).
* **Fast (5–10 mins):** Shrimp (thaws in 5 minutes in cold water, cooks in 3), thin-cut chicken cutlets, or ground turkey/beef.
* **Passive (Set-and-Forget):** Use an Instant Pot or slow cooker on Sundays to cook chicken breasts in salsa or bone broth. Shred and store.

#### 2. Vegetables (The Micronutrient Base)
* **The Sheet Pan Roast:** Once a week, chop broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower, and carrots. Toss in olive oil, salt, and pepper. Roast at 400°F (200°C) for 25 minutes. Store in a large container.
* **The Steam-in-Bag:** Keep frozen broccoli florets, green beans, and edamame in the freezer. They microwave in 3 minutes and are nutritionally identical (often superior) to fresh produce.

#### 3. Carbohydrates (The Energy Source)
* **90-Second Grains:** Keep pouches of pre-cooked quinoa, brown rice, or farro in your pantry. 
* **Legumes:** Canned black beans, chickpeas, and lentils. Rinse and they are ready to eat.

#### 4. Flavor Vehicles (The Catalyst)
Keep a "flavor drawer" in your fridge to instantly elevate bland food:
* Pesto (healthy fats + herbs)
* Kimchi or Sauerkraut (probiotics + acidity)
* Sriracha, Gochujang, or Dijon mustard (low calorie, high flavor)
* Extra virgin olive oil and lemons (for finishing)

### Phase 3: Three "Go-To" 10-Minute Protocols

Here are three highly adaptable, nutritionally dense templates.

#### Protocol A: The "Sheet Pan" (Passive Cooking)
* **Active Time:** 5 minutes | **Passive Time:** 20 minutes
* **Execution:** Place a salmon fillet and a pile of asparagus/broccoli on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper (for zero cleanup). Drizzle with olive oil, salt, pepper, and garlic powder. Bake at 400°F (200°C) for 12–15 minutes. 
* **Nutritional Profile:** High Omega-3s, high fiber, low glycemic index.

#### Protocol B: The "Scientific Stir-Fry"
* **Active Time:** 8 minutes | **Passive Time:** 0 minutes
* **Execution:** Heat avocado oil in a skillet. Toss in pre-chopped frozen stir-fry veggies and frozen edamame. Add a quick-cooking protein (shrimp or cubed tofu). Splash with low-sodium tamari (soy sauce), sesame oil, and ginger. Serve over a 90-second microwave brown rice pouch.
* **Nutritional Profile:** High protein, high fiber, rich in phytonutrients.

#### Protocol C: The "Mediterranean Power Bowl" (No-Cook)
* **Active Time:** 5 minutes | **Passive Time:** 0 minutes
* **Execution:** In a bowl, combine canned chickpeas (rinsed), canned wild tuna, a handful of baby spinach, cherry tomatoes, and kalamata olives. Dress with olive oil, lemon juice, and dried oregano.
* **Nutritional Profile:** High fiber, lean protein, heart-healthy monounsaturated fats.

### Phase 4: Cognitive Offloading (The Habits)

As a scientist, you know that decision fatigue drains willpower. Eliminate decisions around food.

* **Batching (Parallel Processing):** Never cook one portion. If you are pan-searing chicken or boiling quinoa, cook 3–4 portions. The marginal time to cook four chicken breasts instead of one is near zero, but it saves you three future cooking cycles.
* **The "Uniform" Breakfast/Lunch:** Reduce your cognitive load by eating the same healthy breakfast and lunch Monday through Friday (e.g., chia seed pudding with berries for breakfast; a modular power bowl for lunch). Save your culinary variety for dinners and weekends.
* **Outsource the Logistics:** Use grocery delivery services (like Instacart or Amazon Fresh) on a recurring weekly schedule. Set up a "template cart" of your staple proteins, frozen veggies, and grains so ordering takes 2 minutes.

### Summary for the Physician-Scientist:
Treat your kitchen like a wet lab. Standardize your reagents (staple ingredients), optimize your protocols (10-minute recipes), minimize cleanup (one-pan meals), and run your processes in parallel (batch cooking).
