# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a function representing **intracranial pressure** (a familiar concept) modeled as:

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2y + 2xy^3$$

where **x = 2** and **y = 3**.

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### With Respect to x (holding y constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6xy + 2y^3$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 6(2)(3) + 2(3)^3 = 36 + 54 = \boxed{90}$$

> **Interpretation:** If y stays fixed at 3, the function increases at a rate of **90 units per unit increase in x**.

---

### With Respect to y (holding x constant)

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x^2 + 6xy^2$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 3(2)^2 + 6(2)(3)^2 = 12 + 108 = \boxed{120}$$

> **Interpretation:** If x stays fixed at 2, the function increases at a rate of **120 units per unit increase in y**.

---

## Key Insight

| Derivative | Value | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x | 90 | Rate of change in **x-direction only** |
| ∂f/∂y | 120 | Rate of change in **y-direction only** |

The partial derivative isolates **one variable's contribution** to change — analogous to asking *"how does outcome change if I alter only this one parameter?"* — much like isolating a single variable in a multivariate clinical scenario.
