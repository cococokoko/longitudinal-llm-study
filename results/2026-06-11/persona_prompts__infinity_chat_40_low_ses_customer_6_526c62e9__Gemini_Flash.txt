── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common, yet highly sensitive situation. When you are transitioning from "dating" to "buying a house" to "getting married," the lines between individual finances and partnership can get very blurry. 

Since you work long hours in a physically demanding warehouse job, your time and hard-earned money are incredibly valuable. You want to make sure that whatever you contribute is fair, protects your hard work, and supports your future wife without leaving you feeling taken advantage of.

Here is a breakdown of how to look at this situation and the best solutions to handle it fairly.

---

### The Golden Rule: Protect Yourself Before Marriage
Until you are legally married, **you should not put money into a major asset (like a down payment) unless your name is on the deed.** 

If she is buying the house solely in her name, with her own down payment, it is legally *her* house. If you break up before the wedding, any money you put toward the down payment or principal mortgage payments could be legally viewed as a "gift" or "rent," and you may never see it again.

---

### 3 Fair Solutions (From Best to Worst)

#### Solution 1: The "Market Rent" Approach (Safest & Most Common)
Since she is buying the house now and you plan to marry in a year, treat the next year as a transition period. 
* **How it works:** She pays the down payment and the mortgage. You pay her a set, agreed-upon monthly amount as "rent" or your share of the household expenses (utilities, groceries, etc.). 
* **Why it’s fair:** You are contributing to the household you live in, but you aren't paying her mortgage. If you two break up, you simply paid rent to live there. If you get married, you can transition to joint ownership (see below).
* **How to calculate it:** Do not pay half her mortgage if her mortgage is incredibly high. Instead, pay what would be a fair market rate for renting a room in your area, or a percentage based on your income.

#### Solution 2: The "Co-Buying" Approach (If you want to be equal partners now)
If you want to be a co-owner from day one, you need to be on the loan and the title.
* **How it works:** You both put money toward the down payment. Both of your names go on the deed. 
* **Why it’s fair:** You are both building equity from the start.
* **The Catch:** You must have a **Cohabitation Agreement** drawn up by a lawyer *before* closing. This legal document states exactly what happens to the house if you break up (e.g., "The house will be sold and the proceeds split 50/50," or "Partner A can buy out Partner B's share"). 

#### Solution 3: The "Post-Nuptial" Transition (Buy-in after marriage)
She buys the house now on her own. You move in and pay minimal expenses (like utilities/groceries) to help her out, but you save your money.
* **How it works:** Once you get married, you "buy into" the house. You can use the money you saved over the year to pay down a chunk of the mortgage, and she adds your name to the deed.
* **Why it’s fair:** It keeps things clean. You don't risk your hard-earned money while you are unmarried, but you show commitment to the home once the marriage is official.

---

### How to Have the Conversation
Since you work long, exhausting shifts, the last thing you want is to come home to a stressful financial argument. Approach this as a team. 

You can say something like:
> *"I am so excited for you to buy this house, and I’m so excited for our future marriage. Because we aren't married yet, I want to make sure we handle the finances in a way that is fair to both of us and keeps things simple. Let's figure out a fair 'living expense' contribution for me for this next year, and once we get married, we can talk about how to combine our finances and put my name on the deed."*

### Summary Recommendation
For the next year, **do not pay for the house (down payment or mortgage payments).** Instead, pay a fair share of **living expenses (utilities, groceries, and a reasonable "rent" contribution).** 

Once the wedding invites are sent and you are legally wed, you can refinance the home to put both your names on it, or draft a prenuptial agreement that outlines how the house will be treated as joint property moving forward.
