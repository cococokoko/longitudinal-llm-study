You know that the customer recently started a new job and is still on probation.

── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How can I optimize my home office for productivity?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Optimizing your home office is one of the best ways to set yourself up for success, especially when you are in a transition period like starting a new job. When you are on probation, your primary goals are likely **establishing a reliable routine, maintaining high energy and focus, and ensuring seamless communication** with your new team. 

Here is a guide to optimizing your workspace specifically tailored to help you make a stellar impression and stay highly productive during this critical period.

---

### 1. Optimize for "Presence" and Communication
During a probationary period, visibility is key. Since you aren't in a physical office, your video and audio quality *are* your professional presence.
*   **The "Three-Point" Camera Setup:** Position your webcam at eye level (use a laptop stand or a stack of books). Looking down at a camera can feel disengaged, while looking eye-to-eye builds trust.
*   **Prioritize Lighting:** Position your desk so you face a window (natural light is best). If that’s not possible, place a desk lamp behind your monitor pointing toward your face. Avoid having a bright window directly behind you, which turns you into a silhouette on video calls.
*   **Invest in Audio:** If you do nothing else, ensure your audio is crystal clear. A dedicated USB microphone or a high-quality headset with a noise-canceling mic ensures your contributions in meetings are easily heard without background distractions.

### 2. Design for Cognitive Focus (Minimizing Friction)
When you are learning a new role, your brain is processing a massive amount of new information. Your physical environment should minimize cognitive load.
*   **The "Two-Screen" Advantage:** If your budget or company allowance permits, use an external monitor alongside your laptop. This allows you to have your training materials, onboarding documents, or company wiki open on one screen, while actively working or taking notes on the other.
*   **The "Ready-State" Desk:** At the end of each workday, spend 5 minutes clearing your desk. Put away stray papers, wash your coffee mug, and write down your top 3 priorities for the next morning. Walking into a clean workspace the next day reduces morning anxiety and helps you dive straight into high-priority tasks.
*   **Visual Cues for Deep Work:** If you share your home with family or roommates, establish a physical signal (like a closed door, a specific light, or wearing over-ear headphones) that means, *"I am in deep focus/on a call, please do not disturb."* This prevents stressful interruptions during important onboarding moments.

### 3. Ergonomics for High Energy
Physical discomfort is a massive drain on productivity and focus. Since you need to be at your best right now, prioritize your physical setup:
*   **The 90-90-90 Rule:** Adjust your chair so your feet are flat on the floor, your knees are at a 90-degree angle, and your elbows are at a 90-degree angle resting on the desk or armrests. 
*   **Eye Strain Prevention:** Position your monitor about an arm's length away. Use the **20-20-20 rule**: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds to rest your eyes.
*   **Movement Triggers:** When you are eager to prove yourself, it’s easy to sit glued to your chair for 8 hours. This actually degrades your performance. Set a silent alarm to stand up, stretch, or grab a glass of water for 5 minutes every hour. 

### 4. Create a Psychological Boundary
When working from home, the line between "work life" and "home life" blurs quickly. To prevent burnout during your probationary period, you need a clear transition.
*   **The "Commute" Ritual:** Create a 10-minute ritual that starts and ends your workday. In the morning, this could be making a cup of coffee and reading industry news. In the evening, it could be shutting down your laptop, putting it in a drawer (out of sight, out of mind), and taking a quick walk around the block. 
*   **Dedicated Zone:** Even if you don’t have a spare room, designate a specific corner or table that is *only* for work. Avoid working from your bed or couch, as this associates your relaxation spaces with work stress.

### Quick Wins to Do Today:
1.  **Clear the clutter:** Keep only what you use daily on your desk.
2.  **Test your tech:** Do a mock video call with a friend to check your audio, video, and internet connection strength in your workspace.
3.  **Hydration station:** Keep a large water bottle at your desk to keep your energy levels up throughout the day.
