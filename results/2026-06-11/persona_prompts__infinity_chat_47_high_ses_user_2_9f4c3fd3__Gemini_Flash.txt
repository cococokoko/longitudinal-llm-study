### **The Question**
Under the laws of physics, no bird—including the Eastern Bluebird, the Blue Jay, or the Indigo Bunting—actually possesses blue pigment in its feathers. 

By what precise scientific phenomenon do these birds appear blue to the human eye, and how does this differ from how they get their red or yellow coloration?

***

### **The Answer**
The phenomenon is called **structural coloration** (specifically, **coherent scattering** or **constructive interference**). 

*   **The Mechanism:** Blue birds do not have blue pigment. Instead, their feathers contain microscopic keratin structures and air pockets. When light hits the feather, these structures act like tiny prisms, scattering only the shorter, blue wavelengths of light back to our eyes while absorbing the longer wavelengths. 
*   **The Distinction:** This is fundamentally different from red, orange, or yellow bird feathers, which derive their color from **pigmentation** (specifically, carotenoids absorbed from the bird's diet). 

**The "Evidence" (How to prove it):** 
If you grind a red cardinal feather into a powder, the powder remains red because the chemical pigment is still present. If you grind a bluebird feather into a powder, the powder turns a dull grey or brown, because you have destroyed the physical structure that scatters the light.
