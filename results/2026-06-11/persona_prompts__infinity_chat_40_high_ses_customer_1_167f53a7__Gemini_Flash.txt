This is a complex situation that sits at the intersection of legal protection, financial equity, and relationship dynamics. Given your profession as a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital, your financial profile, risk exposure, and earning trajectory are likely significantly different from the average homebuyer. 

In your field, you are trained to assess risk, plan for worst-case scenarios, and make highly calculated decisions. Applying that same analytical framework here is the best way to find a solution that is fair, protective, and supportive of your future marriage.

---

### 1. The Legal Framework: Protect Both Parties
Because you are not yet married, the law treats you as legal strangers. If you contribute to a mortgage or down payment on a house deeded solely in her name, you are essentially making an **unsecured gift** with no legal right to equity if the relationship dissolves. Conversely, if you put your name on the deed but she pays the down payment, she is taking on significant risk.

**The Solution: A Cohabitation Agreement**
Before any money changes hands, you should hire a family law attorney to draft a Cohabitation Agreement. This is a legal contract for unmarried couples that outlines:
*   Who contributed what to the down payment.
*   How monthly expenses (mortgage, taxes, utilities) are split.
*   What happens to the property if you split up before getting married.
*   How the property transitions once you do marry (e.g., does it become marital property, or remain separate?).

### 2. The Financial Framework: Three Ways to Structure the Deal
"Fairness" in a relationship with a high-earning specialist often doesn't mean a 50/50 split. It usually means a split proportional to income, or a clear boundary between "yours, mine, and ours." 

Here are the three best financial models:

#### Option A: The "Tenant-Landlord" Model (Simplest & Safest)
Since she is buying the house now, she buys it entirely on her own. Her name is on the deed and the mortgage. 
*   **How it works:** You pay her a set monthly amount as "rent" (or cover utilities/groceries) that is comfortable for both of you, but you do not contribute to the down payment or principal mortgage payments. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** She builds equity in her asset. You get a place to live without taking on the liability of a mortgage on a home you don't own. 
*   **Post-Marriage:** Once you marry, you can draft a post-nuptial agreement, refinance the mortgage in both names, and add you to the deed, adjusting your financial contributions accordingly.

#### Option B: The Proportional Equity Model (Joint Ownership)
If you want to buy the house *together* right now, you both go on the deed and mortgage.
*   **How it works:** You contribute to the down payment and mortgage proportionally based on your incomes. If you make 70% of the household income, you pay 70% of the costs. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** It prevents her from being financially strained by trying to match your spending power, and prevents you from feeling taken advantage of.
*   **Note:** This *must* be accompanied by a Cohabitation Agreement specifying that if you split, the equity is divided based on actual financial contribution, not a default 50/50.

#### Option C: The "Wait and See" Model (Lowest Risk)
She buys the house within her own budget, using only her money. You do not contribute financially to the housing costs at all. Instead, you save your money in a separate account.
*   **Post-Marriage:** Once married, you can use your accumulated savings to make a large principal payment on the mortgage, renovate the home, or buy a new home together as equal partners.

### 3. The Professional Risk Factor (Asset Protection)
As a neurosurgeon, you operate in a high-liability specialty. Asset protection is a critical component of your financial planning. 

*   **Premarital Assets:** In many jurisdictions, assets owned prior to marriage remain separate property in a divorce, provided they are not commingled. If she buys this house solely in her name with her money, it is her separate asset. If you contribute to it, you commingle funds, which muddies the legal waters.
*   **Malpractice/Liability:** Keeping assets in your spouse’s name can sometimes offer a layer of asset protection against personal liability or lawsuits that exceed your malpractice policy limits (though this varies wildly by state and requires a trust/estate attorney's guidance). 

### Summary Recommendation
The most surgical, low-risk approach is **Option A (The Tenant-Landlord Model)** combined with a **Prenuptial Agreement** down the road. 

1.  **Now:** Let her buy the house within her budget. Pay her a reasonable, agreed-upon monthly amount for living expenses (akin to rent), but do not put your name on the deed or contribute to the down payment.
2.  **In a year (before the wedding):** Draft a Prenuptial Agreement. This is standard practice for physicians to protect pre-marital assets, future earnings, and address how the home's equity will be treated moving forward.
3.  **After Marriage:** Once married, you can legally integrate your finances, add your name to the deed, and begin contributing to the mortgage as a joint enterprise. 

This protects her independence and investment now, protects your high-earning potential and assets, and ensures that when you do combine forces, it is done under the clear legal protection of marriage.
