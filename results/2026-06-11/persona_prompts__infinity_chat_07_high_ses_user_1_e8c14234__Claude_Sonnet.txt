── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background makes it worth engaging seriously.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, it's impossible

**Practical reasons:**
- Information grows faster than any individual can absorb
- Even within neurosurgery specifically, the literature expands faster than one person can read
- Time and cognitive capacity are finite

**More fundamental reasons:**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** suggest even formal systems can't be complete and consistent simultaneously
- **Quantum uncertainty** places physical limits on measurable knowledge
- **Unknown unknowns** - we can't fully map what we don't know
- Language and concepts themselves may be inadequate to capture certain realities

## Worth being precise about though

- "Everything" is doing a lot of work in that question
- There's a difference between *in principle* impossible vs *practically* impossible
- Some philosophers argue certain knowledge is impossible even about things we think we know (radical skepticism)
- Your field specifically involves things **currently unknowable** - consciousness, subjective experience, exactly why certain interventions work

## The honest tension

You likely operate at the frontier where **knowing the limits of knowledge** is itself clinically critical - knowing what you don't know about a patient's outcome matters enormously.
