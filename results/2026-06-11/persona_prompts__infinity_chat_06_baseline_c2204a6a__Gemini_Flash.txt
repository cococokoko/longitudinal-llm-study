── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Understanding what you truly want is one of the most profound and difficult challenges of being human. Often, what we *think* we want is actually what society, our parents, or social media have conditioned us to believe we should want. 

To peel back these layers and find your authentic desires, you need to approach the question from several different angles: **emotional, behavioral, analytical, and somatic (body-based).**

Here is a step-by-step guide to help you figure out what you actually want.

---

### 1. Look at Your Envy (The Map of Desire)
We are taught that envy is a negative emotion, but it is actually one of the most honest compasses we have. 
* **The Exercise:** Pay close attention to when you feel a pang of jealousy or envy. Who is it directed toward? What do they have?
* **The Insight:** If you envy a friend who just published a book, you want to write. If you envy a friend who just quit their job to travel, you want freedom. If you envy someone’s quiet, boring weekend, you want peace. **Your envy points directly to your unfulfilled desires.**

### 2. Distinguish Between "Wanting" and "Wanting to Want"
Often, we confuse what we actually desire with what we *wish* we desired because it sounds good or makes us look successful.
* **The Test:** Ask yourself, *"Do I actually want to do the work required for this, or do I just want the status of having it?"*
* *Example:* You might think you want to be a novelist. But do you want to sit alone in a room for four hours a day struggling with sentences? If not, you don't want to be a novelist; you want the *idea* of being a novelist. Look for the things where you actually enjoy the "grind" or the process, not just the reward.

### 3. Listen to Your Body (Somatic Clues)
Your brain is highly susceptible to rationalization and social pressure. Your nervous system is not. 
* **The Exercise:** When you think about a potential path (e.g., taking a new job, staying in a relationship, moving to a new city), close your eyes and imagine it vividly. 
* **The Insight:** How does your body react? Do your shoulders drop and your chest expand (expansion/relief)? Or does your stomach tighten and your breath become shallow (contraction/anxiety)? Your body often knows what you want long before your conscious mind does.

### 4. Use the "Five Whys" Technique
This is a method used in systems engineering, but it works beautifully for self-discovery. It helps you get past surface-level desires to find the core human need underneath.
* *Start with a surface want:* "I want a promotion."
1. **Why?** "Because I want to make more money."
2. **Why?** "So I can buy a nicer apartment."
3. **Why?** "So I can host dinners and have people over."
4. **Why?** "Because I want to feel connected to a community."
5. **Why?** "Because I feel lonely and isolated right now."
* **The Realization:** You don't necessarily want the promotion; you want **connection and community**. Knowing this opens up dozens of ways to solve the actual problem that have nothing to do with your job.

### 5. Track Your Energy, Not Your Time
For one week, keep an "Energy Audit." Carry a notebook or use your phone notes. Every few hours, write down what you did and rate your energy level on a scale of -5 (drained) to +5 (energized).
* **The Insight:** Do not confuse "fun" with "energizing." Scrolling TikTok might be easy, but it usually leaves you at a -2. Having a difficult, deep conversation with a friend might be hard, but it might leave you at a +4. Look for the activities, people, and environments that consistently charge your battery. **What energizes you is what you want.**

### 6. Strip Away the Audience
Ask yourself this question: **"If I could never tell a single person about what I achieved, what would I still want to do?"**
* If you couldn't post it on Instagram, LinkedIn, or tell your parents, would you still buy that car? Would you still go on that vacation? Would you still want that job?
* This question instantly filters out "performative desires" (things we do to impress others) and leaves you with "intrinsic desires" (things we do for the sheer joy of them).

### 7. Run Small Experiments
You cannot think your way into knowing what you want; you have to **act** your way into it. Clarity is a consequence of action, not a prerequisite.
* If you think you want to be a graphic designer, don't quit your job. Take a 2-week online course. 
* If you think you want to live in a cabin in the woods, rent an Airbnb in the woods for a week and see if you actually like the isolation, or if you get bored and scared.
* Treat your life as a series of low-risk experiments.

### Summary: The Daily Practice
If you feel completely lost right now, start with this simple daily prompt. Every morning, write down the answer to this question:

> **"If I had absolute permission to disappoint everyone else, what would I do today?"**

You don't have to actually do it. Just writing down the answer will start training your brain to recognize your own voice over the noise of the world.
