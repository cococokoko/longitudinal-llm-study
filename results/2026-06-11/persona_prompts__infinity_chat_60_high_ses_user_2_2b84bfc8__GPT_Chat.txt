── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a line of dominoes.

You want to know whether the very last domino will fall down.

For some domino setups, you can look at them and immediately know the answer.

But for other setups, the only way to find out is to knock over the first domino and watch what happens, one domino at a time.

Computational irreducibility is the idea that some things work like that.

Even if you have a very smart computer, there is no faster shortcut to know the final result. The computer has to go through all the steps, just like everyone else.

A simple way to say it:

- Some problems have shortcuts.
- Some problems do not.
- For those problems, the quickest way to know what happens is to let the process happen step by step.

It's a bit like asking, "What will my plant look like in three months?" Sometimes you can't skip ahead and instantly know. You have to let the days pass and watch it grow.

That's computational irreducibility: the future result can't always be compressed into a quicker calculation. Sometimes the journey is the only way to find the destination.
