How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to optimizing placement, reducing interference, and ensuring your equipment matches your needs.

Start with the basics:
- Place your router in a central, open location rather than in a closet, cabinet, or corner of the house.
- Elevate it on a shelf or table rather than placing it on the floor.
- Keep it away from large metal objects, thick concrete walls, microwaves, cordless phone bases, and other electronics that can cause interference.

Optimize your wireless settings:
- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band (if available) for higher speeds and lower congestion.
- Use 2.4 GHz for devices that are farther from the router or behind multiple walls.
- Update your router's firmware regularly.
- Choose less congested Wi‑Fi channels. Many routers can do this automatically.

Upgrade equipment if needed:
- If your router is more than 4–5 years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can provide significant improvements.
- For larger homes, consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system rather than a traditional range extender. Mesh systems generally provide better coverage and roaming.

Improve network design:
- Connect high-bandwidth devices (gaming PCs, TVs, workstations) via Ethernet where possible.
- If using mesh nodes, place them where they still receive a strong signal from the main router—not at the edge of coverage.
- Avoid daisy-chaining multiple extenders, which can reduce performance.

Check your internet connection:
- Run a speed test while connected directly to the modem/router with Ethernet. This helps distinguish Wi‑Fi issues from ISP issues.
- Ensure your internet plan matches your household's usage needs.

For a more tailored recommendation, it would help to know:
- Home size (square footage or square meters)
- Number of floors
- Current router model
- Internet speed from your ISP
- Whether the issue is coverage, speed, reliability, or all three.
