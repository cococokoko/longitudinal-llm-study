── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to say a formal thank you for your time in the subject line of an email?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To strike the right balance of academic formality, respect, and clarity for a subject line, the phrasing should be concise and context-specific. 

Here are several options categorized by the specific context of your email:

### 1. After a Meeting, Interview, or Discussion (Most Common)
These are professional, direct, and highly appropriate for academic or administrative correspondence.
*   **Thank You for Your Time and Insights** *(Highly recommended for academic discussions)*
*   **With Sincere Thanks for Your Time**
*   **Thank You for Your Time today / yesterday**
*   **Appreciation for Your Time and Guidance** *(Excellent if they offered advice or mentorship)*

### 2. After an Inquiry or Request for Assistance
If you are thanking them for reviewing a proposal, reading a draft, or answering a complex query:
*   **Thank You for Your Time and Consideration**
*   **With Appreciation for Your Time and Assistance**
*   **Thank You for Your Valuable Feedback**

### 3. Ultra-Formal / Traditional
If you are writing to a high-ranking administrator (such as a Dean, Provost, or President) or a highly distinguished scholar where maximum deference is preferred:
*   **Expressing My Gratitude for Your Time**
*   **With Sincere Appreciation for Your Time**
*   **A Note of Thanks for Your Time**

### Best Practice Tip for Email Delivery:
To ensure the recipient immediately recognizes the context of the email, it is often best to append the specific topic or your name to the subject line. 

**Examples:**
*   *Thank You for Your Time – [Your Name]*
*   *Thank You for Your Time | [Project/Topic Name]*
*   *With Sincere Thanks for Your Time – [Your Name]*
