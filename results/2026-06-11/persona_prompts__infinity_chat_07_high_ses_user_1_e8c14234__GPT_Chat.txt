I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any meaningful sense, it is impossible for a human being to know everything.

There are several reasons:

- The amount of information in the world is vast and continually increasing. New observations, discoveries, events, and data are generated faster than any individual could absorb them.
- Human cognitive capacity is finite. Memory, attention, and processing ability all have limits, regardless of intelligence or training.
- Knowledge itself is often provisional. In science and medicine, what is accepted today may be revised tomorrow as better evidence emerges.
- Some domains are so specialized that mastery requires years or decades of focused study. Expertise in one area usually comes at the cost of not attaining equal depth in countless others.
- There are philosophical limits as well. Certain truths may be inaccessible in principle, and some questions may not have complete answers. Mathematics, for example, contains results showing limits on what can be proven within a formal system.

For someone in your position as a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital, this reality is often especially visible. Modern medicine has become too large for any one physician to fully command. Even within neurosurgery, no individual can maintain exhaustive knowledge of every subspecialty, every new paper, every evolving technique, every genetic finding, every imaging advance, and every relevant development in adjacent fields.

What is achievable is not omniscience but highly effective expertise:
- Knowing core principles deeply.
- Recognizing the limits of your knowledge.
- Knowing how to find, evaluate, and integrate new information.
- Building networks of colleagues with complementary expertise.
- Continuously updating your understanding.

In many professions, especially medicine, wisdom often consists less in knowing everything than in accurately understanding what you know, what you do not know, and how to bridge the gap when it matters.
