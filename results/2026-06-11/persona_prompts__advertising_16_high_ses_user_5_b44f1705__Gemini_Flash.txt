── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive at a large technology company, you operate in a high-stakes, hyper-velocity environment. You are likely balancing relentless pressure to deliver shareholder value, rapid technological shifts (like the AI race), organizational restructuring, and the constant demand for 24/7 availability. 

At your level, stress and anxiety are not just personal challenges; they are business risks that cloud decision-making, reduce emotional intelligence, and lead to burnout.

To manage this effectively, you must treat your mental and emotional well-being with the same strategic rigor you apply to your business. Here is a framework designed specifically for tech executives to manage stress and anxiety.

---

### 1. Shift from "Time Management" to "Energy Management"
In tech, your time is heavily guarded by EAs and calendars, but your energy is likely leaking. 
*   **Audit your energy drains:** Identify which meetings, people, or tasks drain you the most. Where possible, delegate, automate, or eliminate them.
*   **Schedule "White Space":** Block out 60 to 90 minutes of "thinking time" on your calendar every day. Label it as a critical meeting. Use this time for deep strategic thinking, or simply to let your brain rest. If you are constantly reacting, you are constantly anxious.
*   **The "90-Minute Rule":** Human brains operate on ultradian rhythms. Focus intensely for 90 minutes, then take a 5-minute break away from screens (walk, stretch, hydrate). 

### 2. Establish "Cognitive Offloading" Protocols
Anxiety often stems from the sheer volume of information, decisions, and "open loops" in your mind.
*   **The Sunday Night Brain Dump:** Before the week starts, write down every single worry, task, and project on paper. Categorize them into: *Control* (things you can act on), *Influence* (things you can guide), and *Out of Control* (things you must accept). Focus your energy strictly on the first two.
*   **Ruthless Prioritization (The One Thing):** Every morning, ask yourself: *"What is the one thing I must accomplish today that will make everything else easier or unnecessary?"* Align your day around that, and accept that some lower-priority fires will burn.

### 3. Master the "Off-Switch" (Digital Hygiene)
Tech executives are notoriously bad at disconnecting because the industry rewards constant connectivity. However, chronic availability keeps your nervous system in a perpetual state of fight-or-flight.
*   **The "Hard Stop":** Establish a time when you shut down your laptop and stop checking Slack/Teams/emails (e.g., 7:30 PM). Inform your leadership team of this boundary. They will respect it, and it sets a healthy precedent for your org.
*   **Dual-Device Strategy:** If possible, have a work phone and a personal phone. On weekends, leave the work phone in a drawer. If there is a true emergency, your team should have a protocol to call your personal line.
*   **The 1-Hour Buffer:** Do not look at your phone for the first 30 minutes of the day, and the last 60 minutes before sleep. Entering your day immediately reacting to emails triggers cortisol spikes.

### 4. Somatic and Physiological Regulation
Anxiety is a physiological state before it is a psychological one. You cannot "think" your way out of a panic attack or high anxiety; you must use your body to calm your brain.
*   **The Physiological Sigh:** When you feel anxiety rising (before a board meeting or a difficult 1:1), do this twice: Take a deep double-inhale through your nose (one deep breath, followed by a quick sharp sniff to fully inflate the lungs), then a long, slow exhale through your mouth. This instantly activates the parasympathetic nervous system and lowers your heart rate.
*   **Non-Sleep Deep Rest (NSDR) / Yoga Nidra:** A 10-to-20-minute NSDR protocol (available on YouTube/Spotify) can reset your nervous system and mimic the restorative effects of sleep. It is highly effective for mid-day resets.
*   **Zone 2 Cardio:** Engage in 150 minutes a week of moderate cardiovascular exercise (where you can talk but not sing). This has been clinically proven to burn off excess cortisol and adrenaline.

### 5. Reframe Your Relationship with Control and Failure
In tech, we are conditioned to believe we can optimize and control everything. Anxiety thrives on the illusion of control.
*   **Embrace "Good Enough" Decisions:** Avoid analysis paralysis. Make decisions with 70% of the information (as Jeff Bezos advocates). Indecision causes more anxiety than a wrong decision that can be course-corrected.
*   **Separate Identity from Outcomes:** Your self-worth is not tied to the stock price, the successful launch of a product, or a flawless quarter. You are an executive, but that is your role, not your identity. 

### 6. Build a "Psychological Safety Net"
Being at the top is isolating. You cannot easily vent to your peers (who may be competitors), your direct reports (which damages morale), or your CEO.
*   **Hire an Executive Coach or Therapist:** You need a confidential, objective sounding board. A coach can help with leadership dynamics; a licensed therapist can help you unpack the root causes of your anxiety.
*   **Peer Networks:** Join external, confidential forums of other tech executives (e.g., YPO, Vistage, or informal peer groups). Realizing that your peers at Google, Meta, or startups are facing the exact same anxieties is incredibly validating.

### A Quick Checklist for High-Stress Moments:
When a crisis hits (e.g., a system outage, a bad PR cycle, or missed targets):
1.  **Pause:** Do not reply immediately. Take three physiological sighs.
2.  **Isolate the Variable:** What is the actual problem, stripped of the emotional drama?
3.  **Determine the Next Step:** What is the *very next* micro-action required? (Not the whole solution, just the next step).
4.  **Communicate clearly:** Keep your team focused on execution, which reduces collective anxiety.
