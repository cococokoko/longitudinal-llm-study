As an executive in the technology sector, you are likely familiar with the concepts of **scale, alignment, talent development, and organizational culture**. Interestingly, the concept of "discipleship"—traditionally a religious term—shares deep structural parallels with how modern organizations scale vision and capability. 

At its core, discipleship is the process of transmitting a core set of values, skills, and worldviews from a leader to a follower, with the ultimate goal of the follower becoming a leader who can repeat the process. 

Here is an overview of the different views on discipleship, categorized by theological, philosophical, and organizational frameworks.

---

### 1. Theological Views (The Original Frameworks)
Within the Christian tradition (where the term is most prominent), there are distinct models of how discipleship is executed. These map closely to different management styles.

*   **The Relational/Life-on-Life Model (The "Startup" Approach):**
    *   *Concept:* Modeled after Jesus and his twelve apostles. It is highly personal, informal, and immersive. It relies on proximity, shared experiences, and high-touch mentoring.
    *   *Pros:* Deep cultural alignment, high trust, and holistic development.
    *   *Cons:* Extremely difficult to scale. It is highly dependent on the quality of the individual mentor.
*   **The Catechetical/Instructional Model (The "Training Manual" Approach):**
    *   *Concept:* Focuses on structured curriculum, doctrine, and intellectual mastery. Discipleship is achieved by passing exams, reading texts, and understanding systematic theology.
    *   *Pros:* Highly scalable, standardized, and easily measurable.
    *   *Cons:* Can lead to "head knowledge" without behavioral change; lacks the emotional connection of relational models.
*   **The Missional/Action-Oriented Model (The "Agile/Sprint" Approach):**
    *   *Concept:* Discipleship happens *through* doing. Instead of sitting in a classroom, disciples are immediately sent out on "missions" (projects) and debriefed afterward. Learning is experiential.
    *   *Pros:* High bias for action, rapid feedback loops, and immediate real-world impact.
    *   *Cons:* High risk of burnout; potential for quality control issues if disciples are sent out too quickly.

---

### 2. Philosophical & Secular Views (The "Master-Apprentice" Framework)
Outside of religion, discipleship is the foundation of human progress, art, and philosophy.

*   **The Socratic/Inquiry Model:**
    *   *Concept:* The "discipler" does not give answers but asks probing questions to help the disciple discover the truth themselves. 
    *   *Modern Parallel:* Executive coaching. It assumes the disciple has the latent capability, and the leader’s job is to unlock it through critical thinking.
*   **The Guild/Apprenticeship Model:**
    *   *Concept:* Historically used in crafts (stonemasonry, painting) and now in medicine (residency) and law. The progression is strict: *Observe (I do, you watch) $\rightarrow$ Assist (We do together) $\rightarrow$ Supervise (You do, I watch) $\rightarrow$ Mastery (You do alone).*
    *   *Modern Parallel:* Technical onboarding and shadowing programs in engineering.

---

### 3. The Corporate/Tech View (Scaling Culture and Leadership)
In high-growth technology companies, "discipleship" is the unsung hero of scaling. When a company grows from 50 to 5,000 employees, the founder cannot personally influence everyone. They need "disciples" of the company culture.

*   **The "Culture Carrier" Model:**
    *   *Concept:* Identifying high-potential employees who not only perform well technically but also embody the company’s core values. These individuals are "discipled" by executives to protect and propagate the culture across new offices or departments.
    *   *Tech Example:* Amazon’s "Bar Raisers" or Google’s "Googler-to-Googler" (g2g) training network.
*   **The Succession Planning Model:**
    *   *Concept:* True discipleship in business means training your replacement. It is a shift from "managing" (getting work done through others) to "developing" (building the capacity of others to lead).
    *   *The Metric:* A leader’s success is not measured by their individual output, but by how many of their direct reports are promoted to leadership positions.

### The Executive Takeaway
Whether in faith, philosophy, or a Fortune 500 tech company, **the ultimate test of discipleship is multiplication.** 

If you are a leader, the question is: *Are you creating followers, or are you creating leaders who can create more leaders?* The latter is the only way to achieve true organizational scale.
