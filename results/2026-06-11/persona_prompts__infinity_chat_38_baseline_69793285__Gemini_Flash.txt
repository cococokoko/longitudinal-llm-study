### 1. Biblical and Historical Origins
In the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament), Moloch is associated with the Ammonites, a Canaanite people. He is most infamous for the ritual of **child sacrifice**. 
* **The Ritual:** Scriptures (such as Leviticus and Kings) forbid the Israelites from "passing their children through the fire to Moloch." This involved sacrificing children, usually first-borns, by placing them in the heated bronze arms of a statue of the deity, where they would burn to death.
* **The Name:** The name likely derives from the Hebrew word *Melech* (king), combined with the vowels of *Bosheth* (shame), resulting in *Molech*—essentially meaning "the shameful king."

### 2. In Christian Demonology
During the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, as Christian theologians classified ancient pagan gods as demons, Moloch was integrated into the hierarchy of Hell.
* **John Milton’s *Paradise Lost* (1667):** Milton cemented Moloch’s modern image. In this epic poem, Moloch is one of the chief warriors of Satan’s fallen angels. He is described as the fiercest and strongest devil, covered in the blood of human sacrifices, who eagerly advocates for open, violent war against Heaven rather than sitting in exile.
* **Collin de Plancy’s *Dictionnaire Infernal* (1818):** In later occult writings, Moloch is named as a Prince of the Valley of Tears and a member of the Council of Hell, often depicted with the head of a bull and a crown.

### 3. Cultural and Literary Symbolism
Because of his association with sacrificing the young for future prosperity, "Moloch" has become a powerful metaphor in modern times:
* **Fritz Lang’s *Metropolis* (1927):** In this classic sci-fi film, the protagonist has a vision of a massive, soul-crushing industrial machine turning into the god Moloch, devouring the workers who feed it.
* **Allen Ginsberg’s *Howl* (1955):** In this famous Beat Generation poem, Ginsberg uses "Moloch" as a metaphor for industrial capitalism, war, conformity, and the modern American city (specifically New York) that destroys the minds and souls of the youth.
* **H.G. Wells' *The Time Machine*:** While spelled differently, the **Morlocks**—the subterranean, ape-like post-humans who feed on the peaceful Eloi—are named as a direct nod to Moloch.

### 4. Modern Philosophical Use
In modern game theory and philosophy (notably popularized by essayist Scott Alexander in *Meditations on Moloch*), "Moloch" is used to describe **coordination failures**. It represents a system where individual actors, pursuing their own self-interest, are forced into a "race to the bottom" that ultimately destroys everyone (e.g., tragedy of the commons, arms races, or environmental destruction). In this sense, Moloch is the "demon" of bad incentives.
