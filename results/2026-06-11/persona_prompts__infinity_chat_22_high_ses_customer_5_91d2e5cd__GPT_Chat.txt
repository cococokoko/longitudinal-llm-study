Different views on discipleship.

“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions and even among individual churches. At its core, it generally means becoming a follower of Jesus and growing in faith, character, and obedience. The main differences involve how that growth happens, what it emphasizes, and the role of the church.

Some major views include:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Discipleship is often understood as a personal relationship with Jesus followed by lifelong spiritual growth.
  - Emphasizes Bible study, prayer, evangelism, accountability, and mentoring.
  - Small groups and one-on-one disciple-making relationships are common.
  - Often influenced by Jesus' command to “make disciples” (Matthew 28:19–20).

• Reformed view
  - Strong emphasis on God's sovereignty and the role of Scripture.
  - Discipleship is often seen as growing in holiness through the ordinary means of grace: preaching, sacraments, prayer, and church life.
  - Focuses on doctrinal formation alongside personal transformation.

• Catholic view
  - Discipleship is closely tied to participation in the life of the Church.
  - Growth occurs through the sacraments, prayer, Scripture, moral formation, and service.
  - Saints are often viewed as models of mature discipleship.
  - Emphasizes both personal conversion and ongoing spiritual formation.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Centers on “theosis” or deification—the process of becoming more united with God.
  - Discipleship involves prayer, worship, ascetic practices, repentance, and participation in the sacraments.
  - Often places strong emphasis on spiritual guidance from experienced mentors.

• Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Highlights sanctification and growth in holiness.
  - Encourages spiritual disciplines, accountability groups, and acts of mercy.
  - Emphasizes both personal piety and social concern.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Focuses on the work of the Holy Spirit in the believer's life.
  - Emphasizes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, and personal transformation.
  - Discipleship often includes learning to discern and respond to the Spirit's leading.

• Anabaptist view
  - Emphasizes following Jesus' teachings in daily life, especially regarding peace, community, and service.
  - Discipleship is often understood as practical obedience rather than primarily doctrinal agreement.
  - Community accountability plays a significant role.

• Liberation theology perspectives
  - Emphasize discipleship as participation in God's work of justice and liberation.
  - Focus on serving marginalized communities and addressing systemic injustice.
  - Often integrate spiritual growth with social action.

Key areas of disagreement or emphasis:
• Personal faith vs. communal formation.
• Spiritual disciplines vs. sacramental participation.
• Correct belief (orthodoxy) vs. practical obedience (orthopraxy).
• Individual transformation vs. social transformation.
• Structured mentoring vs. organic spiritual growth.

Despite these differences, most Christian traditions agree that discipleship involves learning from Jesus, being transformed by that relationship, and expressing that transformation through worship, character, and service.
