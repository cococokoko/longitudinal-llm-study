### Project Title: *The Clay Pot Filter: Low-Cost Heavy Metal & Microplastic Filtration for Mekong Delta Communities*

#### The Vietnam Connection (The "Why"):
Water pollution is a critical issue in Vietnam. In the Mekong Delta and rural provinces, communities rely on local river water, which is increasingly contaminated with **microplastics** (from aquaculture and waste) and **heavy metals/pesticides** (from agricultural runoff). Commercial filters are too expensive for many families. 

#### The STEM Concept:
You will test how different locally sourced, dirt-cheap materials—specifically **rice husk ash (a massive agricultural byproduct in Vietnam)**, **crushed coconut shells (activated carbon)**, and **local clay**—can be combined to create a highly effective, gravity-fed water filter.

---

### Why this fits a busy, unpredictable schedule:
*   **No strict timing:** Unlike biological projects (like growing plants or bacteria) which require precise daily maintenance, this is a physical/chemical filtration test. If you get called into a shift or have to work late, your filtration setup can just sit there. It won’t spoil.
*   **Fast setup:** Building the filters takes about 1 hour total. Running a test takes 5 minutes to pour water in, and you can collect the results hours later when you get home.
*   **Cheap materials:** You can buy everything at a local grocery store or hardware store for under $15.

---

### Materials Needed (Approx. Cost: $10 - $15)
1.  **4-5 Empty Plastic Water Bottles** (Free - cut in half to act as the filter funnels).
2.  **Cotton balls or coffee filters** ($2 - to plug the neck of the bottles).
3.  **Play sand and fine gravel** ($3 at a hardware store, or free from outdoors).
4.  **Activated Charcoal** ($5 - aquarium section of a pet store, represents coconut shell charcoal).
5.  **Rice Husk Ash** ($3 - can be found at garden centers, or you can burn a small amount of dry rice husks/straw safely outdoors).
6.  **"Polluted" Water (DIY):** Mix tap water with a pinch of dirt (for turbidity), a drop of food coloring (to represent chemical dyes/pesticides), and a sprinkle of glitter (to represent microplastics).

---

### The Experiment (How to do it):

You will build 3 different filter columns to see which combination cleans the water best.

*   **Filter A (Control):** Sand + Gravel + Cotton.
*   **Filter B (The Charcoal Filter):** Sand + Gravel + Activated Charcoal + Cotton.
*   **Filter C (The Vietnamese Rice Husk Filter):** Sand + Gravel + Rice Husk Ash + Cotton.

**The Process:**
1.  Cut the plastic bottles in half. Turn the top half upside down (like a funnel) and place it inside the bottom half.
2.  Stuff a cotton ball into the neck, then layer your materials inside.
3.  Pour equal amounts of your "polluted" water into each filter.
4.  **Go to work / go about your day.** Let gravity do the work.
5.  When you return, analyze the filtered water in the bottom of the bottles.

---

### How to Measure Results (Data Collection):
You don't need expensive lab equipment to get great data. You can measure success using:
1.  **Visual Clarity (Turbidity):** Hold the filtered water up to a phone flashlight. Rate the clarity on a scale of 1-10, or print a piece of paper with text and see how clearly you can read it through the water.
2.  **Color Removal (Chemical filtration):** See which filter removed the most food coloring. (Activated carbon and rice husk ash are excellent at binding to chemical dyes).
3.  **Microplastic Catch:** Pour the filtered water through a coffee filter to see if any "microplastic" glitter escaped.

*   **The "Wow" Factor:** Rice husk ash is actually a highly studied material in green chemistry because it is rich in silica, which naturally binds to heavy metals. By showing that a waste product from rice farming can clean drinking water, you are presenting a circular-economy solution.
*   **Great Visuals:** Your tri-fold board will look amazing with side-by-side photos of the dirty water before and the clean water after each filter.
