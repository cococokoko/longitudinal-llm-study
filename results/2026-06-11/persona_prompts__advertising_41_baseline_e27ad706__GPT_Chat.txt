How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving a credit score quickly depends on what's currently affecting it, but these are some of the fastest and most effective actions:

- Pay down credit card balances.
  - Credit utilization (how much of your available credit you're using) has a major impact on scores.
  - Aim to keep utilization below 30%, and ideally below 10%.
  - If possible, pay balances down before your card issuer reports to the credit bureaus.

- Make all payments on time.
  - Payment history is typically the largest scoring factor.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders to avoid missing due dates.

- Check your credit reports for errors.
  - Obtain your reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute any incorrect late payments, accounts, balances, or fraudulent activity.

- Ask for a credit limit increase.
  - If approved and you don't increase spending, your utilization ratio will decrease.
  - Some issuers do a soft inquiry; others may do a hard inquiry, so ask first.

- Become an authorized user.
  - If a trusted family member or partner has a long-standing account with low balances and excellent payment history, being added as an authorized user may help your score.

- Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts.
  - New applications can result in hard inquiries and lower the average age of your accounts.

- Bring past-due accounts current.
  - If you're behind on payments, getting accounts current can help prevent further damage and improve future reporting.

- Keep older accounts open.
  - Closing old accounts can reduce available credit and potentially affect the age of your credit history.

Typical timelines:
- Lowering utilization: often reflected within 1–2 billing cycles.
- Correcting report errors: usually several weeks to a few months.
- Building a stronger payment history: generally takes months, but benefits accumulate over time.
