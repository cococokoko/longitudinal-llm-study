# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose a hospital patient care aide's **weekly earnings** depend on two variables:

- **h** = regular hours worked
- **n** = number of night shift hours worked (which pay a bonus)

The earnings function is:

$$E(h, n) = 15h + 22n + 0.5n^2$$

---

## Calculating Partial Derivatives

### ∂E/∂h (rate of change with respect to regular hours)

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial h} = 15$$

*Treat **n** as a constant and differentiate with respect to **h**.*

### ∂E/∂n (rate of change with respect to night hours)

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial n} = 22 + n$$

*Treat **h** as a constant and differentiate with respect to **n**.*

---

## Numerical Illustration

Suppose the aide works **h = 30** regular hours and **n = 10** night hours:

| Partial Derivative | Calculation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ∂E/∂h = 15 | $15 | Each extra regular hour adds **$15** |
| ∂E/∂n = 22 + 10 = **32** | $32 | Each extra night hour adds **$32** |

---

## Verification

At (h=30, n=10):
$$E(30, 10) = 15(30) + 22(10) + 0.5(100) = 450 + 220 + 50 = \$720$$

Add **one more night hour** (n=11):
$$E(30, 11) = 450 + 22(11) + 0.5(121) = 450 + 242 + 60.5 = \$752.50$$

**Difference = $752.50 − $720 = $32.50 ≈ $32** ✓

---

## Key Insight

> A partial derivative measures how earnings change when **one variable changes**, while **holding all other variables fixed** — exactly like asking *"what do I earn for one more night shift, assuming my regular hours stay the same?"*
