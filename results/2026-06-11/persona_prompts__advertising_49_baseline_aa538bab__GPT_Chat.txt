How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what's causing the problem, but these steps often make the biggest difference:

• Place your router centrally
  - Put it in an open area, away from walls, metal objects, and large appliances.
  - Avoid placing it on the floor or inside cabinets.
  - Elevate it on a shelf or desk.

• Optimize the Wi‑Fi band
  - Use 5 GHz or 6 GHz (if available) for higher speeds and less congestion.
  - Use 2.4 GHz for longer range through walls.

• Change the Wi‑Fi channel
  - Nearby networks can interfere with yours.
  - In your router settings, try less crowded channels:
    - 2.4 GHz: usually channels 1, 6, or 11.
    - 5 GHz: select a less congested channel if your router supports it.

• Update firmware
  - Install the latest router firmware for performance, stability, and security improvements.

• Improve security
  - Use WPA2 or WPA3 encryption.
  - Remove unknown devices from your network.

• Reduce interference
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and Bluetooth-heavy devices.

• Upgrade hardware
  - Older routers may struggle with many devices.
  - Consider a Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router if you have many connected devices.

• Use a mesh system for large homes
  - If some rooms have weak signals, a mesh Wi‑Fi system often works better than a traditional extender.

• Connect critical devices with Ethernet
  - For gaming, video calls, or streaming, a wired connection is usually more reliable than Wi‑Fi.

• Check your internet plan
  - Sometimes the Wi‑Fi is fine, but the internet connection itself is the bottleneck.
  - Run speed tests near the router and compare them to your ISP's advertised speeds.
