Best practices for organic gardening focus on building healthy soil, preventing problems before they start, and working with natural systems.

- Build healthy soil
  - Add compost regularly to improve soil structure and fertility.
  - Use well-rotted manure, leaf mold, or other organic matter when appropriate.
  - Avoid synthetic fertilizers that can disrupt soil ecosystems.

- Choose the right plants
  - Select varieties suited to your climate and growing season.
  - Favor disease-resistant cultivars when available.
  - Plant at the proper time of year for your region.

- Practice crop rotation
  - Avoid growing the same plant family in the same location year after year.
  - Rotation can reduce pests, diseases, and nutrient depletion.

- Mulch generously
  - Apply organic mulches such as straw, shredded leaves, or wood chips.
  - Mulch helps retain moisture, suppress weeds, and improve soil as it breaks down.

- Water efficiently
  - Water deeply and less frequently rather than shallowly every day.
  - Water near the base of plants to reduce disease risk.
  - Early morning is often the best time to water.

- Encourage beneficial insects
  - Grow flowering plants that attract pollinators and natural predators.
  - Ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps can help control pests naturally.

- Use natural pest management
  - Inspect plants regularly for early signs of problems.
  - Hand-pick pests when practical.
  - Use physical barriers such as row covers and netting.
  - Consider organic-approved treatments only when necessary and according to label instructions.

- Compost garden waste
  - Composting recycles nutrients and reduces waste.
  - Maintain a balance of "green" materials (fresh plant matter) and "brown" materials (dry leaves, straw, cardboard).

- Control weeds proactively
  - Mulch and dense planting can reduce weed growth.
  - Remove weeds before they set seed.

- Support biodiversity
  - Mix different plant species rather than planting large monocultures.
  - Include native plants where possible to support local wildlife and pollinators.

- Test your soil
  - Periodic soil testing helps identify nutrient deficiencies and pH issues.
  - Amend soil based on test results rather than guessing.

- Keep records
  - Track planting dates, harvests, pest issues, and successful varieties.
  - Records help improve future garden planning.
