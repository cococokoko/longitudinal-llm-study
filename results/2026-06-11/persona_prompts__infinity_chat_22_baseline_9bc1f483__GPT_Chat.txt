Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different Christian traditions and thinkers understand discipleship in different ways, though most agree that it involves following Jesus and being transformed by his teachings. Here are some major views:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Discipleship is often understood as a personal relationship with Jesus that leads to spiritual growth.
  - Emphasis is placed on Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and obedience to Christ.
  - Many evangelicals distinguish between conversion (becoming a Christian) and the ongoing process of discipleship.

• Reformed view
  - Focuses on discipleship as growth in holiness through God's grace.
  - Strong emphasis on Scripture, church membership, and spiritual disciplines.
  - Discipleship is often seen within the framework of God's sovereignty and covenant community.

• Lutheran view
  - Emphasizes faith in Christ and the believer's identity as simultaneously justified and still struggling with sin.
  - Discipleship is viewed as daily repentance and trust in God's promises rather than a process of earning favor with God.
  - The Word and sacraments play a central role.

• Roman Catholic view
  - Discipleship is a lifelong journey of following Christ through faith, participation in the sacraments, prayer, and works of love.
  - Growth in holiness (sanctification) is a key aspect.
  - The Church is seen as the primary community in which discipleship occurs.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Emphasizes participation in the life of God (theosis or deification).
  - Discipleship involves prayer, worship, ascetic practices, and spiritual transformation.
  - The goal is union with God while remaining fully human.

• Anabaptist view
  - Stresses practical obedience to Jesus' teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Often emphasizes nonviolence, simplicity, community, and visible Christian living.
  - Discipleship is considered central to Christian identity.

• Wesleyan/Methodist view
  - Focuses on sanctification and growth in love for God and neighbor.
  - Encourages accountability, spiritual disciplines, and social action.
  - Emphasizes the possibility of significant spiritual maturity through grace.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Highlights the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, and active ministry.
  - Personal experience of God's presence is often emphasized.

• Liberation theology perspective
  - Views discipleship as following Jesus through solidarity with the poor and oppressed.
  - Emphasizes social justice, liberation, and transformation of unjust structures.
  - Often connects spiritual growth with social action.

• Radical discipleship movement
  - Emphasizes costly obedience to Jesus, sometimes drawing on Dietrich Bonhoeffer's concept of "costly grace."
  - Calls for sacrificial living, service, and a countercultural lifestyle.
  - Often challenges nominal or merely cultural Christianity.

Common themes across many traditions include:
• Following Jesus as Lord.
• Learning and obeying his teachings.
• Spiritual transformation.
• Participation in a faith community.
• Service to others.
• Growth in love, faith, and holiness.

The main differences usually concern how transformation occurs (grace, spiritual disciplines, sacraments, community, the Holy Spirit), the role of the Church, and whether discipleship is understood primarily as personal spiritual growth, communal formation, social action, or some combination of these.
