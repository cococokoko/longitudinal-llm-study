This is a substantive question worth thinking through carefully rather than just offering platitudes.

## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately shape international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO) to reflect their interests
- This creates rules that often entrench existing advantages rather than neutral frameworks
- Debt relationships create genuine dependency that constrains sovereign decision-making

**Bargaining asymmetries**
- Economic desperation limits negotiating leverage for poorer states
- Trade agreements frequently reflect power differentials more than mutual benefit
- Sanctions work asymmetrically - they're devastating tools against economically vulnerable states

## Where Analysis Gets Complicated

**Competing interpretations matter**
- Dependency theorists see inequality as deliberately maintained exploitation
- Liberal institutionalists argue integration ultimately benefits all parties
- Realists focus on relative power regardless of absolute gains
- Evidence supports elements of each framework in different contexts

**Empirical tensions**
- Some previously poor nations have dramatically improved positions (South Korea, China)
- Whether this validates or challenges the system is genuinely contested
- China's rise specifically is reshaping traditional North-South dynamics

## Practically Relevant Considerations

- Economic inequality drives migration pressures affecting diplomatic relationships
- Climate negotiations are fundamentally shaped by historical emissions inequality
- Resource dependencies create strategic vulnerabilities exploited geopolitically

**What's your specific analytical interest?** The legal dimensions - treaty enforcement, investment arbitration, sovereign debt restructuring - involve particularly interesting power asymmetries worth examining closely.
