── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at a well-established law firm, your social media strategy must be fundamentally different from that of a solo practitioner or a junior lawyer. You are not just representing yourself; you are representing the institutional prestige of your firm, while simultaneously positioning yourself as an indispensable, modern thought leader.

Your goal is not "virality" or "clout." Your goal is **authority, trust, and high-value origination (client acquisition).**

Here is a strategic blueprint for building a high-impact personal brand on social media as a senior attorney.

---

### 1. Platform Selection: Where to Play
As a senior attorney, your time is highly valuable. Do not spread yourself thin. Focus on one or two platforms where your clients actually make decisions.

*   **LinkedIn (Primary - 80% of effort):** This is your digital office. It is the premier platform for B2B networking, corporate clients, in-house counsel, and referral sources.
*   **X / Twitter (Secondary - Optional):** Useful if your practice area intersects with politics, public policy, tech, venture capital, or finance.
*   **YouTube / Podcasts (Secondary - Optional):** Excellent for deep-dive authority building, but requires a significant time investment.
*   **TikTok / Instagram:** Generally avoid, unless your practice is highly consumer-facing (e.g., high-net-worth family law, personal injury) and you have a specific, highly polished strategy.

---

### 2. Defining Your "Niche within a Niche"
Do not try to be "the lawyer who knows everything." Your brand should be built around a specific **macro-theme** and a **micro-specialty**.

*   *Weak Brand:* "I am a corporate partner at [Firm Name]."
*   *Strong Brand:* "I help Series A tech startups navigate cross-border regulatory hurdles."
*   *Weak Brand:* "I am a commercial litigator."
*   *Strong Brand:* "I write about the intersection of AI, intellectual property, and trade secret protection."

**Ask yourself:** What is the #1 problem my ideal client lies awake at night worrying about? Your content should solve that problem.

---

### 3. The Content Strategy: The "3-Tier" Framework
To maintain your reputation as a senior practitioner, your content must be sophisticated, accurate, and engaging. Use this three-tier framework for your posts:

#### Tier 1: The "Insightful Translator" (50% of content)
Translate complex legal updates, Supreme Court rulings, or regulatory changes into plain English for business leaders.
*   *Example:* "The FTC just banned non-competes. Here are the 3 immediate steps every CEO needs to take today to protect their trade secrets."
*   *Why it works:* It proves your utility and shows you are at the cutting edge of your field.

#### Tier 2: The "Behind-the-Scenes" Strategist (30% of content)
Share lessons learned from your decades of practice without violating attorney-client privilege. Use anonymized case studies.
*   *Example:* "A client recently came to us after a $10M acquisition almost fell through due to a single clause in an IP licensing agreement. Here is how we restructured the deal in 48 hours, and the lesson for any founder looking to exit."
*   *Why it works:* It demonstrates your real-world experience and problem-solving capabilities.

#### Tier 3: The Humanized Leader (20% of content)
People do business with people they like and trust. Share your thoughts on mentorship, firm culture, diversity in law, or your personal productivity habits.
*   *Example:* "After 20 years in BigLaw, I’ve realized the best lawyers aren't the ones who work 80 hours a week—they are the ones who know how to ask the right questions. Here is how I mentor my associates to think critically."
*   *Why it works:* It builds rapport and aids in recruiting top talent to your firm.

---

### 4. Navigating Firm Politics and Compliance
As a senior attorney at a well-established firm, you must navigate internal politics and compliance.

*   **Get Buy-In Early:** Speak with your firm’s marketing/PR department and managing partner. Frame your personal branding as a **business development engine for the firm**, not just a vanity project.
*   **Establish Boundaries:** Never comment on active cases your firm is handling (unless coordinated with PR). Never give explicit legal advice online.
*   **The Disclaimer:** Always include a standard disclaimer in your profile bio (e.g., "Opinions mine. Not legal advice. No attorney-client relationship formed.")

---

### 5. Execution: How to Do This in 2 Hours a Week
You do not have time to post every day manually. You must build a system.

1.  **The "Batching" Method:** Spend 90 minutes on Sunday morning writing 3–4 high-quality posts for the week.
2.  **Use a Scheduler:** Use tools like Buffer, Publer, or Taplio to schedule your posts throughout the week.
3.  **The "Golden 20 Minutes":** Spend 10 minutes before you post and 10 minutes after engaging with other people's content. Comment on posts by in-house counsel, industry leaders, and potential clients. **Your comments are often more valuable than your posts.**

---

### 6. Optimizing Your LinkedIn Profile (Your Digital Landing Page)
When someone reads your post, they will click on your profile. It must look like a premium digital brochure.

*   **The Headline:** Do not just put "Partner at [Firm]." Use: **[What you do] + [Who you help] + [The result].**
    *   *Example:* "Partner at [Firm] | Helping Tech Founders Navigate IP Disputes & Protect Their Assets | Author & Speaker"
*   **The About Section:** Write in the first person ("I"), not the third person ("Mr. Smith is..."). Tell a story about *why* you practice law and *how* you help clients.
*   **The Featured Section:** Pin your best posts, a link to your firm bio, a recent media appearance, or a newsletter.

2.  **Identify 5 key topics** you can speak on with absolute authority.
3.  **Write your first "Insightful Translator" post** breaking down a recent industry development.
