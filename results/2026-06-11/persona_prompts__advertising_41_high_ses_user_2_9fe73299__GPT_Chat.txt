── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If your goal is to improve your credit score as quickly as possible, focus on the factors that can change within one or two reporting cycles:

- Lower your credit utilization
  - This is often the fastest way to see a meaningful increase.
  - Pay down revolving credit card balances, especially cards that are above 30% utilization.
  - For the strongest impact, aim for below 10% utilization on both individual cards and overall credit limits.

- Make all payments on time
  - Payment history is a major scoring factor.
  - If you have any account that is currently past due, bringing it current can help prevent further damage.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Obtain reports from the major credit bureaus.
  - Dispute inaccuracies such as incorrect late payments, balances, or accounts that do not belong to you.
  - Corrections can sometimes produce a significant score increase.

- Ask for a credit limit increase
  - If approved and your spending remains the same, your utilization ratio drops.
  - Some issuers allow this without a hard inquiry; confirm before requesting.

- Avoid applying for new credit unless necessary
  - New applications can create hard inquiries and reduce the average age of your accounts.
  - Multiple applications in a short period can suppress your score.

- Keep older accounts open
  - Closing accounts can reduce available credit and increase utilization.
  - Longer account history generally helps.

- Consider becoming an authorized user
  - If a trusted family member has a long-established account with low utilization and excellent payment history, being added as an authorized user may help, depending on the scoring model and issuer reporting practices.

- If you have collections
  - Paying or settling collections may help under some scoring models, though the impact varies.
  - Request written confirmation of any settlement agreement.

Typical timelines:
- Utilization changes: often reflected within 30–60 days after lenders report updated balances.
- Error corrections: often 30–60 days, depending on the dispute process.
- Payment history improvements: generally take longer and build over time.
