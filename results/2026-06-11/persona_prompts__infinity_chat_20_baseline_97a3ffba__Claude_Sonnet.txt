── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth thinking through carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## Actually, the premise deserves scrutiny

**Arguments people make against mandating equal pay:**
- Enforcement is complex and costly
- Defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult
- May create unintended consequences like reduced hiring of protected groups
- Wage differences sometimes reflect legitimate factors (experience, negotiation, location)

## But strong counterarguments exist

**The premise may be wrong because:**
- Wage discrimination represents market failure, not market efficiency
- Unequal pay for equal work is economically wasteful - misallocating human capital
- It reduces productivity and worker morale
- Evidence suggests gender/racial pay gaps persist even controlling for relevant variables
- Many economies successfully implement equal pay frameworks

## What the evidence actually shows
- Countries with stronger equal pay enforcement don't show obvious economic damage
- Discrimination persists without intervention due to information asymmetries and power imbalances
- The "free market corrects discrimination" argument has weak empirical support historically

## The honest assessment
The question contains an assumption that equal pay mandates harm economies, but this is **contested empirically**. Reasonable people disagree about *mechanisms* of enforcement, not necessarily the principle.
