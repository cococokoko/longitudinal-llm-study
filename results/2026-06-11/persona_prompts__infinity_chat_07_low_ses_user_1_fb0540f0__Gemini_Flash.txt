I work full-time in a fast food job with an unpredictable schedule.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is **yes, it is absolutely impossible to know everything.** 

In fact, even the most brilliant scientists, philosophers, and scholars in history have concluded that the more we learn, the more we realize we don’t know. 

But looking at your situation—working a full-time, unpredictable fast-food job—this question likely isn't just a philosophical one. It sounds like it might be coming from a place of feeling overwhelmed, exhausted, or pressured to keep up with a world that demands too much of your time and energy.

Here is why it is impossible to know everything, and why accepting that is actually a form of freedom—especially for someone in your shoes.

### 1. The "Information Explosion"
The sheer volume of human knowledge is growing at an exponential rate. It is estimated that human knowledge used to double every century; now, with the internet, it doubles every few hours or days. No single human brain has the storage capacity, let alone the lifespan, to process even a fraction of a percent of it. 

### 2. Your Brain Has Limited "Bandwidth"
You work a grueling, unpredictable job. Fast food requires constant physical energy, split-second decision-making, customer service emotional labor, and adaptation to sudden schedule changes. 

When your schedule is unpredictable, your brain is constantly in a state of low-level stress, trying to plan around chaos. This drains your **cognitive bandwidth**. You do not have the mental surplus right now to "know everything," because your brain is rightfully prioritizing survival, rest, and paying the bills.

### 3. The "Dunning-Kruger" Effect
People who think they know everything usually know very little. True wisdom is realizing your own limitations. Accepting that you cannot know everything is actually a sign of high intelligence and self-awareness.

---

### How to find peace with "not knowing" in your situation:

*   **Define "Need-to-Know" vs. "Nice-to-Know":** When you have limited time, ruthlessly filter what you let into your brain. You need to know your schedule, how to do your job safely, how to manage your finances, and how to care for your health. Everything else—politics, pop culture, deep academic subjects—is optional. It is okay to say, *"I don't have the bandwidth to care about or know about that right now."*
*   **Protect your rest:** An unpredictable schedule ruins sleep cycles. When you aren't working, your priority shouldn't be "learning more" or "doing more." It should be resting. Rest is not wasted time; it is maintenance.
*   **Forgive yourself:** If you feel behind in life, or feel like you don't know as much as others, remember that you are playing the game of life on "hard mode" right now. Working full-time in fast food with a chaotic schedule is exhausting. Give yourself credit for surviving and working hard.

You don't need to know everything. You just need to know what keeps you healthy, safe, and moving toward whatever your next step in life is. Everything else is just noise.
