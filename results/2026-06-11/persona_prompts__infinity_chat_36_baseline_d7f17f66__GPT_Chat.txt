── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, it would attract capital until the edge largely disappeared. Markets are competitive and adaptive.

That said, some of the most successful day trading approaches historically have shared common characteristics:

- Market making
  - Continuously buying at the bid and selling at the ask.
  - Profits come from capturing spreads and managing inventory risk.
  - Historically one of the most profitable trading businesses, but it requires technology, speed, and significant infrastructure.

- Statistical arbitrage
  - Exploits temporary pricing discrepancies between related assets.
  - Often highly quantitative and automated.
  - Widely used by hedge funds and proprietary trading firms.

- Momentum trading
  - Buys assets showing strong upward movement and sells weak assets.
  - Common among discretionary day traders.
  - Can be highly profitable during trending markets but suffers during choppy conditions.

- Mean reversion
  - Assumes prices temporarily move too far from equilibrium and then revert.
  - Often effective in range-bound markets.
  - Can experience large losses when trends persist.

- News/event trading
  - Trades around earnings, economic reports, and breaking news.
  - Success depends on speed, interpretation, and risk management.

For individual retail traders, one of the most studied and practical approaches is a momentum strategy combined with strict risk management:
- Trade highly liquid assets.
- Focus on strong relative strength or weakness.
- Use predefined stop losses.
- Keep position sizes small relative to account size.
- Cut losses quickly and let winners run.
- Track performance statistically over hundreds of trades.

A key point: the profitability of a strategy is usually driven less by the entry signal and more by:
- Risk management
- Position sizing
- Transaction costs
- Discipline
- Consistent execution

In professional trading, many firms would rather have a modest edge with excellent risk management than a "perfect" entry signal with poor risk controls.

If your goal is maximizing expected long-term returns rather than the excitement of day trading, broad-market index investing has historically outperformed the vast majority of active traders after costs and taxes.
