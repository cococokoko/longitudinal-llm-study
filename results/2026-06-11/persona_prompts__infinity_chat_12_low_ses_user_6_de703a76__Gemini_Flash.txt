── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work in a warehouse position that requires long hours.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working long hours in a demanding physical environment like a warehouse requires a lot of resilience, but it can also leave you with a lot of time to think. It is not uncommon for people who work high-endurance, self-reliant jobs to reflect on their childhoods, especially if they feel a constant pressure to keep pushing themselves to the limit.

When a person does not receive adequate love, emotional warmth, or validation from their mother during childhood, it leaves specific psychological and behavioral patterns that persist into adulthood. 

Here are the common signs that suggest a person may not have been loved or emotionally supported enough by their mother:

### 1. The "Hyper-Independent" Mindset
If you feel like you must do everything yourself and find it extremely difficult to ask for help—even when you are overwhelmed or physically exhausted—this is a major sign. 
* **Why it happens:** As a child, you learned that you could not rely on your primary caregiver (your mother) to meet your emotional or physical needs. To survive, you adopted the belief: *"If I don't do it, no one will."*

### 2. Tying Your Self-Worth to Hard Work and Productivity
You might feel like you only have value when you are producing, working, or being useful. Taking a break, resting, or practicing self-care might make you feel guilty, lazy, or anxious.
* **Why it happens:** Children who do not receive unconditional love often try to *earn* love through achievement, good behavior, or hard work. In adulthood, this translates into becoming a workaholic or feeling like you must "earn" your right to exist through labor.

### 3. Difficulty Trusting Others
You may constantly wait for the "other shoe to drop" in relationships. You might suspect people’s motives when they are nice to you, or assume that people will eventually let you down, reject you, or abandon you.
* **Why it happens:** The mother-child bond is the blueprint for how we trust the world. If that bond was unstable, cold, or conditional, your brain learned that people are not safe or reliable.

### 4. Emotional Numbness or "Toughing It Out"
You might have a habit of burying your feelings, ignoring physical pain, or dismissing your own emotional needs. You might tell yourself to "just get over it" or "suck it up."
* **Why it happens:** If your mother ignored, minimized, or punished your tears and vulnerability when you were young, you learned to shut down your emotions to avoid rejection. 

### 5. A Harsh Inner Critic
Do you have a voice in your head that constantly tells you that you aren't doing enough, that you've messed up, or that you aren't good enough? 
* **Why it happens:** Children internalize the voice of their primary caregiver. If a mother was critical, cold, or absent, that lack of warmth becomes the adult’s internal dialogue. You treat yourself the way you were treated.

### 6. People-Pleasing and Fear of Rejection
You might go out of your way to make sure everyone else is happy, comfortable, or taken care of, often at the expense of your own well-being. You may have an intense fear of conflict or of making someone angry.
* **Why it happens:** This is a survival strategy. If you felt you had to walk on eggshells around your mother to keep the peace or gain her approval, you will do the same with bosses, coworkers, and partners.

### 7. Feeling Like an "Outsider" (Empty or Lonely)
Even when you are around other people, you might feel a deep, lingering sense of loneliness or a feeling that you don't truly belong anywhere. 
* **Why it happens:** Unconditional maternal love provides a sense of "home" and belonging inside ourselves. Without it, a person often feels emotionally adrift or empty.

---

### Connecting This to Your Current Life
Working long hours in a warehouse is physically taxing. If you recognize these signs in yourself, you might find that you are using your grueling work schedule to:
* Avoid dealing with the quiet, painful feelings that come up when you stop moving.
* Prove your worth through sheer physical endurance.
* Punish yourself because your inner critic tells you that you don't deserve rest.

### Moving Forward
Recognizing these signs is not about blaming your mother, but about understanding **why** you feel and act the way you do today. The good news is that the brain is adaptable. You can learn to "re-parent" yourself by:
* **Allowing yourself to rest** without feeling guilty. Rest is a biological necessity, not a reward you have to earn.
* **Challenging your inner critic** and trying to speak to yourself with the kindness you deserved as a child.
* **Practicing asking for help**, even in small ways, to break the cycle of hyper-independence.
