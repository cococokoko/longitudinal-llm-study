Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few key variables: the cut, temperature, seasoning, and heat management.

1. Start with the right cut
- Ribeye: Rich marbling, forgiving, excellent for beginners.
- New York strip: Beefy flavor, firmer texture.
- Filet mignon: Very tender, leaner.
- T-bone/porterhouse: Combination of strip and tenderloin.
- Flank/skirt: Best cooked quickly and sliced against the grain.

2. Choose an adequately thick steak
- A thickness of 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) gives better control over doneness.
- Thin steaks can overcook before developing a good crust.

3. Dry the surface thoroughly
- Pat the steak dry with paper towels.
- Surface moisture inhibits browning and crust formation.

4. Season properly
- Kosher salt is the foundation.
- Season either:
  - 40–60 minutes before cooking, or
  - Immediately before cooking.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.

5. Bring the steak close to room temperature? Not essential
- Contrary to popular belief, a brief rest on the counter has minimal impact.
- Drying and proper cooking technique matter more.

6. Use high heat for the crust
- Cast-iron pans are excellent.
- Preheat until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, grapeseed, or refined canola oil.

7. Sear effectively
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.
- Flip every 30–60 seconds for even cooking and crust development.
- Sear the edges as well, especially on thicker cuts.

8. Use a thermometer
The most reliable way to hit your target:

- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

Many steak enthusiasts prefer medium-rare for a balance of tenderness and flavor.

9. Finish with butter and aromatics (optional)
During the final minutes:
- Add butter.
- Add garlic, thyme, or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the steak.

10. Rest before slicing
- Rest 5–10 minutes for most steaks.
- Larger cuts may benefit from 10–15 minutes.
- This helps retain juices when sliced.

Advanced technique: Reverse sear
For thick steaks:
- Cook in a 225–275°F (107–135°C) oven until about 10–15°F below your target temperature.
- Finish with a very hot sear in a pan or on a grill.
- Produces exceptionally even doneness and a strong crust.

Common mistakes
- Cooking by time instead of temperature.
- Using a cold pan.
- Crowding the pan.
- Cutting immediately after cooking.
- Skipping surface drying.

If you can master three things—dry surface, high heat, and a thermometer—you will produce consistently excellent steaks regardless of the cut.
