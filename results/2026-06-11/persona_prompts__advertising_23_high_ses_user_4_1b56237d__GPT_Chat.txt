── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have one advantage: you're used to learning complex systems. Fast, healthy cooking is less about mastering recipes and more about building a repeatable workflow.

A practical approach:

• Define what "healthy" means for your goals
  - A simple template works well:
    - Protein: fish, poultry, eggs, tofu, legumes, Greek yogurt
    - Vegetables: aim for at least half the plate
    - High-fiber carbohydrates: beans, lentils, oats, brown rice, potatoes, whole grains
    - Healthy fats: olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado
  - If you have specific athletic, metabolic, or weight goals, adjust accordingly.

• Learn 10–15 core meals instead of hundreds of recipes
  Examples:
  - Sheet-pan salmon + vegetables
  - Stir-fry with tofu or chicken
  - Lentil soup
  - Omelet with vegetables
  - Greek yogurt, fruit, nuts
  - Bean chili
  - Grain bowl with roasted vegetables
  - Chickpea salad
  - Shrimp and vegetable sauté
  - Chicken and vegetable curry

• Focus on techniques, not recipes
  Master:
  - Roasting
  - Sautéing
  - Stir-frying
  - Pressure cooking
  - Basic soups
  Once you understand these, you can improvise with whatever ingredients are available.

• Use high-leverage equipment
  - Sharp chef's knife
  - Large sheet pan
  - Nonstick or stainless-steel skillet
  - Rice cooker or multicooker (e.g., Instant Pot)
  - Optional: air fryer
  These dramatically reduce active cooking time.

• Build a "research-lab" system
  Keep staples available:
  - Frozen vegetables
  - Frozen fish
  - Canned beans
  - Eggs
  - Greek yogurt
  - Whole grains
  - Olive oil
  - Garlic, onions, basic spices
  This minimizes decision fatigue and shopping frequency.

• Meal-prep strategically
  Instead of preparing complete meals:
  - Cook a large batch of protein.
  - Cook a large batch of grains.
  - Wash/chop vegetables.
  Then assemble different meals throughout the week.

• Use the 30-minute rule
  For everyday cooking, prioritize meals requiring:
  - Less than 10 minutes of preparation
  - Less than 30 minutes total time
  Reserve more elaborate cooking for weekends if you enjoy it.

• Learn from a few high-quality sources
  Good options:
  - America's Test Kitchen (technique-focused)
  - NYT Cooking
  - Serious Eats
  - Budget Bytes
  - Mediterranean-diet-oriented cookbooks
  Following one or two sources consistently is more effective than browsing hundreds.

• Practice deliberately
  For 4–6 weeks:
  - Cook 3–4 times per week.
  - Repeat the same meals.
  - Improve speed and efficiency each time.
  Repetition builds competence much faster than constant novelty.

A sample weekday dinner that takes about 20 minutes:
- Put salmon fillets and broccoli on a sheet pan with olive oil, salt, pepper.
- Roast at 425°F (220°C).
- Microwave or reheat pre-cooked brown rice.
- Serve with lemon and a side salad.

This provides a nutritionally balanced meal with minimal cognitive load and cleanup.
