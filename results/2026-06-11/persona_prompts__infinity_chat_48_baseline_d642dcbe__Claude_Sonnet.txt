The Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) witnessed remarkable economic development that transformed China into one of the ancient world's most prosperous civilizations. The early Han emperors adopted a policy of reduced taxation and minimal government interference, allowing the agricultural sector to recover from the devastation of the preceding Qin Dynasty and the civil wars that followed it. As stability was restored, the government implemented key economic reforms, including the standardization of currency through the introduction of the wushu coin, which facilitated trade and commerce across the vast empire. The state also took control of lucrative industries such as salt and iron production, generating significant government revenue while regulating prices and supply. Perhaps most significantly, the Han Dynasty oversaw the opening and expansion of the Silk Road, the network of trade routes connecting China to Central Asia, the Middle East, and eventually Rome, enabling the export of silk, porcelain, and other Chinese goods in exchange for foreign products such as glassware, precious metals, and horses. This flourishing long-distance trade, combined with advances in agricultural technology such as improved iron tools and irrigation systems, helped sustain a growing population and contributed to the cultural and material prosperity that defined the Han Dynasty as a golden age in Chinese history.
