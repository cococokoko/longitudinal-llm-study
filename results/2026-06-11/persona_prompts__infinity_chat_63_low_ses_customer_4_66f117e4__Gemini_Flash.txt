Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To understand a **partial derivative**, it helps to think of a scenario where an outcome depends on more than one factor. 

Since you work in a hospital, let’s use a clinical example that you likely see every day: **calculating a patient’s fluid balance**. 

A patient’s net fluid balance ($B$) depends on two main variables:
1. **Intake ($I$):** Fluids given via IV or taken orally (in milliliters, mL).
2. **Output ($O$):** Fluids lost via urine, drains, etc. (in mL).

Let's say a patient's net fluid balance is calculated by this simple formula:
$$B(I, O) = I - O$$

A **partial derivative** tells us how the final result (the fluid balance) changes when we change *only one* of those variables, while keeping the other one completely constant.

---

### The Numerical Example

Let's look at a patient, Mr. Jones, at a specific moment in time:
* **Current Intake ($I$)** = $1,500\text{ mL}$
* **Current Output ($O$)** = $1,000\text{ mL}$

His current fluid balance is:
$$B = 1,500 - 1,000 = +500\text{ mL}$$

Now, let's look at the two partial derivatives for this scenario.

#### 1. Partial Derivative with respect to Intake: $\frac{\partial B}{\partial I}$
This asks: *"If we increase Mr. Jones's IV intake slightly, but his urine output stays exactly the same, how does his fluid balance change?"*

* Let's increase his intake by $1\text{ mL}$ (from $1,500$ to $1,501\text{ mL}$), while keeping his output locked at exactly $1,000\text{ mL}$.
* New Balance = $1,501 - 1,000 = +501\text{ mL}$.
* The balance increased by exactly $1\text{ mL}$.

So, the partial derivative of Balance with respect to Intake is:
$$\frac{\partial B}{\partial I} = +1$$
*(For every $1\text{ mL}$ increase in intake, the balance goes up by $1\text{ mL}$, assuming output is constant).*

#### 2. Partial Derivative with respect to Output: $\frac{\partial B}{\partial O}$
This asks: *"If Mr. Jones's output increases slightly, but we keep his IV intake exactly the same, how does his fluid balance change?"*

* Let's increase his output by $1\text{ mL}$ (from $1,000$ to $1,001\text{ mL}$), while keeping his intake locked at exactly $1,500\text{ mL}$.
* New Balance = $1,500 - 1,001 = +499\text{ mL}$.
* The balance decreased by $1\text{ mL}$ (which is a change of $-1$).

So, the partial derivative of Balance with respect to Output is:
$$\frac{\partial B}{\partial O} = -1$$
*(For every $1\text{ mL}$ increase in output, the balance goes down by $1\text{ mL}$, assuming intake is constant).*

---

### Why is this "Partial"?
In a real hospital shift, both intake and output are changing at the same time. However, to understand the math of how the system works, we have to isolate them. 

By freezing one variable in place, we can calculate the **partial** effect of the other. That is the core concept of a partial derivative!
