André Gorz is one of the most fertile thinkers for contemporary Marxist theory, particularly because his work bridges the gap between traditional historical materialism, ecological crisis, and the changing nature of work. 

Since you are looking for *new* ideas for a thesis, the goal should be to put Gorz’s mid-to-late 20th-century concepts into conversation with 21st-century material realities. 

Here are four novel thematic directions and thesis angles you could explore:

---

### 1. The "Precarity of the Probationary State" and the Non-Class of Non-Workers
In *Farewell to the Working Class* (1980), Gorz famously introduced the "non-class of non-workers"—those who are underemployed, temporarily employed, or entirely alienated from the traditional identity of "the worker." 

*   **The Thesis Idea:** Analyze how the modern corporate structure uses **extended probationary periods, gig-work contracts, and perpetual precarity** as a deliberate tool of class fragmentation. 
*   **The Gorzian Angle:** Traditional Marxism often views probation or precarity as a temporary transition phase before full integration into the proletariat. A Gorzian analysis would argue that **precarity is no longer a transition, but the destination**. You could explore how the psychological anxiety of "always being on trial" (whether in a new corporate job, a gig platform, or academia) prevents the formation of class consciousness, and how Gorz’s proposal for a Universal Basic Income (UBI) is the only way to sever the link between survival and the performance of "worthiness" to employers.

### 2. Platform Capitalism and the "Colonization of Free Time"
Gorz was deeply concerned with how capitalism colonizes life outside of the workplace. In *Critique of Economic Reason*, he argues that the sphere of autonomy (free time) must be defended against the sphere of heteronomy (market-driven work).

*   **The Thesis Idea:** A critique of the "creator economy," social media monetization, and "side-hustle" culture through a Gorzian lens.
*   **The Gorzian Angle:** Today, algorithms and platform capitalism have turned leisure, hobbies, and social interaction into data-generating labor. When a graduate student writes a Substack, or a hobbyist monetizes their crafts on Etsy, they are converting their "sphere of autonomy" into market value. You could argue that platform capitalism has successfully bypassed Gorz’s defense of free time, turning the "non-worker" into a self-exploiting entrepreneur.

### 3. Political Ecology: Gorz, Degrowth, and the "Eco-Modernist" Debate
Gorz was one of the founders of political ecology (*Ecology as Politics*, 1975). He argued that ecological sustainability is fundamentally incompatible with capitalism because capitalism requires infinite growth.

*   **The Thesis Idea:** Use Gorz to arbitrate the current debate between **Left Eco-Modernism/Accelerationism** (which advocates for high-tech, state-planned growth to solve climate change) and **Degrowth/Post-Growth Marxism**.
*   **The Gorzian Angle:** Gorz was highly skeptical of techno-utopian solutions that centralize power in the hands of experts and state bureaucrats. A great thesis could reconstruct a "Gorzian Eco-Socialism" that rejects both capitalist greenwashing and authoritarian state-socialist industrialism, advocating instead for a decentralized, high-tech but low-energy "convivial" society (drawing on his connection to Ivan Illich).

### 4. The Cognitive Proletariat and the "Intangible Economy"
In his later works, like *Immaterial Capitalism* (2003), Gorz focused on how knowledge, intelligence, and affect have become the primary forces of production.

*   **The Thesis Idea:** The alienation of the "knowledge worker" in the age of Generative AI.
*   **The Gorzian Angle:** Gorz argued that knowledge cannot be private property in the same way physical machinery is, which creates a structural crisis for capitalism. You could examine how the rise of LLMs and AI is an attempt by capital to expropriate the collective intelligence of the "cognitive proletariat" (writers, coders, artists) and turn it into proprietary software. How does Gorz’s theory of "immaterial labor" help us understand the current resistance of creative and intellectual workers?
