# How Stress Affects the Body and Mind

Stress is a natural response designed to help us survive threatening situations, but when it becomes chronic, it can take a serious toll on both physical and mental health.

## Physical Effects

When the brain perceives a threat, it triggers the release of hormones like **cortisol and adrenaline**. These chemicals prepare the body for a "fight or flight" response — increasing heart rate, tensing muscles, and sharpening focus. While useful in short bursts, prolonged exposure to these hormones can lead to:

- High blood pressure and increased risk of heart disease
- Weakened immune system
- Digestive problems and headaches
- Disrupted sleep patterns
- Chronic muscle tension and fatigue

## Mental and Emotional Effects

Stress doesn't just affect the body — it significantly impacts the mind. Persistent stress can impair **memory and concentration**, making everyday tasks feel overwhelming. It is closely linked to anxiety and depression, as elevated cortisol levels disrupt the brain's chemical balance. People under chronic stress may experience irritability, mood swings, and a reduced ability to make decisions.

## The Cycle of Stress

One of the most challenging aspects of stress is that it can become self-reinforcing. Poor sleep caused by stress leads to reduced resilience, which makes stressors feel even more unmanageable.

## Managing Stress

Fortunately, healthy habits can interrupt this cycle. Regular exercise, mindfulness, adequate sleep, and strong social connections are all proven strategies for reducing stress and protecting long-term wellbeing.

Understanding how stress works is the first step toward managing it effectively and maintaining a healthier, more balanced life.
