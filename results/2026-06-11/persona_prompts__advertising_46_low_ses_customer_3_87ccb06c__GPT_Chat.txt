What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you're starting a plant-based diet, it helps to use a mix of reliable nutrition resources, simple recipes, and practical meal-planning tools. Since you're also settling into a new job, resources that emphasize quick, sustainable meals may be especially useful.

Some of the best places to start:

• Nutrition information
  - NutritionFacts.org — Evidence-based information on plant-based nutrition, created by Dr. Michael Greger.
  - Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM) — Guides, meal plans, and nutrition articles.
  - Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health Nutrition Source — Balanced, science-based nutrition guidance.

• Beginner-friendly books
  - "How Not to Die" by Michael Greger, MD
  - "Plant-Based on a Budget" by Toni Okamoto
  - "The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners" by Gabriel Miller
  - "Forks Over Knives: The Cookbook" for simple meal ideas

• Recipe websites
  - Minimalist Baker — Many easy recipes with limited ingredients.
  - Budget Bytes — Has a dedicated vegetarian and vegan section with affordable meals.
  - Rainbow Plant Life — Flavorful recipes and meal-prep ideas.
  - Cookie and Kate — Well-tested vegetarian recipes.

• Meal planning and tracking
  - Cronometer — Useful for checking whether you're getting enough protein, iron, calcium, vitamin B12, and other nutrients.
  - Mealime — Simple meal planning with shopping lists.
  - Eat This Much — Automated meal planning based on your goals.

• YouTube channels
  - Pick Up Limes
  - Rainbow Plant Life
  - The Whole Food Plant Based Cooking Show
  - Plant Chompers (nutrition-focused)

Key nutrients to pay attention to:
• Vitamin B12 (most plant-based eaters should supplement it)
• Protein
• Iron
• Calcium
• Omega-3 fats
• Vitamin D (depending on sun exposure and location)

A simple way to begin is not to overhaul everything at once. Start by building meals around:
• Beans, lentils, tofu, tempeh, or edamame
• Whole grains such as oats, rice, quinoa, or whole-wheat pasta
• Fruits and vegetables
• Nuts and seeds
