I am an executive at a large technology company.

Can you give me a powerful rhetorical question for an essay about the harms of social media on teens?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To make a rhetorical question powerful for an executive audience, it should avoid clichés and instead focus on **accountability, systemic impact, and the tension between innovation and ethics.** 

Here are a few options, depending on the specific angle of your essay:

### Option 1: The "Product vs. Human" Angle (Best for focusing on business models)
> **"We have spent decades perfecting algorithms to capture human attention—but at what point did we decide that the price of that attention should be the mental well-being of an entire generation?"**
*   *Why it works:* It acknowledges the industry’s technological success ("perfecting algorithms") but immediately pivots to the ethical cost, framing it as a conscious trade-off that leaders must answer for.

### Option 2: The "Legacy and Responsibility" Angle (Best for a leadership/executive tone)
> **"If we measure the success of our platforms by engagement metrics, how do we account for the rising deficit in teenage self-esteem, sleep, and mental health?"**
*   *Why it works:* It speaks the language of business ("metrics," "deficit") to highlight the disconnect between corporate KPIs and human reality.

### Option 3: The "Unintended Consequences" Angle (Best for focusing on product design)
> **"We designed these platforms to connect the world, but have we instead created a mirror that reflects only our deepest insecurities, handed it to our children, and told them to look at it twenty-four hours a day?"**
*   *Why it works:* It contrasts the original, noble intent of tech ("to connect") with the visceral, daily reality of teen social media use.

### Option 4: The "Future Outlook" Angle (Best for a call-to-action conclusion)
> **"When the history of this digital golden age is written, will we be remembered as the architects of connection, or the bystanders of a youth mental health crisis?"**
*   *Why it works:* It appeals to the executive’s sense of legacy and long-term impact, forcing the reader to think about how current decisions will be judged in the future. 

**Recommendation:** If you want to strike a tone of **corporate responsibility and self-reflection**, go with **Option 1**. If you want to end on a **provocative call to action**, go with **Option 4**.
