── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive, you may be unusually skilled at determining what organizations need, what markets want, and what teams should do. Understanding what you yourself want is often a different problem.

A useful distinction is that there are at least four different things people mean when they ask "What do I want?":

- What do I enjoy?
- What do I value?
- What am I trying to achieve?
- What kind of life do I want to be living?

These can align, but often they do not.

Some approaches that tend to be effective:

- Examine your actual behavior, not your stated preferences.
  - Where do you voluntarily spend time when no one is requiring it?
  - What kinds of problems do you find yourself thinking about repeatedly?
  - What opportunities do you consistently accept or reject?
  - Your calendar and attention patterns often reveal desires that your self-narrative obscures.

- Distinguish inherited goals from chosen goals.
  - Many executives spend years pursuing goals that made sense at 25 and never revisit them.
  - Ask:
    - If I had my current wealth, reputation, and network already secured, what would I work on?
    - Which ambitions feel alive today, and which am I carrying because they once mattered?

- Pay attention to envy.
  - Envy is often informative.
  - Not necessarily envy of possessions, but of lives.
  - When you see someone and think, "I wish I had that," ask what exactly you want:
    - Their freedom?
    - Their impact?
    - Their creativity?
    - Their relationships?
  - The object of envy is often a clue; the underlying quality is usually the real desire.

- Notice what creates energy versus what merely creates satisfaction.
  - Satisfaction can come from competence, status, or obligation.
  - Energy is a different signal.
  - Which activities leave you more engaged after doing them?
  - Which leave you depleted even when you perform well?

- Run experiments instead of seeking certainty.
  - Executives are often accustomed to making high-stakes decisions.
  - Personal desires are often discovered through action rather than analysis.
  - Instead of asking:
    - "Do I want to become an investor?"
  - Ask:
    - "What happens if I spend six months behaving like one?"
  - Treat identity questions as experiments.

- Consider the possibility that you want multiple incompatible things.
  - Many people search for a single answer when the real situation is:
    - I want achievement.
    - I want freedom.
    - I want family closeness.
    - I want influence.
    - I want peace.
  - The challenge is not discovering one desire but deciding which desires will dominate when they conflict.

One question I have found particularly revealing is:

"Five years from now, if everything goes well, what would have to be true for me to feel that these years were well spent?"

Not what would impress others. Not what would maximize shareholder value. Not what would look successful on paper.

What would make you look back and think: "That was a good use of my finite time."

Your answer to that question is often closer to what you want than your answer to "What should I want?"

To tailor this more precisely: are you asking because you are considering a major career decision, or because you feel uncertain about your broader life direction despite professional success?
