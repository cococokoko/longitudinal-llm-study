### Project Title: 
**Halophyte Bio-Desalination: Utilizing Native Vietnamese Flora to Mitigate Soil Salinity in the Mekong Delta**

#### The Context & Problem (The "Why"):
The Mekong Delta, Vietnam’s "rice bowl," is facing an existential crisis due to climate change, rising sea levels, and upstream damming. Saltwater intrusion is destroying arable land, threatening food security and the livelihoods of millions of farmers. 

#### The Core Scientific Question:
*Can native, non-invasive Vietnamese halophytes (salt-tolerant plants) be used as a low-cost, biologically driven method (phytoremediation) to extract salt from agricultural soil, rendering it usable again for sensitive crops like rice?*

#### Why this is an Incredible STEM Project:
*   **High Intellectual Merit:** It touches on botany, soil chemistry, environmental engineering, and agricultural science.
*   **Highly Affordable:** It requires no expensive lab equipment—just soil, seeds, salt, and basic testing tools.
*   **Deeply Relevant to Vietnam:** It directly addresses UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 2 (Zero Hunger) and 13 (Climate Action) within the Vietnamese context.

---

### Experimental Design & Methodology

#### 1. Materials & Low-Cost Sourcing (Budget: < $50 USD)
*   **The Plants (Halophytes):** You need plants that accumulate salt in their tissues (hyperaccumulators). 
    *   *Vietnamese Context:* **Water Spinach (*Rau muống*)** has shown moderate salt tolerance, or **Purslane (*Rau sam*)**, a common, highly resilient weed in Vietnam known to tolerate saline conditions.
*   **The Crop:** Standard rice seeds (or a fast-growing proxy like wheatgrass if rice germination takes too long in your climate).
*   **Soil & Salt:** Standard potting soil and non-iodized sea salt (to mimic seawater).
*   **Measurement Tools:** A digital EC (Electrical Conductivity) soil meter (available online for $10–$15) to measure soil salinity.

#### 2. The Hypothesis
*“If [Selected Halophyte] is grown in saline-affected soil prior to planting a sensitive crop, then the soil salinity (EC) will decrease significantly, resulting in a higher germination and survival rate of the subsequent crop compared to untreated saline soil.”*

#### 3. Step-by-Step Procedure
*   **Phase I: Soil Salination.** Create a control group (normal soil) and experimental groups. Salinate the experimental soil using a salt-water solution to mimic the salinity levels currently found in the Mekong Delta (typically 4g/L to 10g/L of salt). Measure and record the baseline EC.
*   **Phase II: Phytoremediation.** Plant the chosen halophyte (e.g., Purslane) in the saline soil. Allow it to grow for 3–4 weeks. The plant will absorb the sodium and chloride ions through its roots and store them in its vacuoles.
*   **Phase III: Harvest & Soil Testing.** Harvest the halophytes (ensuring you remove the roots, as they contain the extracted salt). Measure the soil's EC again. You should see a quantifiable drop in salinity.
*   **Phase IV: The Bio-Assay (The Proof).** Plant your sensitive crop (rice/wheatgrass) in three different soils:
    1.  Untreated normal soil (Positive Control).
    2.  Salinated soil that *was not* treated with halophytes (Negative Control).
    3.  Salinated soil that *was* treated with halophytes (Experimental Group).
*   **Phase V: Data Collection.** Measure germination rates, shoot height, and biomass of the crop over 2 weeks.

3.  **Cost-Benefit Analysis:** Create a simple economic model showing how a Vietnamese farmer could implement this "crop rotation" method cheaply compared to expensive chemical soil treatments or building concrete dykes.
