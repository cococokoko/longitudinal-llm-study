── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To understand a partial derivative, it helps to think of it as measuring **how a change in only one variable affects the outcome, while all other variables are held constant.**

Here is a practical, numerical example using a simple real-world scenario: **calculating the area of a rectangle.**

---

### The Scenario: Area of a Rectangle
The area ($A$) of a rectangle depends on two variables: its **length ($L$)** and its **width ($W$)**. 
The formula is:
$$A(L, W) = L \times W$$

Because the area depends on more than one variable, we use **partial derivatives** to see how the area changes when we change only one dimension at a time.

*   $\frac{\partial A}{\partial L}$ (the partial derivative of Area with respect to Length) means: *"How does the area change if we increase the length slightly, but keep the width exactly the same?"*
*   $\frac{\partial A}{\partial W}$ (the partial derivative of Area with respect to Width) means: *"How does the area change if we increase the width slightly, but keep the length exactly the same?"*

---

### The Numerical Example

Let’s start with a specific rectangle:
*   **Length ($L$) = 10 cm**
*   **Width ($W$) = 5 cm**

At this moment, the **Area ($A$)** is:
$$A = 10 \times 5 = 50\text{ cm}^2$$

Now, let's calculate the two partial derivatives at this specific point $(10, 5)$.

---

#### Part 1: Partial Derivative with respect to Length ($\frac{\partial A}{\partial L}$)

We want to see what happens if we increase the length by a tiny amount, say **$\Delta L = 0.1\text{ cm}$**, while keeping the width **constant at $5\text{ cm}$**.

1.  **New Length:** $10 + 0.1 = 10.1\text{ cm}$
2.  **New Area:** $10.1 \times 5 = 50.5\text{ cm}^2$
3.  **Change in Area ($\Delta A$):** $50.5 - 50 = 0.5\text{ cm}^2$

The rate of change is:
$$\frac{\text{Change in Area}}{\text{Change in Length}} = \frac{\Delta A}{\Delta L} = \frac{0.5}{0.1} = 5$$

**Using Calculus:**
If we take the partial derivative of $A = L \times W$ with respect to $L$ (treating $W$ as a constant number):
$$\frac{\partial A}{\partial L} = W$$
At our specific point where $W = 5$, the partial derivative is **$5$**. 

**What this means:** For every $1\text{ cm}$ we add to the length (while keeping width at $5$), the area will increase by $5\text{ cm}^2$.

---

#### Part 2: Partial Derivative with respect to Width ($\frac{\partial A}{\partial W}$)

Now, let's do the opposite. We will increase the width by a tiny amount, say **$\Delta W = 0.1\text{ cm}$**, while keeping the length **constant at $10\text{ cm}$**.

1.  **New Width:** $5 + 0.1 = 5.1\text{ cm}$
2.  **New Area:** $10 \times 5.1 = 51.0\text{ cm}^2$
3.  **Change in Area ($\Delta A$):** $51.0 - 50 = 1.0\text{ cm}^2$

The rate of change is:
$$\frac{\text{Change in Area}}{\text{Change in Width}} = \frac{\Delta A}{\Delta W} = \frac{1.0}{0.1} = 10$$

**Using Calculus:**
If we take the partial derivative of $A = L \times W$ with respect to $W$ (treating $L$ as a constant number):
$$\frac{\partial A}{\partial W} = L$$
At our specific point where $L = 10$, the partial derivative is **$10$**.

**What this means:** For every $1\text{ cm}$ we add to the width (while keeping length at $10$), the area will increase by $10\text{ cm}^2$.

---

### Summary of the Concept

At the dimensions of $10 \times 5$:
*   $\frac{\partial A}{\partial L} = 5$
*   $\frac{\partial A}{\partial W} = 10$

This tells us that at this specific size, **increasing the width has twice the impact on the total area as increasing the length.** Partial derivatives allow us to isolate and measure these individual influences in multi-variable systems.
