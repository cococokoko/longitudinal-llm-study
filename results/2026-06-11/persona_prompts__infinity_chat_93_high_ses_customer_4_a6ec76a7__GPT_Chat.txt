Stress is the body’s natural response to challenges or perceived threats. When a person experiences stress, the brain activates the “fight-or-flight” response, triggering the release of hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol. These hormones increase heart rate, blood pressure, and blood sugar levels, helping the body respond quickly to immediate demands.

In the short term, stress can be beneficial by improving alertness, focus, and reaction time. However, chronic or excessive stress can have significant effects on both physical and mental health. Prolonged elevation of stress hormones may contribute to cardiovascular disease, hypertension, weakened immune function, sleep disturbances, digestive problems, and metabolic disorders.

Stress also affects the mind. Persistent stress can impair concentration, memory, and decision-making. It is associated with increased risk of anxiety, depression, irritability, and emotional exhaustion. Neurobiological studies suggest that chronic stress can alter the function and structure of brain regions involved in learning, memory, and emotional regulation, including the hippocampus, amygdala, and prefrontal cortex.

The impact of stress varies among individuals and depends on factors such as genetics, life experiences, social support, and coping strategies. Effective stress management—including regular physical activity, adequate sleep, social connection, mindfulness practices, and psychological support when needed—can help reduce its negative effects and promote overall well-being.
