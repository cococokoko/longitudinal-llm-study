── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Power asymmetries**
- Wealthy states have greater leverage in negotiations, institutions, and coercive capacity
- Economic dependence creates political vulnerability for poorer states
- This can produce relationships resembling patron-client dynamics even between formally equal sovereigns

**Institutional design**
- Bretton Woods institutions (IMF, World Bank) reflect founding power distributions
- Voting structures often favor wealthy contributors
- This creates legitimate grievances about whose interests rules serve

## Competing Interpretations Worth Considering

**Liberal view**: Trade interdependence reduces conflict and creates mutual interests despite inequality

**Dependency/structuralist view**: The global economy systematically transfers wealth upward, making inequality self-reinforcing

**Realist view**: Economic inequality is primarily a tool of power competition rather than a cause in itself

**Constructivist view**: How states *interpret* inequality matters as much as the inequality itself

## Genuine Tensions and Complications

- Some poor states maintain significant autonomy; some wealthy states face constraints
- Economic inequality doesn't straightforwardly predict alliance patterns
- Rising powers (China, India) complicate simple North-South frameworks
- Domestic inequality within states increasingly affects international behavior

## What Evidence Suggests
- Inequality correlates with instability, migration pressure, and resource competition
- But causation is genuinely contested
