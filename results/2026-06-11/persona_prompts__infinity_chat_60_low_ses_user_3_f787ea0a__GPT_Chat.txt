── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a row of toy blocks.

There's a special rule:

- Look at the blocks.
- Change them in a certain way.
- Then do it again.
- And again.
- And again.

Now suppose you ask, "What will the blocks look like after 1,000 turns?"

You might hope there's a magic shortcut that tells you the answer right away.

Computational irreducibility means that for some systems, there is no shortcut.

The only way to know what happens after 1,000 turns is to do turn 1, then turn 2, then turn 3, and keep going all the way to turn 1,000.

It's like asking:
- "What will a movie look like 10 minutes from now?"

For some movies, you can't know by looking at the first frame. You have to watch the movie unfold.

So, in simple terms:

- Some things can be predicted with a quick calculation.
- Other things are so complex that the fastest way to find out what happens is to let them happen.

That's computational irreducibility. The process can't be "skipped ahead" by a clever shortcut.
