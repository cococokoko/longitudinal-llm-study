── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** can refer to two different things depending on the context:

1. **A Path Graph ($P_n$):** This is the most common informal use, representing a graph where vertices are connected in a single, straight line.
2. **A Line Graph ($L(G)$):** This is a formal graph operation where the edges of one graph become the vertices of a new graph.

Here are examples of both.

---

### Example 1: A Path Graph (The informal "linear" graph)
A **Path Graph** (denoted as $P_n$, where $n$ is the number of vertices) is a graph whose vertices can be listed in the order $v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n$ such that the edges are $\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \dots, \{v_{n-1}, v_n\}$. It looks like a single, unbranching line.

#### **Example: $P_4$ (Path Graph with 4 vertices)**
*   **Vertices ($V$):** $\{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges ($E$):** $\{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$

**Visual Representation:**
```text
A -------- B -------- C -------- D
```
*   **Properties:**
    *   It has $n$ vertices and $n-1$ edges.
    *   The "end" vertices ($A$ and $D$) have a degree of 1.
    *   The "internal" vertices ($B$ and $C$) have a degree of 2.
    *   There are no cycles or branches.

---

### Example 2: A Line Graph (The formal mathematical definition)
In formal graph theory, a **Line Graph** of a graph $G$ (written as $L(G)$) is a graph where:
*   Each vertex of $L(G)$ represents an edge of $G$.
*   Two vertices in $L(G)$ are connected by an edge if and only if their corresponding edges in $G$ share a common vertex.

#### **Example:**
Let's start with a simple star graph $G$:
*   **Vertices of $G$:** $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$
*   **Edges of $G$:** 
    *   $e_1 = (1, 2)$
    *   $e_2 = (1, 3)$
    *   $e_3 = (1, 4)$

*(In graph $G$, all three edges meet at vertex 1).*

```text
Graph G:
   2
   |
   1 --- 3
   |
   4
```

To create the **Line Graph $L(G)$**:
1.  The **vertices** of $L(G)$ are the edges of $G$: $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.
2.  We connect these vertices because $e_1, e_2,$ and $e_3$ all share the vertex $1$ in the original graph.

**Visual Representation of $L(G)$:**
```text
     e1
    /  \
   /    \
  e2 ---- e3
```
The resulting Line Graph $L(G)$ is a triangle (a complete graph $K_3$).
