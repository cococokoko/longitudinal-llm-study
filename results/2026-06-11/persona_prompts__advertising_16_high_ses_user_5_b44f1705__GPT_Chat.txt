Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive often requires addressing both the external demands of the role and the internal habits that can amplify pressure.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Separate urgent from important.
  - Many executives spend most of their time reacting.
  - Block dedicated time each week for strategic work, and protect it as firmly as you would a board meeting.

- Reduce decision overload.
  - Standardize recurring decisions where possible.
  - Delegate ownership, not just tasks, so fewer decisions return to your desk.

- Build recovery into the calendar.
  - High performance is difficult to sustain without periods of recovery.
  - Short breaks between meetings, exercise, sufficient sleep, and time away from work all contribute to better judgment and resilience.

- Create a structured worry process.
  - When a concern arises, write down:
    - What am I worried about?
    - What evidence supports it?
    - What action can I take?
    - If no action is possible, when will I revisit it?
  - This can prevent rumination from consuming attention.

- Maintain a trusted inner circle.
  - Isolation is common in senior leadership.
  - Having a few people with whom you can discuss challenges candidly—whether peers, mentors, coaches, or a therapist—can significantly reduce stress.

- Watch for physical indicators.
  - Persistent sleep disruption, irritability, difficulty concentrating, increased alcohol use, or a constant sense of threat may indicate anxiety is becoming more than routine workplace stress.

- Consider professional support.
  - Executive coaching can help with leadership-specific pressures.
  - If anxiety is frequent, intense, or affecting your work or personal life, a licensed mental health professional can provide targeted strategies and support.

If you share more about the source of the stress—board expectations, organizational change, layoffs, performance pressure, investor relations, workload, or something else—I can offer more specific guidance.
