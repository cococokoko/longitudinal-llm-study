── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality (IAQ) is essential for long-term health, as most people spend about 90% of their time indoors, where pollutant levels can be two to five times higher than outdoor levels. 

To effectively improve indoor air quality, you should focus on three main strategies: **source control** (removing the pollutants), **ventilation** (bringing in clean air), and **air purification** (cleaning the existing air).

Here are the best ways to improve your indoor air quality, categorized by action.

---

### 1. Control the Sources of Pollution (Most Effective)
The most effective way to improve air quality is to eliminate individual sources of pollution or reduce their emissions.

*   **Stop smoking indoors:** Tobacco smoke contains thousands of harmful chemicals and is the single most significant source of indoor air pollution.
*   **Minimize synthetic fragrances:** Avoid chemical air fresheners, scented candles, incense, and heavily perfumed cleaning products. They release volatile organic compounds (VOCs) like formaldehyde and benzene.
*   **Choose low-VOC products:** When buying paint, furniture, carpets, or cleaning supplies, look for "Low-VOC" or "Zero-VOC" labels.
*   **Control moisture to prevent mold:** Mold releases spores that trigger allergies and asthma. 
    *   Keep indoor humidity between **30% and 50%** using dehumidifiers or air conditioners.
    *   Fix plumbing leaks immediately.
    *   Use exhaust fans in the bathroom and kitchen.
*   **Groom and clean up after pets:** Pet dander (dead skin flakes), fur, and saliva are major allergens. Wash pet bedding frequently and bathe pets regularly.

### 2. Improve Ventilation (Bring in Fresh Air)
Increasing the amount of outdoor air coming indoors helps dilute the concentration of indoor pollutants.

*   **Open windows and doors:** When weather and outdoor air quality permit, open windows to create a cross-breeze. Even 15 minutes a day can make a big difference.
*   **Use exhaust fans:** Always run the kitchen exhaust hood when cooking (especially if you have a gas stove) and the bathroom fan when showering. Leave them on for about 20 minutes after you finish.
*   **Upgrade to a mechanical ventilation system:** If you live in a tightly sealed, energy-efficient home, consider installing a Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV) or Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV) to constantly exchange stale indoor air for fresh outdoor air.

### 3. Clean and Filter the Air
If you cannot eliminate the source of pollution, you must filter it out of the air.

*   **Use a portable HEPA air purifier:** High-Efficiency Particulate Air (HEPA) filters capture 99.97% of particles as small as 0.3 microns, including dust, pollen, mold, and bacteria. 
    *   *Tip:* Choose a purifier with an **activated carbon filter** if you also want to remove odors, gases, and VOCs. Ensure the unit is rated for the size of the room (look for the CADR - Clean Air Delivery Rate).
*   **Upgrade your HVAC filter:** If you have central heating or air conditioning, upgrade your filter to one with a **MERV rating of 11 to 13**. These filters are strong enough to trap mold spores, pet dander, and bacteria without restricting your system's airflow. Change these filters every 60 to 90 days.
*   **Keep your HVAC fan on "ON" instead of "AUTO":** This keeps the air constantly circulating through your high-quality filter, even when the heating or cooling isn't running.

### 4. Maintain Cleanliness (Reduce Dust and Allergens)
Regular cleaning reduces the accumulation of dust mites, pet dander, and pollen that settle on surfaces.

*   **Vacuum with a HEPA vacuum:** Standard vacuums often blow fine dust particles back into the air. Use a vacuum equipped with a sealed HEPA filter at least once or twice a week.
*   **Damp-dust surfaces:** Use a damp microfiber cloth to dust surfaces. Dry dusting just kicks dust back into the air.
*   **Wash bedding weekly:** Wash sheets, pillowcases, and blankets weekly in hot water (at least 130°F / 54°C) to kill dust mites.
*   **Use doormats:** Place mats inside and outside entryways to trap dirt, pesticides, and pollutants from shoes. Better yet, adopt a **"no-shoes" policy** inside the house.

### 5. Be Mindful of Combustion (Burning Things)
Combustion particles are highly damaging to lung health.

*   **Ventilate gas stoves:** Gas stoves emit nitrogen dioxide ($NO_2$) and carbon monoxide. Always use the range hood when cooking, or open a nearby window. If possible, consider switching to an induction cooktop.
*   **Maintain wood stoves and fireplaces:** Ensure they are properly vented, and only burn dry, seasoned wood.
*   **Install Carbon Monoxide (CO) detectors:** Place them on every level of your home to warn you of dangerous, odorless CO leaks.

---

### A Note on Houseplants
While it is a popular belief that houseplants "purify" indoor air, scientific studies (including recent reviews of NASA's famous clean air study) show that **houseplants do not improve indoor air quality in a meaningful way**. You would need between 10 and 1,000 plants per square meter of floor space to match the air-cleaning rate of a standard ventilation system or a single air purifier. Enjoy plants for their aesthetic and mental health benefits, but do not rely on them to clean your air.
