── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Discipleship—the process of learning from, following, and becoming like a teacher—is a foundational concept in many religions and philosophies, but it is most prominently discussed within Christianity. Even within Christianity, and across other traditions, there are vastly different views on what discipleship actually means, how it is practiced, and what its ultimate goal is.

Here is an overview of the different views on discipleship, categorized by theological, practical, and interfaith perspectives.

---

### I. Christian Theological Perspectives
Within Christianity, different traditions emphasize different aspects of the relationship between the disciple, the church, and Christ.

#### 1. The Reformed/Covenantal View (Focus on Doctrine and Sovereignty)
*   **Core Idea:** Discipleship is primarily about growing in the knowledge of God’s truth, sound doctrine, and living in covenant community.
*   **Key Emphasis:** High value is placed on catechism (systematic instruction), preaching, and the study of Scripture. 
*   **The View:** Discipleship is a lifelong process of sanctification initiated and sustained by God’s grace. It is highly intellectual but aims at the transformation of the heart and will through the Holy Spirit.

#### 2. The Catholic and Eastern Orthodox View (Focus on Sacraments and Liturgy)
*   **Core Idea:** Discipleship is an initiation into the mystical Body of Christ through the Church, sustained by the sacraments.
*   **Key Emphasis:** In Catholicism, it often involves spiritual direction, imitation of the saints, and works of mercy. In Eastern Orthodoxy, the ultimate goal of discipleship is *theosis* (deification or union with God).
*   **The View:** Discipleship is not just an individual pursuit but a communal, liturgical journey. One cannot be a disciple apart from the Church and the sacraments (especially the Eucharist).

#### 3. The Anabaptist/Radical Reformation View (Focus on Costly Obedience)
*   **Core Idea:** Discipleship (*Nachfolge*) is the literal, radical imitation of the life and teachings of Jesus, particularly the Sermon on the Mount.
*   **Key Emphasis:** Pacifism, community of goods, simplicity, and separation from the corrupting influences of the state/world.
*   **The View:** Influenced heavily by theologian Dietrich Bonhoeffer (*The Cost of Discipleship*), this view rejects "cheap grace." To be a disciple is to suffer with Christ and actively oppose injustice through non-violent love.

#### 4. The Evangelical/Missional View (Focus on Multiplication and Evangelism)
*   **Core Idea:** Discipleship is about fulfilling the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19)—making disciples who make disciples.
*   **Key Emphasis:** Personal evangelism, one-on-one mentoring, Bible study groups, and spiritual multiplication.
*   **The View:** Highly practical and action-oriented. A true disciple is someone who is actively helping others follow Jesus. It often uses structured programs (e.g., The Navigators, Alpha Course).

#### 5. The Liberation Theology View (Focus on Social Justice)
*   **Core Idea:** Discipleship is solidarity with the poor, the marginalized, and the oppressed.
*   **Key Emphasis:** Orthopraxy (right action) over orthodoxy (right belief). 
*   **The View:** To follow Jesus is to fight against systemic sin, poverty, and political oppression. Jesus is viewed as a liberator, and discipleship is a political and social act.

---

### II. Practical and Methodological Views
Even within the same church, leaders disagree on *how* discipleship should be structured.

#### 1. Organic vs. Structured Discipleship
*   **Structured (Programmatic):** Believes discipleship happens best through curriculum, classes, and defined stages of spiritual growth. It is measurable and scalable.
*   **Organic (Relational):** Believes discipleship cannot be mass-produced. It happens through life-on-life relationships, shared meals, and informal mentoring. It is slow and highly personal.

#### 2. Cognitive vs. Behavioral vs. Affective
*   **Cognitive (Information-driven):** "If you know the right things, you will live the right way." Focuses on Bible study and theology.
*   **Behavioral (Action-driven):** "If you do the right things, your heart will follow." Focuses on spiritual disciplines (prayer, fasting, service).
*   **Affective (Heart-driven):** "If you love the right things, your life will change." Focuses on worship, emotional health, and experiencing God's love.

---

### III. Interfaith and Philosophical Perspectives
The concept of discipleship is not unique to Christianity. Other traditions offer distinct models of the teacher-student relationship.

#### 1. Hinduism: The Guru-Shishya Parampara
*   **The View:** Discipleship (*shishya*) is an absolute surrender to a spiritual teacher (*guru*). 
*   **Key Difference:** The guru is often seen as a living embodiment of the divine. Discipleship involves intense personal service to the guru, oral transmission of sacred texts, and experiential realization of truth rather than intellectual assent.

#### 2. Buddhism: The Sangha and Dharma
*   **The View:** Discipleship is about taking refuge in the Buddha (the teacher), the Dharma (the teaching), and the Sangha (the community).
*   **Key Difference:** While teachers (Lamas, Zen Masters) are highly respected, the ultimate goal is self-realization and liberation (Nirvana). The Buddha famously told his disciples to "be lamps unto yourselves" and to test his teachings against their own experience.

#### 3. Judaism: The Rabbi-Talmid Relationship
*   **The View:** In ancient Judaism (which shaped Jesus’ time), a *talmid* (disciple) did not just want to know what the Rabbi knew; they wanted to *be* who the Rabbi was.
*   **Key Difference:** It was highly rigorous. Disciples would literally follow their Rabbi everywhere, dusting themselves in the "dust of his feet," learning how he ate, prayed, argued, and lived out the Torah.

#### 4. Secular/Philosophical Discipleship (Socratic/Stoic)
*   **The View:** In ancient Greece, discipleship was about the pursuit of virtue and wisdom.
*   **Key Difference:** A disciple of Socrates or Seneca was a student of a school of thought. The goal was intellectual independence and mastery of self, rather than worship of the teacher.

---

### Summary of the Tension
Ultimately, the different views on discipleship boil down to a few key questions:
*   *Is it about information (knowing) or transformation (being)?*
*   *Is it an individual journey or a communal obligation?*
*   *Is the goal to imitate the teacher, or to find one's own inner truth?*
*   *Does it happen in the quiet of a monastery/study, or in the noise of the public square?*
