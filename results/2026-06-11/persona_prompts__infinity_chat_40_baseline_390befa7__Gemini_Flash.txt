── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of the most common—and potentially minefield-laden—financial situations couples face. Because you are not yet married, and she is the sole buyer, you are currently in a **legal gray area**. 

To find the "best" solution, you have to balance **financial fairness, legal protection, and relationship harmony.**

Here is a breakdown of the risks, the options, and the best path forward.

---

### The Golden Rule of Pre-Marital Real Estate
**If your name is not on the deed, you should not be paying the mortgage.** 

In the eyes of the law, if you pay toward her mortgage and you two break up, that money is a gift to her. You have no equity, no ownership, and no right to get that money back. Conversely, it is also unfair to your girlfriend if you live there completely rent-free, essentially using her as a free landlord.

Here are the three best ways to handle this, ranked from most recommended to least recommended.

---

### Option 1: The "Fair Rent" Model (Highly Recommended)
Since she is buying the house, she is taking on 100% of the financial risk, the debt, the down payment, and the maintenance costs. You should treat her as your landlord, but with a "partner discount."

*   **How it works:** You pay her a set monthly amount of "rent" and a split of the utilities. 
*   **How to calculate it:** 
    *   *Market Rate:* Look up what it would cost to rent a room/similar house in the area, and pay a portion of that.
    *   *Income-Weighted:* If she makes $60k and you make $40k (a 60/40 split), you pay 40% of the monthly mortgage payment as rent.
*   **The Legal Boundary:** You do **not** pay for repairs, renovations, or HOA fees. If the HVAC breaks, she pays for it because she owns the asset. You only pay "rent" and utilities.
*   **Why it’s fair:** She gets help with the monthly cash flow, and you get a place to live. If you break up, you simply move out (just like leaving an apartment), and she keeps her house. No messy legal battles over who owns what.

### Option 2: The "Save for the Wedding/Future" Model
If you plan to marry in a year, you can view your finances as a team effort already, even if they aren't legally merged.

*   **How it works:** She pays 100% of the mortgage and housing expenses. In exchange, you pay for *everything else* or save aggressively for your joint future.
*   **Example:** You pay for all the groceries, dining out, vacations, and utilities. Or, you take the money you *would* have spent on rent and put it into a high-yield savings account dedicated to your upcoming wedding, honeymoon, or a "future renovations" fund for when you are married.
*   **Why it’s fair:** It keeps the house legally "clean" (she pays for her asset), but you are contributing equally to your shared life and relieving her of other daily expenses.

### Option 3: Co-Buying (Not Recommended Yet)
You could ask to be put on the mortgage and deed now. 
*   **Why you shouldn't do this:** Buying a house with someone you are not married to is highly risky. If you break up, there is no "divorce court" to easily split the asset. You would have to go through a costly legal process called a "partition sale." 
*   **The exception:** If you do this, you *must* hire a real estate attorney to draft a **Cohabitation Agreement** or **No-Nuptial Agreement** detailing exactly who gets what if you split up.

---

### What happens after you get married?
Once you get married, the dynamic changes. Depending on where you live (Community Property states vs. Equitable Distribution states), the house may remain her "separate property" because she bought it before marriage. 

Once you are married, you can:
1.  **Refinance:** Refinance the home into both of your names, effectively making you a co-owner.
2.  **Keep it separate:** Keep the house in her name, but merge your finances so "we" pay the mortgage. 
3.  **Draft a Prenup:** If she put down a massive down payment and wants to protect that specific asset, a prenuptial agreement can state that she keeps her initial down payment equity, but any growth in the home's value *after* marriage is split 50/50.

### The Best Next Step
Sit down with your girlfriend and have an open conversation. Use this script:

> *"I am so excited for you to buy this house, and I'm excited for us to live there together. Since we aren't married yet, I want to make sure we handle the finances in a way that protects both of us. I was thinking I could pay a set amount of 'rent' and split utilities, while you handle the mortgage and home repairs. That way, I'm contributing my fair share, but we keep the legal side simple until we get married. How do you feel about that?"*

A good partner will appreciate your financial maturity and desire to protect both of your interests.
