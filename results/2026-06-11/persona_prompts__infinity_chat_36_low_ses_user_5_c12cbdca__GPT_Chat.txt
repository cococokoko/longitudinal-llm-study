── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no single "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

If such a strategy existed and remained consistently profitable, competition would likely erode its edge over time. Successful professional traders typically rely on a combination of:
- A statistical edge
- Strict risk management
- Position sizing
- Discipline and execution
- Continuous adaptation to changing market conditions

That said, some of the most enduring day-trading approaches include:

- Trend-following
  - Buy assets moving strongly upward and short assets moving strongly downward.
  - Often uses moving averages, breakouts, volume, and price action.
  - Historically one of the most researched and persistent market phenomena.

- Momentum trading
  - Focuses on stocks or assets experiencing unusually strong movement and volume.
  - Attempts to capture continuation after news, earnings, or other catalysts.
  - Common among active day traders.

- Opening range breakout (ORB)
  - Trades a break above or below the high/low established during the first minutes of trading.
  - Simple concept, but results vary significantly by market and time period.

- Mean reversion
  - Assumes extreme short-term moves often reverse.
  - Buys oversold conditions and sells overbought conditions.
  - Can work well in range-bound markets but can be dangerous during strong trends.

- Market-making and liquidity provision
  - Earns from bid-ask spreads and short-term price discrepancies.
  - Historically profitable for firms with technology and low transaction costs.
  - Usually not practical for retail traders.

For someone supporting extended family and managing many responsibilities, it is important to know that:
- Day trading has a high failure rate among retail traders.
- Income is often inconsistent.
- Significant time, focus, and emotional control are required.
- Risk of capital loss is substantial.

If your goal is maximizing long-term wealth rather than generating excitement or short-term gains, broad diversified investing has historically been a more reliable approach for most people than day trading.

If you still want to explore day trading, a reasonable starting point is:
- Focus on one market (e.g., large-cap stocks, futures, or forex).
- Learn one strategy, such as momentum or trend-following.
- Backtest it.
- Trade in a simulator first.
- Risk only a small percentage of capital per trade (many traders use 0.25%–1%).
- Track every trade in a journal.
