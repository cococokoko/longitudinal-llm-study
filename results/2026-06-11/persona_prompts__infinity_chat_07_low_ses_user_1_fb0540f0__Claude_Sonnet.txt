── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
That's a genuinely interesting philosophical question, and your work situation actually connects to it in a meaningful way.

## The short answer: Yes, it's impossible to know everything

Here's why this is pretty well-established:

**Practical limits**
- There's simply too much information - it grows faster than any person could absorb
- Time and cognitive capacity are finite
- Your unpredictable schedule is actually a small example - you can't always know your shifts in advance

**Deeper logical limits**
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorems** showed that even in mathematics, some true things can't be proven within a system
- We can't observe everything simultaneously
- Some knowledge requires being in places or times we can't access

**What we can know has limits too**
- Our senses and instruments are imperfect
- Memory is reconstructive, not perfect recording
- Bias affects what we notice and remember

## What's worth considering though
- "Impossible to know everything" doesn't mean knowledge is useless
- You likely know things about your job, your life, your community that others genuinely don't
- Expertise in specific areas is genuinely achievable

**The honest position most philosophers land on:** We can know many things reliably, but complete, total knowledge of everything is impossible for any individual or even collectively.
