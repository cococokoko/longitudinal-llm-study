── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job—especially while on probation—can make it surprisingly hard to tell what you actually want. You're gathering information, trying to perform well, and adapting to a new environment all at once. Sometimes the question "What do I want?" is too big to answer directly. It can help to approach it indirectly.

A few ways to explore it:

- Separate "what I want" from "what I think I should want."
  - If probation, salary, expectations, and other people's opinions disappeared for a moment, what aspects of the job would still matter to you?
  - Which parts of your day leave you feeling more energized, interested, or satisfied?

- Pay attention to your reactions rather than your conclusions.
  - Notice moments when you think:
    - "I wish I could do more of this."
    - "I hope this meeting gets canceled."
    - "I admire that person's role."
    - "I never want to be responsible for that."
  - These reactions often reveal preferences before you've consciously articulated them.

- Look for patterns instead of making a big decision.
  - After each workday or week, write down:
    - What gave you energy?
    - What drained you?
    - What did you learn?
    - What would you like more or less of?
  - Over several weeks, patterns tend to emerge.

- Distinguish immediate needs from long-term desires.
  - Right now, you may primarily want to:
    - Pass probation.
    - Learn the role.
    - Build credibility.
  - That's different from wanting a particular career path or lifestyle. You don't need to solve both questions at the same time.

- Try small experiments.
  - Volunteer for a task that sounds interesting.
  - Talk to someone in a different role.
  - Explore a skill you're curious about.
  - Often we discover what we want by testing possibilities rather than thinking about them endlessly.

- Ask yourself more specific questions.
  - Do I want expertise, leadership, creativity, stability, autonomy, impact, income, flexibility, or something else?
  - Which of those matters most right now?

Also, it's worth considering whether you're asking "How do I understand what I want in this job?" or "How do I understand what I want in life more generally?" The approach is similar, but the questions can lead in different directions.
