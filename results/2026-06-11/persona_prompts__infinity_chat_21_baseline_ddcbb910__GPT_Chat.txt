── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty typically make several arguments. Here are some of the strongest and most commonly cited ones, along with the reasoning behind them:

- Retribution and justice
  - Supporters argue that certain crimes, such as premeditated murder, are so severe that the only proportionate punishment is death.
  - They believe justice requires that the punishment reflect the seriousness of the offense.
  - Some view this as a moral principle: those who intentionally take innocent lives forfeit their own right to life.

- Deterrence
  - Some argue that the possibility of execution discourages potential offenders from committing the most serious crimes.
  - Even if the deterrent effect is debated, supporters contend that the risk of execution may prevent at least some murders.

- Incapacitation and public safety
  - Execution permanently prevents a convicted murderer from committing future crimes.
  - Supporters note that prisoners can sometimes escape, commit violence in prison, or order crimes from behind bars.
  - They argue that the death penalty removes these risks entirely.

- Closure for victims' families
  - Some families of murder victims report feeling that execution provides a sense of justice or finality.
  - Supporters argue that society owes victims and their families a meaningful response to especially horrific crimes.

- Moral accountability
  - Advocates often argue that treating adults as fully responsible for their actions means accepting the most severe consequences for the most severe crimes.
  - They contend that failing to impose the ultimate punishment can diminish personal accountability.

- Fairness to victims
  - Some supporters argue that focusing solely on the rights of offenders can overlook the suffering of victims.
  - They believe capital punishment demonstrates that society places a high value on innocent life.

- Cost arguments (in theory)
  - Some people believe that executing certain offenders is less costly than housing them for life.
  - In practice, however, legal appeals and procedural safeguards often make death penalty cases more expensive than life imprisonment in many jurisdictions.

- Historical and democratic legitimacy
  - Supporters may point out that the death penalty has existed in many societies across history and continues to be supported by portions of the public.
  - They argue that citizens in a democratic society may legitimately choose to retain it for the most extreme crimes.

It is worth noting that each of these arguments is contested. Opponents commonly respond by pointing to the risk of wrongful execution, questions about deterrence, unequal application across groups, moral objections, and the availability of life imprisonment without parole. The debate often depends on differing views about justice, punishment, human rights, and the role of the state.
