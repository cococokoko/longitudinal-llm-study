── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a row of dominoes set up in a very special pattern.

You ask, "What will the 100th domino do?"

For some things, an adult could look at the pattern and instantly know the answer without knocking any dominoes over.

But computational irreducibility is when there is no shortcut.

The only way to know what the 100th domino will do is to:
- Knock over the 1st domino.
- Watch what happens to the 2nd.
- Then the 3rd.
- Then the 4th.
- And keep going all the way to the 100th.

It's like a recipe where you can't skip ahead to see the finished cake. You have to mix the ingredients, bake it, and wait. There isn't a faster way to know exactly how it turns out.

So, computational irreducibility means:

"The only way to find out what happens is to let the process happen, step by step."

Even very simple rules can create results that nobody can predict without watching all the steps in between.
