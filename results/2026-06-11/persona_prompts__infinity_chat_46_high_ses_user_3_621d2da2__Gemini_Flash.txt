── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you are likely looking for a mathematically precise definition rather than a basic high school algebra plot of $y = mx + b$. 

In graph theory, the term **"linear graph"** is historically used in two distinct ways depending on the subfield or the era of the literature. Below are the two standard interpretations, along with concrete examples for each.

---

### Interpretation 1: A Path Graph ($P_n$)
In modern graph theory, "linear graph" is most commonly used as a synonym for a **path graph** (often denoted $P_n$). This is a tree with no branches, where two vertices have degree 1 (the endpoints) and all other vertices have degree 2.

#### Example: The Path Graph $P_4$
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a simple undirected graph.

*   **Vertex Set:** $V = \{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}$
*   **Edge Set:** $E = \{\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \{v_3, v_4\}\}$

```text
(v1) ——— (v2) ——— (v3) ——— (v4)
```

**Properties:**
*   **Order ($|V|$):** $4$
*   **Size ($|E|$):** $3$ (or $n-1$ in the general case)
*   **Degree Sequence:** $(1, 2, 2, 1)$
*   **Diameter:** $3$
*   **Chromatic Number:** $\chi(G) = 2$

---

### Interpretation 2: A Line Graph ($L(G)$)
In structural and algebraic graph theory, "linear graph" is sometimes used interchangeably with **line graph** (or representative graph). The line graph $L(G)$ of a parent graph $G$ represents the adjacencies between the edges of $G$.

#### Example: $L(K_3)$ (The Line Graph of the Complete Graph on 3 Vertices)
Let the base graph be the triangle $G = K_3$, with vertices $\{a, b, c\}$ and edges:
*   $e_1 = \{a, b\}$
*   $e_2 = \{b, c\}$
*   $e_3 = \{c, a\}$

To construct the linear/line graph $H = L(K_3)$:
*   **Vertex Set of $H$:** $V(H) = \{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ (the edges of $G$ become the vertices of $H$).
*   **Edge Set of $H$:** Two vertices in $H$ are adjacent if their corresponding edges in $G$ share a common vertex. 
    *   $e_1$ and $e_2$ share vertex $b$.
    *   $e_2$ and $e_3$ share vertex $c$.
    *   $e_3$ and $e_1$ share vertex $a$.

Thus, $H$ is also isomorphic to $K_3$. 

*(Note: By Whitney’s Isomorphism Theorem, $L(K_3) \cong K_3$ is a unique case, as for all other connected graphs $G_1$ and $G_2$, $L(G_1) \cong L(G_2)$ implies $G_1 \cong G_2$, with the sole exception of $K_3$ and $K_{1,3}$.)*

---

### Historical Note
If you are reviewing classical 19th or early 20th-century literature (e.g., Whitney, Birkhoff, or early topology), the term "linear graph" was often used simply as a synonym for **"1-dimensional simplicial complex"** (i.e., any standard graph consisting of vertices and edges, as opposed to higher-dimensional cell complexes). Under this definition, *any* standard graph (such as the Petersen Graph or $K_5$) is a linear graph.
