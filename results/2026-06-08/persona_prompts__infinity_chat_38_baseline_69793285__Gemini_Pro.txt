Do you know the demon Morloch?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It is highly likely that you are referring to **Moloch** (also spelled **Molech**, **Molekh**, and sometimes phonetically spelled or adapted in fiction as **Morloch**). 

Moloch is one of the most infamous entities in ancient history, biblical literature, and later Christian demonology. Here is a breakdown of who and what this entity is:

### 1. Historical and Biblical Origins
Originally, Moloch was associated with the ancient Canaanites, Ammonites, and Phoenicians. In the Hebrew Bible (the Old Testament), Moloch is repeatedly condemned as a false god. 
* **The Rituals:** The most notorious aspect of Moloch’s worship was the practice of child sacrifice. According to biblical texts and ancient historians, worshippers would "pass their children through the fire" as an offering to him.
* **The Idol:** Ancient rabbinic texts and later historians described the idol of Moloch as a massive bronze statue with the body of a man and the head of a bull. The statue was hollow, and a roaring fire would be built inside it. The idol's arms were outstretched, and the sacrificial victims were placed into its burning hands. 
* **Location:** These sacrifices supposedly took place in the Valley of Hinnom, just outside Jerusalem. This valley later became known as *Gehenna*, which eventually became the Jewish and Christian metaphor for Hell.

### 2. Transition into Demonology
As Christianity spread, the ancient pagan gods of the Middle East were reclassified as demons. By the Middle Ages and the Renaissance, Moloch was firmly established in grimoires and demonology as a high-ranking Prince of Hell.
* **Paradise Lost:** The English poet John Milton cemented Moloch’s demonic reputation in his 1667 epic poem *Paradise Lost*. Milton portrays Moloch as one of Satan’s chief warriors—a fierce, militant, and bloodthirsty fallen angel who advocates for open, violent war against God.
* **Dictionnaire Infernal:** In Collin de Plancy’s famous 19th-century book on demonology, Moloch is listed as the "Prince of the Land of Tears" and a member of the infernal council.

### 3. "Morloch" vs. "Morlock" (A Quick Distinction)
If you specifically saw the spelling **Morloch** or **Morlock**, it could be one of two things:
* **Pop Culture/Fantasy:** Many fantasy authors, video games (like *Dungeons & Dragons* or *World of Warcraft*), and horror movies tweak the spelling of Moloch to "Morloch" for copyright reasons or to make it sound more unique to their specific universe.
* **H.G. Wells' *The Time Machine*:** You might also be thinking of the **Morlocks**. In H.G. Wells' classic 1895 sci-fi novel, the Morlocks are not demons, but a terrifying, subterranean, ape-like evolution of humanity that comes out at night to feed on the peaceful, surface-dwelling humans (the Eloi). 

**Summary:** If you are looking for the demon, you are looking for **Moloch**—an ancient Near-Eastern deity associated with fire and sacrifice, who was later transformed by Christian tradition into a violent, warmongering Prince of Hell.
