── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The concept of **discipleship**—at its core, the process of learning from, imitating, and adhering to the teachings of a master or teacher—is central to many religious and philosophical traditions, particularly Christianity. 

However, how discipleship is understood, practiced, and prioritized varies significantly. Below is an overview of the different views on discipleship, categorized by theological tradition, practical methodology, and modern debates.

---

### 1. Theological and Denominational Perspectives

Different Christian traditions view the ultimate goal and context of discipleship through different theological lenses.

#### A. The Protestant/Evangelical View: Personal Relationship and the Great Commission
In modern Evangelicalism, discipleship is heavily focused on personal spiritual growth and obedience to the "Great Commission" (Matthew 28:19-20).
*   **Key Focus:** Personal relationship with Jesus Christ, Bible study, prayer, and evangelism.
*   **The Goal:** To help an individual grow in faith so that they, in turn, can make other disciples (often called "multiplication").
*   **Key Thinkers:** Dietrich Bonhoeffer (who warned against "cheap grace" without discipleship) and Dallas Willard (who emphasized spiritual disciplines).

#### B. The Roman Catholic View: Sacramental and Ecclesial
For Catholics, discipleship is deeply tied to the Church (the ecclesia), the sacraments, and the imitation of Christ and the saints.
*   **Key Focus:** Participation in the sacraments (especially the Eucharist), liturgy, and works of mercy. Discipleship is not just an individual journey but a communal one mediated through the Church.
*   **The Goal:** Holiness, spiritual formation, and alignment with the teachings of the Magisterium (the Church’s teaching authority).
*   **Key Concept:** *Sequela Christi* (the following of Christ), often modeled by the saints and religious orders (e.g., Franciscans, Jesuits).

#### C. The Eastern Orthodox View: *Theosis* (Deification)
In Eastern Orthodoxy, discipleship is viewed through the lens of *theosis*—the transformative process of becoming more like God and sharing in His divine nature.
*   **Key Focus:** Asceticism (fasting, prayer, self-denial), liturgical worship, and guidance from spiritual fathers/mothers (elders).
*   **The Goal:** Union with God. Discipleship is less about learning information and more about the healing and transformation of the human soul.

#### D. The Anabaptist/Radical Reformation View: Radical Obedience and Peace
Anabaptists (Mennonites, Amish, etc.) view discipleship (*Nachfolge*) as the literal, daily following of Jesus’ teachings, particularly the Sermon on the Mount.
*   **Key Focus:** Pacifism, community accountability, simplicity, and separation from the sinful patterns of the world.
*   **The Goal:** Creating a visible, counter-cultural community of peace and justice that reflects the Kingdom of God on earth.

---

### 2. Methodological Perspectives (How Discipleship Happens)

Even within the same denomination, churches and leaders disagree on the best *method* for making disciples.

#### A. The Cognitive/Educational Model
*   **The View:** Discipleship is primarily about **right belief (orthodoxy)**. If people understand correct doctrine and theology, their behavior will change.
*   **Practice:** Classroom settings, Sunday school, lecture-style Bible studies, and reading books.

#### B. The Relational/Organic Model ("Life-on-Life")
*   **The View:** Discipleship cannot be learned in a classroom; it must be caught through relationships. 
*   **Practice:** One-on-one mentoring, small groups, and sharing daily life. A mature believer invites a newer believer into their life to observe how they handle marriage, work, and trials.

#### C. The Missional/Action-Oriented Model
*   **The View:** Discipleship is about **right practice (orthopraxy)**. We learn to follow Jesus by doing what Jesus did.
*   **Practice:** Serving the poor, fighting for social justice, and engaging in community outreach. Discipleship happens "on the road" while doing mission work.

---

### 3. Modern Debates and Tensions

In contemporary theology and church ministry, several debates shape how discipleship is viewed.

#### A. "Decisionism" vs. Lifelong Discipleship
*   **The Tension:** Many critics argue that modern Western churches focus too much on getting people to make a "decision" for salvation (e.g., saying a sinner's prayer) rather than inviting them into a lifelong journey of discipleship
