That depends a lot on what you mean by "achieved anything."

Many people reach 60 and measure themselves against a very narrow set of accomplishments:
- Career success
- Wealth
- Status
- Awards
- Recognition
- Marriage, children, or grandchildren
- Major creative or athletic achievements

If those are the standards you're using, it's possible you feel you've fallen short of goals you once imagined.

But there is another question: what have you actually spent the last 60 years doing?

For example:
- Did you work and support yourself?
- Did you help family members, friends, neighbors, or coworkers?
- Did you survive difficult periods?
- Did you learn things, travel, read, create, care for animals, maintain relationships, or contribute to your community?
- Did you experience love, friendship, curiosity, joy, grief, or growth?

Many of those things never appear on a résumé, but they are still significant parts of a life.

As for what you've "missed out on," nobody reaches 60 without missing out on something. Every life excludes countless alternatives. Some people who built successful careers missed close relationships. Some who raised families missed adventures. Some who traveled the world missed stability. There is no version of life that contains everything.

It may help to separate two different ideas:
- Things you genuinely wanted and still regret not pursuing.
- Things you think you should have achieved because society treats them as markers of success.

The first category can point toward unfinished goals. The second often creates suffering without providing much guidance.

At 60, there are certainly things that cannot be done in exactly the same way they could have been at 20. But there are also many things that remain possible:
- Learning new skills
- Building friendships
- Creating art, writing, music, or craftsmanship
- Volunteering or mentoring
- Starting projects or small businesses
- Traveling
- Improving health and fitness
- Finding purpose in service, study, or creativity
