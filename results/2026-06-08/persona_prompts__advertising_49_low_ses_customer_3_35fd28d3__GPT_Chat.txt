If your home Wi‑Fi feels slow, unreliable, or has dead spots, these are usually the most effective improvements:

- Move your router to a central, open location.
  - Keep it off the floor.
  - Avoid placing it inside cabinets or behind large furniture.
  - Keep it away from microwaves, cordless phones, and other electronics that can cause interference.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when possible.
  - These bands are usually faster than 2.4 GHz.
  - 2.4 GHz travels farther but is often more congested.

- Update your router's firmware.
  - Manufacturers regularly release performance and security improvements.

- Change the Wi‑Fi channel.
  - In crowded apartment buildings or neighborhoods, neighboring networks can interfere with yours.
  - Many routers can automatically select the best channel.

- Upgrade older equipment.
  - If your router is several years old, a newer Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router can provide better speed and coverage.

- Use Ethernet for stationary devices.
  - PCs, game consoles, TVs, and workstations perform best when wired directly to the router.

- Add a mesh Wi‑Fi system if coverage is the problem.
  - Mesh systems are often more effective than traditional Wi‑Fi extenders for larger homes.

- Check your internet plan.
  - Sometimes the Wi‑Fi is fine, but the internet connection itself is the bottleneck.

- Reduce unnecessary device usage.
  - Large downloads, cloud backups, and video streaming on multiple devices can consume bandwidth.

- Restart networking equipment occasionally.
  - Rebooting the modem and router can resolve temporary performance issues.
