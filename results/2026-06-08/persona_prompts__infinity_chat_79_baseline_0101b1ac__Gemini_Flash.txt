Here is an incredible, highly relevant, and budget-friendly STEM fair project tailored specifically to Vietnam’s environmental and agricultural context. 

### Project Title: 
**"The Golden Husk: Upcycling Vietnam’s Rice Waste into Low-Cost Water Filters to Combat Groundwater Contamination"**

---

### 1. The Vietnam Connection (The "Why")
*   **The Problem:** Vietnam is one of the world’s largest exporters of rice, which generates millions of tons of **rice husks** (agricultural waste) annually. Often, these are burned, causing air pollution. At the same time, rural areas in the Mekong and Red River Deltas suffer from **groundwater contamination** (arsenic, heavy metals, and pesticides).
*   **The Solution:** This project uses **Rice Husk Biochar** (carbonized rice husks) and **Coconut Coir** (another abundant Vietnamese waste product) to create an ultra-low-cost, highly effective water filtration system.

---

### 2. The Science Behind It
When rice husks are burned in a low-oxygen environment (pyrolysis), they turn into **biochar**. Rice husk biochar is unique because it is naturally rich in **silica** and has a highly porous structure. This structure acts like a magnet, trapping heavy metals, microplastics, and organic pollutants through a process called *adsorption*. 

---

*   **Coconut Coir/Fiber:** Free or extremely cheap (from local markets).
*   **Empty 1.5L Plastic Bottles:** Free (upcycled).

### 4. How to Do It (Step-by-Step)

#### Step 1: Make the Biochar (The Chemistry)
1. Place dry rice husks inside a clean metal tin can. Punch a few small holes in the lid to let gases escape.
2. Place the tin in a safe outdoor fire (or charcoal stove) for 30–40 minutes. This burns the husks in a low-oxygen environment, turning them into black biochar (activated carbon) rather than white ash.
3. Let it cool, then crush it into a coarse powder.

#### Step 2: Build the Filter Column (The Engineering)
1. Cut the bottom off a 1.5L plastic bottle and flip it upside down (like a funnel).
2. Layer the materials from bottom (bottle neck) to top:
   *   **Layer 1 (Bottom):** Cotton pad or tightly packed coconut coir (to stop particles from escaping).
   *   **Layer 2:** Fine sand (to filter suspended solids).
   *   **Layer 3:** **Your Rice Husk Biochar** (the active chemical filter).
   *   **Layer 4:** Coarse sand.
   *   **Layer 5 (Top):** Gravel (to disperse water flow).

#### Step 3: The Experiment (The Science & Math)
To make this a winning STEM project, you must test variables. Do not just show *that* it works; show *how well* it works.
*   **Variable to test:** Compare the filtration efficiency of **Rice Husk Biochar** vs. **Standard Charcoal** vs. **No Biochar (just sand/gravel)**.
*   **How to test:** 
    *   Mix water with a known amount of food coloring (representing organic pollutants) or use muddy water.
    *   Pour equal amounts through your different filters.
    *   **Measure:** Use a smartphone camera and a free colorimeter app (like *Color Grab*) to measure the intensity of the color before and after filtration. Alternatively, use a cheap TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) meter to measure water purity.
    *   **Time:** Measure the flow rate (how many mL per minute).
