── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How to plan an environmentally friendly wedding?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Planning an environmentally friendly (or "eco-friendly") wedding doesn't mean you have to sacrifice style or tradition. In fact, sustainable choices often lead to more unique, personalized, and memorable celebrations. 

Here is a step-by-step guide to planning a beautiful, low-impact wedding, broken down by category.

---

### 1. The Venue & Location
The venue sets the tone for your wedding and is one of the biggest factors in your carbon footprint.
*   **Keep it in one place:** Host your ceremony and reception at the same venue to eliminate the need for guests to drive between locations.
*   **Embrace the outdoors:** Parks, botanical gardens, beaches, and vineyards provide natural beauty, drastically reducing the need for imported flowers, decor, and artificial lighting.
*   **Look for green certifications:** Ask venues about their sustainability practices. Do they compost? Use solar power? Have LEED certification? 
*   **Keep it local:** Choose a location central to the majority of your guests to minimize air travel and long drives.

### 2. Invitations & Stationery
Paper waste is a massive issue in the wedding industry, but it is easily avoidable.
*   **Go digital:** Use digital save-the-dates and invitations. Websites like Paperless Post or Greenvelope offer stunning, formal digital options.
*   **Create a wedding website:** Instead of printing multiple inserts (directions, accommodations, RSVP cards), put all the details on a wedding website.
*   **Eco-friendly paper:** If you must use paper, choose 100% recycled paper, bamboo paper, or "seed paper" (which guests can plant to grow wildflowers). Ensure your stationer uses soy- or water-based inks.

### 3. Decor & Flowers
The "reduce, reuse, recycle" mantra is highly applicable here.
*   **Ban floral foam:** Traditional green floral foam is a toxic, single-use microplastic. Ask your florist to use sustainable mechanics like chicken wire or water frogs.
*   **Choose local and seasonal:** Imported flowers have a massive carbon footprint due to refrigerated air transport. Ask your florist to source from local flower farms.
*   **Use potted plants:** Instead of cut flowers, use potted plants, herbs, or succulents as centerpieces. Guests can take them home afterward.
*   **Rent, don't buy:** Rent your linens, tableware, arches, and furniture. If you want specific decor, buy it secondhand from thrift stores or Facebook Marketplace (and resell it after).
*   **Eco-friendly confetti:** Swap plastic confetti or rice for dried flower petals, punched leaves, or birdseed. 

### 4. Food & Drink
Catering is a great place to support local economies and reduce waste.
*   **Eat local and seasonal:** Work with a caterer who sources ingredients from local farms. 
*   **Offer more plant-based options:** Meat (especially beef) has a high environmental impact. Consider a fully vegetarian menu, or at least ensure 50% of your options are plant-based.
*   **Plated vs. Buffet:** Plated dinners generate significantly less food waste than buffets, which require caterers to over-prepare.
*   **Sustainable sipping:** Serve local wines and craft beers. Opt for kegs or large-batch signature cocktails in glass dispensers rather than individual bottles or cans.
*   **Plan for leftovers:** Ask your caterer if they partner with local food banks or shelters to donate untouched leftover food.

### 5. Attire & Rings
Fast fashion has made its way into the wedding industry, but you can buck the trend.
*   **Vintage or secondhand:** Buying a pre-loved wedding dress or suit is incredibly eco-friendly. Sites like Stillwhite, Nearly Newlywed, or local vintage boutiques are goldmines.
*   **Rent your attire:** Renting suits and tuxedos is common, but you can also rent wedding dresses and bridesmaid dresses through services like Rent the Runway.
*   **Ethical designers:** If buying new, look for designers who use sustainable fabrics (organic cotton, peace silk) and ethical labor practices.
