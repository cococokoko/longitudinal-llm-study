### Option 1: The "Clean" Approach (She buys it, you contribute to living expenses)
If she initiated the process of buying a house, let her buy it in **her name only**, using **her own funds and credit**. 
*   **How it works:** She owns the asset and takes on the mortgage. If you move in with her, you pay her a fair market "rent" and split utilities.

### Option 2: The "Joint Venture" (You buy it together now)
If you want to contribute to the down payment and be an equal partner in the house right now, you must treat this like a business transaction.
*   **How it works:** You both go on the deed and the mortgage. **Crucially, you must have a real estate attorney draft a Cohabitation Agreement.** 
*   **Why it’s fair:** A cohabitation agreement acts as a "prenup for a house." It dictates exactly what happens to the house if you break up before the wedding (e.g., one buys the other out, or the house is sold and the proceeds are split based on initial contribution percentages). 
*   **The Risk:** Without this agreement, if you break up, forcing the sale of a jointly owned home is a legal nightmare.

### Option 3: The "Gift" (Highly Discouraged)
You pay for the down payment or the house outright, but the house goes in *her* name.
*   **Why you shouldn't do this:** In the eyes of the law, this is a gift. If the relationship ends before the wedding, you have zero legal right to get that money back. Furthermore, depending on the amount, it could trigger gift tax reporting requirements. It also creates an immediate power imbalance in the relationship.
