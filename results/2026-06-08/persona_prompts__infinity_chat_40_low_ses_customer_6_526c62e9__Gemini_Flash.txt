### The Golden Rule of Pre-Marital Real Estate
Before looking at numbers, you have to understand the legal reality: **If your name is not on the deed, you do not own the house.** 

If you contribute to the down payment or pay the mortgage directly before you are married, you are putting money into an asset that you have zero legal claim to. If the relationship were to end, that money is legally considered a gift to her, and you would walk away with nothing. 

Therefore, the "best" solution depends on how you structure your contributions *before* and *after* the wedding.

---

### Option 1: The "Fair Rent" Approach (Safest & Simplest)
This is the most highly recommended path for couples who are engaged but not yet married. 

*   **How it works:** Your girlfriend pays the down payment and the mortgage (since it is her asset). You do not contribute to the mortgage principal, home insurance, or major repairs (like a new roof). Instead, you pay her a flat, agreed-upon monthly "rent" and split daily living expenses (utilities, groceries).
*   **What is "Fair Rent"?** This shouldn't necessarily be half her mortgage. A fair amount is usually **half of what it would cost to rent a similar place in your area**, or a percentage of your income. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** 
    *   **For her:** She gets help with the monthly cash flow, but she takes on 100% of the risk and the long-term reward of homeownership.
    *   **For you:** You get a place to live, you contribute your fair share to the household, but your hard-earned money isn't being locked into an asset you don't own. You can use your savings to prepare for the wedding or your future joint goals.

### Option 2: The "Wait Until the Wedding" Approach
If you want to be a true co-owner of this home, the cleanest way to do it is to wait until you are legally married.

*   **How it works:** You use the "Fair Rent" approach (Option 1) for the next year. Once you get married, you sit down and decide how to merge your finances. 
*   **The Transition:** After the wedding, you can contact the mortgage lender to refinance the home and add your name to both the mortgage and the deed. At that point, you can inject some of your savings into the home (perhaps paying down a chunk of the principal) and begin splitting the mortgage 50/50 as true equal partners.
