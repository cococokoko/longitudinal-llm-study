First, take a deep breath. Being a single parent balancing work and childcare is one of the hardest jobs in the world. You are carrying a massive load, and the fact that you are looking for strategies shows how dedicated you are to your family. 

The detail you shared—that your apartment costs $100 a month—is actually your **biggest superpower** right now. Whether you are in subsidized housing, living in a country with a very low cost of living, or renting from family, having housing costs that low gives you a massive advantage. Most single parents spend 30% to 50% of their income on rent. Because you don't have to, you can redirect your energy and money toward survival and stability.

Here is a step-by-step guide on how to survive, balance your life, and eventually thrive.

### 1. Maximize Your Housing Advantage
Since your rent is incredibly low, your primary financial goal should be protecting your income for other necessities.
*   **Build a "Buffer" Fund:** Take whatever money you *would* be spending on normal rent and put a small amount away every month. Even $10 a week builds an emergency fund that will save you when a child gets sick or a car breaks down.
*   **Budget Ruthlessly:** Track exactly where the rest of your money goes. Food, transportation, and childcare are likely your biggest expenses. 

### 2. Hack Your Childcare
Childcare is the biggest hurdle for working single parents. If you can't afford traditional daycare, you have to get creative:
*   **Childcare Swaps (The Co-op Model):** Find 1 or 2 other single parents in your area or at your workplace. Offer to watch their kids on your days/hours off, in exchange for them watching yours when you work. 
*   **Look for Subsidies:** Depending on where you live, there are often government vouchers or subsidies for childcare for single, working parents. 
*   **Community Programs:** Look into local community centers, YMCAs, Boys & Girls Clubs, or religious institutions. They often offer free or heavily discounted after-school programs or summer camps.

### 3. Optimize Your Work Life
You need a job that works *with* your life, not against it.
*   **Ask for Flexibility:** If you haven't already, have an honest conversation with your boss. Ask if there is any flexibility in your schedule—such as working four 10-hour days, shifting your hours to match school schedules, or working from home one day a week.
*   **Consider "Child-Friendly" Jobs:** If your current job is too rigid, look for jobs where childcare is built-in. Working at a daycare, a school, or a gym often comes with free or discounted childcare for employees.
*   **Set Hard Boundaries:** When you are off the clock, be off the clock. You do not have the extra energy to give your employer free labor. 

### 4. Tap Into Every Available Resource
There is no shame in using resources designed to help you survive. 
*   **Food Assistance:** Look into local food banks, pantries, and government food assistance (like SNAP or WIC if you are in the US). If you can get your groceries for free or cheap, that frees up cash for childcare.
*   **Healthcare:** Ensure you and your children are enrolled in whatever free or low-cost state/national healthcare is available to you.
*   **Buy Nothing Groups:** Join local Facebook "Buy Nothing" groups. People constantly give away high-quality kids' clothes, toys, furniture, and even pantry staples for free.
