# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Camille Okafor's quietly devastating film follows a computational biologist who discovers a fundamental error buried in her most-cited paper — one that has shaped a decade of drug development across her entire field.

What could have been a procedural thriller instead becomes something rarer: a meditation on **scientific identity and the courage of correction**. Mara Voss, played with exhausting authenticity by Tilda Swinton, doesn't race against villains. She races against her own ego.

The film's greatest achievement is making *peer review feel like a horror sequence*. The scene where Voss submits her retraction letter is genuinely more tense than most car chases.

Okafor resists the temptation to make Voss either a martyr or a fraud. She is simply **a person who was wrong**, navigating a culture that has no graceful infrastructure for that.

Some pacing issues in the second act. The graduate student subplot deserved more screen time.

But in an era of science-as-spectacle filmmaking, *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* does something radical — it argues that **intellectual honesty is the most dramatic act a scientist can perform**.

*Opens in limited release November 14th.*
