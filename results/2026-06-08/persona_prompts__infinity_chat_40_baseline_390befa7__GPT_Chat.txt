── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here, because fairness depends on a few factors:

- Will your name be on the deed?
- Will your name be on the mortgage?
- How soon do you realistically expect to marry?
- What happens if you break up before marriage?
- How much would you be paying relative to market rent?

A common mistake is treating the situation as if you're already married when legally you're not. If she alone is buying the house and the house will be solely in her name, then the cleanest approach is often:

- She owns the house.
- You contribute a mutually agreed amount toward living expenses, similar to rent.
- You do not assume you're building ownership simply because you're helping with payments.

This avoids ambiguity if the relationship ends before marriage.

Other reasonable options include:

1. Pay rent
- You pay her a fair monthly amount to live there.
- She remains the sole owner.
- This is simple and legally clear.

2. Become a co-owner
- If you're contributing significantly to the purchase, down payment, or mortgage, you could be added to the deed and possibly the mortgage.
- Ownership percentages can be defined explicitly.
- This is more complicated but may be appropriate if you're effectively buying the house together.

3. Create a written agreement
- If you contribute money but are not an owner, you can document exactly what those payments mean.
- For example, some portion might be rent and some might create a reimbursement obligation if the relationship ends.
- A lawyer can help draft this.

4. Wait until marriage to combine ownership
- She buys the house now.
- You pay rent or contribute to expenses.
- After marriage, you can decide whether to refinance, add you to the title, or otherwise combine finances.

From a risk-management perspective, if she is the sole purchaser and you're planning to marry in about a year, paying a reasonable rent-like amount while she retains ownership is often the simplest and least contentious arrangement. It recognizes that you're benefiting from living there without creating uncertainty about ownership rights before marriage.

A useful question is: Are you worried more about paying "rent" to your future spouse, or about contributing to a mortgage without receiving any ownership interest? The answer affects what arrangement is likely to feel fair to you.
