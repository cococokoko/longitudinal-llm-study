In graph theory, a common example of a "linear graph" is a path graph, where vertices are arranged in a single line and each vertex is connected only to its immediate neighbor(s).

Example: Path graph P5

Vertices:
- V = {A, B, C, D, E}

Edges:
- E = {(A,B), (B,C), (C,D), (D,E)}

Visual representation:

A — B — C — D — E

This graph is called a path of length 4 (because it has 4 edges) and is one of the simplest linear structures in graph theory. Each internal vertex (B, C, D) has degree 2, while the endpoints (A, E) have degree 1.
