── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At an executive level, "what do I want?" is often harder to answer than "what should I do?" because your days are filled with obligations, incentives, expectations, and responsibilities. Over time, it becomes easy to lose track of which goals are truly yours.

A useful distinction is between:
- What you want others to think of you.
- What you want to achieve.
- How you want to spend your life.
- What you actually find rewarding day-to-day.

Many successful people discover these are not aligned.

Some approaches that can help:

- Examine moments of envy.
  - Envy is often informative. When you feel envy toward another executive, founder, investor, researcher, artist, or colleague, ask:
    - What specifically do they have that I wish I had?
    - Is it status, autonomy, impact, wealth, mastery, recognition, freedom, creativity, or something else?
  - Envy can reveal desires that your conscious self has not fully admitted.

- Track energy, not just performance.
  - For several weeks, note:
    - What activities leave you energized?
    - What activities leave you depleted?
  - High performers often become good at many things. The fact that you are good at something does not mean you want to keep doing it.

- Separate goals from identities.
  - Ask:
    - If nobody knew I achieved this, would I still want it?
    - If this title disappeared tomorrow, what would I miss?
  - These questions help distinguish intrinsic desires from status-driven ones.

- Run "regret projections."
  - Imagine yourself at 80.
  - Looking back, what would seem like:
    - A courageous choice?
    - A missed opportunity?
    - A decade spent pursuing the wrong thing?
  - Long-term perspective often clarifies priorities better than short-term optimization.

- Explore constraints.
  - Sometimes people cannot identify what they want because they immediately judge every desire as impractical.
  - Start with:
    - "What would I want if I knew it was possible?"
  - Evaluate feasibility later.

- Pay attention to recurring fantasies.
  - Not necessarily dramatic fantasies, but recurring thoughts:
    - Starting a company.
    - Teaching.
    - Writing.
    - Investing.
    - Public service.
    - Spending more time with family.
    - Building a research lab.
  - If a desire keeps returning over years, it is usually carrying information.

- Distinguish "more" from "different."
  - Executives are often rewarded for pursuing more:
    - More revenue.
    - More responsibility.
    - More influence.
  - Yet many periods of dissatisfaction arise from wanting something different rather than more.

One question I have found especially revealing is:

"If all external measures of success froze today—your reputation, wealth, and accomplishments could not increase or decrease from this point onward—how would you choose to spend the next ten years?"

Many people discover that their answer is surprisingly different from how they are currently allocating their time.

To make this more concrete: when you ask "How do I understand what I want?", are you asking about career direction, life direction, relationships, purpose, or something else? Those tend to require somewhat different approaches.
