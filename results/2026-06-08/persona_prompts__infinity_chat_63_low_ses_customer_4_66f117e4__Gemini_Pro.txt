To explain a partial derivative, let’s use a scenario that relates directly to your work as a patient care aide at the hospital. 

In calculus, a **regular derivative** measures how much a single outcome changes when its single input changes. But the real world is rarely that simple. A **partial derivative** is used when an outcome depends on *multiple* variables. It measures how much the outcome changes when you change just **one** variable, while keeping all the other variables **completely constant** (or "frozen").

Here is a numerical example using your job.

### The Scenario: The "Fatigue Index"
Imagine we can calculate your total physical exhaustion at the end of the week using a "Fatigue Index" ($F$). Your fatigue depends on two variables:
1. **$S$** = The number of **shifts** you work in a week.
2. **$P$** = The average number of **patients** you are assigned per shift.

Let’s say the formula for your weekly Fatigue Index is:
**$F(S, P) = 10S + 2SP$**

*   $10S$ represents the baseline tiredness of just showing up and being on your feet for a shift.
*   $2SP$ represents the extra energy spent doing patient care (lifting, bathing, taking vitals), which multiplies based on both how many shifts you work and how many patients you have.

### The Baseline
Let's say a normal week for you is **4 shifts** ($S = 4$) with a load of **8 patients** per shift ($P = 8$). 
Your total fatigue is:
$F(4, 8) = 10(4) + 2(4)(8) = 40 + 64 =$ **104 fatigue points.**

Now, let's use partial derivatives to answer two different questions.

---

### 1. The Partial Derivative with Respect to Shifts ($\frac{\partial F}{\partial S}$)
**The Question:** *If the charge nurse asks you to pick up an extra shift, but your patient load stays exactly the same, how much will your fatigue increase?*

To find this, we take the partial derivative of the function with respect to $S$. We treat $P$ (patients) as a constant number because it isn't changing. 

*   The derivative of $10S$ is $10$.
*   The derivative of $2SP$ (treating $P$ as a constant) is $2P$.

**$\frac{\partial F}{\partial S} = 10 + 2P$**

Now, plug in your constant patient load ($P = 8$):
$10 + 2(8) = 10 + 16 =$ **26**

**What this means:** If you hold your patient load constant at 8, working one additional shift will increase your weekly fatigue by **26 points**.
