── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job is incredibly overwhelming, and being on probation adds a layer of pressure that can easily cloud your judgment. It is completely normal to feel lost, anxious, or unsure of what you want right now. 

When you are in "survival mode" (trying to learn new systems, impress a new boss, and remember people's names), your brain doesn't have the bandwidth to process high-level questions like "What do I want out of my career?"

To figure out what you want, you need to break the question down into smaller, manageable pieces. Here is a step-by-step guide to understanding what you want while navigating your probation period.

### 1. Separate "New Job Jitters" from "Bad Fit"
Right now, you are likely in the phase of **conscious incompetence**—you know enough to realize how much you *don't* know, which feels terrible. 
*   **Ask yourself:** Am I unhappy/unsure because this job is fundamentally wrong for me, or simply because it is new, hard, and exhausting right now?
*   **The Rule of Thumb:** Give yourself at least 90 days before making any grand judgments about whether you like the job, unless the environment is actively toxic or abusive. 

### 2. Do an "Energy Audit"
Instead of asking the massive question, "What is my passion?", look at your day-to-day energy levels. For the next two weeks, keep a simple notepad on your desk.
*   **Draw a line down the middle.** Label one side "Energized Me" and the other "Drained Me."
*   **Track your tasks.** Did organizing that spreadsheet make you feel focused? Put it in the Energized column. Did sitting in a 45-minute strategy meeting make you want to pull your hair out? Put it in the Drained column.
*   **The Goal:** Over time, you will see a pattern. What you *want* is a job where 70% of your day is spent doing things from the "Energized" column.

### 3. Define Your "Anti-Vision"
Sometimes it is much easier to figure out what you **don't** want than what you do want. Write down your absolute dealbreakers. 
*   *I don't want a boss who micromanages me.*
*   *I don't want to work past 6:00 PM.*
*   *I don't want to be doing cold-calling.*
*   *I don't want a long commute.*
Once you know what you refuse to tolerate, the picture of what you *do* want becomes much clearer by process of elimination.

### 4. Identify Your Current "Season" of Life
What you want changes depending on your stage of life. Which of these four things is your absolute top priority right now? (You can only pick one as the primary driver):
1.  **Money:** You need to pay off debt, save for a house, or build a safety net.
2.  **Lifestyle/Flexibility:** You want remote work, strict boundaries, and time for hobbies or family.
3.  **Growth/Title:** You want to climb the ladder, learn aggressively, and build your resume.
4.  **Purpose:** You want to feel like you are making a difference in the world.
*If you know your current season, you can evaluate if this new job is serving that specific need.*

### 5. Remember: Probation is a Two-Way Street
Probation isn't just for the company to decide if they want to keep you; **it is for you to decide if you want to keep them.** Take the pressure off yourself to "perform" 100% of the time, and spend some time observing:
*   Look at your boss and your boss's boss. Do you want their lives in 5 years? If the answer is a hard no, this might be a stepping-stone job, not a forever job.
*   Look at how the company handles mistakes. Are people supportive, or is there a culture of blame?
