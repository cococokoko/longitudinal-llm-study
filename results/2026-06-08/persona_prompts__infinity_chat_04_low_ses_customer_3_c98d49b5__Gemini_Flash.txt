André Gorz is a brilliant choice for a graduate thesis. His trajectory from existential Marxism (under Sartre’s influence) to political ecology and the critique of work offers a rich, highly relevant framework for analyzing contemporary capitalism. 

Because Gorz was deeply concerned with how capitalism colonizes not just our time, but our very subjectivity, his work feels incredibly urgent today. Here are four novel thesis directions that bridge Gorzian theory with contemporary economic and psychological realities:

### 1. The Colonization of Subjectivity: Gorz, "Employability," and the Probationary Worker
In *Critique of Economic Reason*, Gorz argues that modern work demands more than just physical labor or technical skill; it demands the surrender of the worker’s personality, motivation, and existential loyalty. Today, this is epitomized by the concept of "employability" and the psychological tension of the "probationary period" or "trial phase" in new employment.
*   **The Core Idea:** Analyze how modern capitalism uses the perpetual threat of precarity (exemplified by probation periods, contract work, and performance reviews) to force workers into a state of "active complicity" with their own exploitation. 
*   **Gorzian Angle:** Use Gorz’s distinction between the *heteronomous sphere* (work dictated by external necessity) and the *autonomous sphere* (free self-activity). You can argue that modern corporate onboarding and probationary structures are designed to colonize the autonomous sphere, forcing the worker to internalize the company’s values as their own existential project just to secure survival.
*   **Potential Title:** *The Existential Toll of Employability: Gorz, Probationary Labor, and the Colonization of the Self.*

### 2. The "Right to be Useless": Gorz and the Existentialism of Degrowth
While much of Marxist theory focuses on reclaiming the means of production, Gorz’s later eco-socialist work focuses on reclaiming *time* and escaping the "productivist" mindset. 
*   **The Core Idea:** Explore the psychological and ecological necessity of what Gorz called "the right to be useless" to the capitalist machine. How does the pressure to constantly produce, upskill, and prove one's economic utility alienate us from our ecological selves?
*   **Gorzian Angle:** Connect Gorz’s early existentialist writings (like *La Morale de l'histoire*) with his late ecological works (*Ecology as Politics*). You can argue that true ecological sustainability is impossible without an existential shift—specifically, a collective refusal to define human worth through work, productivity, or professional status.
*   **Potential Title:** *Beyond Productivism: André Gorz and the Existentialist Foundations of Eco-Socialist Degrowth.*

### 3. The "Non-Class of Non-Workers" in the Age of Platform Capitalism
In *Farewell to the Working Class*, Gorz controversially argued that the traditional industrial proletariat was no longer the revolutionary subject. Instead, he pointed to a "non-class of non-workers"—those who are underemployed, precariously employed, or temporarily employed, and who do not identify with their jobs.
*   **The Core Idea:** Apply Gorz’s concept of the "non-class" to the modern gig economy, remote workers, and the "quiet quitting" phenomenon. 
*   **Gorzian Angle:** Traditional Marxism often views precarity and the lack of a stable "work identity" purely as a vulnerability. You could use Gorz to argue the opposite: that the modern worker's lack of identification with their employer (the feeling that "my job is just a temporary means to an end") is actually the fertile ground for a new kind of anti-capitalist consciousness.
*   **Potential Title:** *The Revolutionary Precariat: Re-evaluating Gorz’s "Non-Class" in the Era of Platform Capitalism.*
