How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a backyard vegetable garden can be fairly simple, especially if you choose a few easy crops and set it up to be low-maintenance.

1. Pick the right location
- Choose a spot that gets at least 6–8 hours of sunlight per day.
- Make sure water is easy to access.
- Avoid areas where water pools after rain.

2. Start small
- A garden bed around 4 ft × 8 ft (1.2 m × 2.4 m) is enough for beginners.
- It's easier to manage a small successful garden than a large overwhelming one.

3. Check and improve your soil
- Most vegetables grow best in loose, well-draining soil.
- Mix in compost or other organic matter before planting.
- If your soil is poor, consider a raised bed filled with quality garden soil.

4. Choose beginner-friendly vegetables
Good options include:
- Tomatoes
- Lettuce
- Bush beans
- Zucchini
- Cucumbers
- Radishes
- Green onions
- Peppers

5. Plant at the right time
- Look up your local last frost date.
- Cool-season crops (lettuce, spinach, peas) can often be planted earlier.
- Warm-season crops (tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers) should usually wait until frost danger has passed.

6. Water consistently
- Most vegetables need about 1 inch (2.5 cm) of water per week, more during hot weather.
- Water deeply rather than lightly.
- A drip irrigation system or soaker hose can save time if your work schedule changes from week to week.

7. Mulch
- Add 2–3 inches of straw, shredded leaves, or wood chips around plants.
- Mulch helps retain moisture and reduces weeds.

8. Keep up with basic maintenance
- Check the garden a few times per week for weeds, pests, and watering needs.
- Harvest vegetables regularly to encourage continued production.

For someone with an unpredictable work schedule, low-maintenance crops such as bush beans, zucchini, peppers, and cherry tomatoes are often easier to manage than crops that need frequent harvesting or care.
