── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At its core, the stress response is an evolutionary survival mechanism, but chronic activation shifts this adaptive system into a primary driver of systemic pathology. When a perceived threat triggers the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and the sympathetic nervous system, a cascade of glucocorticoids and catecholamines floods the system. 

Somatic consequences of this chronic allostatic load are profound. Sustained sympathetic tone elevates blood pressure and promotes endothelial dysfunction, accelerating cardiovascular disease. Concurrently, elevated cortisol disrupts metabolic homeostasis—inducing insulin resistance and visceral adiposity—while dysregulating the immune system. This shifts the body toward a chronic, low-grade pro-inflammatory state, impairing wound healing and exacerbating autoimmune conditions.

Neurologically, chronic stress drives structural remodeling in the brain. Sustained glucocorticoid exposure induces dendritic atrophy in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus, impairing executive function, working memory, and neurogenesis. Conversely, it triggers dendritic hypertrophy in the basolateral amygdala, hyper-sensitizing the fear response. This neurobiological shift manifests clinically as heightened anxiety, depressive phenotypes, and cognitive deficits.

Ultimately, the transition from acute adaptation to chronic allostatic overload represents a systemic failure of homeostatic regulation, directly bridging psychological distress with multi-organ pathology.
