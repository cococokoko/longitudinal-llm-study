── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The relationship we have with our mothers often serves as our first blueprint for love, trust, and connection. When a child does not receive adequate emotional warmth, validation, or love from their mother, it can create what psychologists often call the "Mother Wound." 

Because children rely on their caregivers for survival, they rarely blame the parent for a lack of love; instead, they blame themselves. This manifests in various psychological and behavioral patterns in adulthood. 

Here are some common signs that you may not have been loved, validated, or emotionally nurtured enough by your mother as a child:

### 1. Your Relationship with Yourself
*   **A deep, underlying feeling of being "flawed":** You may carry a persistent, quiet belief that there is something fundamentally wrong with you, or that you are inherently unlovable.
*   **A harsh inner critic:** The voice in your head is often punishing, critical, and unforgiving. This is often an internalization of a mother who was highly critical, demanding, or emotionally absent.
*   **Perfectionism:** You may feel that you have to be perfect to earn your right to take up space. You might subconsciously believe that if you achieve enough, look perfect, or do everything right, you will finally be worthy of love.
*   **Hyper-independence:** You have an extreme "I can do it myself" mentality. Because you learned early on that your emotional needs would not be met by your primary caregiver, you decided it was safer to never rely on anyone.

### 2. How You Handle Emotions
*   **Difficulty identifying or trusting your feelings:** If your mother dismissed, mocked, or ignored your emotions ("Stop crying," "You're too sensitive"), you may have learned to disconnect from your feelings. You might constantly second-guess your own reality.
*   **Discomfort with affection:** When people show you genuine love, praise, or physical affection, it might feel uncomfortable, overwhelming, or suspicious. You might wonder, *"What do they want from me?"*
*   **A tendency to suppress anger or sadness:** You may have learned that expressing negative emotions resulted in your mother withdrawing her love or becoming angry, so you learned to swallow your feelings to keep the peace.

### 3. Your Adult Relationships
*   **People-pleasing and poor boundaries:** You may constantly put others' needs before your own, fearing that if you say "no" or set a boundary, people will abandon you. You trade your authenticity for attachment.
*   **Attracting emotionally unavailable partners:** We are subconsciously drawn to what is familiar. If your mother was emotionally distant, critical, or inconsistent, you may find yourself repeatedly drawn to romantic partners or friends who treat you the same way, hoping to finally "win" their love.
*   **Anxious or avoidant attachment:** You might be terrified of abandonment, leading you to cling tightly to partners (anxious). Alternatively, you might push people away the moment they get too close, fearing that intimacy will lead to pain (avoidant).

### 4. Your Current Dynamic with Your Mother
*   **Interactions feel draining or empty:** Spending time with her might leave you feeling exhausted, unseen, or emotionally hollow, rather than comforted or supported.
*   **The "Fantasy Mother" hope:** You might find yourself continually trying to explain your feelings to her, hoping that *this time* she will understand, apologize, or give you the maternal love you crave, only to be disappointed again.
*   **Guilt and obligation:** Your relationship with her may be driven entirely by a sense of duty or guilt, rather than genuine joy or connection. 

### Important Things to Remember:
*   **Emotional neglect is often invisible.** Your mother may have provided for you physically (food, shelter, education) but failed to provide for you emotionally. Emotional neglect is defined by what *didn't* happen (hugs, comforting words, emotional safety), making it hard to pinpoint.
*   **It was not your fault.** A parent's inability to love a child properly is a reflection of the parent's own trauma, limitations, and emotional immaturity—**never** a reflection of the child's worth. You were always worthy of love.

If these signs resonate deeply with you, it can be incredibly helpful to work with a trauma-informed therapist. They can help you navigate the grief of the Mother Wound and teach you how to "reparent" yourself, offering yourself the compassion, love, and validation you deserved all along.
