In graph theory, a **linear graph** is more commonly called a **path graph**. It is a graph where all the points (called *nodes* or *vertices*) connect in a single, straight sequence without any branching, skipping, or loops. 

To make this concept easy to visualize, let’s use an example straight from your work at the hospital: **A patient's sequential journey through a clinic.**

Imagine you are escorting a newly arrived patient through a strict, step-by-step admission process. 

### The Hospital Example
*   **The Nodes (Vertices):** These are the specific rooms or stations the patient must visit.
*   **The Edges (Lines):** These are the hallways or the direct transitions connecting one room to the next.

Here is what your linear graph looks like:

**[Waiting Room]** —— **[Vitals Station]** —— **[Exam Room]** —— **[Discharge Desk]**

### Why is this a Linear (Path) Graph?
In graph theory, this specific setup has a few strict rules that make it "linear":

1.  **Two Endpoints:** The Waiting Room and the Discharge Desk are the endpoints. In graph theory, these nodes have a "degree of 1" because they only have one line connecting them to the rest of the graph. You start at one, and you end at the other.
2.  **Middle Points:** The Vitals Station (where you might take their blood pressure) and the Exam Room are the internal nodes. They have a "degree of 2" because they connect to exactly two things: the step right before them, and the step right after them.
3.  **No Branching:** The patient cannot go from the Waiting Room straight to the Exam Room. They *must* pass through the Vitals Station. There are no alternate routes or side-quests. 
4.  **No Loops:** The patient doesn't go backward. The flow goes in one continuous line.

If the hospital suddenly added a hallway that allowed patients to skip the Vitals Station and go straight to the Exam Room, it would create a triangle shape in the workflow. At that point, it would **no longer** be a linear graph!
