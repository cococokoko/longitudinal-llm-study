── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you have a row of dominoes.

You ask, "If I knock over the first domino, what will the 100th domino do?"

Sometimes it's easy to know. If all the dominoes are lined up the same way, you can quickly say, "The 100th domino will fall."

But imagine each domino follows a tiny rule, and the way one domino moves affects the next in complicated ways. Now, to find out what the 100th domino does, you can't use a shortcut. You have to watch domino 1, then domino 2, then domino 3, all the way to domino 100.

Computational irreducibility is the idea that some things are like that. There is no faster way to know what happens than to let each step happen, one by one.

Another way to think about it:

- If I ask, "What is 2 + 2?" you can jump straight to the answer: 4.
- If I ask, "What will this very complicated game look like after a million moves?" sometimes there is no shortcut.
- The only way to know is to play all million moves.

So computational irreducibility means:

- Some processes can't be predicted with a clever shortcut.
- To know the result, you must follow the process step by step.
- Even a super-smart computer may have to do all the work rather than skip ahead.

It's like reading a mystery book where nobody can tell you the ending without actually reading through the story.
