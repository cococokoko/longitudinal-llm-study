How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi depends on what problem you're experiencing (slow speeds, weak signal, dropped connections, congestion), but these steps help most homes:

- Place the router centrally
  - Put it in an open area, elevated on a shelf or table.
  - Avoid closets, cabinets, basements, and corners of the house.
  - Keep it away from large metal objects and appliances.

- Use the 5 GHz or 6 GHz band when available
  - 2.4 GHz travels farther but is often more crowded.
  - 5 GHz and 6 GHz typically provide higher speeds and less interference over shorter distances.

- Update router firmware
  - Install firmware updates from the router manufacturer to improve performance and security.

- Optimize Wi‑Fi channels
  - In crowded neighborhoods, neighboring networks can interfere.
  - Use your router's automatic channel selection or manually choose a less congested channel.

- Upgrade old equipment
  - If your router is several years old, consider a Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 6E router.
  - Older devices may limit overall performance.

- Reduce interference
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and Bluetooth-heavy devices when possible.

- Use a mesh Wi‑Fi system for larger homes
  - Mesh systems provide better coverage than a single router in many multi-room or multi-story homes.

- Add wired connections where possible
  - Connect stationary devices (PCs, TVs, game consoles) via Ethernet to reduce wireless congestion.

- Check internet speed separately
  - Run a speed test near the router and compare it to your internet plan.
  - If speeds are poor even next to the router, the issue may be with your internet service rather than Wi‑Fi coverage.

- Secure your network
  - Use WPA3 (or WPA2 if WPA3 isn't available).
  - A strong password prevents unauthorized users from consuming bandwidth.
