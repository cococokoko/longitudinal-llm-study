# The Effects of Stress on Body and Mind

Stress triggers a cascade of physiological responses rooted in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and sympathetic nervous system activation. When a perceived threat is encountered, cortisol and catecholamines — primarily epinephrine and norepinephrine — are rapidly released, preparing the organism for fight-or-flight response.

**Physiologically**, acute stress elevates heart rate, blood pressure, and glucose availability while temporarily suppressing non-essential functions including digestion, immune activity, and reproductive processes. Chronic stress, however, sustains these disruptions pathologically. Prolonged cortisol elevation contributes to cardiovascular disease, metabolic dysregulation, immunosuppression, and accelerated cellular aging through telomere shortening — mechanisms your research group likely encounters across multiple disease contexts.

**Neurologically**, chronic stress induces structural and functional remodeling. The hippocampus, rich in glucocorticoid receptors, demonstrates measurable volume reduction under sustained cortisol exposure, impairing memory consolidation and cognitive flexibility. Simultaneously, the amygdala undergoes hypertrophy and heightened reactivity, reinforcing anxiety and threat-detection loops. Prefrontal cortical thinning further compromises executive function and emotional regulation.

**Psychologically**, these neurobiological changes manifest as impaired concentration, mood dysregulation, and increased vulnerability to anxiety and depressive disorders. The bidirectional relationship between psychological perception and physiological response creates self-reinforcing cycles that are clinically challenging to interrupt.

Critically, individual stress responses vary considerably based on genetic polymorphisms in glucocorticoid receptor genes, early-life adversity, and social support networks — variables increasingly relevant to precision medicine approaches in stress-related disease research.

Understanding these mechanisms at both molecular and systems levels remains essential for developing targeted interventions across the spectrum from prevention to treatment.
