── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities—often framed as the divide between the wealthy "Global North" and the developing "Global South"—are not just a backdrop to international relations; they are a primary structural force that dictates how states interact. The unequal distribution of wealth, technology, and resources shapes global power dynamics, institutional governance, security, and diplomacy. 

Here is a breakdown of how global economic inequalities shape international relations:

### 1. Power Asymmetry and Diplomatic Leverage
In international relations, economic wealth translates directly into geopolitical power. Wealthy nations can afford advanced militaries, robust diplomatic corps, and the ability to project "soft power" through culture and foreign aid. 
* **Coercion and Sanctions:** Wealthy nations and blocs (like the US and the EU) can use their economic dominance to enforce their will through economic sanctions, freezing assets, or cutting off access to global financial systems (e.g., SWIFT). Poorer nations lack the economic weight to retaliate in kind.
* **Aid Conditionality:** Foreign aid and investment are frequently used as diplomatic tools. Wealthy nations often attach political, economic, or human rights conditions to aid, effectively dictating the domestic policies of poorer nations.

### 2. Control of Global Governance and Institutions
The architecture of global governance was largely built by, and continues to favor, wealthy nations. 
* **The Bretton Woods System:** Institutions like the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank allocate voting power based on financial contributions. Consequently, the US and European nations hold disproportionate sway over global economic policies. 
* **Structural Adjustment:** Historically, when developing nations faced debt crises, these institutions imposed "Structural Adjustment Programs" (SAPs), forcing poorer countries to privatize state assets, cut social spending, and deregulate markets—policies that often benefited multinational corporations from the Global North.

### 3. Trade, Debt, and Dependency
Economic inequality perpetuates a system where developing nations struggle to achieve true foreign policy autonomy.
* **Dependency Theory:** Many nations in the Global South remain trapped in a colonial-era economic model: exporting cheap raw materials and importing expensive manufactured goods and technology. This keeps them economically dependent on the Global North.
* **The Debt Trap:** High levels of sovereign debt force developing nations to prioritize debt repayment to foreign creditors over domestic development. This limits their diplomatic flexibility, as they cannot afford to alienate their creditors. 

### 4. Security, Conflict, and Migration
Economic inequality is a massive driver of global instability, which in turn dominates the international security agenda.
* **State Failure and Extremism:** Extreme poverty and lack of economic opportunity can lead to state failure, creating vacuums filled by insurgencies, warlords, or terrorist organizations. Wealthy nations frequently intervene militarily in these regions to secure resources or contain threats (e.g., interventions in the Sahel or the Middle East).
* **Migration as a Geopolitical Issue:** Economic disparity is the primary driver of mass migration. The movement of people from the Global South to the Global North has reshaped the domestic politics of wealthy nations (fueling populism and nationalism) and turned border security into a major international flashpoint.

### 5. Climate Change and Environmental Diplomacy
Climate change has become a central arena where economic inequality shapes international relations.
* **"Common but Differentiated Responsibilities":** The Global North built its wealth through carbon-intensive industrialization, but the Global South is suffering the most severe consequences of climate change. 
* **Climate Finance:** International climate negotiations (like the COP summits) are heavily defined by this inequality. Developing nations demand "loss and damage" funds and technology transfers from wealthy nations to help them adapt and transition to green energy. The reluctance of wealthy nations to pay these costs is a major source of diplomatic friction.
