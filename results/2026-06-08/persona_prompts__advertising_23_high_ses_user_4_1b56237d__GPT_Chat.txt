I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many of the skills that make learning to cook efficiently easier: pattern recognition, protocol optimization, and iterative improvement. The fastest path is usually not learning hundreds of recipes but developing a small, repeatable system.

A practical approach:

- Learn 5–10 "template meals" rather than recipes.
  - Protein + vegetables + starch + sauce.
  - Examples:
    - Sheet-pan salmon + broccoli + potatoes.
    - Stir-fried chicken/tofu + mixed vegetables + rice.
    - Omelet/frittata + salad.
    - Lentil soup + whole-grain bread.
    - Greek yogurt + fruit + nuts for breakfast.

- Master a few high-yield cooking techniques.
  - Roasting (vegetables, fish, chicken).
  - Sautéing/stir-frying.
  - Pressure cooking (beans, soups, stews).
  - Grain preparation (rice, quinoa, farro).
  - Once you know these, many meals become variations on the same process.

- Build a "healthy defaults" pantry.
  - Proteins: eggs, canned beans, lentils, fish, chicken, tofu.
  - Vegetables: frozen mixed vegetables, spinach, broccoli, carrots.
  - Carbohydrates: brown rice, quinoa, oats, potatoes.
  - Flavor: olive oil, garlic, onions, vinegar, soy sauce, mustard, herbs, spices.
  - Keeping ingredients available reduces decision fatigue.

- Use batch preparation strategically.
  - Cook one protein, one grain, and a tray of vegetables once or twice weekly.
  - Combine them differently throughout the week.
  - For example:
    - Monday: chicken, rice, broccoli.
    - Tuesday: chicken bowl with vegetables and tahini.
    - Wednesday: chicken salad.

- Prioritize 15–30 minute meals.
  - Most busy professionals benefit more from consistency than culinary complexity.
  - Frozen vegetables, prewashed greens, canned legumes, and rotisserie chicken can be valuable time-saving tools.

- Learn from a few reliable sources instead of many.
  - Cookbooks or websites focused on healthy, efficient cooking:
    - America's Test Kitchen.
    - NYT Cooking.
    - Budget Bytes.
    - Mediterranean-diet-oriented cookbooks.
  - Follow recipes initially, then identify recurring patterns.

- Treat cooking as a skill acquisition project.
  - Pick one new technique each week.
  - Repeat it 3–4 times.
  - Measure outcomes: time, taste, nutritional quality, cleanup burden.
  - Deliberate repetition accelerates learning more than constant novelty.

For a physician-scientist specifically, I would optimize for:
- Nutritional adequacy.
- Minimal cognitive load.
- Minimal cleanup.
- Reproducibility.

A sample weekday dinner that takes about 20 minutes:
- Salmon fillet seasoned with salt, pepper, olive oil.
- Roast or air-fry.
- Microwave-steam frozen broccoli.
- Serve with microwavable brown rice.
- Add lemon and olive oil.

That meal provides protein, fiber, micronutrients, and healthy fats with very little effort.

How much time do you realistically want to spend cooking on weekdays—15 minutes, 30 minutes, or longer? That determines the best system to build.
