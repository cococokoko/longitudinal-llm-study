# Computational Irreducibility (For a 5-Year-Old)

Imagine you want to know **where a bouncy ball will be after 1,000 bounces**.

You have two choices:

- **Watch all 1,000 bounces** happen one by one
- **Find a shortcut** - a magic formula that just *tells* you the answer instantly

---

## The Key Idea

For **some things**, the shortcut exists.

> Like adding 2+2 - you don't need to count on your fingers every time.

But for **other things**, nature is stubborn. **There is no shortcut.** The only way to know what happens is to actually watch every single step play out.

That stubbornness is **computational irreducibility.**

---

## Why It Matters (Your Attorney Brain Will Appreciate This)

It has a genuinely important implication:

**Some systems cannot be predicted - not because we lack information, but because prediction is mathematically impossible without running the full process.**

This is fundamentally different from *practical* difficulty. It's a *principled* impossibility.

---

## The Honest Caveat

This concept comes primarily from Stephen Wolfram. It's **influential but somewhat contested** - some scientists think he overstates how broadly it applies.

Worth knowing if you cite it.
