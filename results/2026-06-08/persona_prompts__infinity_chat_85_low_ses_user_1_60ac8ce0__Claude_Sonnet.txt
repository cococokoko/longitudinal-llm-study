── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work full-time in a fast food job with an unpredictable schedule.

Can you give me a powerful rhetorical question for an essay about the harms of social media on teens?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here's a strong rhetorical question for your essay:

**"If we wouldn't hand a teenager a product known to increase anxiety, depression, and self-harm, why do we celebrate handing them a smartphone?"**

This works well because it:
- Creates a **direct comparison** that's hard to dismiss
- Forces the reader to **confront an inconsistency** in common thinking
- Doesn't require a stated answer - the implication is clear

A few alternatives depending on your angle:

- **On comparison culture:** "When did we decide it was acceptable to let teenagers measure their worth in likes?"
- **On corporate responsibility:** "Would we trust cigarette companies to regulate themselves around children - so why do we trust social media companies?"
- **On inaction:** "How many studies showing harm to children are enough before we demand change?"

The strongest rhetorical questions for this topic tend to **expose a contradiction** or **challenge the reader's passive acceptance** of the status quo.
