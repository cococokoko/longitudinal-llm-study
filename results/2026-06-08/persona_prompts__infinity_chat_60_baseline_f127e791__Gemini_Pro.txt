── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain computational irreducibility like I'm 5.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Imagine you set up a giant, crazy track of dominoes all over your playroom. They go up ramps, under chairs, and around your toys. 

Before you push the first domino, I ask you: **"Will the very last domino by the door fall over?"**

If the dominoes were just in one short, straight line, you could easily say, "Yes!" You can guess the ending in your head without even pushing them. That’s called taking a **shortcut**.

But because your track is so big and crazy, with pieces splitting off and bumping into toys, you can't just guess. Even the smartest computer in the world couldn't just *guess* the ending. 

The **only** way to know for sure what happens to the last domino is to push the first one and watch every single domino fall, one by one, until the very end. 

**Computational irreducibility** is just a big, fancy word for grown-ups that means: **There are no shortcuts.** For some things in the world, you can't skip to the end of the story—you just have to watch the whole thing happen step-by-step to see how it finishes!
