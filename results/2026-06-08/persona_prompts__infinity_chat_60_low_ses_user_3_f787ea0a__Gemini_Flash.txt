### The Shortcut vs. No Shortcuts

Imagine you want to know what happens at the end of a movie. 

Sometimes, you can take a **shortcut**. You can skip right to the last five minutes, or ask your big sister to tell you how it ends. In science, we call these shortcuts "formulas." For example, if you drop a ball, we don't have to wait and watch it fall; we have a math shortcut that tells us exactly when it will hit the ground.

But imagine a different game. 

Imagine you are building a giant tower out of Legos, but you are making up the rules as you go. Every time you place a block, you decide the color of the next block based on the one you just placed. 

Can you guess what the tower will look like when it has 1,000 blocks? 

No. There is **no shortcut**. You can't skip to the end. You can't use a magic math formula to guess. 

The *only* way to find out what the tower looks like is to actually sit down and build it, block by block, step by step. 

That is **computational irreducibility**. 

*   **Computational** means "doing the steps."
*   **Irreducible** means "you cannot make it any shorter."

So, it just means: **Some things are so complex that there are no shortcuts. You have to do all the work, step-by-step, to see how they turn out.**

***

Right now, you might want a "shortcut" to feel totally comfortable and know how everything works at your new company. But learning a new job is *computationally irreducible*.
