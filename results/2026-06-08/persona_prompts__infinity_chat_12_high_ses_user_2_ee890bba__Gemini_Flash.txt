── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney, you are likely trained to look at evidence, analyze patterns, and understand the root causes of complex behaviors. When applying this analytical lens inward, looking at the impact of maternal under-loving (often referred to in psychology as maternal deprivation, emotional neglect, or insecure attachment) reveals distinct, predictable patterns. 

In high-achieving professionals, these childhood wounds often manifest not as failure, but as a specific type of hyper-functional survival strategy. 

Here are the primary signs—categorized by behavioral, relational, and internal patterns—that suggest a person did not receive adequate emotional warmth, validation, or love from their mother during childhood.

---

### 1. The High-Achiever Paradox (The "Human Doing" vs. "Human Being")
For children who do not receive unconditional love, achievement often becomes a proxy for worth. If you were not loved for *who you were*, you learned you must be loved for *what you can do*.
*   **Chronic Perfectionism:** An underlying belief that any mistake will result in total rejection or exposure as "unworthy."
*   **Conditional Self-Worth:** Your self-esteem is entirely dependent on your latest victory, case won, or professional accolade. The high of achievement fades almost instantly, replaced by the anxiety of the next task.
*   **Hyper-Independence:** A deeply ingrained belief that "I can only rely on myself." Asking for help is viewed not just as a weakness, but as a safety risk. In childhood, relying on an emotionally unavailable mother led to disappointment, so you adapted by becoming entirely self-sufficient.
*   **Inability to Rest:** Feeling intense guilt, anxiety, or worthlessness when not being productive. Relaxation feels dangerous because, historically, safety was tied to performance.

### 2. Relational and Attachment Patterns
The relationship with the mother is the blueprint for all future relationships. When that blueprint is flawed, adult relationships carry specific distortions.
*   **The "Armor" of Emotional Distance (Avoidant Attachment):** Keeping colleagues, friends, and even romantic partners at arm's length. You may be highly respected and socially skilled, but you rarely let people see your vulnerability. 
*   **Hyper-Vigilance to Rejection:** An exquisite sensitivity to micro-shifts in other people’s moods. You might constantly scan partners, clients, or partners at your firm for signs of disapproval, interpreting neutral signals as negative.
*   **People-Pleasing and Boundary Issues:** Over-functioning in relationships—taking on other people's emotional burdens, saying "yes" when you want to say "no," and fearing that setting a boundary will cause people to leave you.
*   **Attraction to "Difficult" Partners:** A subconscious tendency to choose romantic partners who are emotionally unavailable, critical, or hard to please, because this dynamic feels familiar and "safe" to the nervous system.

### 3. The Internal Landscape
The way a mother speaks to her child eventually becomes the child’s inner monologue. 
*   **A Relentless Inner Critic:** An internal voice that is harsh, punitive, and unforgiving. It acts as a preemptive defense mechanism: if you beat yourself up first, it won't hurt as bad when others do.
*   **Deep-Seated Imposter Syndrome:** Despite objective, verifiable success (like reaching senior status at a prestigious firm), a persistent feeling that you are a "fraud" and that people will eventually realize you don't belong.
*   **Difficulty Receiving Care:** Feeling deeply uncomfortable, suspicious, or unworthy when someone offers genuine compliments, love, or care. You may feel an immediate urge to "pay it back" to balance the ledger.
*   **Alexithymia (Emotional Numbness):** Difficulty identifying or expressing what you are feeling. If your childhood emotions were ignored, ridiculed, or met with anger, you learned to disconnect your brain from your body to survive.

### 4. Somatic and Physiological Signs
The body keeps the score of childhood emotional neglect.
*   **Chronic Hyper-Arousal:** Living in a constant state of "fight or flight." This can manifest as chronic muscle tension (especially in the jaw, neck, and shoulders), digestive issues, or insomnia.
*   **The "Void" or Chronic Emptiness:** A vague, persistent feeling of loneliness or emptiness that cannot be filled by food, alcohol, work, or success.
