── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory literature, the term "linear graph" can occasionally be ambiguous. Depending on the specific context or the era of the literature you are referencing, it typically refers to one of two distinct concepts: a **path graph** or a **line graph**. 

Here is a precise example of each to ensure your intended context is covered.

### 1. "Linear Graph" as a Path Graph ($P_n$)
In introductory contexts, a linear graph simply refers to a tree with maximum degree 2—commonly known as a path graph. Its vertices can be drawn in a single, unbranched line.

**Example: The Path Graph $P_4$**
*   **Vertex Set ($V$):** $\{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}$
*   **Edge Set ($E$):** $\{\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \{v_3, v_4\}\}$

### 2. "Linear Graph" as a Line Graph ($L(G)$)
In older literature (and occasionally in physics or chemistry contexts applying graph theory), "linear graph" is used synonymously with the *line graph* (or edge graph) of a given graph $G$. The line graph $L(G)$ represents the adjacencies between edges of $G$.

**Example: The Line Graph of a Claw Graph ($K_{1,3}$)**
*   Let $G$ be the bipartite claw graph $K_{1,3}$, consisting of a central vertex $u$ and three leaves $x, y,$ and $z$.
*   The edges of $G$ are $e_1=\{u,x\}$, $e_2=\{u,y\}$, and $e_3=\{u,z\}$.
*   To construct the line graph $L(G)$, these edges become the vertices: $V(L(G)) = \{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.
*   Because $e_1, e_2,$ and $e_3$ all mutually intersect at the central vertex $u$ in $G$, they are all adjacent to one another in $L(G)$.
*   Therefore, the line graph $L(K_{1,3})$ is the complete graph $K_3$ (a triangle).
