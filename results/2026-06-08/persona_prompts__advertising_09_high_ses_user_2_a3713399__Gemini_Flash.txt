I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

Building a personal brand on social media.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at a well-established law firm, your personal brand is one of your most valuable assets. On social media, your goal is not to become an "influencer" in the traditional sense, but to build **digital authority**—positioning yourself as the go-to expert for your specific practice area, attracting high-value clients, and recruiting top talent.

Because your time is highly valuable and the stakes for your reputation are high, your social media strategy must be **efficient, sophisticated, and compliant.**

Here is a strategic blueprint for building your personal brand on social media.

---

### Phase 1: Platform Selection (Where to Play)

Do not try to be everywhere. Focus on the platforms where your clients and peers actually spend their time.

1.  **LinkedIn (Primary - 90% of your effort):** This is the undisputed B2B platform. Your clients (General Counsel, C-Suite executives, business owners) and referral sources are here.
2.  **X/Twitter (Secondary - Optional):** Useful if your practice involves public policy, tech, crypto, or if you want to connect with journalists and academics.
3.  **Substack (Optional):** Excellent if you want to write deep-dive, long-form legal analysis that is delivered directly to subscribers' inboxes.

*Avoid TikTok and Instagram unless your practice is highly consumer-facing (e.g., high-net-worth family law or personal injury).*

---

### Phase 2: Defining Your "Niche" and Voice

Do not post about "the law" in general. Define your **Content Pillars**—the 3 to 4 topics you want to be known for.

*   **Example (M&A Attorney):**
    *   *Pillar 1:* Trends in mid-market tech acquisitions.
    *   *Pillar 2:* Common pitfalls in due diligence.
    *   *Pillar 3:* Mentorship and developing junior associates at big firms.
*   **Your Voice:** Authoritative yet accessible. Avoid "legalese." Write the way you would speak to a client over lunch—clear, pragmatic, and focused on business outcomes, not just legal theory.

---

### Phase 3: The Content Strategy (What to Post)

The best legal content translates complex legal developments into **business implications**. Your audience doesn't just want to know *what* the law is; they want to know *how it affects their bottom line*.

#### 1. The "So What?" Analysis (Newsjacking)
When a major ruling, regulation, or industry event occurs, explain it immediately.
*   *Structure:* "The FTC just banned non-competes. Here are the 3 things every CEO needs to do by Friday to protect their IP."

#### 2. De-identified Case Studies
Share your wins and lessons learned without violating confidentiality.
*   *Structure:* "We recently helped a client navigate a hostile takeover attempt. Here is how we used a 'poison pill' strategy to buy them 30 days of negotiating leverage."

#### 3. The "Behind the Curtain" Post
Humanize yourself. People do business with people they like and trust.
*   *Topics:* Lessons from 20 years in practice, how you prepare for oral arguments, or a photo from a panel you spoke on.

#### 4. The "Myth Buster"
Address common misconceptions in your practice area.
*   *Structure:* "Many founders think [X] protects their trademark. It doesn't. Here’s why."

---

*   **The Sunday Batch (1 Hour):** Write 3 posts for the week on Sunday evening or Monday morning. Use a scheduling tool (like Buffer or Publer) to schedule them.
