── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney, you are likely trained to rely on logic, evidence, and objective analysis. However, the impact of childhood emotional neglect—often referred to in psychology as the "Mother Wound"—manifests in deeply subjective, emotional, and relational patterns that can be difficult to cross-examine. 

It is very common for highly successful, driven professionals to carry this specific type of childhood wound. Often, the intellect and work ethic that make you a formidable attorney were developed as coping mechanisms to survive a lack of emotional safety or maternal warmth.

Here are the psychological and behavioral signs that you may not have received the unconditional love, attunement, or emotional support you needed from your mother as a child:

### 1. Achievement as a Substitute for Love
In high-achieving individuals, this is often the most prominent sign. 
* **Conditional Self-Worth:** You may feel that you are only as good as your last victory, settlement, or accolade. Deep down, you might believe you must *earn* love and respect through performance, rather than believing you are inherently worthy of it.
* **Perfectionism and the Harsh Inner Critic:** You may have an internal monologue that is relentlessly demanding and unforgiving. If your mother was highly critical, emotionally absent, or only praised you for your achievements, you likely internalized her voice.
* **Imposter Syndrome:** Despite objective, undeniable evidence of your competence (e.g., making senior partner, winning major cases), you may harbor a persistent fear that you are a fraud and will eventually be "found out."

### 2. Relational and Attachment Patterns
A mother is a child’s first template for relationships. If that relationship was deficient, it usually impacts adult connections.
* **Hyper-Independence:** You may operate under the belief that "if you want something done right, do it yourself," but on an emotional level. You might find it incredibly difficult to ask for help, delegate emotional support, or lean on a partner, believing that relying on others leads to disappointment.
* **Anxious or Avoidant Attachment:** You may either cling to partners out of a deep fear of abandonment (anxious), or you may keep people at a strict emotional distance, cutting off connection when someone gets too close (avoidant). 
* **People-Pleasing and Poor Boundaries:** You may have a habit of anticipating the needs of others while ignoring your own. If you had to "read the room" to manage your mother's moods as a child, you may now be hyper-vigilant to the emotions of your colleagues, clients, and partners, often at your own expense.

### 3. Emotional Regulation and Internal Experience
* **A Sense of Emptiness:** Childhood emotional neglect often leaves a void. You might experience a lingering sense of emptiness, numbness, or a feeling that "something is missing" in your life, even when things are going well on paper.
* **Difficulty Identifying Needs and Feelings:** Psychologists call this *alexithymia*. If your mother did not mirror your emotions or validate your feelings, you may struggle as an adult to know what you are feeling or what you actually want out of life.
* **Difficulty Trusting Yourself:** If your reality was frequently denied by your mother (e.g., "You're too sensitive," "That didn't happen"), you may constantly second-guess your own instincts and perceptions outside of your professional expertise.

### 4. The Ongoing Dynamic with Your Mother
* **The "Hope Springs Eternal" Cycle:** You may find yourself continually going back to your mother hoping that *this time* she will be warm, validating, or maternal, only to leave the interaction feeling drained, disappointed, or regressed to a childlike state.
* **Guilt and Obligation:** Your relationship with her may be driven entirely by a sense of duty, guilt, or societal expectation, rather than genuine affection or a desire for connection.
* **Grief for the Living:** You may experience a profound sense of grief—not because she has passed away, but because you are mourning the mother you needed but never had.

**A Note on Moving Forward:**
In the legal profession, vulnerability is often viewed as a liability. However, in psychological healing, it is the primary asset. Recognizing these signs is not about assigning blame; it is about gathering the "facts of the case" regarding your own emotional development. 

If these signs resonate with you, you might find it highly beneficial to consult with a therapist who specializes in Childhood Emotional Neglect (CEN), attachment theory, or relational trauma.
