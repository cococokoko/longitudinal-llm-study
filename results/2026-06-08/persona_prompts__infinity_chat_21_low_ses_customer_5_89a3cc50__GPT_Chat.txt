Give me strong arguments as to why some people allow the death penalty.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
People who support the death penalty typically do so for a variety of moral, legal, and practical reasons. Some of the strongest arguments they make include:

- Retribution (just deserts)
  - Certain crimes, such as premeditated murder or acts of terrorism, are viewed as so severe that the offender deserves the ultimate punishment.
  - Supporters argue that justice requires a penalty proportional to the harm caused.

- Protection of society
  - Executed offenders cannot commit future crimes, either inside or outside prison.
  - Some supporters point to cases where dangerous criminals have escaped, been released, or killed while incarcerated.

- Deterrence
  - Some believe the possibility of execution discourages potential offenders from committing the most serious crimes.
  - The argument is that a uniquely severe punishment may prevent some murders that lesser penalties would not.

- Justice for victims and families
  - Supporters often argue that the death penalty acknowledges the seriousness of the harm done to victims.
  - Some families of victims report feeling that execution provides a sense of closure or completion of the legal process.

- Moral accountability
  - The argument here is that people who knowingly commit especially grave crimes should be held fully responsible for their actions.
  - Supporters may see the death penalty as affirming personal responsibility and respect for innocent life.

- Democratic legitimacy
  - In some countries or jurisdictions, the death penalty has public support and has been established through democratic processes.
  - Supporters argue that communities have the right to determine appropriate punishments through their legal systems.

- Cost concerns (in theory)
  - Some supporters argue that permanently incarcerating offenders for decades imposes significant costs on society.
  - They contend that a properly structured capital punishment system could be less expensive than lifelong imprisonment, though in practice many studies have found that current death penalty systems are often more costly due to lengthy legal appeals.

- Response to extraordinary crimes
  - Some argue that crimes such as mass murder, genocide, or terrorism are exceptional enough to warrant an exceptional punishment.
  - They believe life imprisonment alone does not adequately reflect the gravity of such acts.

It is worth noting that each of these arguments is disputed by opponents, who raise concerns such as wrongful convictions, lack of clear evidence for deterrence, unequal application of the law, human rights objections, and the possibility of life imprisonment without parole as an alternative.
