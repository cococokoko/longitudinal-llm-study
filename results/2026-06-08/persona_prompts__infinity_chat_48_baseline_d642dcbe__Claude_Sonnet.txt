The Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) witnessed remarkable economic development that transformed China into one of the ancient world's most prosperous civilizations. The early Han emperors adopted a policy of light taxation and minimal government interference, allowing the agricultural sector to recover from the devastation of the preceding Qin Dynasty and the civil wars that followed it. As the population grew and agricultural productivity increased, surplus wealth fueled the expansion of commerce and trade. The government played a significant role in economic management by implementing state monopolies on essential commodities such as iron and salt, which generated substantial revenue for the imperial treasury while also regulating prices and supply. The opening and expansion of the Silk Road under Emperor Wu proved to be a transformative development, connecting China to Central Asia, the Middle East, and eventually the Mediterranean world, enabling the export of silk, ceramics, and other luxury goods in exchange for precious metals, glassware, and exotic products. Coinage was standardized, with the wuzhu coin becoming the dominant currency and facilitating commercial transactions across the empire. Urban centers grew as hubs of trade and craft production, and a merchant class emerged, though it remained socially subordinate to farmers and officials in the Confucian social hierarchy. Together, these developments established a sophisticated and dynamic economy that supported a population of roughly 60 million people and laid the foundation for China's enduring economic traditions.
