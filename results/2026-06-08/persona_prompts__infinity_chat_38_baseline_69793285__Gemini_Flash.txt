Do you know the demon Morloch?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, though the name is most commonly spelled **Moloch** (or **Molech**). In historical, biblical, and demonological traditions, Moloch is one of the most infamous figures, associated with fire, sacrifice, and extreme cruelty. 

Here is a breakdown of who Moloch/Morloch is, from his ancient origins to his depiction as a demon.

---

### 1. Ancient Origins: The Canaanite Deity
Originally, Moloch was not a demon, but a god worshiped by ancient Canaanite and Phoenician civilizations in the Middle East. 
* **The Name:** The name likely derives from the Hebrew word *melech*, meaning "king." 
* **Child Sacrifice:** Moloch is most famously (and infamously) associated with child sacrifice. According to ancient accounts and biblical texts, worshipers would sacrifice their firstborn children to Moloch by placing them in the fire. 
* **The Bronze Statue:** Classical writers described a giant bronze statue of Moloch with the head of a bull and the body of a man. The statue’s arms were outstretched over a fire pit. Children were placed on the bronze hands, sliding down into the furnace below. To drown out the screams of the children, drums and horns were played loudly.

*(Note: Modern historians debate whether "Moloch" was the name of an actual god, or if the word "mlk" referred to the *type* of sacrifice itself, but in Judeo-Christian tradition, he is firmly established as a specific entity.)*

### 2. In the Bible
In the Hebrew Bible (Old Testament), God strictly forbids the Israelites from worshiping Moloch. 
* In **Leviticus 18:21**, it is written: *"Do not give any of your children to be sacrificed to Molech, for you must not profane the name of your God."*
* Despite these warnings, several kings of Judah, including King Solomon (under the influence of his foreign wives) and King Manasseh, built altars to Moloch in the Valley of Hinnom (Gehenna), just outside Jerusalem. This valley later became a symbol of hell.

### 3. Transition to Demonology
With the rise of Christianity, ancient pagan gods were reclassified as demons. Moloch became one of the chief fallen angels in Christian demonology.

* **John Milton’s *Paradise Lost* (1667):** Milton cemented Moloch’s reputation in literature. In this epic poem, Moloch is one of the chief warriors of Satan. He is described as the fiercest and strongest of the fallen angels, "besmeared with blood of human sacrifice, and parents' tears." He is a militaristic demon who argues for open, violent war against Heaven.
* **The *Dictionnaire Infernal* (1818):** In this famous book on demonology, Moloch is listed as a prince of the underworld. He is often depicted as a member of Satan's infernal council, specifically serving as the "Grand Cross of the Order of the Fly."

### 4. Modern Metaphorical Meaning
Today, the name "Moloch" is often used metaphorically to describe a system, institution, or society that demands the sacrifice of its most vulnerable (often children or the future) for the sake of power, wealth, or progress.
* **Allen Ginsberg’s *Howl* (1956):** In this famous poem, Ginsberg uses Moloch as a symbol for industrialization, capitalism, war, and the soul-crushing nature of modern cities.
* **In Pop Culture:** The concept of Moloch appears in movies (like Fritz Lang's silent masterpiece *Metropolis*, where a giant machine is envisioned as Moloch devouring workers), video games, and fantasy novels.
