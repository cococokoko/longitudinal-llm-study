── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. For any human being, it is impossible to know everything.

There are several independent reasons:

- Finite lifespan: The amount of information that exists vastly exceeds what a person can learn during a lifetime.
- Finite cognitive capacity: Human memory, attention, and processing ability are limited.
- Continual discovery: New facts, theories, technologies, and observations are constantly being generated.
- Distributed expertise: Modern knowledge is so extensive that even within a single field—such as neurosurgery—no one person can master every subspecialty in complete detail.
- Fundamental limits: There are things that may be unknowable in principle, not just in practice. Examples include certain results from mathematics, logic, and physics:
  - Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that sufficiently powerful formal systems contain true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
  - Computational theory identifies problems that are undecidable.
  - Some interpretations of physics suggest limits on what can be measured or predicted.

There is also a useful distinction between:
- "Knowing everything that is currently known by humanity."
- "Knowing every true fact about reality."

The first is already beyond any individual. The second is even more unattainable, because reality may contain an effectively unbounded number of facts, and our access to them is constrained.

In practice, expertise is less about exhaustive knowledge and more about developing reliable methods for acquiring, evaluating, and applying knowledge. That is one reason highly accomplished specialists continue learning throughout their careers: the target itself keeps moving.
