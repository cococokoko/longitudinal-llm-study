── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately influence international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO)
- This shapes rules that often favor existing economic arrangements
- Developing nations frequently face conditionality that constrains their policy choices

**Dependency relationships**
- Trade patterns can create asymmetric vulnerabilities
- Debt relationships give creditors significant leverage over debtor nations
- Resource dependence shapes both domestic politics and foreign policy options

## Genuine Tensions in the Analysis

**Competing interpretations exist:**
- Dependency theorists argue global inequality is *reproduced* by international economic structures
- Liberal institutionalists argue integration, despite imperfections, creates mutual benefits
- Both capture real phenomena - the evidence is genuinely mixed

**Where inequality clearly matters:**
- Climate negotiations (who pays for transition costs)
- Pandemic response (vaccine distribution revealed stark power differences)
- Migration pressure and border politics
- Security alliances often involve economic bargaining

## Important Complications

- Some previously poor nations have dramatically improved positions (South Korea, China)
- Inequality *within* nations sometimes matters more than between nations
- Economic interdependence can both constrain conflict and create new friction points

## What's Actually Contested

Whether current arrangements primarily reflect exploitation, mutual benefit, or something more complicated is genuinely debated among serious scholars.
