── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Starting a new role is the perfect time to set up healthy financial habits. 

Because you are currently on probation, your strategy should be split into two parts: **protecting yourself during probation** and **setting up your long-term retirement engine.**

Here is a step-by-step guide on the best ways to save for retirement, tailored to your current situation.

---

### Phase 1: During Your Probation Period (Safety First)

While you are on probation, your primary goal is financial security. If things don’t work out with the job, you need a safety net.

1.  **Build/Secure Your Emergency Fund:** 
    *   Before aggressively investing for retirement, ensure you have **3 to 6 months of living expenses** in a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA). Currently, HYSAs are paying around 4–5% interest. 
    *   *Why?* Retirement accounts carry penalties if you withdraw money early. If you lose your job during probation, you cannot easily use retirement funds to pay rent.
2.  **Read Your Benefits Handbook:**
    *   Find out when you are eligible for the company’s **401(k) or 403(b) plan**. Some companies let you contribute on Day 1; others make you wait 3 to 6 months (often matching the probation period).
    *   Find out if there is an **employer match** (e.g., "we match 100% up to 4% of your salary"). This is free money.
    *   Check the **vesting schedule**. "Vesting" is how long you must work there to keep the employer’s matching contributions. If you leave during probation, you will lose unvested match money, but you *always* keep the money you contributed yourself.

---

### Phase 2: The Retirement "Order of Operations"

Once your emergency fund is secure and you know your company's benefits, use this standard financial hierarchy to route your money:

#### Step 1: Contribute to your 401(k) up to the Employer Match
*   If your employer matches up to 4%, contribute exactly 4%. 
*   **Why:** This is an immediate 100% return on your investment. Never pass up free money.
*   *Note:* If you are allowed to contribute during probation, do so. If not, skip to Step 2 until your probation ends.

#### Step 2: Open and Max Out a Roth IRA
*   An Individual Retirement Account (IRA) is an account you open yourself (through brokerages like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Charles Schwab).
*   **Why a Roth IRA?** You pay taxes on the money now, but it grows **100% tax-free**, and you pay $0 in taxes when you withdraw it in retirement. 
*   **Flexibility:** With a Roth IRA, you can withdraw your *contributions* (but not the earnings) at any time without penalty. This acts as a secondary emergency backup.
*   *Limit:* In 2024, you can contribute up to $7,000 per year.

#### Step 3: Utilize a Health Savings Account (HSA) – *The Secret Weapon*
*   If your new job offers a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP), you qualify for an HSA.
*   **Why:** HSAs have a "triple tax advantage": contributions are tax-deductible, the money grows tax-free, and withdrawals are tax-free if used for medical expenses. After age 65, it acts just like a traditional IRA (you can withdraw money for any reason, paying normal income tax).

#### Step 4: Go Back to Your 401(k)
*   If you still have money left over to save, go back to your employer 401(k) and increase your contribution percentage. The maximum contribution limit for a 401(k) in 2024 is $23,000.

---
