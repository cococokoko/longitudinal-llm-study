Since you're on probation in a new job, you're in a position many people are thinking about right now: how to build a career when automation and AI are becoming more capable.

No one knows exactly how the future of work will unfold, but several trends are already visible.

1. Many tasks will be automated, not necessarily entire jobs

Historically, technology has tended to automate parts of jobs rather than eliminate all work in a profession. For example:
- Software helps accountants but hasn't eliminated accounting.
- AI assists programmers but hasn't removed the need for software engineers.
- Automation supports doctors, lawyers, and analysts, but human judgment remains important.

A common pattern is that routine, repetitive, and predictable tasks become automated first.

2. Human oversight becomes more valuable

As AI systems take on more work, organizations often need people who can:
- Verify outputs
- Make judgment calls
- Handle exceptions
- Manage risk
- Take responsibility for outcomes

Many employers may increasingly value workers who can effectively supervise and collaborate with automated systems.

3. Interpersonal skills may become more important

Skills that involve:
- Building trust
- Negotiation
- Leadership
- Teaching
- Customer relationships
- Team coordination

are often harder to automate because they depend on human relationships and social context.

4. Adaptability may matter more than specialization alone

Technical expertise remains valuable, but workers who can:
- Learn new tools quickly
- Move across functions
- Continuously update their skills

may be better positioned than those whose expertise is tied to a single process or technology.

5. Productivity expectations may rise

When workers gain access to powerful AI tools, employers may expect:
- Faster output
- Higher quality
- Broader responsibilities

This could mean fewer people are needed for certain tasks, while those who remain are expected to accomplish more.

6. New jobs will emerge, but not necessarily at the same pace

Technology often creates new industries and occupations. The challenge is that:
- New jobs may require different skills.
- Geographic locations may differ.
- Transition periods can be disruptive.

The benefits of automation are not always evenly distributed.

7. The biggest uncertainty is economic and political, not technical

The future of work depends not only on what AI can do, but also on:
- Government policy
- Education systems
- Labor markets
- Regulation
- Corporate decisions
- Social attitudes toward work

Different countries may experience very different outcomes.

For someone early in a new role, a practical approach is to focus on becoming excellent at three things:
- Understanding your industry deeply.
- Learning how to use AI and automation tools effectively.
- Developing communication, judgment, and relationship-building skills.

Those capabilities tend to remain valuable even as the tools themselves change.
