### The Project Title:
**"The Circular Economy of Water: Engineering Bio-Adsorbents from Spent Coffee Grounds and Rice Husks to Remediate Textile Wastewater"**

### The Vietnam Connection:
This project sits at the intersection of two of Vietnam’s largest industries:
1. **Agriculture:** Vietnam is the world’s second-largest exporter of coffee and a top exporter of rice. This generates millions of tons of agricultural waste (spent grounds and husks) annually.
2. **Textiles/Garments:** The booming garment industry is a massive economic driver for Vietnam, but it struggles with wastewater management. Synthetic dyes and heavy metals often pollute local waterways, impacting communities and ecosystems.

### The Core Concept:
Students will upcycle local agricultural waste into "bio-adsorbents" to filter and remove synthetic dyes from simulated textile wastewater. They will quantify the efficacy of these low-cost filters using a DIY smartphone spectrophotometer.

### The Experimental Design (The Academic Rigor):
Instead of a binary "did it clean the water?" approach, this project allows students to engage in university-level analytical chemistry and environmental engineering concepts at a fraction of the cost.

1. **Preparation of Bio-Adsorbents:** Students collect spent coffee grounds (free from any local café) and rice husks. They wash, dry, and sieve them to uniform particle sizes. 
2. **Chemical Activation (Optional but recommended):** Students can test "raw" biomass versus biomass activated with cheap, household chemicals (e.g., soaking in mild citric acid or baking soda solutions) to increase the surface area and porosity of the adsorbents.
3. **Simulating Wastewater:** Students create a standardized polluted water sample using distilled water and a measurable concentration of Methylene Blue (available cheaply at aquarium stores) or standard food coloring.
4. **Data Collection via Smartphone Colorimetry:** This is the "incredible" factor. Instead of buying a $2,000 spectrophotometer, students can build a colorimeter using a cardboard box, a standardized LED light source, and a smartphone. By using a free RGB color-picker app, they can measure the absorbance of the water before and after filtration. They can use Beer-Lambert’s Law to create a calibration curve and calculate the exact concentration of dye removed.
