The concept of "discipleship"—the process of growing in faith, character, and practice, and helping others do the same—can look very different depending on who you ask. 

Because you are juggling the heavy demands of being a single parent and managing a career, some of these views might feel overwhelming, while others might fit perfectly into your daily rhythm. 

Here is a breakdown of the different views on discipleship, along with how they practically intersect with the life of a busy working parent:

### 1. The "Everyday Life" (Organic) View
*   **The Concept:** This view (often based on Deuteronomy 6 in the Bible) believes discipleship isn't an event you attend, but a lifestyle you live. It happens in the mundane moments: driving in the car, eating dinner, or dealing with a toddler's meltdown. 
*   **How it fits your life:** **This is your superpower.** You don't need to add another meeting to your calendar to do this. Discipleship here looks like modeling patience after a long day at work, praying with your child before bed, or having a quick conversation about kindness on the way to school. Parenting *is* discipleship.

### 2. The Relational/Mentorship View (Life-on-Life)
*   **The Concept:** This view focuses on a one-on-one dynamic. It’s the idea of an older, wiser person pouring into a younger person, much like an apprenticeship. It’s highly relational and customized to the individual.
*   **How it fits your life:** As a single parent, you are constantly pouring out energy. You desperately need someone pouring into *you*. However, scheduling a formal weekly coffee meetup might be impossible. For you, this might look like finding a mentor who has also navigated single parenthood, who is willing to talk on the phone during your commute, or who doesn't mind coming over to chat while you fold laundry.

### 3. The Academic/Classroom View
*   **The Concept:** This is the traditional view held by many modern institutions. It equates discipleship with education: attending a class, completing a Bible study workbook, listening to sermons, or mastering theological concepts.
*   **How it fits your life:** Sitting down for a two-hour evening class when you have to manage dinner, bath time, and bedtime alone is incredibly difficult. If you lean toward this style of learning, you have to "hack" it: listen to theology or personal growth podcasts while working or commuting, or use an audio Bible app while doing chores. 

### 4. The Community/Small Group View
*   **The Concept:** This view argues that discipleship cannot happen in isolation; it requires a "village." It focuses on small groups of peers who share meals, confess struggles, and support one another.
*   **How it fits your life:** Community is vital for single parents, but the logistics of attending a small group can be a nightmare if childcare isn't provided. The best version of this for you is a group of other parents where the kids can play in the living room while the adults talk in the kitchen, or a church group that explicitly provides free childcare so you can actually get a mental break.

### 5. The Missional/Action-Oriented View
*   **The Concept:** This view believes you learn best by *doing*. Discipleship is seen as serving the poor, volunteering, and actively loving your neighbors. You grow by stepping out of your comfort zone.
*   **How it fits your life:** When you are already stretched thin, "volunteering" can feel like a heavy burden. The key here is integration. You don't have to join a formal outreach committee. Missional discipleship for you might look like baking cookies with your child and taking them to an elderly neighbor, or simply doing your secular job with a high level of integrity and kindness.

### A Thought for Your Season of Life
When looking at these different views, the biggest trap for a working single parent is **guilt**—feeling like you aren't doing "enough" because you can't attend the classes or the retreats. 

In your current season, the best view of discipleship is **integration**. You don't need to add discipleship to your to-do list; you just need to invite God into the things you are already doing: working, parenting, resting
