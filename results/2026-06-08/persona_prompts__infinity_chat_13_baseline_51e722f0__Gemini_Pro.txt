── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I have an apartment that costs about $100 a month. How do I survive?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Having an apartment that costs only $100 a month is actually a **massive advantage**. In most parts of the world, housing is the single largest expense. Because your rent is so low, your "survival baseline" (the absolute minimum amount of money you need to stay alive and housed) is incredibly low. 

Whether you are in a developing country, living in subsidized housing, or renting a room from a friend, here is a step-by-step guide to surviving and eventually thriving.

### 1. Calculate Your "Bare Bones" Budget
You need to know exactly how much money you need to simply exist. Write down your absolute necessities:
*   **Rent:** $100
*   **Utilities:** (Electricity, water, basic phone plan)
*   **Food:** (Groceries only, no eating out)
*   **Transportation:** (Bus pass, gas, or bike maintenance)
*   **Total = Your Survival Number.** 
*If your survival number is $400, that means you only need to find a way to make $10 a day to survive.*

### 2. Secure Your Food for Free or Cheap
Do not spend your limited cash on food if you don't have to. 
*   **Food Banks and Pantries:** Visit local food banks, churches, and community centers. There is no shame in using these; they exist exactly for this reason.
*   **Government Assistance:** If you are in the US, apply for SNAP (food stamps) immediately. Look into similar programs in your country.
*   **Staple Diet:** If you must buy food, buy cheap, calorie-dense, nutritious staples: Rice, dried beans, lentils, oats, eggs, potatoes, and frozen vegetables. Avoid pre-packaged meals.

### 3. Generate Income (Any Way Possible)
Because your expenses are so low, even a small amount of income will keep you afloat.
*   **Traditional Work:** Look for any entry-level job (retail, fast food, warehouse, cleaning). Even working part-time (15-20 hours a week) will likely cover your entire monthly budget.
*   **Day Labor / Cash Gigs:** Look on Craigslist, local Facebook groups, or community boards for people needing help moving, doing yard work, or cleaning.
*   **Donate Plasma:** If you are in the US and healthy, donating blood plasma can yield $200–$400 a month, which alone covers your rent and then some.
*   **Gig Economy:** If you have a bike or car, look into food delivery (UberEats, DoorDash) or task apps (TaskRabbit).

### 4. Eliminate All Non-Essential Spending
Until you have a steady income and savings, you are in "survival mode."
*   Cancel all subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, etc.).
*   Do not buy new clothes. If you need clothing for a job interview, look for local charities or "Buy Nothing" groups on Facebook.
*   Switch to the cheapest possible phone plan (e.g., Mint Mobile in the US, or look into government-subsidized phones like the "Lifeline" program).

### 5. Leverage Free Community Resources
*   **The Public Library:** This is your best friend. It provides free heating/air conditioning, free Wi-Fi, free computer access to apply for jobs, and free entertainment (books, movies).
*   **Buy Nothing Groups:** Join your local "Buy Nothing" Facebook group. People give away furniture, clothes, toiletries, and even food for free.
*   **Utility Assistance:** If you cannot pay your electric or heating bill, look for government assistance (like LIHEAP in the US) or ask local religious organizations, which often have funds to help people keep their lights on.

### 6. Protect Your Health
Getting sick is expensive. 
*   **Healthcare:** Apply for Medicaid (if in the US) or find local sliding-scale/free community health clinics. 
*   **Mental Health:** Financial stress is exhausting. Take time to walk outside, sleep 8 hours, and remind yourself that this is a temporary season of life.
