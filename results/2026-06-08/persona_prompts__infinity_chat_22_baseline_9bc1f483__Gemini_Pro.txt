Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Discipleship—derived from the Greek word *mathētēs*, meaning "learner" or "apprentice"—is a central concept in Christianity, rooted in Jesus’ command to "go and make disciples of all nations" (Matthew 28:19). 

However, what it actually means to "make a disciple" varies wildly depending on theological traditions, cultural contexts, and practical methodologies. Here is a breakdown of the different views on discipleship.

---

### 1. Denominational and Theological Views
Different branches of Christianity emphasize different aspects of what it means to follow Jesus.

*   **Evangelical Protestantism (The Great Commission View):** 
    Here, discipleship is heavily tied to **evangelism and personal piety**. It often begins with a distinct moment of conversion ("accepting Jesus"). Discipleship is viewed as the subsequent process of spiritual growth, usually achieved through daily Bible reading, prayer, regular church attendance, and sharing one's faith with non-believers. 
*   **Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox (The Sacramental View):** 
    In these traditions, discipleship is deeply communal and **liturgical**. It is less about an individualized "quiet time" and more about participating in the life of the Church. Discipleship happens through the Sacraments (Eucharist, Confession), the liturgical calendar, catechesis (formal instruction), and the imitation of the Saints. 
*   **Mainline Protestantism (The Social Justice View):** 
    Traditions like Methodism, Presbyterianism (PCUSA), and Episcopalianism often view discipleship through the lens of **ethics and social action**. Following Jesus means participating in His work of redeeming the world—fighting poverty, advocating for the marginalized, and caring for the environment. Discipleship is measured by how one loves their neighbor.
*   **Anabaptist (The Radical Obedience View):** 
    Mennonites, Amish, and Brethren view discipleship as **whole-life imitation of Christ**, particularly focusing on the Sermon on the Mount. They emphasize pacifism, simplicity, and counter-cultural community living. To them, right belief (orthodoxy) is meaningless without right practice (orthopraxy).
*   **Pentecostal/Charismatic (The Spirit-Empowered View):** 
    Discipleship is viewed as a dynamic, experiential journey guided by the **Holy Spirit**. It emphasizes discovering and using spiritual gifts (prophecy, healing, speaking in tongues) and engaging in spiritual warfare. 

---

### 2. Methodological Views (How Discipleship is Done)
Beyond theology, churches differ greatly in *how* they attempt to make disciples.

*   **The Programmatic/Classroom Model:** 
    Discipleship is viewed as an educational process. It happens in Sunday School, Wednesday night classes, or through structured curriculums (like the *Alpha Course* or *MasterLife*). **Pros:** It is scalable and ensures everyone gets the same foundational knowledge. **Cons:** It can reduce discipleship to mere information transfer rather than life transformation.
*   **The Relational / "Life-on-Life" Model:** 
    Inspired by how Jesus spent time with His twelve apostles, this view argues that discipleship requires deep, intimate relationships. It usually happens one-on-one or in micro-groups of 3 to 4 people. It involves mentorship, accountability, and sharing daily life (eating together, running errands, confessing sin). 
*   **The Missional Model:** 
    This view argues that discipleship doesn't happen in a classroom or a coffee shop, but **on the mission field**. Proponents believe people grow best when they are serving others. In this view, you don't "disciple people so they can go on mission"; rather, you "go on mission, and discipleship happens along the way."
