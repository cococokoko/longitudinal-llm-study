── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, the term "linear graph" most commonly refers to a **Path Graph** (denoted as $P_n$). 

Here is a complete example of a path graph, along with its formal definition, properties, and an alternative meaning of "linear" in graph theory.

---

### 1. The Primary Example: The Path Graph ($P_4$)

A path graph is a graph where the vertices are connected in a single, continuous, non-branching line. 

Let’s look at the path graph with 4 vertices, denoted as **$P_4$**.

#### Visual Representation:
```text
  (A) --------- (B) --------- (C) --------- (D)
```

#### Formal Definition:
*   **Vertices (Nodes), $V$:** $\{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges (Connections), $E$:** $\{(A, B), (B, C), (C, D)\}$

#### Key Properties of this Graph:
1.  **Number of Vertices ($n$):** 4
2.  **Number of Edges ($e$):** 3 (In any path graph, the number of edges is always $n - 1$).
3.  **Degrees of Vertices:** 
    *   The "end" vertices ($A$ and $D$) have a degree of **1** (they are connected to only one other vertex).
    *   The "internal" vertices ($B$ and $C$) have a degree of **2** (they are connected to two other vertices).
4.  **Acyclic:** It contains no cycles (you cannot start at a vertex and follow a path back to it without retracing your steps).

#### Real-World Analogy:
A one-way subway line with four stations: Station A $\rightarrow$ Station B $\rightarrow$ Station C $\rightarrow$ Station D.

---

### 2. Alternative Meaning: "Line Graph" $L(G)$

In advanced graph theory, there is a concept called a **Line Graph** (sometimes called a linear graph). A line graph $L(G)$ represents the *connections between the edges* of another graph $G$.

#### Example:
Imagine a simple triangle graph $G$ (a cycle of 3 vertices: $X, Y, Z$).
*   The edges of $G$ are $e_1 (XY)$, $e_2 (YZ)$, and $e_3 (ZX)$.

To create the **Line Graph $L(G)$**:
1.  Turn the *edges* of $G$ into the *vertices* of $L(G)$. So, the new vertices are $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$.
2.  Connect two vertices in $L(G)$ if their corresponding edges in $G$ shared a corner. 
    *   $e_1$ and $e_2$ share vertex $Y$.
    *   $e_2$ and $e_3$ share vertex $Z$.
    *   $e_3$ and $e_1$ share vertex $X$.

In this case, the Line Graph $L(G)$ also forms a triangle.
