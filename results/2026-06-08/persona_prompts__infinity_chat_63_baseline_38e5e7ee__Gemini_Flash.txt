── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To understand a **partial derivative**, think of it as measuring how a situation changes when you **change only one variable** and keep everything else exactly the same.

Here is a simple, real-world numerical example.

---

### The Scenario: The Cost of a Pizza
Let’s say the cost ($C$) of making a pizza depends on two things:
1. **$d$**: The diameter of the pizza in inches.
2. **$t$**: The number of toppings.

We can write this as a function: 
$$C(d, t) = 0.1d^2 + 2t$$

*   The $0.1d^2$ term means larger pizzas require quadratically more dough and cheese.
*   The $2t$ term means each topping costs flat $2.

#### Our Starting Point (The Base Case)
Let's start with a **10-inch pizza with 3 toppings** ($d = 10$, $t = 3$).
The cost of this pizza is:
$$C(10, 3) = 0.1(10)^2 + 2(3) = 10 + 6 = \$16$$

---

### 1. Partial Derivative with respect to Toppings ($\frac{\partial C}{\partial t}$)
This asks: **"If we keep the size of the pizza constant, how does the cost change for each additional topping?"**

To find this, we treat the diameter ($d$) as a constant (a fixed number) and take the derivative with respect to $t$:
$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(0.1d^2 + 2t)$$

Since $d$ is treated as a constant, $0.1d^2$ is also a constant, and its derivative is $0$. The derivative of $2t$ is $2$.
$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = 2$$

#### Numerical Illustration:
If we increase the toppings from **3 to 4**, keeping the size at **10 inches**:
*   New Cost: $C(10, 4) = 0.1(10)^2 + 2(4) = 10 + 8 = \$18$
