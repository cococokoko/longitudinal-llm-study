── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities—often conceptualized as the divide between the "Global North" (developed, wealthy nations) and the "Global South" (developing, poorer nations)—are not just financial metrics; they are the foundational scaffolding of modern international relations (IR). 

These inequalities shape how states interact, who holds power in global institutions, where conflicts arise, and how global crises like climate change and migration are managed. 

Here is a detailed breakdown of how global economic inequalities shape international relations.

---

### 1. Power Asymmetry in Global Governance
Economic wealth translates directly into structural power within international organizations. The rules of the global game are largely written by and for wealthy nations.
* **Institutional Control:** In institutions like the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, voting power is determined by financial contributions. This gives the US and Europe de facto veto power over global economic policies, often forcing developing nations to accept austerity measures or market liberalization as conditions for loans.
* **The UN Security Council:** The permanent five (P5) members with veto power represent the dominant economic and military victors of World War II. Despite the economic rise of other nations, the structure remains rigid, reflecting historical wealth distribution.

### 2. Dependency, Debt, and Foreign Policy Leverage
Economic inequality creates relationships of dependency, which wealthy nations (and increasingly, emerging powers) use as foreign policy leverage.
* **Neo-Colonialism and Dependency Theory:** Critics argue that the global economic system keeps developing nations as exporters of cheap raw materials and importers of expensive manufactured goods. This prevents industrialization and maintains a hierarchy of dependency.
* **Debt-Trap Diplomacy:** Wealthy nations and institutions use debt to influence the foreign policies of poorer states. For example, China’s Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) has provided massive infrastructure loans to developing nations. When these nations cannot repay, China has sometimes acquired strategic assets (like the Hambantota Port in Sri Lanka), shifting regional geopolitical balances.
* **Foreign Aid as a Tool:** Foreign aid is rarely purely altruistic; it is often used to buy diplomatic alignment, secure votes in the UN, or gain access to local markets and military bases.

### 3. Security, Conflict, and State Fragility
Economic inequality is a major driver of domestic instability, which quickly spills over into international security crises.
* **State Failure and Terrorism:** Extreme poverty and lack of economic opportunity weaken state capacity. "Failed states" (e.g., Somalia, parts of the Sahel) become breeding grounds for transnational terrorism, piracy, and organized crime, forcing international military interventions.
* **Resource Wars:** Economic desperation can drive states or rebel groups to fight over scarce, valuable resources (like coltan, oil, or diamonds). These local conflicts often attract foreign intervention by multinational corporations and major powers seeking to secure supply chains.

### 4. Migration and Border Geopolitics
The vast wealth gap between nations is the primary driver of global migration, which has become one of the most contentious issues in modern IR.
* **Weaponization of Migration:** Border control has become a major diplomatic bargaining chip. Countries like Turkey, Morocco, and Libya have used their position as transit routes for migrants to extract financial aid and political concessions from the European Union.
* **Domestic Political Shifts:** Mass migration driven by economic disparity has fueled the rise of populist, nationalist, and protectionist political parties in the Global North. This shift has led to more isolationist foreign policies, trade wars, and a retreat from multilateralism (e.g., Brexit, "America First").
