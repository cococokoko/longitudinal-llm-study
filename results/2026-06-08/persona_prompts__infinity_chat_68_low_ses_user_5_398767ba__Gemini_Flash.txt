── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I support extended family members and manage many household responsibilities.

Give me a tip for managing a team of coworkers.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Because you already manage household logistics and support extended family, you possess two of the most valuable leadership skills in existence: **high emotional intelligence (EQ)** and **complex logistical coordination**. You are likely already an expert at crisis management, patience, and balancing competing needs.

However, the biggest risk for someone with your background is **"over-functioning"**—taking on too much responsibility for others' success and burning yourself out. 

Here is your tailored tip for managing a team of coworkers:

### Shift from "Caretaker" to "Facilitator" (The Art of Empowering Delegation)

At home, you may often have to step in and "do" because family dynamics or household survival require it. At work, your goal is the opposite: **to make yourself unnecessary for daily operations.**

Here is how to apply this:

#### 1. Establish "Chore Charts" (Clear Ownership)
In a household, things run smoother when everyone knows who is on dishes and who is on trash. At work, apply this through **Role Clarity**. 
* **The Action:** Never assign a task to "the team." Assign it to a specific person. Ensure every project has one clear owner who is responsible for the outcome, even if others are helping. 

#### 2. Coach, Don't Rescue
When a family member struggles, our instinct is often to step in and fix it to keep the peace or save time. If you do this with coworkers, you will create dependency and double your workload.
* **The Action:** When a team member comes to you with a problem, resist the urge to solve it. Instead, ask: *"What do you think the next step should be?"* or *"What options have you considered?"* Guide them to the answer so they learn to trust their own judgment.

#### 3. Protect Your Energy (Set Professional Boundaries)
Because you are used to supporting extended family, you likely have a high tolerance for emotional labor. Coworkers will instinctively sense this and may bring their complaints, anxieties, and conflicts to you.
* **The Action:** Set boundaries on your time. You can be a supportive manager without being a therapist. If a coworker is venting, redirect them to action: *"I hear that this is frustrating. What is one thing we can control today to make this better?"*

**The takeaway:** You already know how to coordinate people. Your goal as a manager is to **delegate the execution so you can focus on the strategy.** Treat your team like capable adults who need direction and resources, not rescue.
