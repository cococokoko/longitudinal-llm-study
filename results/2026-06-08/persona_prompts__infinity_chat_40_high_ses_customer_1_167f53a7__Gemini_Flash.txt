This is a classic risk-management and asset-structuring challenge. Because you are planning to marry in a year, you are currently in a transitional legal phase: you have the financial integration of a married couple but the legal exposure of two single individuals. 

Given your career trajectory, there is likely a significant income and asset asymmetry to consider. To protect both your relationship and your respective financial interests, you need a solution that is legally clean, financially equitable, and emotionally sound.

Here is a structured framework to help you determine the best path forward, divided into immediate (pre-marital) and long-term (post-marital) strategies.

---

### Phase 1: Pre-Marriage (The Next 12–18 Months)
The golden rule of pre-marital real estate is **clean demarcation**. Mixing funds without a legal marriage or a formal contract creates immense legal vulnerability. 

There are two primary ways to handle this phase:

#### Option A: The "Tenant" Model (Safest & Simplest)
*   **How it works:** Your girlfriend buys the house entirely in her name, using her own funds for the down payment and securing the mortgage based on her income alone. You move in but do not contribute to the down payment or the mortgage principal.
*   **Your contribution:** You pay a agreed-upon monthly amount to cover shared living expenses (utilities, groceries, HOA fees) or a set "rent" that is fair but does not build equity. 
*   **Why it works:** It keeps the asset 100% hers. If the relationship ends before the wedding, there is no messy division of property or disputes over who paid for what. It also preserves her autonomy in buying "her" home.

#### Option B: The "Co-Investment" Model (Requires Legal Counsel)
*   **How it works:** You contribute to the down payment or mortgage now, and your name goes on the deed. 
*   **The Requirement:** You **must** have a family law attorney draft a **Cohabitation Agreement** (or a Tenants-in-Common Agreement). This document explicitly states who contributed what, how expenses are split, and exactly how the equity will be divided or bought out if you split up before getting married.
*   **Why it’s risky without a contract:** In many jurisdictions, if you contribute to her mortgage without a contract or being on the deed, those payments can be legally viewed as "gifts," and you will have no claim to that equity if the relationship ends.

---

### Phase 2: Post-Marriage (The Long-Term)
Once you are married, the legal landscape changes. In many states, pre-marital assets remain separate property, but any appreciation in the home’s value during the marriage, or any mortgage payments made using marital funds (your salaries), can become joint property.

To transition the house into a joint asset fairly, consider these steps:

1.  **The Prenuptial Agreement:** 
    As a physician with high earning potential, a prenup is a standard and necessary business tool. It is not about planning for divorce; it is about defining financial expectations. Your prenup should explicitly address how her house (and its future appreciation) will be treated.
2.  **The "Buy-In" Strategy:** 
    After marriage, you can "buy in" to the home to achieve 50/50 equity. For example, you could make a lump-sum payment toward the principal mortgage balance equal to half of her initial down payment plus half of the equity she built. At that point, you refinance the mortgage into both of your names and add you to the deed.

---
