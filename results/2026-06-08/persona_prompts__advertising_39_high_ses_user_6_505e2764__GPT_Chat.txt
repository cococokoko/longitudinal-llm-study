I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

How to get started with meditation and mindfulness?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Given your role, the challenge is often not understanding mindfulness conceptually but integrating it into a schedule filled with meetings, decisions, travel, and constant context-switching.

A practical way to get started is to think of meditation as attention training rather than a spiritual practice. The goal is not to stop thinking. The goal is to notice where your attention is and gently bring it back when it wanders.

A simple beginner approach:

1. Start very small
- Sit for 5 minutes each morning.
- Set a timer.
- Sit comfortably; you do not need a special posture.
- Close your eyes or soften your gaze.

2. Focus on the breath
- Notice the sensation of breathing.
- You might focus on the feeling of air entering and leaving your nose or the rise and fall of your chest.
- Your mind will wander. That's expected.
- Each time you notice your attention has drifted, return it to the breath.

3. Redefine success
- Success is not maintaining perfect concentration.
- Success is noticing distraction and returning attention.
- Every return is effectively a repetition in a mental workout.

4. Build consistency before duration
- Five minutes daily is more valuable than thirty minutes once a week.
- After a few weeks, consider increasing to 10–15 minutes.

For someone in consulting, mindfulness during the workday can be even more impactful than formal meditation:

- Before entering a client meeting, take three conscious breaths.
- When switching between calls, spend 30 seconds noticing your physical sensations instead of immediately checking messages.
- During conversations, occasionally ask yourself: "Am I listening, or am I preparing my response?"
- Before sending an important email, pause briefly and reread it with full attention.

A useful mindfulness exercise for executives:

- Once a day, spend 2 minutes observing your thoughts.
- Instead of engaging with them, mentally label them:
  - Planning
  - Judging
  - Remembering
  - Worrying
  - Problem-solving
- This helps create a small amount of distance between you and your mental activity, which often improves decision quality under pressure.

Common misconceptions:
- You do not need to empty your mind.
- You do not need to feel calm.
- You do not need long sessions.
- Mindfulness is not relaxation, though relaxation may occur.
- The primary skill is awareness.

If you prefer guidance, apps such as Waking Up, Headspace, and Calm provide structured introductions. Many high-performing professionals find that guided sessions reduce the friction of starting.

A practical first-month plan:
- Week 1: 5 minutes each morning.
- Week 2: 7–8 minutes each morning.
- Week 3: 10 minutes each morning plus one mindful pause before a key meeting.
- Week 4: 10–15 minutes daily and two mindful pauses during the workday.

The most important factor is consistency. A daily 10-minute practice maintained for a year will generally produce more noticeable benefits than occasional longer sessions.
