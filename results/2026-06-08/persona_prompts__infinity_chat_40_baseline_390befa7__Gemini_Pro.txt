── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common situation for modern couples, and it’s incredibly smart that you are thinking about this *before* she closes on the house and before you move in together. 

Because you are not married yet, the law views you as two separate individuals. Until you say "I do," you need to protect both your relationship and your individual finances. 

Here is a breakdown of the most common, fair solutions, and how to handle the transition from dating to marriage.

### Phase 1: Before the Marriage (The Next 1–2 Years)
Until you are legally married, **do not contribute to the down payment or pay for major renovations** unless your name is on the deed. If you break up, you will likely lose that money. 

If you plan to live with her in this house before you get married, here are the three most common ways to handle the monthly expenses fairly:

**Option A: The "Fair Market Rent" Approach (Most Recommended)**
*   **How it works:** You look at what it would cost to rent a room or a similar half-house in your area. You pay her that amount as "rent," plus half the utilities. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** You need a place to live, and you would be paying rent anywhere else. She takes on all the legal risk, maintenance costs (like a broken furnace or a leaky roof), and property taxes. She builds equity, but she also carries the financial burden of homeownership. 

**Option B: The "Split the Mortgage" Approach**
*   **How it works:** You split the monthly mortgage payment and utilities 50/50 (or proportionally based on your incomes). 
*   **Why it’s fair:** It feels like an equal partnership. 
*   **The catch:** Some partners feel resentful paying half a mortgage when they aren't building any equity. If you do this, she must be 100% responsible for all home repairs and maintenance, since she is the sole owner.

**Option C: The "Alternative Expenses" Approach**
*   **How it works:** She pays the entire mortgage, property taxes, and home insurance. You pay for all the groceries, utilities, internet, and perhaps date nights. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** It keeps the house strictly as *her* financial asset, preventing any legal blurring of lines, but you still contribute equally to your shared life.

*Note: Whichever option you choose, it is highly recommended to sign a simple **Cohabitation Agreement** or a basic lease. It sounds unromantic, but it protects both of you and sets clear expectations.*

### Phase 2: After the Marriage
Once you get married, the dynamic shifts from "mine and yours" to "ours." How you handle the house depends on your shared financial philosophy. 

**Option A: Add you to the deed**
After you are married, she can file a "quitclaim deed" to add your name to the title of the house. At this point, you become equal owners, and you should start splitting all costs (mortgage, maintenance, renovations) equally or from a joint bank account. 

**Option B: Keep it separate, but buy your next house together**
Some couples decide that the house bought before marriage remains the sole property of the original buyer. You might continue paying "rent" or splitting expenses until you both decide to sell that house and buy a new one together, with both of your names on the mortgage and deed.

**Option C: A Prenuptial Agreement**
Because she is bringing a massive asset into the marriage, she may want a prenup to protect the equity she built *before* you got married. A fair prenup might state that if you divorce, she gets her original down payment and pre-marriage equity back, but any equity built *during* the marriage is split 50/50. 

### How to have this conversation
The "best" solution is the one you both agree on without resentment. You need to sit down with her and say something like:

> *"I'm so proud of you for buying this house, and I can't wait to marry you and build our life together. Because we aren't married yet, I want to make sure we have a clear, fair plan for how we handle the house finances over the next year, and how that will change once we get married. What are your thoughts on how we should split things?"*

**Key Takeaways:**
1. **Do not pay for capital improvements** (new roof, remodeling) before marriage.
2. **Do pay your fair share of living expenses** (rent, utilities, groceries).
3. **Communicate your timeline.** Let her know that once you are married, you want to revisit the financial arrangement so you are building wealth as a team.
