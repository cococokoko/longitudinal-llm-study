── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working long hours in a warehouse means you are putting a lot of physical effort and time into earning your paycheck. When you work that hard for your money, it is completely understandable that you want to be smart about your finances and ensure that any arrangement you make is fair to both of you. 

This is a very common dilemma for couples transitioning from dating to marriage. The tricky part is that until you are legally married, any money you put toward the principal of a house solely in her name is legally a gift. If things were to go south, you would walk away with no equity in the home. 

Here are the best, most fair solutions to handle this situation until you tie the knot:

**1. The "Fair Market Rent" Approach (Most Recommended)**
Instead of thinking of it as "paying her mortgage," think of it as paying rent and sharing living expenses. 
*   **How it works:** You agree on a set amount to pay her each month. This amount should be based on what it would cost to rent a similar room or half an apartment in your area—*not* necessarily half of her mortgage. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** You need a place to live, and paying rent is a normal living expense. However, because she is the one gaining equity and the long-term financial benefit of the house, she should be responsible for the actual mortgage, property taxes, and home repairs. You split utilities and groceries 50/50.

**2. The "Future Savings" Approach**
Since you plan to marry in a year, you are about to have a lot of joint expenses coming up (a wedding, a honeymoon, or future joint investments).
*   **How it works:** You pay a smaller, nominal amount in rent/utilities to her, but you take the bulk of your hard-earned warehouse paycheck and put it into a high-yield savings account dedicated to your future together. 
*   **Why it’s fair:** It allows her to handle the asset she is purchasing, while you actively contribute to your shared future. Once you are married, that savings account becomes joint money.

**3. Draft a Cohabitation Agreement**
If she expects you to pay exactly half the mortgage, or if she wants you to help with the down payment or major renovations, you need to protect yourself.
*   **How it works:** You hire a lawyer to draft a simple cohabitation agreement. This document states that if you split up, you are entitled to get back the money you invested in the house, or a percentage of the home's equity.
*   **Why it’s fair:** It treats the house as a business arrangement until the marriage legally binds your assets. 

**What you should avoid doing right now:**
*   **Do not pay for major home improvements.** If the house needs a new roof or a kitchen remodel, she should pay for it. If you pay for it, you are increasing the value of an asset you do not own.
*   **Do not drain your savings for her down payment.** Unless your name is going on the deed alongside hers, keep your savings in your own account. 

**The Next Step:**
Pick a day when you are off work and rested—not right after a grueling warehouse shift—and sit down with her. Say something like: *"I'm so proud of you for buying this house, and I can't wait to marry you next year. I want to make sure we set up our finances right now in a way that feels fair to both of us. How should we handle living expenses until we are officially married?"* 

Once you are married, you can look into refinancing or filing a quitclaim deed to add your name to the house, at which point your incomes and assets will naturally combine.
