Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak comes down to a few key techniques rather than complicated recipes.

1. Start with a good cut
- Ribeye: rich, well-marbled, very forgiving.
- New York strip: beefy flavor, firmer texture.
- Filet mignon: extremely tender, leaner.
- Sirloin: more affordable, still flavorful.

2. Bring the steak closer to room temperature
- Take it out of the refrigerator about 30–60 minutes before cooking.
- Pat it dry with paper towels.

3. Season generously
- Use coarse salt and freshly ground black pepper.
- Season all sides just before cooking, or salt up to a few hours in advance for deeper flavor.

4. Use high heat
- A cast-iron skillet is excellent for creating a crust.
- Heat the pan until it's very hot before adding the steak.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado oil or canola oil.

5. Focus on the sear
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it for the first few minutes.
- Flip once a deep brown crust forms.
- For thicker steaks, sear both sides and finish in a 400°F (205°C) oven if needed.

6. Use temperature, not time
Target internal temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

A meat thermometer is one of the best tools for consistency.

7. Butter-baste for extra flavor
- During the final minute or two, add:
  - Butter
  - Crushed garlic
  - Fresh thyme or rosemary
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak.

8. Rest before slicing
- Let the steak rest 5–10 minutes.
- This helps retain juices.

9. Slice correctly
- Cut against the grain to maximize tenderness.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Cooking a cold steak straight from the refrigerator.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Constantly flipping or moving the steak.
- Skipping the resting period.
- Relying solely on cooking times instead of temperature.

For someone learning, a 1–1.5 inch ribeye or New York strip cooked to medium-rare in a cast-iron skillet is one of the easiest and most rewarding places to start.
