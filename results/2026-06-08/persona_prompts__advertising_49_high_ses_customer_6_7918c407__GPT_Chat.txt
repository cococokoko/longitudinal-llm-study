How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home Wi‑Fi usually comes down to optimizing placement, reducing interference, upgrading equipment where needed, and tuning settings.

Start with the highest-impact actions:

- Move the router to a central, open location.
  - Place it as close as possible to where devices are used.
  - Keep it elevated (e.g., on a shelf), not on the floor.
  - Avoid closets, cabinets, and corners of the house.

- Reduce interference.
  - Keep the router away from microwaves, cordless phones, baby monitors, and large metal objects.
  - In apartment buildings, neighboring Wi‑Fi networks can cause congestion.

- Use the right Wi‑Fi band.
  - 5 GHz: Faster speeds, shorter range.
  - 2.4 GHz: Longer range, slower speeds.
  - 6 GHz (Wi‑Fi 6E/7): Fastest and least congested, but shorter range and requires compatible devices.

- Check router placement relative to walls.
  - Concrete, brick, stone, and metal can significantly weaken signals.
  - If coverage is poor on another floor, place the router as close as practical to that area.

- Update firmware.
  - Router manufacturers regularly release updates that improve performance, stability, and security.

- Optimize channels.
  - On 2.4 GHz, channels 1, 6, and 11 are typically best.
  - Many modern routers automatically select channels, but manual optimization can help in crowded environments.

If your home is larger:

- Consider a mesh Wi‑Fi system.
  - Often the best solution for homes with multiple floors or more than ~2,000 sq ft (185 sq m).
  - Systems from eero, Google Nest, TP-Link Deco, Asus ZenWiFi, and others can provide seamless coverage.

- Use wired backhaul when possible.
  - Connecting mesh nodes with Ethernet dramatically improves performance.

- Add Ethernet for fixed devices.
  - TVs, gaming consoles, desktop PCs, and home office equipment often perform better on wired connections and free up Wi‑Fi capacity.

Evaluate whether the router itself is the bottleneck:

- If the router is more than 4–5 years old, upgrading can provide substantial improvements.
- Look for Wi‑Fi 6 or Wi‑Fi 7 models if many devices are connected simultaneously.

A practical diagnostic approach:
1. Run a speed test next to the router.
2. Run the same test in problem areas.
3. If speeds drop sharply only in distant rooms, coverage is the issue (mesh/access points).
4. If speeds are poor even next to the router, investigate ISP speed, router age, or device limitations.
