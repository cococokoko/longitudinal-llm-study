── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is there life on other planets?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The short answer is: **we don’t know yet.** As of today, there is no definitive scientific proof that life exists anywhere beyond Earth. 

However, most astronomers and astrobiologists believe it is **highly probable** that life exists elsewhere in the universe. Here is a breakdown of why scientists are optimistic, where they are looking, and how they are trying to find it.

### 1. The Numbers Game
The universe is unimaginably vast. 
* Our home galaxy, the Milky Way, contains an estimated **100 to 400 billion stars**, and many of those stars have planets orbiting them. 
* Beyond our galaxy, there are an estimated **2 trillion other galaxies** in the observable universe. 
Statistically speaking, even if the conditions required for life are incredibly rare, the sheer number of planets makes it highly unlikely that Earth is the only one to ever host life.

### 2. Where are we looking in our Solar System?
Scientists aren't currently expecting to find intelligent aliens in our solar system, but they are actively hunting for microbial life (like bacteria). The top candidates are:
* **Mars:** Billions of years ago, Mars had rivers, lakes, and a thicker atmosphere. NASA’s *Perseverance* rover is currently exploring an ancient river delta, collecting rock samples that may contain fossilized signs of ancient microbial life.
* **Europa (Moon of Jupiter) & Enceladus (Moon of Saturn):** Both of these moons are covered in thick shells of ice, but scientists know they have vast, deep oceans of liquid water underneath. Enceladus even shoots geysers of water into space, which spacecraft have flown through and found to contain organic molecules. 
* **Titan (Moon of Saturn):** Titan has a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane and ethane. If life exists there, it would have a completely different chemical makeup than life on Earth.

### 3. Where are we looking beyond our Solar System?
Planets that orbit other stars are called **exoplanets**. Scientists have discovered over 5,500 exoplanets so far. 
When looking at these planets, scientists look for the **"Goldilocks Zone"** (or Habitable Zone)—the distance from a star where a planet is not too hot and not too cold, allowing liquid water to exist on its surface. 

### 4. How are we looking?
* **Telescopes:** The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and other advanced observatories are now able to look at the atmospheres of distant exoplanets. They are searching for **biosignatures**—gases like oxygen, methane, or phosphine that, when found together, strongly suggest the presence of biological life.
* **SETI (Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence):** Scientists use massive radio telescopes to listen for artificial radio signals or laser flashes coming from distant star systems, which would indicate a technologically advanced civilization.

### The Fermi Paradox
This brings up a famous question posed by physicist Enrico Fermi: **"Where is everybody?"** If the universe is so old and so vast, and life is likely, why haven't we seen any signs of aliens yet? 

There are many theories: perhaps intelligent life is extremely rare, perhaps civilizations destroy themselves before they can travel the stars, or perhaps space is simply too vast for us to have crossed paths yet.

**Summary:** While we have zero proof of alien life right now, we are currently in a golden age of space exploration. With the technology we have today, many scientists believe we could find evidence of extraterrestrial life—whether it's a fossilized microbe on Mars or a chemical signature in a distant planet's atmosphere—within the next few decades.
