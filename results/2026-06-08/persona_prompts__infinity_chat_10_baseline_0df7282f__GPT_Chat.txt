In humans, is there a correlation between intelligence and compassion?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, there appears to be some correlation between intelligence and certain forms of compassion, but the relationship is modest, complex, and depends heavily on how both intelligence and compassion are defined and measured.

Research findings include:

- Intelligence and empathy:
  - Cognitive empathy (the ability to understand another person's perspective or mental state) tends to show a positive association with intelligence, especially verbal intelligence.
  - Emotional empathy (the tendency to emotionally share another person's feelings) is less consistently related to intelligence. Some studies find weak positive relationships, while others find little or no correlation.

- Intelligence and prosocial behavior:
  - People with higher cognitive ability often score slightly higher on measures of tolerance, cooperation, and concern for broader social issues.
  - Higher intelligence has been linked in some studies to greater support for humanitarian values and reduced prejudice, though these effects are influenced by education, culture, and socioeconomic factors.

- Intelligence does not guarantee compassion:
  - A highly intelligent person may be very skilled at understanding others yet choose to act selfishly.
  - Intelligence can be used for altruistic purposes or for manipulation. The ability to understand people and the motivation to help them are distinct traits.

- Compassion depends on many other factors:
  - Personality traits (especially agreeableness and honesty-humility)
  - Upbringing and social environment
  - Moral values
  - Life experiences
  - Cultural norms

A useful distinction is that intelligence may help someone understand suffering and anticipate the consequences of actions, but compassion involves caring about others' welfare and being motivated to help. Those capacities often coexist, but neither requires the other.

So the best summary is: there is generally a weak-to-moderate positive relationship between intelligence and some aspects of empathy and prosociality, but compassion is not simply a byproduct of intelligence, and many highly compassionate people are not exceptionally intelligent, while some highly intelligent people are not especially compassionate.
