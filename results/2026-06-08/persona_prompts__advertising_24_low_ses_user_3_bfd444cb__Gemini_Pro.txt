── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Thinking about retirement this early in your tenure is a fantastic financial habit. 

Being on probation adds a slight twist to your strategy. Because your job isn't 100% secure yet, and because some employer benefits might not kick in until your probation ends, you need a strategy that balances **saving for the future** with **protecting yourself in the present**.

Here is a step-by-step guide to the best ways to save for retirement while on probation:

### 1. Prioritize an Emergency Fund First
Before locking your money away in retirement accounts (which often have penalties if you withdraw early), make sure you have a cash cushion. 
* **Why it matters now:** If the probation period doesn't work out, you will need cash to live on while you job hunt. 
* **Action:** Aim to save 3 to 6 months of essential living expenses in a **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)**. This money remains completely liquid and accessible, but still earns a decent interest rate.

### 2. Check Your Employer’s 401(k) or 403(b) Rules
Many employers offer retirement plans, but the rules vary wildly regarding *when* you can participate.
* **If you are eligible immediately:** Contribute at least enough to get the full employer match (e.g., if they match up to 3% of your salary, contribute 3%). **An employer match is free money and a 100% return on your investment.**
* **If there is a waiting period:** Many companies make you wait 90 days, 6 months, or even a year before you can participate or receive a match. Find out exactly when you become eligible, and set a calendar reminder to enroll on that exact date. 

### 3. Open an Individual Retirement Account (IRA)
If your employer plan has a waiting period, or if you want to save beyond the employer match, an IRA is your best friend. IRAs are tied to *you*, not your employer, so your probation status doesn't matter. You can open one at brokerages like Vanguard, Fidelity, or Charles Schwab. You can contribute up to $7,000 in 2024.
* **Roth IRA:** You contribute after-tax money, but it grows tax-free, and you pay no taxes when you withdraw it in retirement. **Bonus for probation:** You can withdraw your *contributions* (but not your earnings) from a Roth IRA at any time without penalty. This makes it a great backup emergency fund if you were to lose your job.
* **Traditional IRA:** You contribute pre-tax money (lowering your tax bill this year), but you pay taxes when you withdraw it in retirement. 

### 4. Look into a Health Savings Account (HSA)
If your new job offers a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP), you may have access to an HSA. 
* **Why it’s great:** It is triple-tax-advantaged. Money goes in tax-free, grows tax-free, and comes out tax-free if used for medical expenses. 
* **The retirement secret:** Once you turn 65, you can withdraw money from an HSA for *any* reason without penalty (you just pay normal income tax on it, like a Traditional IRA). 

### 5. Automate Your Savings
The best way to save is to never see the money in the first place. 
* Set up your direct deposit so that a percentage of your paycheck goes directly into your savings or IRA. 
* Even if you can only afford $50 a month right now while you build your emergency fund, building the *habit* of saving is the most important step.

### Summary Checklist for Your Probation Period:
1. **Build cash:** Funnel extra money into a High-Yield Savings Account until you have a 3-month safety net.
2. **Get the match (if available):** If your company allows it during probation, contribute enough to get the 401(k) match.
3. **Use a Roth IRA:** If you can't use the 401(k) yet, open a Roth IRA and start putting a little money there.
4. **Plan for the transition:** Find out the exact date your probation ends. Plan to celebrate, and then immediately log into your HR portal to increase your retirement contributions!
