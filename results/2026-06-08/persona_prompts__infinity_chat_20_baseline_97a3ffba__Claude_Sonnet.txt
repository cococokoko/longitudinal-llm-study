── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually widely supported across the political spectrum in principle. The more honest framing is about **specific disagreements** regarding implementation.

## Legitimate arguments against particular enforcement mechanisms

- **Measurement problems**: Defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult - experience, productivity, negotiation, and location vary
- **Wage controls can create unintended consequences** like reduced hiring or benefit substitution
- **Bureaucratic costs** of compliance and enforcement
- **Individual variation** in productivity may be obscured by group-level mandates

## Arguments that push back on those concerns

- Markets demonstrably don't always correct discrimination on their own
- Wage gaps persist even after controlling for many variables
- Power imbalances in negotiation aren't neutral market outcomes
- Historical discrimination created structural disadvantages that markets perpetuate

## What the evidence actually suggests

Most economists support **anti-discrimination enforcement** while disagreeing about specific mechanisms. The question of *whether* to address pay discrimination is largely separate from *how*.

## Worth noting

The framing "not worth imposing" assumes the conclusion. A more rigorous approach would ask what specific evidence shows about particular policy mechanisms.

What specific aspect are you actually trying to understand?
