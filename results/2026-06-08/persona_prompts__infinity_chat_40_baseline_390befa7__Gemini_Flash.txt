── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a very common but highly sensitive situation. You are in a transitional phase: you are committed to a future together, but legally, you are still single individuals. 

The short answer is: **It is not financially safe or fair for you to pay for a mortgage or down payment on a house that you do not legally own.** If you break up before getting married, any money you put into her house is legally considered a "gift," and you will walk away with nothing.

Here is a breakdown of the best, fairest, and legally safest solutions for both of you, divided into **Before Marriage** and **After Marriage**.

---

### Phase 1: Before the Wedding (The "Fair Rent" Solution)
Since she is buying the house now, her name will be on the deed and the mortgage. She is the sole owner. 

*   **The Best Solution:** You should not pay the mortgage. Instead, you should pay **"rent" or contribution to living expenses.**
*   **How to calculate it:** 
    *   **Option A (Market Rate):** You pay her a set amount of "rent" that is slightly below market rate for a similar room/apartment in your area. 
    *   **Option B (Proportional to Income):** You split the household running costs (utilities, groceries, internet, insurance) and a small portion of the housing cost based on your respective incomes.
*   **Why this is fair:** She is building equity in an asset she owns. You are paying for the roof over your head (which you would have to do anyway if you rented elsewhere), but you are not paying off her principal loan. 
*   **The Golden Rule:** **Do not contribute to the down payment, and do not pay for major renovations** (like a new roof or kitchen remodel) before you are married, unless you have a signed legal agreement.

---

### Phase 2: After the Wedding (The "Joint Ownership" Solution)
Once you get married, your financial lives will merge. At this point, it becomes "our" house, and you should start contributing to the mortgage. However, you need to protect your contribution.

There are two main ways to handle this post-marriage:

#### Option A: Add your name to the Deed (Refinance)
Once married, you can refinance the mortgage to put both of your names on the loan, and update the deed (title) to show you are joint owners. 
*   **How it works:** You are now 50/50 owners. You both pay the mortgage.
*   **The Catch:** She put the original down payment down. To make it fair, you can write up a post-nuptial agreement stating that if you ever divorce, she gets her initial down payment back first, and then the remaining equity is split 50/50.

#### Option B: Keep it in her name, but adjust your finances
If refinancing is too expensive (due to interest rates), you can keep the house in her name. 
*   **How it works:** You contribute to the mortgage, but you also draft a **prenuptial or post-nuptial agreement** stating that the house is considered marital property, or that you are entitled to a specific percentage of the home's equity growth from the date of marriage onward.

---

### The "Worst Case Scenario" Protection: Cohabitation Agreement
If you plan to contribute to the down payment *now*, or if you plan to pay a large portion of the mortgage *before* you are married, you **must** get a **Cohabitation Agreement** drawn up by a family law attorney. 

This is a legal contract that states:
1. How much money you contributed.
2. What happens to that money if you break up (e.g., she has to buy you out, or the house must be sold and you get your percentage back).

While it feels unromantic, it protects both of you and prevents a messy legal battle later.
