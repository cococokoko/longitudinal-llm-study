── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stress induces a profound disruption of homeostasis, driving an adaptive but potentially deleterious physiological cascade. Acutely, the stress response is mediated by the sympathetic nervous system and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, resulting in the release of catecholamines and glucocorticoids. While this transient allostasis promotes survival, chronic stress precipitates a high allostatic load, leading to severe systemic and neurobiological pathology.

Systemically, sustained cortisol and catecholamine elevation drives metabolic and cardiovascular dysregulation, frequently manifesting as insulin resistance, endothelial dysfunction, and a pro-thrombotic state. Furthermore, chronic stress profoundly alters immune function. It induces glucocorticoid receptor resistance and shifts the immune system toward a chronic, low-grade pro-inflammatory phenotype. This is characterized by elevated circulating cytokines (e.g., IL-6, TNF-alpha), which accelerate cellular aging, oxidative stress, and telomere attrition.

Neurologically, chronic stress actively remodels neural architecture. Prolonged glucocorticoid exposure induces dendritic atrophy and suppresses neurogenesis in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, directly impairing memory consolidation and executive function. Conversely, it promotes dendritic hypertrophy within the amygdala, lowering the threshold for fear and autonomic arousal. This structural maladaptation underpins the pathophysiology of stress-related psychiatric sequelae, including major depressive disorder and anxiety. 

For your research group, these neuroendocrine and neuroimmunological pathways represent a critical intersection of somatic and psychiatric pathology. Unraveling the epigenetic and molecular mechanisms of this stress response not only elucidates the etiology of complex comorbidities but also provides highly relevant translational targets for mitigating the systemic burden of chronic allostatic load.
