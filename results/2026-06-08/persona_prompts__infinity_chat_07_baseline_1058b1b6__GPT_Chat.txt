── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes—at least based on our current understanding of logic, mathematics, physics, and the limits of computation, it appears impossible for any being to know literally everything.

There are several reasons:

- Logical limits:
  - In mathematics, Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that any sufficiently powerful formal system contains true statements that cannot be proven within that system.
  - This suggests there are limits to what can be known through formal reasoning alone.

- Computational limits:
  - Alan Turing showed that some problems are undecidable. For example, there is no general procedure that can determine in every case whether a computer program will eventually stop or run forever.
  - Some truths may therefore be impossible to derive by any algorithm.

- Physical limits:
  - Quantum mechanics places fundamental limits on what can be known simultaneously about certain properties of a system.
  - There are also practical limits on information storage, processing, and observation within the universe.

- Self-reference:
  - A system trying to contain complete knowledge of itself can run into paradoxes and limitations similar to those found in logic and computation.

There is also a philosophical distinction:

- If "everything" means all facts that exist, many philosophers and scientists think complete knowledge is impossible.
- If "everything" means all facts that are knowable in principle, it's still unclear whether the set of knowable facts is finite, infinite, or even fully accessible to any single knower.

One interesting twist is that proving "nothing can know everything" is itself difficult. The strongest claim supported by current knowledge is that there are deep and apparently unavoidable limits on complete knowledge.

So while we can't rule out every imaginable concept of omniscience, modern mathematics and science strongly suggest that total knowledge is unattainable for any finite being—and perhaps for any system whatsoever.
