As a tenured professor, you have a strong advantage on YouTube: expertise, credibility, and access to ideas that many people find interesting but difficult to understand. The challenge is translating academic knowledge into content that people choose to watch.

A useful approach:

- Define a clear audience
  - Undergraduates?
  - Graduate students?
  - General public?
  - Professionals in your field?
  - Lifelong learners?

- Choose a specific channel identity
  - Explaining current research
  - Debunking misconceptions
  - Teaching core concepts
  - Academic career advice
  - Commentary on developments in your discipline
  - Interviews with researchers

- Focus on viewer value, not lecture delivery
  - A great lecture is not automatically a great YouTube video.
  - Open with a question, mystery, controversy, or surprising fact.
  - Explain ideas through stories, examples, and visuals.

- Create videos around questions people are already asking
  - Use YouTube search suggestions.
  - Look at Reddit, Quora, Google Trends, and news topics.
  - Examples:
    - "Why do economists disagree about inflation?"
    - "What archaeologists actually know about ancient civilizations"
    - "The biggest misconception about climate models"

- Keep production quality adequate, not perfect
  - Clear audio matters more than expensive cameras.
  - Good lighting and editing help.
  - Simple visuals, diagrams, and slides can be effective.

- Develop a publishing rhythm
  - Consistency is more important than intensity.
  - One strong video per week or every two weeks is often sustainable.

- Use compelling titles and thumbnails
  - Avoid academic paper titles.
  - Instead of:
    - "An Introduction to Bayesian Inference"
  - Consider:
    - "Why Humans Are Terrible at Understanding Probability"

- Build a content portfolio
  - Evergreen videos that remain useful for years.
  - Timely videos tied to current events.
  - Short clips that drive discovery.

- Engage with viewers
  - Reply to comments.
  - Ask for questions.
  - Use audience feedback to guide future topics.

- Collaborate
  - Appear on podcasts.
  - Interview colleagues.
  - Work with creators outside academia.

Common mistake among academics:
- Prioritizing completeness over engagement.
- YouTube rewards clarity, curiosity, and accessibility. A video that explains one idea exceptionally well often performs better than a comprehensive lecture.
