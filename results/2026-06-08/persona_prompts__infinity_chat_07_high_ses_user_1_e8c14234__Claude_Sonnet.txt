I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting epistemological question, and your background makes it worth engaging seriously.

## The straightforward answer: Yes, it's impossible

**Practical reasons:**
- Information grows faster than any individual can absorb
- Even within neurosurgery specifically, the literature expands faster than one person can read
- Time and cognitive capacity are finite

**More fundamental reasons:**
- Some things may be **genuinely unknowable** (certain quantum states, consciousness's hard problem)
- Knowledge requires **verification**, not just information accumulation
- Some domains may have **inherent limits** (Gödel's incompleteness theorems suggest even mathematics has this)

## Worth being precise about though

- "Everything" is doing a lot of work in that question
- There's a difference between **knowing all facts** vs **understanding all principles**
- Expertise like yours involves knowing **what matters** and **how to reason** in a domain, which is arguably more valuable than encyclopedic recall

## The honest tension

Some philosophers argue we should be **humble about what we don't know** while others note that **functional expertise is real** - you genuinely know things most people don't.

The more interesting question might be: **what are the most important things we currently don't know** in your field?
