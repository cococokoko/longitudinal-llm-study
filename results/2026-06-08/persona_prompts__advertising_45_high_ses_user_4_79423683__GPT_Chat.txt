── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many transferable skills that help with DIY: structured problem-solving, comfort with technical information, attention to detail, and a habit of learning from multiple sources before acting. The biggest adjustment is that home repair is highly practical and tactile, so hands-on repetition matters more than conceptual understanding alone.

A good approach is to learn in layers:

1. Build a foundation in how houses work
- Learn the major systems:
  - Structure (framing, walls, roofs)
  - Plumbing
  - Electrical
  - HVAC
  - Insulation and moisture control
  - Finishes (drywall, paint, flooring, trim)
- Understanding these systems helps you diagnose problems rather than memorizing repairs.

Useful resources:
- "How Your House Works" by Charlie Wing
- "The Visual Handbook of Building and Remodeling" by Charlie Wing
- Family Handyman
- This Old House (website and YouTube)

2. Learn from high-quality video channels
Some channels are excellent for beginners because they explain principles, not just procedures:
- This Old House
- Home Renovision DIY
- The Honest Carpenter
- Everyday Home Repairs
- See Jane Drill
- Essential Craftsman (especially broader building concepts)

When watching:
- Compare multiple sources before attempting a project.
- Notice where experts disagree and investigate why.

3. Create a progression of projects
Start with low-risk projects:
- Caulking
- Painting
- Drywall patching
- Replacing hardware
- Installing shelves
- Weatherstripping doors
- Replacing faucets and showerheads

Intermediate:
- Installing light fixtures
- Replacing outlets and switches
- Simple trim carpentry
- Toilet replacement
- Flooring installation

Advanced:
- Tile work
- Framing
- Running new plumbing lines
- New electrical circuits
- Structural modifications

Avoid jumping directly into projects where mistakes can cause:
- Fire
- Flooding
- Structural damage

4. Learn tools systematically
Many beginners struggle because they lack tool familiarity.

Focus first on:
- Tape measure
- Stud finder
- Drill/driver
- Impact driver
- Circular saw
- Oscillating multi-tool
- Level
- Basic hand tools

Choose a project and learn the tools required rather than buying everything at once.

5. Practice on noncritical work
Create opportunities to make mistakes safely:
- Build shop shelves
- Construct simple furniture
- Repair a shed
- Work on garage storage systems

This is similar to learning procedural skills in medicine before performing them in higher-stakes settings.

6. Understand building codes and permits
Learn:
- Local building codes
- Permit requirements
- Inspection processes

Even if you are capable of performing the work, code compliance affects:
- Safety
- Insurance
- Home value
- Future resale

7. Develop a diagnostic mindset
A common mistake is treating symptoms rather than causes.

For example:
- Peeling paint → investigate moisture.
- Water stain on ceiling → find roof, plumbing, or condensation source.
- Cracked drywall → assess structural movement versus cosmetic issues.

The best DIYers spend substantial time diagnosing before repairing.

8. Learn from tradespeople
If you hire professionals:
- Observe respectfully.
- Ask questions.
- Understand their reasoning.
- Request explanations of failure mechanisms.

Many homeowners learn more from one day watching a skilled electrician, plumber, or carpenter than from dozens of videos.

9. Build a reference library
Keep:
- Manuals
- Wiring diagrams
- Paint records
- Appliance information
- Photos of repairs before walls are closed

Treat your house almost like a long-term research project with organized documentation.

10. Know when not to DIY
Projects often worth hiring out:
- Major service-panel electrical work
- Significant structural changes
- Complex roofing
- Large foundation repairs
- Hazardous-material remediation

The goal is not to do everything yourself. The goal is to understand enough to decide intelligently what to do yourself and what to delegate.

If your goal is to become genuinely competent rather than occasionally handy, spending 2–3 hours per week learning plus completing one small project each month for a year will typically give you a surprisingly broad skill set. Most proficiency comes from completing actual projects, not from accumulating more information.
