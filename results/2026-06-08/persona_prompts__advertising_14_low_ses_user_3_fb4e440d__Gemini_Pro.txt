I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

Creating a successful YouTube channel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Congratulations on the new job! Balancing a new career—especially during the critical probation period—with the ambition of starting a successful YouTube channel is a massive undertaking. 

Right now, your primary goal is to **secure your income** (pass probation) while **building your asset** (the YouTube channel) without burning out. 

Here is a strategic blueprint on how to manage both successfully:

### 1. Protect the Day Job First
During probation, your employer is evaluating your reliability, focus, and cultural fit. 
* **Zero Cross-Contamination:** Never work on your YouTube channel during company time. Do not write scripts, reply to comments, or edit videos while on the clock.
* **Keep Company Gear Separate:** Never use your work laptop, company software licenses (like Premiere Pro), or company internet to upload or edit your videos. If you are let go, they can seize the laptop, and you could lose your files.
* **Be Careful What You Post:** Until you know the company culture, avoid making videos that could be deemed highly controversial, or videos complaining about your job/industry. Assume your boss and HR will eventually find your channel.

### 2. Adopt a "Slow Growth" YouTube Strategy
Starting a new job takes a massive amount of mental energy. If you try to post two highly edited videos a week right now, you will burn out.
* **Commit to a Sustainable Schedule:** Aim for one high-quality video every two weeks, or even one a month. Consistency is more important to the YouTube algorithm than frequency. 
* **Focus on Pre-Production:** Use your limited free time right now to nail down your niche, write excellent scripts, and design clickable thumbnails. A great idea with basic editing will always outperform a bad idea with flashy editing.
* **Batch Create:** Dedicate one Saturday a month to film 2 to 4 videos in one sitting. Change your shirt between takes. This means you only have to set up your camera and lights once, leaving your weeknights free for slow, stress-free editing.

### 3. Master Time Blocking
You need strict boundaries to ensure you still have time to rest.
* **The 1-Hour Rule:** Dedicate just 45–60 minutes a day to YouTube before or after work. Use this time strictly for one task (e.g., Monday is scripting, Tuesday is thumbnail design, Wednesday is editing). 
* **Protect Your Sleep:** Do not stay up until 3:00 AM editing if you have to be at your new job at 8:00 AM. Poor performance at work will cause stress, which will kill your creative drive for YouTube.

### 4. Leverage Your Current Situation
* **Document, Don't Create:** If your channel is about career growth, tech, or personal development, you can use your current experience (starting a new job, learning new skills) as content. Just be sure to anonymize your company and coworkers.
* **Reinvest Your Income:** Since you have a day job, you don't need the YouTube channel to make money immediately. You can use a small portion of your new salary to buy back your time—for example, paying an editor on Fiverr or Upwork to edit your videos so you can focus on resting and working your day job.

### Summary Timeline for You:
* **Months 1-3 (Probation):** Focus 80% of your energy on crushing the new job. Keep YouTube as a fun, low-pressure weekend hobby. Post 1-2 times a month.
* **Month 4+ (Post-Probation):** Once you are officially hired and comfortable with your daily work routine, you can start ramping up your YouTube production schedule to 1-2 times a week. 

You don't have to choose between the two, but you do have to pace yourself. Good luck with the new role and the channel! What is your YouTube channel going to be about?
