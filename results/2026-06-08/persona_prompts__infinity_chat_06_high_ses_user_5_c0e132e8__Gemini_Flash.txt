── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It is incredibly common for high-achieving executives, particularly in technology, to reach a point where they realize they don’t actually know what they want. 

You have likely spent the last 15 to 25 years optimizing for **external validation metrics**: promotions, scale, compensation, headcount, shipping products, and market share. When you spend decades optimizing for what the *system* wants, your internal compass—the mechanism that detects your own desires—atrophies.

To understand what you want, you need to treat this not as an existential crisis, but as a **discovery and design problem**. 

Here is a structured framework to help you debug your desires and find clarity.

---

### Phase 1: The Diagnostic (Data Gathering)
Before you can figure out what you want in the future, you must audit your present. You need data, not just feelings.

*   **The Energy Audit:** For the next two weeks, keep a notebook or a spreadsheet. At the end of every meeting, task, or interaction, rate your energy level on a scale of -5 (drained) to +5 (energized). 
    *   *The Goal:* Do not look at what you are *good* at (you are likely good at many things that drain you). Look at what gives you net-positive energy. Is it mentoring? Solving architecture problems? Negotiating? Deep focus time?
*   **The Calendar Forensic:** Look back at your calendar over the last quarter. Color-code it:
    *   *Red:* Things you did out of obligation, fear, or inertia.
    *   *Green:* Things you looked forward to.
    *   *Blue:* Deep work/creative flow.
    *   What is the ratio? If your calendar is 90% Red, you don't hate your career; you hate your current calendar.

---

### Phase 2: Stripping Away "Mimetic Desire"
In high-status environments like tech, we suffer from what philosopher René Girard called *mimetic desire*—we want things because other people want them. You might think you want to be a CEO, start a VC fund, or join a board simply because that’s what people in your peer group do.

To isolate your true desires, apply these filters:

*   **The "No Audience" Test:** If you achieved your next goal, but you were legally forbidden from ever putting it on LinkedIn, telling your peers, or talking about it at dinner parties, **would you still want to do it?** If the answer is no, you want the *status* of the thing, not the *thing itself*.
*   **The "Day in the Life" Reality Check:** We often desire *titles*, not *roles*. You might want the title of "Founder," but do you want the daily reality of founder-led sales, fundraising, and constant rejection? Write down the actual, unglamorous daily tasks of the life you think you want. Do you actually want to do those tasks?

---

### Phase 3: Structured Frameworks for Discovery
As a tech executive, you are used to frameworks. Use these three to map your internal landscape.

#### 1. Inversion (The "Anti-Vision")
It is often much easier to define what you *don't* want than what you do. 
*   Write a detailed description of your absolute worst-case professional day five years from now. 
*   *Example:* "I wake up at 6 AM to check Slack. I spend 8 hours in back-to-back alignment meetings about a product I don't care about. I have to manage political infighting. I don't have time to exercise."
*   Once you have the anti-vision, **invert it**. This gives you your non-negotiable boundaries.
