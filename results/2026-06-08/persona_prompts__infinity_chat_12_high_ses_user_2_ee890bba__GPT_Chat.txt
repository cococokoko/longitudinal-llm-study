── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no single set of signs that can reliably prove a child "was not loved enough" by their mother. Love, attachment, caregiving quality, emotional availability, trauma, mental illness, stress, and family circumstances can all interact in complex ways.

That said, adults who experienced chronic emotional neglect, inconsistent caregiving, or a lack of maternal warmth sometimes report patterns such as:

- Difficulty believing they are genuinely lovable.
- A persistent feeling of emotional emptiness or loneliness, even in relationships.
- Strong fear of rejection, abandonment, or being replaced.
- Excessive people-pleasing and prioritizing others' needs over their own.
- Difficulty identifying, expressing, or trusting their own emotions.
- Feeling uncomfortable receiving affection, praise, or care.
- Constantly seeking external validation, achievement, or status to feel worthy.
- Harsh self-criticism and perfectionism.
- Difficulty setting boundaries.
- Attraction to emotionally unavailable or inconsistent partners.
- A sense that they must earn love through performance or usefulness.
- Trouble asking for help or depending on others.
- Feeling unseen, unheard, or emotionally misunderstood throughout life.

More specific childhood experiences that can contribute to these outcomes include:
- Rarely receiving affection, comfort, or encouragement.
- Having emotional needs dismissed, mocked, or ignored.
- Being valued mainly for achievement, obedience, or caretaking.
- Experiencing unpredictable warmth and withdrawal.
- Being expected to meet the parent's emotional needs.
- Chronic criticism, shaming, or comparison to others.

It is important to distinguish between:
- A mother who loved her child but struggled with emotional availability due to depression, addiction, trauma, illness, or overwhelming stress.
- A mother who was consistently neglectful, rejecting, abusive, or indifferent.

The effects on the child can sometimes look similar even when the underlying reasons differ.

If you're evaluating this question in a personal context, one useful indicator is not whether your mother said she loved you, but whether, over time, you felt emotionally safe, comforted, accepted, and important in her presence. A child can be materially provided for and still experience significant emotional deprivation.
