The debate surrounding the death penalty is deeply complex, involving profound moral, legal, and practical considerations. Those who support or allow the death penalty generally base their views on several key arguments:

### 1. Retributive Justice ("Just Deserts")
This is one of the oldest and most common arguments for capital punishment. It is based on the moral principle of proportionality—that the punishment should fit the severity of the crime. 
* **The Principle:** Proponents argue that certain crimes, such as premeditated murder, are so heinous that any punishment short of death is inadequate. In this view, taking a life forfeits the perpetrator's right to their own life.
* **Moral Balance:** For many, executing the offender is the only way to fully acknowledge the severity of the crime and restore a sense of moral order and justice to society.

### 2. Closure and Justice for Victims' Families
Supporters often emphasize the impact of heinous crimes on the victims' families and communities.
* **Providing Resolution:** For families who have lost loved ones to violent crime, the finality of the death penalty can offer a sense of closure that ongoing appeals or life sentences may not. 
* **Validation of Loss:** It serves as a societal statement that the victim's life was of immense value, and that the ultimate price must be paid for taking it.
