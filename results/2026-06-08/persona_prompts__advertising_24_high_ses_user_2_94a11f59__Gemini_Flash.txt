I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney at a well-established law firm, your retirement planning needs are distinct from the average investor. You likely face a high tax bracket, complex compensation structures (especially if you are an equity or non-equity partner), and a demanding schedule that limits your time to manage investments. 

For someone in your position, the goal is not just saving, but **maximizing tax efficiency, asset protection, and wealth preservation.**

Here are the best ways to save for retirement, tailored specifically for a senior legal professional.

---

### 1. Maximize Firm-Sponsored Plans (The Foundation)
Depending on whether you are a W-2 employee (Counsel/Senior Associate) or a K-1 partner, your firm-sponsored options will vary, but you should maximize them in this order:

*   **The 401(k) and Profit-Sharing Plan:** 
    *   **W-2/Partner:** Max out your personal pre-tax contributions ($23,000 in 2024, plus a $7,500 catch-up contribution if you are age 50 or older). 
    *   **Profit-Sharing:** Many established firms offer profit-sharing. Ensure you are maximizing this. The total defined contribution limit (employee + employer/firm match) is $69,000 for 2024 ($76,500 if age 50+).
*   **Defined Benefit / Cash Balance Plan:** 
    *   This is the **single most powerful tool** for high-earning senior attorneys (especially partners). 
    *   These plans allow you to contribute significant pre-tax dollars—often **$100,000 to $300,000+ per year**, depending on your age. 
    *   The contributions are tax-deductible for the firm (or you, if K-1), drastically reducing your current year tax bill while building a massive retirement nest egg that can eventually be rolled into an IRA.

### 2. Utilize "Backdoor" Roth Strategies
Because of your high income, you are phased out of making direct contributions to a Roth IRA. However, you can use legal "loopholes" to access tax-free growth.

*   **The Backdoor Roth IRA:** 
    *   You contribute after-tax dollars to a Traditional IRA and immediately convert it to a Roth IRA. 
    *   *Caveat:* Beware of the "Pro-Rata Rule." If you have other traditional IRAs (like a SEP-IRA or Rollover IRA), this conversion will be taxed proportionally. You may need to roll existing IRAs into your firm’s 401(k) first to clear the path.
*   **The Mega Backdoor Roth:** 
    *   If your firm’s 401(k) plan allows for **after-tax contributions** and **in-service distributions** (or conversions), you can contribute up to an additional ~$46,000 (depending on your age and other contributions) and immediately convert it to a Roth 401(k) or Roth IRA.
