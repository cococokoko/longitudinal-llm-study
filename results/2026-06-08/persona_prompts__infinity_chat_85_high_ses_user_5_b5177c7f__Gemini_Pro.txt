── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

Can you give me a powerful rhetorical question for an essay about the harms of social media on teens?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Because you are writing from the perspective of a tech executive, your rhetorical question will carry the most weight if it acknowledges the industry's power, its original intentions, and the unintended consequences of its business models. 

Here are a few powerful options, depending on the specific angle of your essay:

**The "Business Model vs. Human Cost" Angle (Best for the introduction)**
> *"If our platforms are free, why are our teenagers paying the highest price?"*

**The "Insider's Dilemma" Angle (Best for the body of the essay)**
> *"Must we accept that a generation’s anxiety is simply the cost of our engagement metrics, or is it time to admit this is a bug we can no longer afford to ignore?"*

**The "Legacy" Angle (Best for the conclusion)**
> *"Will history remember us as the architects who connected the world, or as the engineers who dismantled the mental well-being of our children?"*

**The "Misdirected Innovation" Angle (Best for a thought-provoking pivot)**
> *"We possess the computing power to map the human genome and explore the stars; why, then, are we using our greatest technological advancements to exploit the psychological vulnerabilities of a thirteen-year-old?"*

**The "Attention Economy" Angle (Short and punchy)**
> *"When did capturing a user's attention become synonymous with capturing their peace of mind?"*

**A tip for an executive writing this:** The most powerful rhetorical device you have is **"We."** By saying "we" instead of "they" (the tech industry), you take ownership. It transforms the essay from a standard critique into a brave, insider's call to action.
