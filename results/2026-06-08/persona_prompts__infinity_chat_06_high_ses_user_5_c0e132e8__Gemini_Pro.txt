── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As an executive at a large tech company, your brain is likely optimized for solving complex external problems: scaling systems, managing stakeholders, hitting quarterly targets, and anticipating market shifts. 

The irony of executive success is that the skills required to build a company’s vision often drown out your ability to hear your own. You are likely suffering from a low "signal-to-noise" ratio regarding your internal desires.

To figure out what you want, you need to approach yourself the way you would approach a complex, ambiguous product discovery phase. Here is a framework designed for an analytical, high-capacity mind to decode what you actually want.

---

### Phase 1: Clear the Noise (System Reset)
You cannot run a deep diagnostic while the CPU is maxed out at 99%. 

*   **Step away from the "Urgent":** You need unstructured time. This doesn't mean a family vacation where you are managing logistics. It means 3 to 5 days completely alone, with no agenda, no Slack, and no email. 
*   **Beware of "Borrowed Desires":** In large tech companies, it is incredibly easy to adopt the desires of your peers (e.g., becoming a C-level exec, joining a high-profile board, starting a VC fund, buying a specific type of house). You must separate what *you* want from what the Silicon Valley/tech ecosystem *tends to value*.

### Phase 2: Run the Diagnostics (Data Gathering)
Instead of asking the massive, paralyzing question ("What is my purpose?"), gather data on your current state through smaller, actionable queries.

*   **The Calendar Energy Audit:** Print out your calendar from the last three months. Highlight in **green** the meetings, tasks, and interactions that gave you energy. Highlight in **red** the ones that drained you. Look for the patterns. Do you love mentoring but hate resource allocation? Do you love product strategy but despise board politics? Your desires are hidden in your green highlights.
*   **The Inversion Principle (The Anti-Vision):** Charlie Munger famously advocated for inversion. If you don't know what you want, define exactly what you *do not* want. Write down your nightmare scenario for five years from now. What does your Tuesday look like? Who are you working with? What is your health like? Once you define the anti-vision, the path to what you *do* want becomes the exact opposite.
*   **The Jealousy Map:** Jealousy is a highly accurate compass. Look at your network. Who are you secretly envious of? Is it the peer who just made CEO? The former colleague who quit to bootstrap a tiny, low-stress SaaS business? The friend who took a sabbatical to write a book? Figure out *why* you are jealous of them. It points directly to an unmet desire.

### Phase 3: Define the Dimensions of "Want"
"What I want" is too broad. Break it down into four distinct pillars:

1.  **Intellectual:** What scale of problem do you want to solve? Do you want to build from 0 to 1, scale from 10 to 100, or optimize from 100 to 1,000? 
2.  **Lifestyle & Autonomy:** How much control do you want over your time? Are you willing to travel 100 days a year for the right role, or do you want to be home for dinner every night? 
3.  **Financial (The "Enough" Number):** Large tech companies offer "golden handcuffs" (RSUs, massive bonuses). You need to calculate your "enough" number. Once your lifestyle is funded, money transitions from a utility to a scorecard. Are you chasing more money because you need it, or because it's how you keep score?
4.  **Ego & Legacy:** Be honest with yourself. How much does prestige matter to you? There is no wrong answer, but lying to yourself about needing external validation will lead to bad career choices.

### Phase 4: Prototype and Test (Agile Discovery)
Do not make a massive, abrupt pivot based on a weekend of journaling. Run small pilots to test your hypotheses about what you want.
