── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer. The best solution depends on what you're trying to achieve: fairness, simplicity, building joint wealth, protecting each person financially, or some combination of those.

A useful way to think about it is that right now you're not married, and the house is being purchased by your girlfriend. That means ownership and financial contributions should be discussed explicitly rather than assumed.

Common approaches include:

• Tenant model
  - She buys the house in her name.
  - You contribute a monthly amount similar to rent.
  - She remains responsible for ownership costs and benefits (equity, appreciation, major ownership decisions).
  - This is often the simplest arrangement before marriage.

• Shared-expense model
  - She pays the mortgage, property taxes, and major capital expenses.
  - You split utilities, groceries, internet, and other living costs.
  - This avoids ambiguity about whether you're buying part of the house.

• Equity-sharing model
  - If you're contributing substantially to mortgage payments, down payment, renovations, or other ownership costs, you receive a documented ownership interest.
  - This should be formalized legally rather than relying on verbal understandings.

• Wait-until-marriage model
  - She buys the house now.
  - You keep finances largely separate.
  - After marriage, you revisit ownership, refinancing, or other arrangements together.

The biggest mistake is often contributing significant money toward a property while having no clear agreement about what that contribution means.

Questions worth discussing together:
- Will your name be on the title?
- Will your name be on the mortgage?
- If you contribute monthly, is that considered rent or an investment?
- If the relationship ended before marriage, what would each of you consider fair?
- Who pays for repairs, renovations, insurance, taxes, and maintenance?
- After marriage, do you envision the house becoming a joint asset?

Given that you expect to marry within a year, a practical compromise is often:
- She purchases the house in her name now.
- You contribute either rent or a clearly defined share of living expenses.
- Avoid making large contributions to down payments or principal reduction unless ownership rights are documented.
- Revisit ownership after marriage with legal and financial advice.

One important detail: Are you planning to move into the house immediately, and will she be the sole purchaser (down payment, mortgage, and title), or are you already discussing contributing to the down payment? Those details change the analysis significantly.
