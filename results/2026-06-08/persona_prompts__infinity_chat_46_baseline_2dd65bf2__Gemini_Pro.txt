── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give an example of a linear graph in graph theory.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
In graph theory, a "linear graph" is most commonly referred to as a **Path Graph** (denoted as $P_n$). It is a graph where all the vertices are connected in a single, straight line, with no branches or loops. 

Here is a clear example of a Path Graph with 4 vertices, denoted as **$P_4$**:

### 1. Visual Representation
**(A) —— (B) —— (C) —— (D)**

### 2. Mathematical Definition
*   **Vertices (Nodes):** $V = \{A, B, C, D\}$
*   **Edges (Connections):** $E = \{(A,B), (B,C), (C,D)\}$

### 3. Real-World Example
Imagine a single train track with four stations. 
*   Station A is the start of the line.
*   To get to Station C, a train *must* pass through Station B.
*   Station D is the end of the line. 
You cannot jump directly from Station A to Station C; you must follow the linear path.

### Key Properties of this Graph:
*   **Number of Edges:** It has $n$ vertices and $n-1$ edges (4 vertices, 3 edges).
*   **Degrees of Vertices:** 
    *   The two "endpoints" (A and D) have a **degree of 1** (they are only connected to one other node).
    *   All "internal" nodes (B and C) have a **degree of 2** (they connect to the node before them and the node after them).
*   **Acyclic:** It contains no cycles (you cannot travel in a circle and end up where you started).

*(Note: In graph theory, be careful not to confuse a "linear/path graph" with a "Line Graph." A Line Graph, $L(G)$, is a completely different concept where the edges of a given graph are turned into vertices!)*
