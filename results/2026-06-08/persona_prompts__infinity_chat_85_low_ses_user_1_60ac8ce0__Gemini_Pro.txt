── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work full-time in a fast food job with an unpredictable schedule.

Can you give me a powerful rhetorical question for an essay about the harms of social media on teens?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Because you work a demanding job with an unpredictable schedule, you know exactly what it means to live in the *real* world—where time is valuable, energy is limited, and hard work is required. 

You can actually use that perspective to make your essay stronger. Here are a few powerful rhetorical questions, broken down by the specific "angle" your essay might be taking. 

**If your essay is about how social media steals teens' time and energy:**
> "If we would never allow a stranger to steal hours of a teenager's day, why do we so willingly let an algorithm do it?"

**If your essay is about mental health and unrealistic expectations (this one contrasts real life with fake internet life):**
> "How can we expect teenagers to build genuine self-esteem when they are forced to measure their real, everyday lives against the filtered, edited, and artificial highlight reels of billions of strangers?"

**If your essay is about loneliness and isolation:**
> "In an era where teenagers have never been more digitally 'connected,' why has an entire generation never felt so profoundly alone?"

**If your essay is about addiction and tech companies:**
> "When did we decide it was acceptable to hand the psychological development of our youth over to tech billionaires whose only goal is to keep them scrolling?"

**If you want to tie in your own perspective of hard work:**
> "While many young people are working exhausting, unpredictable shifts just to get by in the real world, why do we allow social media to convince them that they are failing if they don't have overnight viral fame and effortless luxury?"

**Writing Tip for a Busy Student:** 
The best place to put a rhetorical question is either at the very beginning of your essay (as the "hook" to grab the reader's attention) or at the very end of your conclusion (to leave the reader thinking about your topic long after they finish reading).
