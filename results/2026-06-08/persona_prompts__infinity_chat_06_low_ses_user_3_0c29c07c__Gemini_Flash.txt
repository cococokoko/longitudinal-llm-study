── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job is a vulnerable time. When you are on probation, the pressure to perform and "fit in" can be so intense that it drowns out your own inner voice. You might be so focused on *them* liking *you* that you forget to ask if *you* like *them*.

To understand what you want during this critical transition phase, you need to shift from "survival mode" to "observer mode." 

Here is a step-by-step framework to help you figure out what you actually want.

---

### 1. Reframe the Probation Period
First, remember that **probation is a two-way street.** 
The company is testing your skills and fit, but you are also testing their culture, management, and the reality of the role. You are not a passive applicant hoping to be chosen; you are an active evaluator. This shift in mindset reduces anxiety and empowers you to look at the job objectively.

### 2. Conduct an "Energy Audit" (The Daily Log)
For the next two weeks, keep a private notebook or digital document. At the end of every workday, write down:
*   **What energized you?** (e.g., "Solving that Excel issue," "The brainstorming session," "Working alone for two hours.")
*   **What drained you?** (e.g., "The chaotic morning meeting," "Writing that specific report," "The lack of clear instructions.")
*   **What left you feeling neutral?**

**Why this works:** We often think we want one thing (e.g., "I want to be a manager") but find the actual day-to-day tasks of that role draining. Your energy levels do not lie. Look for patterns after two weeks.

### 3. Separate the "Learning Curve" from the "Job Reality"
The first 30 to 90 days of any job are usually uncomfortable. You don't know where things are, you don't know the unwritten rules, and you might feel incompetent. 
*   **Ask yourself:** *Am I unhappy because I dislike this career path, or am I just uncomfortable because I am learning something new?*
*   If it’s just the learning curve, give yourself grace. What you "want" might just be a sense of mastery, which only comes with time.

### 4. Identify Your "Career Anchors"
What is non-negotiable for you? Rank these five factors in order of importance *right now* in your life:
1.  **Money & Security:** High pay, stability, good benefits.
2.  **Autonomy:** Freedom to do your work your way, flexible hours, remote work.
3.  **Alignment/Purpose:** Believing in the company’s mission; feeling like your work matters.
4.  **Growth & Mastery:** Learning new skills, clear path to promotion, mentorship.
5.  **People & Culture:** A collaborative, friendly, low-stress environment.

Once you rank these, look at your current job. Does it align with your top two anchors? If your top anchor is "Autonomy" but your new boss micromanages you, that is why you feel unsettled.

### 5. Use the "Via Negativa" (Process of Elimination)
Sometimes it is incredibly hard to know what you *do* want, but very easy to know what you *don't* want. 
Write down a list of your "absolute negatives" based on past jobs and this current one. 
*   *Example:* "I do not want to work weekends." "I do not want to work in a highly bureaucratic environment." "I do not want a job that requires constant client-facing small talk."
By defining the boundaries of what you hate, the picture of what you want becomes much clearer.

### 6. Look Around You (The "Future Self" Test)
Look at your manager, your manager’s manager, and your senior colleagues. 
*   Do you want their jobs? 
*   Do you want their lifestyles? 
*   Do they seem happy, balanced, and intellectually stimulated?
If the answer is a resounding "no," then this company (or this specific career path) may not be what you want in the long term. That is valuable data.
