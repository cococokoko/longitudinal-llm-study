Running a marathon is as much about preparation and pacing as it is about fitness. A few high-impact tips:

Training
- Build mileage gradually. A common guideline is to increase weekly mileage by no more than about 10% at a time.
- Prioritize consistency over occasional heroic workouts. Regular training weeks matter more than a few exceptional runs.
- Include a weekly long run to develop endurance and confidence.
- Practice running at your intended marathon pace during some training sessions.
- Schedule recovery weeks every few weeks to reduce fatigue and lower injury risk.
- Strength train 1–2 times per week, focusing on core, glutes, hamstrings, and calves.

Race strategy
- Start slower than feels necessary. Many marathon runners lose significant time by going out too fast.
- Break the race into segments (for example: miles 1–10, 11–20, and the final 10K) rather than focusing on the full distance.
- Run your own race. Avoid getting pulled into other runners' pacing decisions.

Nutrition and hydration
- Practice your race-day fueling during training. Never try new nutrition products on race day.
- Aim to take in carbohydrates regularly during the race, based on your training experience and tolerance.
- Hydrate consistently but avoid excessive fluid intake.
- Pay attention to electrolyte replacement, especially in warm conditions.

Recovery and injury prevention
- Respect early warning signs of injury rather than trying to push through them.
- Prioritize sleep; it is one of the most effective recovery tools available.
- Easy runs should truly be easy. Recovery is part of training.

Race week
- Reduce training volume (taper) rather than trying to gain fitness at the last minute.
- Prepare logistics in advance: transportation, gear, bib pickup, nutrition, and weather contingencies.
- Stick to familiar foods and routines.

Mental approach
- Expect difficult moments. Nearly every marathon includes periods where pace, energy, or motivation fluctuate.
- Have a plan for how you'll respond when discomfort arrives—focus on form, fueling, and the next milestone rather than the finish line.

For someone with a structured, performance-oriented mindset, one useful principle is to treat marathon preparation as a risk-management exercise: success is usually driven less by maximizing any single workout and more by avoiding missed training, injury, pacing errors, and fueling mistakes.
