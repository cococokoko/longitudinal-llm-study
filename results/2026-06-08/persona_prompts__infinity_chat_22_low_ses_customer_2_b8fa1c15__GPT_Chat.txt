In Christianity, "discipleship" generally means following Jesus and growing in faith, but different traditions emphasize different aspects of it.

Some common views include:

- Evangelical view
  - Focuses on a personal relationship with Jesus, Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and spiritual growth.
  - Discipleship often happens through mentoring, small groups, and intentional spiritual practices.

- Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as a lifelong journey lived through participation in the Church, the sacraments, prayer, service, and moral formation.
  - Emphasizes becoming more like Christ within the community of faith.

- Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation (often called theosis or deification), becoming united with God through grace.
  - Prayer, worship, fasting, and participation in the life of the Church are central.

- Mainline Protestant view
  - Often highlights following Jesus through social responsibility, justice, compassion, and community involvement alongside personal faith.
  - Discipleship includes both spiritual formation and service to society.

- Anabaptist view
  - Strongly emphasizes obedience to Jesus' teachings, especially regarding peace, simplicity, and community.
  - Following Christ's example in daily life is often seen as the heart of discipleship.

- Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Stresses the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers for holy living, worship, and ministry.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, and active faith.

There are also different approaches that cut across denominations:

- Knowledge-centered: learning doctrine and Scripture.
- Relationship-centered: mentoring and spiritual friendship.
- Mission-centered: serving others and sharing the faith.
- Formation-centered: developing Christlike character through spiritual disciplines.

Despite these differences, most Christian traditions agree that discipleship involves learning from Jesus, following his teachings, and growing in love for God and others.
