I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist leading a research group, your cognitive load is already maxed out between clinical duties, grant writing, managing your lab, and staying current on literature. 

To learn to cook healthy meals quickly, you should **stop treating cooking as an art and start treating it as a standardized protocol.** You don't need to become a chef; you need a high-efficiency system that minimizes decision fatigue and active prep time.

Here is a step-by-step guide tailored to your schedule and analytical mindset.

### Phase 1: Optimize Your "Lab" (Equipment & Ingredients)
You wouldn't run an assay with dull pipettes. Don't cook with bad tools.
*   **The Core Tools:** Buy one high-quality 8-inch Chef’s knife, a large cutting board, and a meat thermometer. The thermometer eliminates the guesswork (and anxiety) of undercooking or overcooking protein.
*   **The Time-Machines:** Invest in an **Air Fryer** (roasts vegetables and proteins in half the time of an oven, with minimal oil) and an **Instant Pot** (allows you to throw frozen chicken or tough cuts of meat into a pot and have them ready in 30 minutes).
*   **Strategic Outsourcing:** Buy time. Purchase pre-washed greens, pre-minced garlic, frozen vegetables (which are flash-frozen at peak nutrition), and pre-cooked grains (like microwaveable brown rice or quinoa packets). 

### Phase 2: Master "Formulas," Not Recipes
Recipes require reading, measuring, and high cognitive load. Formulas are plug-and-play variables. Memorize this basic formula:
**Lean Protein + Fibrous Vegetable + Complex Carb + Flavor Profile**

Here are three high-speed protocols based on this formula:

**1. The Sheet Pan Protocol (5 mins active, 20 mins passive)**
*   *Method:* Line a baking sheet with parchment paper (zero cleanup). Put a protein (salmon, chicken breast) on one side. Put chopped veggies (broccoli, bell peppers) on the other. Toss with olive oil, salt, and pepper. Roast at 400°F (200°C) for 15-20 minutes.
*   *Carb:* Serve over microwaveable quinoa.

**2. The "Bowl" Protocol (Assembly only)**
*   *Method:* Start with a base of greens (spinach/arugula) and a grain (brown rice). Add a pre-cooked protein (rotisserie chicken or canned chickpeas). Add a healthy fat (avocado or nuts). 
*   *Flavor:* Top with a store-bought, low-sugar vinaigrette or tahini dressing.

**3. The Air Fryer Protocol (15 mins total)**
*   *Method:* Toss a white fish (cod, tilapia) or tofu in spices. Put it in the air fryer at 375°F for 10-12 minutes. Throw a bag of frozen broccoli in the microwave. Combine.

### Phase 3: The "Batching" System
Just as you batch experiments to save time, batch your food prep. Do not try to cook a complex meal every night.
*   **Component Prep:** On Sunday, don't prep whole meals (which get soggy). Prep *components*. Roast a massive tray of vegetables. Cook a large batch of grains. Grill 4-5 chicken breasts. During the week, you are just assembling these components and reheating them.
*   **Cook Once, Eat Twice:** Never cook dinner without making enough for tomorrow’s lunch. This instantly cuts your weekly cooking time in half.
