── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
The fastest ways to improve a credit score depend on what's currently affecting it, but these are generally the highest-impact actions:

- Lower your credit utilization ratio
  - If you have revolving credit card balances, paying them down can produce one of the quickest score increases.
  - Aim for overall utilization below 30%, and ideally below 10% for maximum scoring benefit.
  - If possible, make payments before the statement closing date so lower balances are reported to the credit bureaus.

- Check your credit reports for errors
  - Obtain reports from the major credit bureaus and review them for:
    - Incorrect late payments
    - Accounts that do not belong to you
    - Incorrect balances
    - Outdated negative information
  - Disputing and correcting errors can improve scores if inaccurate information is removed.

- Bring any past-due accounts current
  - If you have accounts that are currently delinquent, becoming current can help prevent further damage.
  - Recent payment history is an important scoring factor.

- Request a credit limit increase
  - If approved without a hard inquiry (ask first), a higher limit can reduce your utilization ratio without requiring additional payments.
  - Do not increase spending simply because more credit is available.

- Become an authorized user
  - If a trusted family member or spouse has a long-established account with excellent payment history and low utilization, being added as an authorized user may help some scoring models.

- Avoid new credit applications unless necessary
  - Multiple hard inquiries and newly opened accounts can temporarily lower scores.

- Use rapid rescore (in specific lending situations)
  - If you're applying for a mortgage or certain other loans, a lender may be able to use a rapid rescore process after you've paid down balances or corrected errors. This can update information faster than the normal reporting cycle.

What generally does not improve scores quickly:
- Closing old credit cards (often hurts utilization and account age).
- Paying off installment loans alone (the effect is usually less dramatic than reducing revolving credit card balances).
- Opening several new accounts.
