If you're starting a plant-based diet, a mix of evidence-based nutrition resources, practical recipes, and meal-planning tools can make the transition much easier.

Good evidence-based resources:
- NutritionFacts.org (Dr. Michael Greger): Research summaries and practical guidance on whole-food plant-based eating.
- The Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM): Free meal plans, nutrition guides, and recipes.
- NHS, Mayo Clinic, and Harvard Nutrition Source: Balanced information on meeting nutrient needs with a plant-based diet.

Useful books:
- "How Not to Die" by Michael Greger
- "How Not to Diet" by Michael Greger
- "Plant-Based on a Budget" by Toni Okamoto
- "The Vegan Starter Kit" by Neal Barnard

Recipe websites:
- Minimalist Baker (many simple plant-based recipes)
- Rainbow Plant Life
- Cookie and Kate
- Budget Bytes (has a dedicated vegetarian/plant-based section)
- Forks Over Knives recipes

Apps and tools:
- Cronometer: Helps track nutrients, especially useful when learning how to meet protein, iron, calcium, and vitamin B12 needs.
- Mealime: Simple meal planning and grocery lists.
- Forks Meal Planner: Plant-based meal planning.

Key nutrients to pay attention to:
- Vitamin B12 (usually requires supplementation on a fully plant-based diet)
- Protein
- Iron
- Calcium
- Iodine
- Omega-3 fats

A practical way to start:
- Replace one meal per day with a plant-based option.
- Build meals around beans, lentils, tofu, tempeh, whole grains, vegetables, fruits, nuts, and seeds.
- Keep a few easy staples on hand (canned beans, frozen vegetables, oats, peanut butter, tofu).

Since you've recently started a new job and may have limited time while settling in, it can help to focus on simple meals that take 15–20 minutes or less rather than trying to overhaul your diet all at once.
