How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

This is a substantive question worth thinking through carefully rather than just offering platitudes.

## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately shape international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO) to reflect their interests
- This creates rules that often entrench existing advantages rather than neutral frameworks
- Debt relationships create genuine dependency that constrains sovereign decision-making

**Bargaining asymmetries**
- Economic desperation limits negotiating leverage for poorer states
- Trade agreements frequently reflect power imbalances rather than mutual benefit
- Sanctions work precisely because economic vulnerability is unevenly distributed

## Competing Analytical Frameworks

**Liberal institutionalists** emphasize that interdependence creates mutual interests and cooperation incentives, reducing conflict

**Dependency theorists** argue the global economy systematically extracts value from periphery to core, making inequality self-reinforcing

**Realists** focus on how economic capacity translates directly into military and diplomatic power

## Honest Tensions Worth Acknowledging

- Some previously poor nations have dramatically improved positions (South Korea, China), challenging purely structural explanations
- Foreign aid relationships are genuinely complicated - sometimes helpful, sometimes creating dependency
- Economic inequality doesn't straightforwardly predict conflict; some wealthy nations are aggressive, some poor nations are stable

## Practically Relevant Considerations

For legal work involving international arbitration, trade disputes, or human rights frameworks, the institutional capture question is particularly significant - who writes the rules matters enormously.
