── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting empirical question. Here's an honest assessment of what the research suggests:

## What Research Actually Shows

**Some positive correlations found:**
- Higher cognitive ability correlates with broader moral circle consideration
- Intelligence associates with reduced in-group bias in some studies
- Cognitive empathy (perspective-taking) correlates moderately with IQ

**Complicating factors:**
- Emotional/affective compassion shows **weak or inconsistent** correlation with intelligence
- High intelligence can enable **rationalization** of self-serving behavior equally well
- "Moral licensing" effects can actually reduce compassionate behavior in high-achievers
- Intelligence may shift *how* someone reasons morally without changing underlying motivation

## Honest Caveats
- Measurement problems are significant - both intelligence and compassion are difficult to operationalize
- Publication bias likely affects this literature
- Clinical observations sometimes contradict research findings
- The relationship may be **non-linear**

## Clinically Relevant Consideration
You likely observe directly that **technical brilliance and genuine patient compassion are clearly dissociable** - which itself is meaningful evidence.

## Bottom Line
The correlation, if it exists, appears **modest and heavily context-dependent**, and probably shouldn't be assumed in either direction.
