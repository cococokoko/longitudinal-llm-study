── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Tips for running a marathon.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Running a marathon (26.2 miles / 42.195 km) is an incredible physical and mental achievement. Whether you are aiming for a personal best or just want to cross the finish line, preparation is everything. 

Here is a comprehensive guide to running a marathon, broken down into the phases of your journey:

### 1. Training Smart (Months Before)
*   **Follow a Plan:** Don’t just wing it. Find a 16- to 20-week training plan that fits your current fitness level (e.g., Hal Higdon, Nike Run Club, or a local running coach). 
*   **Prioritize the Long Run:** Your weekly long run is the most important workout. It teaches your body to burn fat for fuel and gets your muscles and joints used to the "time on feet."
*   **Obey the 10% Rule:** Never increase your total weekly mileage by more than 10% from the previous week. This is crucial for preventing injuries like shin splints and stress fractures.
*   **Incorporate Strength and Mobility:** Running is highly repetitive. Do basic strength training (squats, lunges, core work) 1–2 times a week to build resilient muscles and prevent injury.
*   **Respect Rest Days:** Fitness is built during recovery, not just during the workout. Take your rest days seriously.

### 2. Nutrition & Hydration (The "Fourth Discipline")
*   **The Golden Rule: Nothing New on Race Day.** Do not wear new shoes, new socks, or eat a new type of energy gel on race day. Practice your race-day outfit and nutrition during your long training runs.
*   **Practice Fueling:** Your body can only store enough glycogen (carbs) for about 90–120 minutes of running. You must take in calories during the race. Practice eating energy gels, chews, or real food every 30–45 minutes during your long runs.
*   **Hydrate with Electrolytes:** Drinking only water can lead to hyponatremia (low sodium). Alternate between water and sports drinks to keep your electrolytes balanced.
*   **Carb-Load Correctly:** In the 2–3 days before the race, increase your carbohydrate intake (pasta, rice, potatoes, bread). Don't overeat the night before; a massive, heavy meal can cause stomach issues on race morning.

### 3. The Taper (2–3 Weeks Before)
*   **Trust the Taper:** About 2 to 3 weeks before the race, your training plan will have you drastically reduce your mileage. You will likely feel sluggish, anxious, or phantom pains (known as "taper tantrums"). This is normal. Your body is healing and storing energy.
*   **Sleep:** The sleep you get two nights before the race is actually more important than the night before (when nerves will likely keep you awake). 

### 4. Race Day Strategy
*   **Lube Up:** Chafing is the enemy. Apply anti-chafe balm (like BodyGlide or Vaseline) generously to your thighs, underarms, nipples (for men), and anywhere sports bras or seams might rub.
*   **Start Slow:** The biggest rookie mistake is starting too fast because of the adrenaline and cheering crowds. Run the first 3–4 miles *slower* than your goal pace. 
*   **Run the Tangents:** Marathons are measured along the shortest possible route. Try to run in straight lines and take the inside of curves so you don't end up running 26.8 miles instead of 26.2.
*   **Break It Down:** Thinking about 26.2 miles is overwhelming. Break the race into chunks. A common strategy is the 10/10/10K method: Run the first 10 miles with your head (smart and slow), the next 10 miles with your legs (steady pace), and the final 10K (6.2 miles) with your heart (pure grit).
*   **Have a Mantra:** When you hit "the wall" (usually around mile 18–20), your brain will tell you to stop. Have a short, positive phrase ready to repeat to yourself, like "Light and smooth," "I can do hard things," or "Relentless forward progress."

### 5. Post-Race Recovery
*   **Keep Moving:** When you cross the finish line, do not sit down immediately. Walk around for 10–15 minutes to let your heart rate drop and prevent your legs from seizing up.
*   **Eat and Drink:** Get some protein and carbs into your system within 30 minutes of finishing to kickstart muscle repair. 
*   **Take Time Off:** Take at least a full week off from running after a marathon. Do light walking, cycling, or swimming to promote blood flow, but give your joints and muscles a break.
