“Discipleship” is understood differently across Christian traditions, though most agree it involves following Jesus, growing in faith, and learning to live according to his teachings.

Some major perspectives include:

• Evangelical Protestant view
  - Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus and ongoing spiritual growth.
  - Discipleship often involves Bible study, prayer, mentoring, accountability groups, and evangelism.
  - A common theme is helping believers become more like Christ and make new disciples.

• Catholic view
  - Sees discipleship as a lifelong journey within the Church.
  - Emphasizes participation in the sacraments, prayer, moral formation, service, and obedience to Christ.
  - Spiritual direction, saints as models, and communal worship play important roles.

• Eastern Orthodox view
  - Focuses on spiritual transformation (theosis), becoming united with God through grace.
  - Discipleship includes prayer, fasting, participation in the liturgy and sacraments, and guidance from spiritual elders.
  - The emphasis is often on inner spiritual formation and holiness.

• Mainline Protestant view
  - Often highlights both personal faith and social responsibility.
  - Discipleship may involve worship, study, service, justice work, and engagement with societal issues.
  - Following Jesus is seen as having implications for both individual and community life.

• Anabaptist view (e.g., Mennonites)
  - Strong emphasis on actively obeying Jesus' teachings, especially the Sermon on the Mount.
  - Discipleship is expressed through community, peacebuilding, simplicity, and service.
  - Faith is often understood as something demonstrated through daily practice.

• Pentecostal and Charismatic view
  - Emphasizes the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
  - Discipleship includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, personal transformation, and evangelism.
  - Experiential aspects of faith are often given significant importance.

Beyond denominational differences, there are also different theological emphases:

• Knowledge-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on learning doctrine and Scripture.

• Formation-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on habits, character, and spiritual disciplines.

• Mission-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on serving others and spreading the faith.

• Community-centered discipleship
  - Focuses on growth through relationships and participation in a faith community.

• Radical discipleship
  - Emphasizes costly commitment, sacrifice, and living counterculturally in response to Jesus' teachings.

At its broadest, discipleship can be described as the process of learning from and following Jesus, but Christian traditions differ on what aspects of that process deserve the greatest emphasis and how it is best practiced.
