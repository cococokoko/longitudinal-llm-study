── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best techniques for effective online teaching?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney, you are likely accustomed to commanding a room, whether in a courtroom, a boardroom, or a lecture hall. However, the digital environment operates under different psychological and physiological rules. Online, attention spans are shorter, distractions are a click away, and the "energy" of a physical room is absent. 

To translate your expertise effectively to the screen—whether you are teaching law students, training junior associates, or presenting a CLE—you must shift from being a "sage on the stage" to a "facilitator of learning." 

Here are the best, evidence-based techniques for effective online teaching, tailored for a high-level professional context.

---

### 1. Structure for the "Digital Brain" (Chunking & Flipping)
Online learners suffer from rapid cognitive overload. To combat "Zoom fatigue," restructure how you deliver information.

*   **The "Flipped Classroom" Model:** Do not use synchronous (live) video time to read slides or lecture on basic black-letter law. Send reading materials, case briefs, or short pre-recorded video lectures (10–15 minutes) *before* the session. Use the live session exclusively for high-level analysis, Socratic dialogue, and application.
*   **The 15-Minute Rule (Chunking):** Never speak uninterrupted for more than 15 minutes. Break your session into tight modules:
    *   *Minutes 0–15:* Concept introduction/framing.
    *   *Minutes 15–25:* Interactive element (poll, scenario analysis).
    *   *Minutes 25–40:* Deep dive/application.
    *   *Minutes 40–50:* Small group work or Q&A.

### 2. Adapt the Socratic Method for the Screen
The traditional Socratic method can cause paralyzing anxiety online, leading to students turning off their cameras or disconnecting. Modify it to maintain rigor without losing your audience.

*   **"Warm Calling" instead of Cold Calling:** Send a private chat to a student 5 minutes before you call on them: *"Hi Sarah, in a few minutes I’m going to ask you to analyze the holding in the X case. Just giving you a heads-up."* This reduces panic and yields much higher-quality responses.
*   **The "Chat Waterfall" (or Chat Storm):** Ask a question (e.g., *"What is the primary ethical issue in this hypothetical?"*). Instruct everyone to type their answer in the chat box but **not** hit "Enter" until you say "Go." This prevents "groupthink" and forces 100% active participation.
*   **Polling for Hypotheicals:** Present a complex fact pattern and launch a poll with 3–4 potential legal outcomes. Once the results are in, call on students who voted for different options to defend their positions.

### 3. Leverage Collaborative Technology
Passive listening leads to attrition. Force active engagement using digital tools.
