── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Global economic inequalities have a major influence on international relations because differences in wealth, resources, technology, and economic power affect how countries interact, cooperate, and compete.

Some key ways they shape international relations include:

• Power and influence
  - Wealthier countries often have greater political and military influence in global affairs.
  - They can shape international institutions, trade rules, and financial systems to reflect their interests.
  - Economic strength often translates into diplomatic leverage.

• Trade relationships
  - Richer countries tend to export high-value manufactured goods and services, while poorer countries may depend more heavily on exporting raw materials or low-cost labor.
  - This can create unequal trade relationships where developing countries receive a smaller share of the benefits from global commerce.

• Foreign aid and development
  - Wealthier nations and international organizations provide aid, loans, and development assistance to poorer countries.
  - Such support can improve infrastructure, health, and education, but it may also create dependency or give donors political influence.

• Debt and financial dependence
  - Many developing countries rely on international borrowing to finance development.
  - High debt burdens can limit economic growth and affect domestic and foreign policy decisions.
  - International lenders may require economic reforms as conditions for loans.

• Migration
  - Economic disparities encourage migration from lower-income countries to higher-income countries in search of better opportunities.
  - Migration can strengthen economic ties through remittances but may also generate political tensions over immigration policies.

• Conflict and instability
  - Severe economic inequality between and within countries can contribute to political instability, social unrest, and, in some cases, conflict.
  - Economic hardship may increase competition over resources and opportunities.

• Global governance
  - Wealthier states often have greater influence in institutions such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF), World Bank, and G7.
  - Developing countries have argued for reforms that would give them a stronger voice in global decision-making.

• Climate and sustainability issues
  - Economic inequalities affect debates over climate change.
  - Developing countries often argue that wealthier nations, having contributed more to historical emissions, should bear a larger share of the costs of mitigation and adaptation.

Examples:
• China's rapid economic rise has increased its influence in international organizations, trade, and infrastructure investment through initiatives such as the Belt and Road Initiative.
• Disagreements between developed and developing countries in World Trade Organization negotiations often reflect differing economic interests and levels of development.
• Debt crises in countries such as Argentina, Sri Lanka, and others have had significant diplomatic and economic consequences.

In summary, global economic inequalities shape international relations by influencing power balances, trade patterns, development opportunities, migration, and participation in global governance. They are a central factor in both cooperation and conflict among states.
