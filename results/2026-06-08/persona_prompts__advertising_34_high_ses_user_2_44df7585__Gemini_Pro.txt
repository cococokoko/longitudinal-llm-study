── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best techniques for effective online teaching?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a senior attorney, you already possess the core skills of a master teacher: you know how to distill complex information, persuade an audience, and guide people through intricate fact patterns. 

However, translating the gravitas of the courtroom, the boardroom, or the traditional law school lecture hall to an online environment requires a shift in strategy. Online audiences—whether they are junior associates, clients, or peers in a CLE—are highly susceptible to "multitasking" (checking emails or billing time). 

Here are the best techniques for effective online teaching, tailored specifically to your role as a senior legal professional:

### 1. Structure for the Digital Attention Span
Online attention spans are significantly shorter than in-person ones. You cannot rely on a 60-minute uninterrupted lecture.
*   **The "10-Minute Rule" (Chunking):** Break your presentation into 10- to 15-minute modules. After each module, change the stimulus. For example: 10 minutes of explaining a new regulatory framework, followed by a 5-minute hypothetical, followed by a quick Q&A.
*   **Provide a Clear Roadmap:** Just as you would in an appellate argument, tell them exactly where you are going. Use an agenda slide and refer back to it as you transition between topics so participants can track your progress.

### 2. Drive Engagement (The Anti-Multitasking Strategy)
If your audience is passive, they will check their email. You must force active participation.
*   **The "Modified Socratic Method":** Traditional cold-calling can be awkward online due to lag and mute buttons. Instead, use "warm calling" or chat-based Socratic questioning. Present a fact pattern and say, *"Take 60 seconds to spot the primary liability issue here, and type your answer in the chat. Don't hit send until I say go."* This forces everyone to think, rather than waiting for one person to answer.
*   **Use Polling for Issue Spotting:** If you are teaching a CLE or training associates, use Zoom/Teams polling. Give them a scenario and ask them to vote on the likely outcome (e.g., *Motion Granted, Motion Denied, or Need More Facts*). Discuss the results.
*   **Breakout Rooms for Application:** If you are training associates, lecture is less effective than practice. Put them in breakout rooms of 3–4 people for 10 minutes to mark up a problematic indemnification clause or outline a cross-examination. 

### 3. Rethink Your "Exhibits" (Visual Design)
Lawyers are notoriously guilty of "Death by PowerPoint"—pasting entire statutes or paragraphs of case law onto slides. This is fatal in online teaching.
*   **Visuals Over Text:** Your slides should be the *exhibits* to your testimony, not your script. Use timelines for case histories, flowcharts for statutory frameworks, and diagrams for corporate structures.
*   **Highlight the Operative Language:** If you must show text (e.g., a specific contractual provision), put only that sentence on the screen and bold/highlight the three operative words they need to focus on.
*   **Use the Screen as a Whiteboard:** Use a tablet or the annotation tool on your platform to circle, underline, or draw on your slides live. Movement on the screen recaptures wandering eyes.

### 4. Master "Digital Executive Presence"
Your authority and presence must translate through a webcam.
*   **Eye Contact is the Camera Lens:** When you are making a critical point, look directly into the camera lens, not at the faces on your screen. Looking at the lens simulates direct eye contact with the viewer.
*   **Stand Up:** If possible, use a standing desk. Standing opens up your diaphragm, gives your voice more resonance, and naturally injects more energy and authority into your body language.
*   **Pacing and Pausing:** Online audio compresses your voice, and lag can swallow your words. Speak slightly slower than you would in person, and use deliberate, 3-to-4-second pauses after making a complex point
