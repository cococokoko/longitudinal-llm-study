As a partner at a major consulting firm, you already possess a masterclass understanding of market dynamics, competitive strategy, and operational scaling. You are used to solving complex problems for massive organizations. 

However, going from advising Fortune 500s to building a "zero-to-one" small online business requires a very specific gear shift. The challenges you'll face aren't about strategy—they are about scrappy execution, avoiding over-engineering, and managing your highly constrained time.

Here is a playbook for approaching your new online business, tailored to your specific background:

### 1. Beware the "Consultant’s Curse" (Analysis Paralysis)
In your day job, a flawed strategy can cost a client millions, so deep diligence is required. In a small online business, speed to market is your greatest asset. 
* **The Shift:** Trade perfect data for market feedback. Your MVP (Minimum Viable Product) should almost embarrass you. If you spend months building financial models or a 50-page GTM strategy before making your first sale, you are moving too slowly. 
* **The Rule:** Launch the ugliest, simplest version of your offer within 14 to 30 days to test the hypothesis. 

### 2. Leverage Capital to Buy Time
As a partner, your time is your most scarce and valuable resource, but you likely have capital to deploy. Do not spend your weekends figuring out how to integrate Stripe with Shopify or writing SEO copy.
* **Hire an "Operator":** Find a hungry, competent generalist (often a former junior consultant or a sharp startup operator) to act as your fractional GM or project manager. 
* **Outsource the Scrappy Work:** Use platforms like Upwork or specialized agencies to handle web design, ad buying, and copywriting. Your role should be **Capital Allocator and Chief Strategist**, not the technician.

### 3. Choose a "Partner-Friendly" Business Model
Because of your firm commitments, you need a business model that doesn't require you to be on customer support calls at 2 PM on a Tuesday. 
* **High-Ticket B2B / Productized Services:** You already know how to sell to executives. Can you package a specific solution (e.g., specialized data analytics, executive coaching, niche industry reports) into a subscription or flat-fee service?
* **E-commerce / Dropshipping / FBA:** Highly automatable once set up, but requires upfront capital for inventory and ad spend (which you have). 
* **SaaS (Software as a Service):** Great multiples, but requires managing developers. Only go this route if you have a technical co-founder or a highly vetted dev agency.

### 4. Keep the Tech Stack Lean
Do not build custom software unless software *is* the product. Use off-the-shelf, no-code tools to validate your idea.
* **Websites:** Shopify (E-com), Webflow or Framer (Landing pages/Services).
* **Operations:** Airtable or Notion (CRM/Databases), Zapier (Automations).
* **Payments:** Stripe.

### 5. The "Zero-to-One" Framework
Since you appreciate a good framework, here is your immediate roadmap:
1. **Hypothesis:** What is the specific pain point, and who is the target audience? (Keep the niche incredibly narrow to start).
2. **Validation:** Set up a single landing page. Run $500–$1,000 in targeted LinkedIn or Meta ads. See if people actually click and convert (even if it's just capturing emails for a waitlist).
3. **Fulfillment:** Manually fulfill the first 10 orders/clients. Do things that don't scale so you understand the mechanics.
4. **Systematize:** Once you hit 10 sales, document the process (SOPs) and hand it off to your operator.
