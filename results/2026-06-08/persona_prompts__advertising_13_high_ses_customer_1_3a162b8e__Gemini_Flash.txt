### Phase I: Anatomy of the Cut (Pre-Operative Assessment)
Before beginning, you must understand the structural composition of your specimen. For premium results, select a cut at least **1.5 to 2 inches thick**. Thin cuts cook too quickly, making it mathematically difficult to achieve a sear without overcooking the core.

*   **The Ribeye (*Longissimus dorsi* and *Spinalis dorsi*):** Highly marbled with intramuscular fat. The *spinalis* (cap) is highly tender and flavorful. This cut requires higher heat to properly render the abundant fat.
*   **The Filet Mignon (*Psoas major*):** A non-weight-bearing muscle. It has minimal connective tissue and intramuscular fat, making it exceptionally tender but less flavorful. It requires careful temperature monitoring, as it dries out quickly.
*   **The New York Strip (*Longissimus dorsi*):** A fine, uniform grain with a thick lateral fat cap. It offers a balanced compromise between tenderness and rich beef flavor.

---

### Phase II: Pre-Treatment (Dry Brining)
The most critical step occurs 12 to 24 hours before cooking. 

1.  **The Osmotic Gradient:** Liberally salt the steak with kosher salt on all sides and place it on a wire rack set inside a baking sheet in the refrigerator. 
2.  **The Mechanism:** 
    *   Initially, the salt draws moisture out of the meat via osmosis, dissolving into a concentrated brine on the surface.
    *   Over the next several hours, the salt denatures the surface proteins, allowing the muscle fibers to relax and reabsorb the seasoned liquid.
    *   The refrigerator’s fan circulates dry air, desiccating the surface of the meat. **A dry surface is mandatory for the Maillard reaction**; wet surfaces must first boil off their moisture (at 212°F/100°C) before browning can begin, leading to an overcooked interior.

---

### Phase III: The Thermal Strategy (The Reverse Sear)
Traditional cooking methods (searing first, then baking) create a steep thermal gradient: a charred exterior, a thick band of overcooked gray meat, and a small core of target temperature. 

The **Reverse Sear** flips this protocol. By warming the meat slowly first, you achieve a uniform edge-to-edge pink interior with a razor-thin gray band.
