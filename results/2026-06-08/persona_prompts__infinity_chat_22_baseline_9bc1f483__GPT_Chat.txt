Different views on discipleship.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
“Discipleship” generally means being a follower, learner, or student of a teacher or way of life. Different religious traditions, denominations, and theological perspectives understand it in different ways. Here are some major views, especially within Christianity:

Christian Views of Discipleship

1. Evangelical Protestant View
- Emphasizes a personal relationship with Jesus Christ.
- Discipleship involves Bible study, prayer, evangelism, and obedience to Christ.
- Often focuses on spiritual growth through mentoring, small groups, and personal devotion.
- Key passage: Matthew 28:19–20 ("make disciples of all nations").

2. Reformed (Calvinist) View
- Sees discipleship as part of God's work of sanctification in believers.
- Strong emphasis on Scripture, doctrine, and participation in the church.
- Growth in holiness is understood as empowered by God's grace rather than human effort alone.

3. Wesleyan/Methodist View
- Highlights ongoing spiritual transformation and holiness.
- Encourages practices such as prayer, accountability groups, service, and works of mercy.
- Stresses cooperation with God's grace in becoming more Christlike.

4. Roman Catholic View
- Discipleship is lived through participation in the Church and the sacraments.
- Involves prayer, moral formation, service, and growth in virtue.
- Saints are often viewed as models of mature discipleship.

5. Eastern Orthodox View
- Focuses on the process of theosis (union with God or participation in divine life).
- Emphasizes spiritual disciplines, liturgy, prayer, fasting, and guidance from spiritual elders.
- Discipleship is understood as transformation into Christ's likeness.

6. Anabaptist View
- Stresses practical obedience to Jesus' teachings.
- Emphasizes community, peace, simplicity, and nonviolence.
- Discipleship is often seen as visible and communal rather than primarily doctrinal.

7. Pentecostal and Charismatic View
- Focuses on the work of the Holy Spirit in empowering believers.
- Includes spiritual gifts, prayer, worship, and evangelism.
- Discipleship often emphasizes spiritual experience alongside biblical teaching.

Academic and Theological Perspectives

1. Discipleship as Apprenticeship
- Popular among some contemporary writers.
- Sees discipleship as learning to live as Jesus lived through intentional practice and imitation.

2. Discipleship as Formation
- Focuses on shaping character, habits, and identity.
- Draws attention to how communities and practices form people over time.

3. Discipleship as Mission
- Emphasizes participation in God's work in the world.
- Following Jesus includes social action, justice, evangelism, and service.

Other Religious Traditions

- In Judaism, discipleship may resemble the relationship between a student and a rabbi, centered on learning and living Torah.
- In Islam, discipleship-like relationships exist between students and religious scholars or spiritual masters, particularly in Sufism.
- In Buddhism, discipleship often involves following the teachings of the Buddha under the guidance of teachers and monastic communities.

Common Themes Across Many Views
- Learning from a teacher or tradition.
- Personal transformation.
- Participation in a community.
- Practice and obedience, not merely acquiring knowledge.
- Passing teachings on to others.

The differences usually center on questions such as:
- How transformation happens (grace, effort, sacraments, spiritual disciplines, etc.).
- The role of the church or community.
- The importance of doctrine versus practice.
- Whether discipleship is primarily personal, communal, spiritual, or missional.
