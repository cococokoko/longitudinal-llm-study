Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive can be particularly challenging because the sources of pressure are often persistent rather than temporary: high-stakes decisions, organizational responsibility, competing demands, uncertainty, and limited time to recover.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Distinguish stress from anxiety.
  - Stress is often a response to identifiable demands.
  - Anxiety often involves anticipation of future problems or outcomes.
  - Different causes benefit from different responses. For stress, workload and prioritization matter. For anxiety, uncertainty management and cognitive strategies often matter more.

- Reduce decision load.
  - Identify which decisions truly require your attention.
  - Delegate or systematize recurring decisions.
  - Reserve peak cognitive hours for the highest-impact work.

- Create structured recovery periods.
  - Short breaks during the day can improve performance more than continuous effort.
  - Protect sleep aggressively; chronic sleep loss amplifies both stress and anxiety.
  - Regular exercise is one of the most evidence-supported interventions for reducing anxiety symptoms.

- Limit unproductive rumination.
  - If you find yourself repeatedly thinking about a problem without making progress, convert the concern into an action list.
  - Separate what you can influence from what you cannot.

- Build a trusted sounding board.
  - Many executives benefit from a coach, mentor, therapist, or peer group where they can discuss concerns candidly.
  - Isolation tends to increase anxiety.

- Monitor early warning signs.
  - Irritability, difficulty concentrating, disrupted sleep, increased alcohol use, persistent worry, or emotional exhaustion can indicate that stress is becoming problematic.

- Maintain perspective through metrics.
  - Anxiety often thrives in ambiguity.
  - Define objective indicators of success and review them regularly rather than relying solely on moment-to-moment feelings.

If your stress or anxiety is significantly affecting sleep, decision-making, relationships, or overall functioning, it may be worthwhile to speak with a mental health professional. Many high-performing leaders find that targeted support improves both well-being and effectiveness.
