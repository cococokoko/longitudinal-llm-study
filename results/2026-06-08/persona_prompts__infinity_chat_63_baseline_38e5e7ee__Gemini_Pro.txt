── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Give a numerical example to illustrate the concept of partial derivative.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
To understand a **partial derivative**, think of it as asking: *"If I change exactly ONE ingredient in my recipe by a tiny amount, but keep all the other ingredients exactly the same, how much does the final dish change?"*

Here is a step-by-step numerical example to illustrate this.

### 1. The Setup
Imagine a machine that calculates a score based on two inputs: **$x$** and **$y$**. The formula for the machine is:
**$f(x, y) = x^2 + 2xy$**

Let’s set our starting point at **$x = 2$** and **$y = 3$**.
The baseline score is:
$f(2, 3) = (2)^2 + 2(2)(3) = 4 + 12 =$ **$16$**

Now, we want to find the partial derivatives. We will do this in two ways: using **Calculus** (the exact formula) and using **Numbers** (to prove how it works in reality).

---

### 2. Partial Derivative with respect to $x$
*Concept: How fast does the score change if we tweak $x$, but freeze $y$ at 3?*

**The Calculus Way:**
To find the partial derivative with respect to $x$ (written as $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$), we treat $y$ as a constant number and take the normal derivative of $x$.
