Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cookery comes down to understanding a few core variables: the cut, temperature control, seasoning, and resting. Once those are consistent, excellent results become repeatable.

Key principles

• Start with a quality cut:
  - Ribeye: rich marbling, forgiving.
  - Strip steak (New York strip): good balance of tenderness and beef flavor.
  - Tenderloin/filet: very tender, milder flavor.
  - T-bone/porterhouse: combines strip and tenderloin.
  - Thickness matters. Steaks around 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) are easier to cook precisely.

• Dry the surface:
  - Pat the steak thoroughly dry before cooking.
  - Surface moisture inhibits browning and crust formation.

• Salt appropriately:
  - Salt at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before it goes into the pan.
  - Salting during the intermediate period can temporarily draw moisture to the surface.

• Use high heat for browning:
  - The flavorful crust comes from the Maillard reaction.
  - Cast iron, carbon steel, a hot grill, or a broiler all work well.

Temperature targets

Internal temperature is more reliable than cooking time.

• Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
• Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
• Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
• Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)
• Well done: 160°F+ (71°C+)

Remove the steak about 5°F (3°C) below your target because carryover cooking continues during resting.

Pan-searing method

1. Bring steak close to room temperature for 20–30 minutes if convenient.
2. Dry thoroughly and season.
3. Heat a heavy skillet until very hot.
4. Add a high-smoke-point oil.
5. Sear 1–3 minutes per side depending on thickness.
6. For thicker steaks, reduce heat or finish in a 375–425°F (190–220°C) oven.
7. Add butter, garlic, and herbs near the end and baste.
8. Rest 5–10 minutes before slicing.

Reverse-sear method

Particularly effective for thick steaks.

1. Cook steak in a low oven (225–275°F / 107–135°C) until it is about 10–15°F (6–8°C) below target temperature.
2. Rest briefly if desired.
3. Sear in an extremely hot pan or grill for 45–90 seconds per side.
4. Serve after a short rest.

This method often produces the most even internal doneness.

Grilling tips

• Create two heat zones: one hot, one cooler.
• Sear over direct heat, then move to indirect heat if needed.
• Keep the lid closed as much as possible.
• Use a thermometer rather than relying solely on timing.

Common mistakes

• Cooking by time alone.
• Not drying the steak before cooking.
• Flipping too infrequently or too often out of concern—either can work if temperature is monitored.
• Cutting immediately after cooking.
• Using a dull knife for serving.

Advanced refinements

• Dry-brining: Salt the steak uncovered in the refrigerator for 12–24 hours.
• Compound butter: Finish with butter mixed with herbs, garlic, shallots, or blue cheese.
• Dry-aged beef: Provides more concentrated flavor and a different texture.
• Sous vide followed by searing: Offers maximal temperature precision.

A useful mindset is to approach steak as a temperature-management problem rather than a timing problem. Much as in surgery, measurement and control generally outperform estimation when precision matters. A good instant-read thermometer is often the single tool that most improves consistency.
