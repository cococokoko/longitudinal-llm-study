Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak is a rite of passage for any home cook. It transforms a simple piece of meat into a culinary experience. To achieve steakhouse-quality results at home, you need to understand the process from the butcher counter to the cutting board. 

Here is your comprehensive guide to mastering the art of cooking steak.

---

### Phase 1: The Selection (Choosing Your Canvas)
You cannot cook a great steak if you start with bad meat. 
*   **The Cut:** 
    *   *Ribeye:* The king of flavor. High fat content (marbling) makes it rich and forgiving to cook.
    *   *New York Strip:* A great balance of beefy flavor and tenderness, with a tight texture.
    *   *Filet Mignon:* The most tender cut, but very lean. Mild in flavor; often paired with sauces or bacon.
    *   *Sirloin:* Lean, budget-friendly, and very beefy. Best cooked no more than medium-rare.
*   **Thickness:** Always aim for a steak that is **1.5 to 2 inches thick**. Thin steaks will overcook in the center before a good crust can form on the outside.
*   **Marbling:** Look for thin, white webs of fat running *through* the red meat. This intramuscular fat melts during cooking, basting the steak from the inside out.

### Phase 2: The Preparation (The Secret to the Crust)
How you treat the steak before it hits the heat dictates the final result.
*   **Dry Brining (Crucial Step):** Salt your steak generously with coarse Kosher salt 1 to 24 hours before cooking, and leave it uncovered in the fridge. The salt draws out moisture, dissolves, and is reabsorbed, seasoning the meat deeply and tenderizing it. 
*   **Bone-Dry Surface:** Moisture is the enemy of a good sear. If water is on the surface of the steak, the heat will create steam instead of a crust. Before cooking, pat the steak aggressively dry with paper towels.
*   **Pepper Later (Optional):** Black pepper can burn at high searing temperatures, turning bitter. Consider adding your pepper *after* the sear, or right at the end of cooking.

### Phase 3: The Cooking Methods
There are two foolproof methods for cooking a thick steak.

#### Method A: The Classic Cast-Iron Pan Sear (Best for steaks under 1.5 inches)
1.  Heat a cast-iron skillet until it is smoking hot. 
2.  Add a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, or grapeseed—*not* extra virgin olive oil).
3.  Lay the steak in the pan (away from you so oil doesn't splash). Sear for 2-3 minutes per side until a deep brown crust forms (the Maillard reaction).
4.  **Butter Basting (Arroser):** In the last minute of cooking, drop the heat to medium. Add 2 tablespoons of butter, a crushed garlic clove, and a sprig of fresh rosemary or thyme. Tilt the pan and use a spoon to continuously bathe the steak in the foaming, aromatic butter.

*This method guarantees a perfectly even pink center from edge to edge.*
