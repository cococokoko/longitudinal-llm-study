── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Understanding what you want is often less about finding a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experiences.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice what energizes you.
  - After activities, conversations, projects, or days, ask:
    - What left me feeling engaged?
    - What made me lose track of time?
    - What made me curious enough to keep going?
  - Energy is often a better clue than achievement.

- Pay attention to recurring desires.
  - Many wants come and go. Some keep returning for months or years.
  - If an idea, goal, lifestyle, or interest repeatedly comes back despite being ignored, that's worth investigating.

- Distinguish between your wants and others' expectations.
  - Ask:
    - If nobody would judge me, what would I choose?
    - If nobody would praise me, what would I still choose?
  - Sometimes we mistake approval for desire.

- Use experiments instead of introspection alone.
  - You can think about a career, relationship, hobby, or move for years without clarity.
  - Small tests often reveal more:
    - Take a class.
    - Volunteer.
    - Go on a date.
    - Build a prototype.
    - Spend a weekend living as if you've already made the choice.

- Look at moments of envy.
  - Envy can be uncomfortable, but it often points toward something you value.
  - Ask: What specifically am I envious of? Freedom? Recognition? Creativity? Community?

- Imagine different futures.
  - Picture yourself five years from now on two different paths.
  - Which future creates relief?
  - Which creates regret?
  - Strong emotional reactions can reveal preferences you haven't fully acknowledged.

- Accept that wanting can be unclear.
  - Many people assume they should know exactly what they want before acting.
  - Often the sequence is the opposite: act, learn, refine, repeat.

A useful question is: "What decision am I trying to understand what I want about?" The answer can look very different if you're thinking about career, relationships, where to live, identity, or something else.
