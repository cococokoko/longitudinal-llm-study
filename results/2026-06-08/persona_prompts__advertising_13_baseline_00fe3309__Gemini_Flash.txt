── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak is one of the most rewarding culinary skills you can acquire. It is a perfect blend of science, technique, and sensory intuition. Whether you want a crusty, butter-basted ribeye or a perfectly edge-to-edge pink strip steak, this guide will take you from novice to steak master.

---

### Phase 1: The Foundation (Selection)
Great steak starts at the butcher counter. No amount of culinary technique can save a poor piece of meat.

*   **The Cut:**
    *   **Ribeye:** Highly marbled with fat. Extremely flavorful, juicy, and forgiving. Best for pan-searing or grilling.
    *   **New York Strip:** A great balance of deep beefy flavor and tenderness.
    *   **Filet Mignon:** Incredibly tender, but lean. It has less flavor, so it benefits greatly from butter-basting or sauces.
    *   **T-Bone / Porterhouse:** Best of both worlds (Filet on one side, Strip on the other), but tricky to cook evenly because the two sides cook at different rates.
*   **Thickness:** Always buy steaks that are **at least 1.5 inches thick**. Thin steaks will overcook in the center before you can get a good crust on the outside.
*   **Marbling (The White Flecks):** Look for steaks with lots of white flecks of fat distributed throughout the meat (intramuscular fat), not just a strip of fat on the edge. This is what makes the steak juicy and flavorful. Look for USDA Prime (best) or USDA Choice.

---

### Phase 2: The Prep (Crucial Steps)
Before the steak touches the heat, you must prepare it.

1.  **Bring to Room Temp:** Take the steak out of the fridge **30–45 minutes before cooking**. A cold steak will drop the pan's temperature and cook unevenly.
2.  **Dry It Completely:** Moisture is the enemy of a good crust. Pat the steak completely dry with paper towels.
3.  **Season Generously:** Use coarse Kosher salt and freshly cracked black pepper. Season all sides, including the edges.
    *   *Pro Tip (Dry Brining):* If you have time, salt your steak 12 to 24 hours in advance and leave it uncovered on a wire rack in the fridge. This draws moisture out, concentrates the flavor, and dries the surface for an incredible crust.

---

### Phase 3: The Cooking Techniques
There are two champion methods for cooking steak at home.

#### Method A: The Classic Pan-Sear & Butter Baste (Best for steaks under 1.5" thick)
This is the French bistro method. It yields a deep crust and a rich, savory flavor.

*   **The Gear:** A heavy cast-iron skillet or stainless steel pan. (Do not use non-stick; it cannot handle the heat).
*   **The Oil:** Use a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil). **Do not use extra virgin olive oil or butter to start**—they will burn.

1.  Heat your skillet over high heat until the oil is shimmering and just starting to smoke.
2.  Lay the steak in the pan *away* from you so oil doesn't splash.
3.  Sear for about 2 minutes without moving it to build a crust. Flip.
4.  **The Baste (Arrosé):** Once flipped, turn the heat down to medium. Add 2–3 tablespoons of unsalted butter, a few smashed cloves of garlic, and a sprig of rosemary or thyme to the pan.
5.  Tilt the pan slightly and use a large spoon to continuously scoop the melted, foaming butter over the top of the steak. This cooks the steak gently and infuses it with rich flavor.

#### Method B: The Reverse Sear (Best for thick cuts, 1.5" to 2"+)
This modern method guarantees a perfect, even pink color from edge to edge with no grey "overcooked"
