── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I work in a warehouse position that requires long hours.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Working long hours in a warehouse is physically and mentally exhausting. When you are doing repetitive, physically demanding work for extended periods, your mind has a lot of time to wander. It is very common for deep, unresolved emotional questions to surface during those long shifts. 

The feeling of not being loved enough by a mother—often referred to in psychology as the "Mother Wound" or childhood emotional neglect—leaves a lasting impact on how we navigate adulthood. 

Here are some common signs that you may not have received the emotional nourishment, validation, or love you needed from your mom as a child:

### 1. How You View Yourself
*   **A harsh inner critic:** You are incredibly hard on yourself. If you make a mistake, your internal voice is punishing rather than forgiving.
*   **Feeling you have to "earn" love:** You might believe that you are only valuable if you are producing, working hard, or doing things for others. *(This often translates into overworking—like taking on grueling hours—because you subconsciously feel your worth is tied to your output).*
*   **A deep sense of being fundamentally flawed:** A lingering, quiet feeling that there is something "wrong" with you, or that if people truly knew you, they wouldn't love you.
*   **Hyper-independence:** You refuse to ask for help, even when you are drowning. You learned early on that you could only rely on yourself, so you carry the weight of the world on your own shoulders.

### 2. How You Handle Relationships
*   **People-pleasing:** You constantly put others' needs before your own to keep the peace or to make people like you, often at the expense of your own physical and mental health.
*   **Fear of abandonment:** You might cling tightly to relationships, or conversely, push people away before they can leave you. 
*   **Difficulty setting boundaries:** You have a hard time saying "no." You might tolerate poor treatment from friends, romantic partners, or bosses because you weren't taught what healthy, unconditional love looks like.
*   **Attracting emotionally unavailable people:** We often subconsciously seek out what is familiar. If your mother was emotionally distant, you might find yourself chasing partners or friends who are also distant, trying to finally "win" their love.

### 3. How You Process Emotions
*   **Feeling like a burden:** You hide your true feelings, struggles, or needs because you feel like expressing them will annoy or push people away.
*   **Difficulty identifying your emotions:** If your feelings weren't validated as a child, you might struggle as an adult to know what you are actually feeling. You might just feel a general sense of "numbness" or underlying anxiety.
*   **Extreme reactions to criticism:** Because your self-esteem wasn't built on a solid foundation of unconditional maternal love, even constructive criticism can feel like a devastating personal attack.

### 4. Your Relationship with Your Mother Now
*   **Complicated grief:** You might feel a sense of grief or emptiness when you see other people having warm, loving relationships with their mothers.
*   **Guilt:** You might feel guilty for being angry at her, especially if she provided for you physically (food, shelter) but neglected you emotionally. 
*   **Seeking a "surrogate" mother:** You might find yourself intensely attaching to older female coworkers, bosses, or friends, subconsciously hoping they will provide the maternal warmth you missed out on.

**A Note on Your Job:**
People who experienced emotional neglect in childhood often grow up to be incredibly hard workers. Because you weren't emotionally taken care of, you learned to survive. Working long, grueling hours in a warehouse requires a massive amount of resilience and grit—traits you likely developed as a survival mechanism in childhood. 

**What to do next:**
If these signs resonate with you, please know that **it is not your fault.** A child is never responsible for the love they do or do not receive. 

Since you work long hours, finding time for yourself is hard, but try to practice self-compassion. When that harsh inner critic speaks up, try to talk to yourself the way you would talk to a good friend. If your employer offers an Employee Assistance Program (EAP), you might be able to get a few free therapy sessions to help you unpack this. Books like *Adult Children of Emotionally Immature Parents* by Lindsay C. Gibson are also incredible resources that you could listen to as an audiobook during your shifts or commutes. 

Take care of yourself out there on the warehouse floor. Acknowledging these feelings is the first, and often hardest, step toward healing.
