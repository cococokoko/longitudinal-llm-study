── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is one of the most profound and difficult challenges in life. Often, we struggle to answer this question because our own desires are buried under the expectations of our parents, society, or our own fears.

To understand what you want, you have to shift from trying to "think" your way to an answer, to "experimenting" your way there. Here is a step-by-step framework to help you uncover your true desires.

---

### Phase 1: Clear the Noise
Before you can hear your own voice, you have to quiet the voices of others. 

*   **Audit your "Shoulds":** Make a list of everything you think you *should* be doing. Then, ask yourself: *Whose voice is telling me this?* Is it your parents? Social media? Societal pressure? Cross out the things that don't actually belong to you.
*   **Stop looking at the finish line:** We often paralyze ourselves by trying to figure out what we want for the *rest of our lives*. You don't need to know what you want forever. You only need to know what you want **next**.

### Phase 2: Ask Better Questions
"What do I want?" is often too big and abstract. Try asking your brain these backdoor questions instead:

*   **What do I absolutely NOT want?** (The Inversion Principle). It is usually much easier to identify what makes us miserable. Do you hate commuting? Do you hate working alone? Do you hate cold weather? By eliminating what you don't want, you narrow down the options of what you *do* want.
*   **What kind of pain am I willing to tolerate?** Every dream job, relationship, or lifestyle comes with a struggle. If you want to be an entrepreneur, you must tolerate financial uncertainty. If you want to be an artist, you must tolerate rejection. What flavor of "hard" is worth it to you?
*   **Who am I jealous of?** Envy is an incredibly accurate compass. We don't get jealous of people who have things we don't care about. If you feel a pang of jealousy toward a friend who travels constantly, or someone who just bought a house, or someone who started a bakery—pay attention. Your envy is pointing directly at a hidden desire.
*   **When do I lose track of time?** Think about the activities that put you in a "flow state," where hours pass like minutes. This points to your natural curiosities and passions.

### Phase 3: Actionable Exercises
Grab a pen and paper and try these exercises.

**1. The "Perfect Average Tuesday" Exercise**
Don't imagine a vacation; imagine a normal, random Tuesday five years from now. 
*   What time do you wake up?
*   Where are you living (city, country, suburbs)?
*   Who is in the house with you?
*   What do you do for the first two hours of the day?
*   What kind of work are you doing? Are you managing people, or working alone? 
*   *Focus on the feeling and the routine, not just the glamour.*

**2. The "5 Whys"**
When you think you want something, drill down to the root cause by asking "Why?" five times.
*   *I want to make a lot of money.* (Why?)
*   *Because I want to quit my job.* (Why?)
*   *Because I hate having a boss.* (Why?)
*   *Because I want control over my own schedule.* (Why?)
*   *Because I want to spend more time with my family and hobbies.*
*   **Conclusion:** You don't necessarily want to be a millionaire; you want autonomy and time freedom. There are many ways to get that without becoming a millionaire.

### Phase 4: Experimentation (The Most Important Step)
**You cannot think your way into clarity; you have to act your way into it.** 

Clarity comes from collision. You have to collide with the real world to see how you feel.
