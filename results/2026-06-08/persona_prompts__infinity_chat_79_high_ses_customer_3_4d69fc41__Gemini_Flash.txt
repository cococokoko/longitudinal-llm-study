### Project Title: 
**Development of a Circular-Economy Biosorbent from Vietnamese Agricultural Waste (Rice Husks/Coffee Grounds) for Heavy Metal Remediation, Coupled with Smartphone-Based Colorimetric Quantification.**

### The Vietnam Context
1. **The Waste Problem:** Vietnam is one of the world’s largest producers of rice (Mekong and Red River Deltas) and coffee (Central Highlands). This generates millions of tons of agricultural waste (rice husks and spent coffee grounds) annually, much of which is openly burned, contributing to air pollution.
2. **The Water Problem:** Groundwater in the Red River and Mekong Deltas is notoriously contaminated with naturally occurring arsenic and anthropogenic heavy metals (such as chromium and lead from industrial runoff), posing severe health risks to rural communities relying on tube wells.

### The Core Scientific Concept
This project investigates the **biosorption** capacity of chemically modified agricultural waste to remove heavy metals from water. To make the project truly "incredible" and self-contained for a STEM fair, it couples environmental chemistry with a **low-cost optical detection system** using a smartphone camera to quantify filtration efficiency.

---

### Experimental Design & Methodology

#### Phase 1: Green Synthesis of the Biosorbent (The Chemistry)
The student will compare the efficacy of two abundant Vietnamese waste products: **spent coffee grounds (SCG)** and **rice husks (RH)**.
* **Surface Modification:** Raw biomass has limited adsorption capacity. The student will functionalize the surface of the biomass to increase active binding sites. 
  * *Method:* Treat the biomass with a weak organic acid (like citric acid, easily sourced) or coat it with iron oxide nanoparticles synthesized from household steel wool and vinegar. This introduces carboxyl groups or iron oxides that have a high affinity for heavy metal ions (like Copper $Cu^{2+}$ or Iron $Fe^{3+}$, which are safe to handle in a school lab setting as proxies for arsenic/lead).

#### Phase 2: The Filtration System (The Engineering)
* Build a simple, gravity-fed column filtration system using upcycled plastic bottles.
* Pack the columns with varying ratios of sand, gravel, and the modified biosorbent.
* **Variables to test:** Contact time (flow rate), pH of the influent water, and the ratio of biosorbent to sand.

#### Phase 3: Smartphone-Based Colorimetric Quantification (The Physics/CS)
Instead of relying on expensive laboratory spectrophotometers (like ICP-MS), the student will build a **closed-box smartphone spectrophotometer** to measure concentration changes.
* **The Setup:** A small cardboard box with a fixed smartphone mount, a cheap LED light source, and a slot for a cuvette (or clear glass vial).
* **The Chemistry:** Use a safe colorimetric indicator. For example, if testing Copper ($Cu^{2+}$) as the target pollutant, adding household ammonia turns the solution a deep blue. The intensity of the blue is proportional to the copper concentration (Beer-Lambert Law).
* **The Analysis:** The student can write a simple Python script or use a free color-picker app (like *Color Grab*) to extract the RGB/HSV values of the solution. By plotting the Red channel intensity against known calibration standards, they can construct a highly accurate calibration curve to calculate the percentage of metal removed by their filter.
