For those of us who spend our lives navigating the delicate bridge between the laboratory bench and the patient’s bedside, cinema’s depiction of science is usually a source of mild irritation. Justine Triet’s *Double-Blind* is the magnificent, anxiety-inducing antidote to those tropes. It is, without question, the most authentic and gripping portrayal of academic medicine and clinical research ever put to film.

The film stars Frances McDormand as Dr. Evelyn Vance, a formidable physician-scientist leading a translational oncology lab at a prestigious, cash-strapped university. The thriller elements of *Double-Blind* do not rely on rogue viruses or sci-fi mutations. Instead, the antagonist is the agonizing reality of the scientific process itself. Triet captures the claustrophobic pressure of lab culture with documentary-like precision. The sound design is a symphony of humming centrifuges, pipetting clicks, and the low, tense murmur of lab meetings. McDormand is spectacular, capturing the unique exhaustion of a PI who must simultaneously play the roles of visionary researcher, compassionate clinician, mentor, and fundraiser. What makes *Double-Blind* a masterpiece, however, is its refusal to offer easy moral victories. It understands that in high-stakes research, the line between "data cleaning" and "data manipulation" can sometimes be blurred by the desperate desire to do good.
