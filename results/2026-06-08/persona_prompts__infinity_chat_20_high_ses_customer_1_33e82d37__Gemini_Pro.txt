Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
When economists argue against imposing strict "equal pay for equal work" mandates on an economy, they are generally not arguing against the moral principle of fairness. Rather, they are pointing out the severe economic inefficiencies, market distortions, and unintended consequences that arise when a central authority tries to enforce a standardized wage metric. 

Given your vantage point as a neurosurgeon in a major teaching hospital, the economic arguments against these mandates can be clearly illustrated through the realities of highly specialized medicine.

Here is why many economists argue that imposing strict equal pay mandates is detrimental to an economy:

**1. The Impossibility of Defining "Equal Work"**
In a strict regulatory environment, "equal work" is usually defined by job title, hours worked, and basic qualifications. But as you know from your own department, two people with the exact same title rarely deliver the exact same value. 

Imagine two attending neurosurgeons. Both work 60 hours a week. However, Surgeon A takes on highly complex, high-risk skull-base tumors, mentors three residents, brings in NIH grant funding, and has a complication rate of 1%. Surgeon B handles routine elective spine surgeries, does not publish, and has a complication rate of 4%. 

Under a strict "equal pay for equal work" mandate based on title and hours, a hospital might be legally forced to pay them identically to avoid litigation. This ignores Relative Value Units (RVUs), case mix index, academic contributions, and patient outcomes. In the broader economy, mandating equal pay strips away the ability to price-in these crucial, nuanced differences in human capital and output.

**2. Destruction of Incentives and Meritocracy**
If compensation is legally locked to a job description rather than individual performance, the incentive to excel diminishes. If Surgeon A knows they will be paid the exact same as Surgeon B regardless of their extra effort, they may stop taking on the highest-risk cases, stop staying late for emergency craniotomies, or stop pursuing innovative research. 

In the wider economy, when exceptional performance cannot be rewarded with exceptional pay due to parity mandates, overall productivity stagnates. Innovation and efficiency are driven by the promise of disproportionate reward for disproportionate value creation.

**3. The Ignorance of "Compensating Differentials"**
Economists use the term *compensating differentials* to explain why people accept different pay for seemingly similar jobs based on non-monetary factors. 

For example, a neurosurgeon in a private practice in a rural area might make significantly more money than you do at a major teaching hospital. However, you might accept lower pay in exchange for the prestige of the institution, the intellectual stimulation of academic medicine, the support of residents and fellows, and access to cutting-edge technology. 

If a government mandates equal pay across an industry, it destroys the market's ability to offer these trade-offs. Employers who offer great benefits, flexible hours, or low-stress environments would be forced to pay the same cash wages as employers who offer grueling, high-stress environments, leading to massive labor misallocations.

**4. Unintended Consequences for Vulnerable Workers**
When you impose a price floor or a wage mandate, employers become hyper-selective. If a business is forced to pay a uniform high rate for a specific role, they will only hire the absolute most experienced, lowest-risk candidates. 

This locks out younger, less experienced, or non-traditional workers who might otherwise compete for a job by offering to work for slightly less while they build their skills. In this way, equal pay mandates can actually increase unemployment among the very demographics they are often intended to protect.

**5. Compliance Costs and Defensive Management**
Just as the threat of malpractice lawsuits leads to "defensive medicine" (ordering unnecessary scans to cover one's bases), the threat of wage-disparity lawsuits leads to "defensive management." 

To comply with equal pay mandates, companies often create rigid, bureaucratic pay scales. They eliminate flexible work arrangements, bonuses, and customized compensation packages because any deviation from the standard formula could trigger a lawsuit. The administrative burden of proving that every wage difference is justified by a state-approved metric drains resources away from patient care, R&D, and actual economic growth.

**Summary**
The argument against imposing "equal pay for equal work" is not an argument for discrimination. It is an acknowledgment that human labor—especially at the highest levels of skill and specialization—is incredibly complex. A blunt legislative mandate cannot accurately measure the difference in value between two workers, and attempting to do so forces the market into a rigid, inefficient structure that ultimately stifles excellence and productivity.
