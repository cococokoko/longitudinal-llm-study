── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
It takes a lot of courage to ask a question like this, especially when you are already dealing with the physical and mental exhaustion of working long hours in a demanding warehouse job. 

There is actually a strong connection between the type of work we do—and how we treat ourselves at work—and the emotional wounds we carry from childhood. When a child does not receive adequate love, warmth, or validation from their mother (often called "maternal deprivation" or "under-mothering"), it shapes how they view their own worth as an adult.

Here are the common signs that a person did not receive enough love or emotional safety from their mother, categorized by how they show up in daily life, relationships, and even in your career.

---

### 1. Your Relationship with Work and Productivity
Since you mentioned your demanding warehouse job, it is highly relevant to look at how childhood wounds affect your work ethic:
*   **Tying Your Worth to Your Labor:** You feel that you only have value when you are producing, working, or exhausting yourself. If you aren't working, you feel guilty or anxious. You may feel you have to "earn" your right to exist through hard labor.
*   **Difficulty Resting:** When you finally have a day off, you can’t relax. Rest feels like "laziness" rather than a basic human need. This is often because, as a child, you were only noticed or praised when you were being "useful" or quiet.
*   **Accepting Poor Treatment:** You might tolerate harsh working conditions, long hours, or low appreciation because, deep down, you are used to your physical and emotional needs being ignored. 

### 2. Your Relationship with Yourself
*   **A Harsh Inner Critic:** You have a constant voice in your head telling you that you aren’t doing enough, you aren’t good enough, or that you are a failure. This voice is often the internalized version of an emotionally absent or critical mother.
*   **Chronic Self-Doubt:** You struggle to trust your own decisions, feelings, or perceptions. You constantly look to others to tell you if you are doing a good job.
*   **Feeling Like an "Imposter":** Even when you succeed, you feel like a fraud. You feel that if people *really* knew you, they would reject you.

### 3. Your Relationship with Others
*   **Hyper-Independence:** You have a "me against the world" mentality. You refuse to ask for help, even when you are drowning in work or emotional stress. You believe that asking for help makes you weak or a burden, because when you needed help as a child, no one was there.
*   **People-Pleasing:** You go out of your way to make others happy, often at the expense of your own health and boundaries. You fear that if you say "no," people will abandon you.
*   **Fear of Intimacy or Abandonment:** You might find it hard to let people get close to you. You might think, *“If I let them see the real me, they will leave, just like my mother did (emotionally or physically).”*

### 4. Emotional Patterns
*   **Emotional Numbness:** You might find it hard to feel joy, or you might suppress your anger and sadness. In a demanding job, this might look like "powering through" physical pain or emotional exhaustion without acknowledging it.
*   **An "Empty" Feeling:** A persistent, low-grade feeling of loneliness or emptiness that doesn't go away, even when you are around other people.
*   **Difficulty Identifying Your Needs:** If someone asks you, "What do you need right now?" you might genuinely not know. Because your needs were ignored in childhood, you learned to disconnect from them entirely.

Physical exhaustion can sometimes be a way to distract ourselves from emotional pain. It is easier to say, *"I am tired because of my 12-hour shift,"* than to say, *"I am tired because I feel unloved and alone."*
