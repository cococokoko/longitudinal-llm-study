── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Healthy meal planning on a budget.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Eating healthy on a budget is entirely possible, but it requires a shift in how you plan, shop, and cook. The secret is minimizing food waste, relying on inexpensive staple ingredients, and doing a little bit of prep work. 

Here is a comprehensive guide to healthy meal planning on a budget.

---

### Phase 1: The Planning Stage (Before You Shop)
The most money is saved before you even step foot in the grocery store.

*   **Shop your pantry first:** Before writing a list, look in your fridge, freezer, and pantry. If you have half a bag of rice and some frozen broccoli, plan a meal around those to avoid buying new ingredients.
*   **Plan around sales:** Check your local grocery store’s weekly flyer (usually available online or via apps like Flipp). If chicken thighs or bell peppers are on sale, make them the star of your meals that week.
*   **Embrace "Theme Nights" or Repetition:** You don’t need 7 different breakfasts and 7 different dinners. Eat oatmeal every morning, and plan to eat dinner leftovers for lunch the next day. 
*   **Make a strict list:** Once you have your meals planned, write down exactly what you need. When you go to the store, **do not deviate from the list.**

### Phase 2: The Shopping Strategy
How you navigate the store dictates how much you spend.

*   **Buy frozen fruits and veggies:** Frozen produce is picked at peak ripeness and flash-frozen, meaning it is just as nutritious as fresh. It’s also significantly cheaper and will never rot in your crisper drawer.
*   **Choose store brands:** Generic or store-brand items (like canned beans, oats, and spices) are almost always cheaper than name brands and are usually identical in quality and nutrition.
*   **Look high and low:** Grocery stores place the most expensive items at eye level. Look at the top and bottom shelves for cheaper alternatives.
*   **Buy whole foods, not convenience foods:** Pre-chopped vegetables, bagged salads, and instant rice cost a premium. Buy whole carrots, heads of lettuce, and bags of dry rice to save money.

### Phase 3: The Ultimate Budget-Friendly Grocery List
Focus your meals around these cheap, nutrient-dense ingredients:

*   **Proteins:** Eggs, canned tuna/salmon, dried or canned beans, lentils, tofu, and chicken thighs (much cheaper than chicken breasts).
*   **Carbohydrates:** Oats, brown rice, potatoes, sweet potatoes, whole wheat pasta, and quinoa (if bought in bulk).
*   **Fats:** Peanut butter, olive oil, sunflower seeds, and flaxseeds (cheaper than chia or hemp seeds).
*   **Produce (Fresh):** Cabbage, carrots, onions, garlic, bananas, apples, and seasonal vegetables.
*   **Produce (Frozen):** Spinach, broccoli, mixed berries, and peas.

### Phase 4: Cooking & Prepping Hacks
Make your food stretch further.

*   **Stretch your meat:** Meat is usually the most expensive item on a grocery list. If a recipe calls for a pound of ground beef, use half a pound and replace the rest with black beans or finely chopped mushrooms. 
*   **Batch cook:** Make large pots of soups, stews, curries, or chili. These are incredibly cheap to make, freeze beautifully, and provide meals for days.
*   **Save your scraps:** Keep a large Ziploc bag in your freezer. Toss in onion skins, carrot peels, celery tops, and leftover chicken bones. When the bag is full, boil it in water to make free, highly nutritious homemade broth.
*   **Store food properly:** Put a paper towel in your box of leafy greens to absorb moisture and keep them fresh longer. Store potatoes and onions in cool, dark places (but not together, as they make each other spoil faster).

### A Sample 3-Day Budget Meal Plan

**Day 1**
*   **Breakfast:** Oatmeal cooked with milk (or water), topped with a sliced banana and a spoonful of peanut butter.
*   **Lunch:** "Fridge-clearing" bowl: Brown rice topped with canned black beans, frozen corn, salsa, and a fried egg.
*   **Dinner:** Lentil and vegetable soup (made with carrots, onions, celery, and canned diced tomatoes) with a slice of whole-wheat bread.

**Day 2**
*   **Breakfast:** 2 scrambled eggs with a handful of frozen spinach mixed in, plus a piece of toast.
*   **Lunch:** Leftover lentil and vegetable soup.
*   **Dinner:** Baked chicken thighs with roasted sweet potatoes and steamed frozen broccoli.
