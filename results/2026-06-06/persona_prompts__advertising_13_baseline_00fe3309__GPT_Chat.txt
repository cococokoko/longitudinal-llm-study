── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak cooking comes down to understanding a few key variables: the cut, seasoning, heat management, and doneness. Once you control those, you can consistently cook excellent steaks.

1. Choose the Right Cut
Common steak cuts include:
- Ribeye: Richly marbled, flavorful, forgiving to cook.
- New York Strip: Firm texture, beefy flavor.
- Filet Mignon: Extremely tender, leaner.
- T-Bone/Porterhouse: Combination of strip and tenderloin.
- Sirloin: Leaner and more affordable.

For learning, ribeye or New York strip are often the easiest because their fat content helps prevent drying out.

2. Start with a Thick Steak
- Aim for 1 to 1.5 inches (2.5–4 cm) thick.
- Thin steaks cook so quickly that achieving a good crust without overcooking is more difficult.

3. Season Properly
- Salt generously at least 40 minutes before cooking, or immediately before cooking.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.
- Keep it simple while learning: salt and pepper are enough.

4. Bring the Surface Dry
- Pat the steak dry with paper towels.
- A dry surface promotes browning and crust formation.

5. Use High Heat
For pan-searing:
- Preheat a heavy skillet (cast iron is excellent).
- Add a high-smoke-point oil such as avocado, canola, or grapeseed oil.
- The pan should be very hot before the steak goes in.

6. Build a Crust
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.
- Sear 2–4 minutes per side depending on thickness.
- Flip when a deep brown crust forms.

7. Add Butter and Aromatics (Optional)
Near the end:
- Add butter, garlic, and herbs such as thyme or rosemary.
- Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak repeatedly.

8. Use a Thermometer
Temperature is the most reliable indicator of doneness.

Pull the steak from heat at approximately:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

The temperature will continue to rise a few degrees while resting.

9. Rest the Steak
- Rest 5–10 minutes after cooking.
- This helps retain juices when sliced.

10. Learn One Method Well
Pan-sear:
- Great for consistency and crust.

Reverse-sear:
- Cook gently in a low oven until nearly done.
- Finish with a very hot sear.
- Excellent for thick steaks and precise doneness.

Grill:
- Produces smoky flavor and attractive grill marks.
- Requires more attention to heat zones.

Common Mistakes
- Cooking cold, wet steaks.
- Using a pan that isn't hot enough.
- Constantly moving the steak.
- Relying solely on cooking time instead of temperature.
- Skipping the resting period.

A simple beginner workflow:
1. Buy a 1.5-inch ribeye.
2. Salt it 1 hour before cooking.
3. Pat dry.
4. Sear in a hot cast-iron pan with oil.
5. Flip after a strong crust develops.
6. Add butter, garlic, and thyme.
7. Cook until 130°F (54°C) internal temperature.
8. Rest 5–10 minutes.
9. Slice and serve.

After a few steaks cooked with a thermometer, you'll start recognizing the visual and tactile cues that experienced cooks use instinctively.
