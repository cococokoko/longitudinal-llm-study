# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Consider a function representing **intracranial pressure** (a familiar context):

$$f(x, y) = 3x^2 + 2xy + y^3$$

where **x = 2** and **y = 3**.

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### ∂f/∂x (holding y constant)

Treat **y as a fixed constant**, differentiate with respect to x:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6x + 2y$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 6(2) + 2(3) = 12 + 6 = \boxed{18}$$

### ∂f/∂y (holding x constant)

Treat **x as a fixed constant**, differentiate with respect to y:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x + 3y^2$$

**At (2, 3):**

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(2,3)} = 2(2) + 3(9) = 4 + 27 = \boxed{31}$$

---

## Interpretation

| Partial Derivative | Value | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ∂f/∂x = 18 | 18 | f increases by **~18 units** per unit increase in x, *while y stays fixed* |
| ∂f/∂y = 31 | 31 | f increases by **~31 units** per unit increase in y, *while x stays fixed* |

---

## Clinical Analogy

This mirrors how you might think about **cerebral perfusion pressure**:

> CPP = MAP − ICP

If you're adjusting **MAP** (holding ICP constant), you're essentially computing a partial derivative — measuring the rate of change of CPP with respect to **one variable at a time**, while the other is held fixed.

The key insight: **each variable's influence is isolated**, just as you'd isolate one physiological parameter when titrating treatment.
