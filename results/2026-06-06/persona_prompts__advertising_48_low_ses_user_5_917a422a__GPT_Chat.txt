Best practices for organic gardening.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Organic gardening focuses on building healthy soil, encouraging biodiversity, and minimizing synthetic inputs. Some widely recommended practices include:

- Build healthy soil
  - Add compost regularly to improve soil structure and fertility.
  - Use well-rotted manure, leaf mold, or other organic matter when appropriate.
  - Avoid excessive tilling, which can disrupt soil life.

- Choose the right plants
  - Select varieties suited to your climate and growing conditions.
  - Use disease-resistant cultivars when available.
  - Plant at the proper time for your region.

- Practice crop rotation
  - Avoid planting the same plant family in the same location year after year.
  - Rotation can help reduce pest and disease buildup.

- Use mulch
  - Apply organic mulches such as straw, shredded leaves, or wood chips.
  - Mulch helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and regulate soil temperature.

- Water efficiently
  - Water deeply and less frequently rather than shallowly and often.
  - Water near the base of plants to reduce disease risk.
  - Consider drip irrigation or soaker hoses.

- Encourage beneficial insects and wildlife
  - Grow a variety of flowering plants to attract pollinators and natural predators.
  - Provide habitat such as native plants, insect shelters, or bird-friendly areas.

- Manage pests naturally
  - Inspect plants regularly for early signs of problems.
  - Remove heavily infested plant material when necessary.
  - Use physical barriers such as row covers, netting, or collars.
  - Hand-pick larger pests when practical.

- Control weeds proactively
  - Mulch to reduce weed growth.
  - Remove weeds before they set seed.
  - Use cultivation carefully to avoid damaging crop roots.

- Feed plants organically
  - Use compost, organic fertilizers, or soil amendments based on soil needs.
  - Consider a soil test to identify nutrient deficiencies.

- Maintain garden diversity
  - Mix plant species rather than growing large monocultures.
  - Companion planting and interplanting can help create a more resilient garden ecosystem.

- Keep records
  - Track planting dates, varieties, yields, pest issues, and weather conditions.
  - Records help improve decisions from season to season.
