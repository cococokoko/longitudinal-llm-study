Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Managing stress and anxiety as an executive is often different from managing it in other roles because the sources are persistent: responsibility, uncertainty, visibility, high-stakes decisions, and limited recovery time. The goal is usually not to eliminate stress, but to keep it from degrading judgment, health, relationships, and performance.

A few approaches that tend to be effective:

- Separate pressure from anxiety.
  - Pressure comes from real demands and deadlines.
  - Anxiety often comes from anticipated future problems, worst-case scenarios, or a sense of needing to control outcomes that cannot be controlled.
  - When stressed, ask: "What specifically requires action today?" This can reduce mental load by converting vague concern into concrete decisions.

- Protect decision quality.
  - Chronic stress narrows thinking and can increase reactive decision-making.
  - Schedule your most important decisions when your energy is highest.
  - Avoid making major strategic decisions when exhausted, angry, or immediately after a crisis unless timing truly requires it.

- Create structured recovery.
  - Many executives work hard but recover poorly.
  - Recovery is not simply the absence of work; it is activities that reduce cognitive load and physiological arousal.
  - Examples include exercise, time outdoors, hobbies that require attention, meaningful social interaction, and consistent sleep.

- Manage information exposure.
  - Senior leaders can be inundated with problems all day.
  - Consider designated times for reviewing messages, news, dashboards, or escalation reports rather than maintaining constant monitoring.
  - Continuous partial attention can create a persistent state of low-grade anxiety.

- Build a trusted circle.
  - Leadership can be isolating.
  - Having a few people with whom you can discuss uncertainty, mistakes, and concerns candidly can significantly reduce psychological burden.
  - This may include peers, mentors, executive coaches, or close personal relationships.

- Distinguish solvable problems from uncertainties.
  - Solvable problems deserve plans.
  - Uncertainties deserve scenarios and contingency thinking.
  - Repeatedly analyzing uncertain outcomes without new information rarely improves results and often fuels anxiety.

- Maintain physical fundamentals.
  - Sleep quality has an outsized effect on emotional regulation and executive function.
  - Regular exercise can reduce baseline anxiety and improve resilience.
  - Monitor caffeine, alcohol, and other substances if stress levels are high, as they can amplify anxiety in some people.

- Watch for warning signs.
  - Irritability, loss of enjoyment, difficulty concentrating, persistent sleep disruption, increased reliance on alcohol or stimulants, and inability to disconnect from work may indicate stress is becoming harmful rather than productive.

If your stress and anxiety have become persistent, intense, or are affecting sleep, health, relationships, or work performance, speaking with a mental health professional can be valuable. Many high-performing executives find that targeted therapy, coaching, or stress-management interventions improve both well-being and leadership effectiveness.
