── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you're looking for the fastest ways to improve your credit score, focus on the factors that credit scoring models weigh most heavily:

1. Lower your credit card utilization
- Try to keep balances below 30% of each card's limit.
- For the biggest benefit, aim for below 10%.
- If possible, make a payment before your statement closes so a lower balance gets reported.

Example:
- A card with a $1,000 limit and a $700 balance has 70% utilization.
- Paying it down to $100 reduces utilization to 10%, which can help your score relatively quickly.

2. Make every payment on time
- Payment history is one of the most important factors.
- Set up automatic payments or calendar reminders, especially if childcare and work responsibilities make schedules hectic.
- Even one missed payment can significantly hurt your score.

3. Check your credit reports for errors
- Review your reports from the major credit bureaus.
- Dispute any incorrect late payments, accounts you don't recognize, or inaccurate balances.
- Correcting errors can sometimes result in a noticeable score increase.

4. Ask for a credit limit increase
- If your issuer grants a higher limit without a hard credit inquiry, your utilization ratio may drop immediately.
- Avoid increasing spending after the limit increase.

5. Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts
- New applications can generate hard inquiries and lower the average age of your accounts.
- Apply only when necessary.

6. Bring past-due accounts current
- If any accounts are behind, getting them current can prevent further damage.
- If you're struggling, contact the lender to discuss hardship or payment arrangements.

7. Become an authorized user (if available)
- If a trusted family member has a long-standing credit card with a good payment history and low balance, being added as an authorized user may help your credit profile.

How quickly can scores improve?
- Lowering utilization can affect your score as soon as the next reporting cycle (often within 30–60 days).
- Correcting report errors may take several weeks.
- Building a strong payment history usually takes several months.
