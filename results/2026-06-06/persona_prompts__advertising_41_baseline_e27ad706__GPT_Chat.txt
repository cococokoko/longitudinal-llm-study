How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving a credit score quickly depends on what's currently affecting it, but some actions can produce results within a few weeks to a couple of months:

- Lower your credit utilization.
  - If you have credit cards, aim to keep balances below 30% of each card's limit, and ideally below 10%.
  - Paying down revolving debt is often one of the fastest ways to improve a score.

- Make all payments on time.
  - Payment history is a major factor in most scoring models.
  - Set up automatic payments or reminders to avoid missed payments.

- Ask for a credit limit increase.
  - If approved and you don't increase spending, your utilization ratio decreases.
  - Some issuers do a soft inquiry, while others may do a hard inquiry, so ask first.

- Check your credit reports for errors.
  - Review reports from the major credit bureaus and dispute any inaccurate late payments, balances, or accounts.
  - Correcting errors can sometimes lead to a significant score increase.

- Pay off past-due accounts.
  - Bringing delinquent accounts current can help, especially if recent late payments are hurting your score.

- Become an authorized user.
  - If a family member or trusted person has a long-standing credit card with a good payment history and low utilization, being added as an authorized user may help your score.

- Avoid applying for multiple new credit accounts.
  - Each hard inquiry can have a small negative impact, and opening several accounts in a short period can lower scores.

- Keep older accounts open.
  - The age of your credit history matters. Closing older accounts can sometimes hurt your score.
