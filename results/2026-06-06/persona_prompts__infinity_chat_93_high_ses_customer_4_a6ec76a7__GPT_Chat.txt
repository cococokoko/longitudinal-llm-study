Stress is the body’s response to challenges, threats, or demands. When a person encounters a stressful situation, the brain activates the “fight-or-flight” response, triggering the release of hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol. These hormones increase heart rate, blood pressure, and energy availability, helping the body respond quickly to immediate challenges.

In the short term, stress can be beneficial by improving alertness, focus, and performance. However, chronic or excessive stress can have significant effects on both physical and mental health. Persistent elevation of stress hormones may contribute to cardiovascular disease, weakened immune function, sleep disturbances, headaches, digestive problems, and metabolic changes.

Stress also affects the mind. It can impair concentration, memory, and decision-making, particularly when stress is prolonged. Individuals experiencing chronic stress may be at increased risk of anxiety, depression, irritability, and emotional exhaustion. The relationship between stress and health is complex, involving interactions among the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems. Effective stress management strategies—such as regular exercise, adequate sleep, social support, mindfulness practices, and psychological counseling when needed—can help reduce the negative effects of stress and promote overall well-being.
