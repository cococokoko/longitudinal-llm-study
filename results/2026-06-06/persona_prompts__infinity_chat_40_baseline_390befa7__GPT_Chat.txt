── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer here. The best solution depends on how you balance finances, risk, and expectations as a couple.

A key principle: if the house is being purchased solely in your girlfriend's name before you're married, then she is taking ownership risk and building equity. Any money you contribute should be discussed in light of that fact.

Some common approaches:

- Pay rent, like a tenant.
  - She owns the house.
  - You contribute a mutually agreed monthly amount toward living expenses.
  - This is often the cleanest option before marriage.
  - The amount doesn't necessarily have to equal half the mortgage; it can be based on your incomes and local rental values.

- Split household expenses, but not the mortgage principal.
  - For example, you help with utilities, groceries, internet, maintenance, etc.
  - She pays the mortgage.
  - This avoids ambiguity about ownership.

- Contribute to the mortgage in exchange for a formal ownership interest.
  - If you're paying substantially toward the purchase or mortgage, you may want a legal agreement giving you a share of the property.
  - This requires legal documentation and should be handled by a real-estate attorney.

- Wait until marriage and then revisit ownership.
  - Depending on local law, you may later decide to add your name to the title, refinance jointly, or make other arrangements.

Questions worth discussing together:
- How much would you be contributing?
- Are your incomes similar?
- Is she expecting you to help build equity in a house you don't own?
- If you broke up before marriage, what would each of you consider fair?
- If you marry, do you expect the house to remain her separate property or become a shared asset?

A practical middle ground many couples use is: before marriage, the homeowner owns the house and the non-owner pays a reasonable rent or share of expenses. If marriage happens and finances become more integrated, they revisit ownership and equity at that point.
