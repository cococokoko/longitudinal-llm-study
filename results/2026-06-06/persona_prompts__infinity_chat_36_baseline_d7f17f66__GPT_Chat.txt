There is no single "best and most profitable" day trading strategy of all time that works consistently across all markets and periods.

If such a strategy existed and remained reliable, it would attract capital until the edge largely disappeared. Markets are competitive and constantly changing.

That said, some of the most successful day-trading approaches historically have shared a few characteristics:

- They exploit a specific, measurable edge.
- They have strict risk management.
- They are tested on large amounts of data.
- They focus on consistency rather than huge individual wins.

Examples of strategies that have been profitable for some traders and firms:

- Trend-following / momentum
  - Buy assets showing strong upward momentum and sell weak assets.
  - Often used in stocks, futures, and forex.
  - Can produce large gains during strong trends.

- Mean reversion
  - Bet that unusually large short-term moves will partially reverse.
  - Common among quantitative traders.
  - Often works best in range-bound markets.

- Market making
  - Profit from the bid-ask spread while managing inventory risk.
  - Historically one of the most consistently profitable professional trading businesses.
  - Requires technology, capital, and execution advantages.

- Opening range breakout
  - Trade breakouts from the first minutes of the trading session.
  - Popular among discretionary day traders.
  - Works better in volatile markets.

- Statistical arbitrage
  - Use mathematical models to identify temporary pricing inefficiencies.
  - Widely used by hedge funds and proprietary trading firms.
  - Usually requires automation and substantial data analysis.

If your definition of "most profitable of all time" includes professional firms rather than retail traders, quantitative market-making and statistical-arbitrage strategies have likely generated some of the largest cumulative profits over decades.

For an individual retail trader, the most important factor is usually not the specific strategy but:
- Risk management
- Position sizing
- Discipline
- Transaction costs
- Having a verifiable edge backed by testing
