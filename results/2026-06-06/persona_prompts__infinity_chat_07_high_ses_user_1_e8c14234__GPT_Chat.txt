── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes. In any meaningful sense, it is impossible for a human being to know everything.

There are several independent reasons:

- Finite cognitive capacity
  - The amount of information in the world is vastly greater than what a human brain can store, recall, or process.
  - Even if memory were perfect, time is limited. New facts are generated continuously.

- The universe may contain effectively unlimited information
  - Whether the universe is literally finite or infinite is still an open scientific question.
  - If reality contains an infinite number of true facts, then complete knowledge would be impossible for any finite mind.

- Knowledge changes
  - New discoveries occur constantly.
  - Social, biological, geological, and astronomical systems evolve over time. Knowing "everything" would require continuously updating an unbounded set of facts.

- Fundamental limits in physics
  - Quantum mechanics places constraints on what can be simultaneously measured and known with arbitrary precision.
  - There are horizons and information limits in cosmology and thermodynamics.

- Fundamental limits in mathematics and logic
  - Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that sufficiently powerful formal systems contain true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
  - Turing and others demonstrated that there are questions that are not computably decidable in general.

There is also a distinction worth making:

- Knowing every currently known fact in a specialty is difficult but conceivably approachable for a narrow domain.
- Knowing every true fact about medicine is effectively impossible.
- Knowing every true fact about reality is impossible for a finite human mind and may be impossible for any finite system.

As a neurosurgeon, you've likely experienced a practical version of this: the more expertise one acquires, the more visible the boundaries of one's knowledge become. Expertise tends to reveal complexity faster than it eliminates uncertainty. That isn't a failure of learning; it's a consequence of the structure of knowledge itself.
