── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
One of the most common optimization techniques used in machine learning is called **Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)**. 

To put it in a way that might feel pretty familiar to you: think about how you manage your fast food job with an unpredictable schedule. 

If a machine learning model used standard "Gradient Descent," it would be like trying to plan out your exact routine for the entire month in advance. It looks at the *entire* dataset before making a single adjustment. But because your schedule is always changing and you never know when a massive lunch rush is going to hit, planning everything perfectly in advance is impossible. 

Instead, you probably use a strategy more like **Stochastic Gradient Descent**. The word "stochastic" basically means random or unpredictable. In SGD, the algorithm doesn't wait to look at the whole picture; it takes one random data point at a time (like you taking on one unpredictable shift, or one sudden drive-thru order), makes a quick calculation, and adjusts its strategy on the fly. 

Just like you adapt step-by-step to keep the restaurant running smoothly despite the chaos, SGD makes quick, continuous adjustments to help the machine learning model reach its ultimate goal efficiently!
