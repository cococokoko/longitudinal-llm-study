If you're referring to Christian discipleship, different traditions and theologians emphasize different aspects of what it means to be a disciple (a follower of Jesus). Some major perspectives include:

- Conversion-centered discipleship
  - Emphasizes personal faith, repentance, and a relationship with Christ.
  - Common in many evangelical traditions.
  - Focuses on spiritual growth through prayer, Bible study, and evangelism.

- Apprenticeship discipleship
  - Views discipleship as learning to live as Jesus lived.
  - Emphasizes formation of character, habits, and practices.
  - Often associated with authors such as Dallas Willard and John Mark Comer.

- Sacramental discipleship
  - Common in Catholic, Orthodox, and some Anglican traditions.
  - Spiritual growth occurs through participation in the Church's sacramental life, worship, and community.
  - Places strong emphasis on tradition and spiritual formation over a lifetime.

- Social justice and liberation perspectives
  - Emphasize following Jesus through service, justice, and solidarity with the poor and marginalized.
  - Influential in liberation theology and many mainline Protestant traditions.
  - Focuses on both personal transformation and societal change.

- Missional discipleship
  - Sees discipleship and mission as inseparable.
  - Followers of Jesus are called to participate actively in God's work in the world.
  - Emphasizes community engagement, church planting, and evangelism.

- Monastic and contemplative discipleship
  - Focuses on prayer, simplicity, spiritual discipline, and deep communion with God.
  - Draws from traditions such as Benedictine, Carmelite, and Orthodox spirituality.
  - Often values silence, reflection, and intentional community.

- Community-centered discipleship
  - Emphasizes that disciples are formed primarily within a community rather than as isolated individuals.
  - Common in Anabaptist traditions and many contemporary discipleship movements.
  - Highlights accountability, shared practices, and mutual support.

Some key debates among these perspectives include:
- Whether discipleship is primarily personal or communal.
- Whether the emphasis should be on spiritual formation, evangelism, social action, or some combination.
- The role of the Church, sacraments, and tradition in shaping disciples.
- Whether discipleship is best understood as belief, behavior, belonging, or all three.

Many churches today attempt to integrate several of these approaches, combining personal faith, spiritual practices, community life, mission, and service.
