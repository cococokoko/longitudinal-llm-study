── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am an executive at a large technology company.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Many successful executives discover that being effective at getting things makes it harder to know what they actually want.

A common pattern is that your life becomes optimized around:
- Responsibilities
- Expectations
- Opportunities
- Metrics
- Other people's needs

As a result, you become skilled at answering:
- "What should I do?"
- "What is the highest-leverage move?"
- "What is expected of me?"

But not:
- "What do I genuinely want?"

One useful way to approach the question is to separate wants into different layers.

1. Distinguish desire from obligation

For any major area of life, ask:
- If nobody expected anything from me, what would I choose?
- If this opportunity disappeared tomorrow, would I miss it or just its status?
- Am I pursuing this because I want it, or because I have become identified with being the kind of person who pursues it?

Many executives discover that some goals are inherited rather than chosen.

2. Look at your behavior, not your narrative

People often have inaccurate theories about what they want.

Instead, examine:
- What activities absorb your attention?
- What topics do you return to repeatedly?
- What tradeoffs do you consistently make?
- What do you spend discretionary time on when nobody is watching?

Behavior often reveals desires that self-descriptions conceal.

3. Pay attention to envy

Envy is uncomfortable but informative.

When you envy someone, ask:
- What specifically am I envious of?
- Their wealth?
- Their freedom?
- Their influence?
- Their creativity?
- Their family life?
- Their peace of mind?

The answer often points toward a neglected desire.

4. Imagine success and inspect your reaction

Take a goal and imagine that it is fully achieved.

For example:
- You become CEO.
- Your company doubles in value.
- You retire with complete financial security.

Then ask:
- What happens the next morning?
- Does the image feel energizing, empty, relieving, exciting, or constraining?

Many goals are attractive only as pursuits, not destinations.

5. Distinguish means from ends

People frequently want something because they believe it will produce something else.

For example:
- Money → security
- Promotion → recognition
- Recognition → significance
- Control → freedom

Keep asking:
- "And what would that give me?"

Repeat until you reach something that no longer points to another objective.

6. Observe moments of aliveness

Over several months, keep a simple record of moments when you felt:
- Deeply engaged
- Curious
- Proud
- At peace
- Fully yourself

Do not analyze immediately.

Patterns usually emerge:
- Building
- Teaching
- Solving difficult problems
- Mentoring
- Creating
- Leading
- Exploring

Your wants are often embedded in these recurring experiences.

7. Accept that wanting can be ambiguous

Many people assume they should discover one definitive answer.

In reality, mature desires are often tensions:
- Achievement and freedom
- Status and authenticity
- Impact and peace
- Leadership and family
- Growth and stability

The goal is not always to eliminate the conflict. Sometimes it is to understand it clearly enough to make deliberate choices.

One question I have found particularly revealing is:

"If I could no longer use success, achievement, or external validation as evidence that my life was going well, what would convince me that I was living well?"

The answer is rarely immediate, but it often gets closer to what a person actually wants.
