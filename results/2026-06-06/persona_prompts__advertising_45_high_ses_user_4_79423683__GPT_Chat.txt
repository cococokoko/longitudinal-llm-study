── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn DIY home repairs and improvements?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have many transferable skills that help with DIY: troubleshooting, learning technical procedures, working systematically, and respecting safety constraints. The main challenge is usually developing practical experience and tool familiarity.

A structured approach tends to work best:

- Start with a "home systems" framework:
  - Plumbing
  - Electrical
  - HVAC
  - Carpentry
  - Drywall and painting
  - Roofing and exterior maintenance

- Learn the principles before the procedures.
  - Understand how household electrical circuits work.
  - Learn basic plumbing concepts (supply, drain, vent systems).
  - Understand wall construction, framing, insulation, and moisture management.
  - This makes it easier to adapt when your house differs from a tutorial.

Good learning resources:

- Books
  - Home Improvement 1-2-3 (The Home Depot)
  - Black & Decker Complete Guide series
  - Reader's Digest Complete Do-It-Yourself Manual
  - Family Handyman publications

- YouTube channels
  - This Old House
  - HouseImprovements
  - See Jane Drill
  - Essential Craftsman
  - Everyday Home Repairs

- Community resources
  - Adult education classes
  - Community college workshops
  - Maker spaces
  - Habitat for Humanity volunteer builds (excellent hands-on experience)

Build skills progressively:

1. Beginner projects
   - Replace cabinet hardware
   - Patch small drywall holes
   - Caulk bathrooms and kitchens
   - Install shelving
   - Replace faucet aerators
   - Paint a room

2. Intermediate projects
   - Replace faucets
   - Install light fixtures
   - Repair drywall sections
   - Install baseboards and trim
   - Replace toilets
   - Build simple furniture or workbenches

3. Advanced projects
   - Tile work
   - Framing non-structural walls
   - Larger plumbing repairs
   - Electrical circuit additions
   - Deck construction

Develop a tool strategy:

- Buy tools as projects require them rather than purchasing everything upfront.
- Core starter kit:
  - Tape measure
  - Level
  - Utility knife
  - Hammer
  - Screwdriver set
  - Adjustable wrench
  - Pliers
  - Drill/driver
  - Stud finder
  - Flashlight or headlamp

Use a research workflow similar to scientific work:

- Define the problem precisely.
- Read multiple sources.
- Watch several demonstrations.
- Identify failure modes and safety risks.
- Create a step-by-step plan.
- Perform the repair.
- Evaluate the outcome and document what you learned.

Know when not to DIY:

- Service panel electrical work
- Major structural modifications
- Gas line work
- Roofing at significant heights
- Work requiring permits that you do not fully understand

One particularly effective method for analytically minded professionals is to choose one small project every month. Over a year, you accumulate a surprisingly broad skill set without becoming overwhelmed. The combination of repeated small projects and targeted learning usually develops competence faster than trying to master all of home repair theory first.
