You know that the customer is a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving productivity while working from home.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
For a senior attorney working from home, productivity challenges are often less about time management and more about controlling interruptions, prioritizing high-value work, and maintaining responsiveness without becoming reactive.

Some approaches that tend to be effective:

- Time-block substantive legal work
  - Reserve uninterrupted blocks for drafting, strategy, analysis, and complex review.
  - Protect these periods as firmly as you would a client meeting.
  - Schedule email and administrative work around them rather than throughout them.

- Separate urgent from important
  - Not every incoming request deserves immediate attention.
  - Maintain a daily list of:
    - Client emergencies
    - Time-sensitive matters
    - Strategic or long-term matters
  - Review priorities each morning and again mid-afternoon.

- Create communication windows
  - Constant email monitoring can fragment attention.
  - Consider checking email at designated intervals while remaining available for truly urgent issues through a separate channel.

- Establish a dedicated workspace
  - A consistent work environment reduces context switching.
  - If possible, use a separate office with a door, professional video-conferencing setup, and minimal household distractions.

- Structure the day around energy levels
  - Reserve your highest-focus hours for the most demanding legal work.
  - Use lower-energy periods for calls, administrative tasks, billing entries, and routine correspondence.

- Standardize recurring work
  - Develop templates, checklists, and model documents for frequently handled matters.
  - Delegate routine preparation where appropriate and focus personal attention on judgment-intensive work.

- Manage meetings intentionally
  - Decline or shorten meetings that do not require your participation.
  - Request agendas in advance.
  - Batch internal meetings together when possible to preserve larger blocks of uninterrupted work.

- Keep a visible task system
  - Whether using Outlook, a legal practice management platform, or a simple task list, maintain one trusted system.
  - Avoid tracking tasks across multiple disconnected tools.

- Protect transitions
  - Working from home can blur boundaries.
  - A defined start-of-day review and end-of-day planning routine helps maintain continuity and reduces after-hours mental load.

- Measure output, not activity
  - Focus on completed filings, drafted agreements, resolved client issues, business development progress, and other substantive outcomes rather than hours spent appearing available.

A practical framework is to divide each day into three categories:
- Deep legal work
- Client and team communication
- Administrative and business-management tasks

Scheduling each category deliberately often produces greater gains than trying to work faster throughout the day.
