── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Why is equal pay for equal work not worth imposing on an economy?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a question worth examining carefully rather than just accepting the premise.

## The premise deserves scrutiny

"Equal pay for equal work" is actually widely supported across the political spectrum in principle. The real debates are about:

## Legitimate economic concerns raised about implementation

- **Measurement problems**: Defining "equal work" is genuinely difficult - productivity, experience, and output vary individually
- **Wage controls** can create inefficiencies if imposed rigidly
- **Compliance costs** for businesses
- **Unintended consequences** like reduced hiring of protected groups

## But counterarguments are substantial

- Wage discrimination creates **market inefficiencies** by misallocating labor
- It **suppresses productivity** by demotivating workers
- Evidence suggests discrimination **does exist** beyond productivity differences
- Many economists support anti-discrimination enforcement as **correcting market failures**

## What the evidence actually shows

- Uncontrolled pay gaps partly reflect discrimination, partly occupational sorting
- The "controlled gap" (same job, same qualifications) is smaller but **still exists**
- Markets don't always self-correct discrimination efficiently

## Honest assessment

The claim that equal pay laws aren't worth imposing is a **contestable position**, not an established economic consensus. Reasonable people disagree based on values and empirical interpretations.
