Running a marathon successfully is usually more about preparation and pacing than raw fitness. A few practical principles:

Training
- Follow a structured plan that gradually increases mileage over 12–20 weeks.
- Build weekly volume slowly (many runners use a maximum increase of around 10% per week as a rough guideline).
- Include one long run each week to develop endurance.
- Incorporate easier recovery runs; adaptation happens during recovery, not just training.
- Consider one weekly quality session (tempo run, intervals, or marathon-pace work) depending on your experience level.
- Strength training 1–2 times per week can reduce injury risk and improve running economy.

Pacing
- Start conservatively. One of the most common marathon mistakes is running the first half too fast.
- Practice your target marathon pace during training.
- If uncertain, err on the side of a slightly slower start and build into the race.

Nutrition and Hydration
- Test your race-day fueling strategy during long training runs.
- Many runners consume carbohydrates during the race (often in the form of gels, chews, or sports drinks).
- Hydrate consistently, but avoid dramatically overdrinking.
- In the days before the race, focus on adequate carbohydrate intake and hydration rather than trying anything unusual.

Race-Week Preparation
- Taper training volume before the race to arrive rested.
- Prioritize sleep throughout the week, not just the night before.
- Lay out gear, nutrition, and logistics in advance to reduce race-morning stress.

Race-Day Execution
- Stick to a pacing plan, especially early.
- Fuel and hydrate according to your practiced strategy.
- Break the race into smaller segments mentally rather than focusing on all 26.2 miles (42.2 km) at once.
- Expect some discomfort late in the race and prepare mentally for it.

Injury Prevention
- Increase training load gradually.
- Pay attention to persistent pain rather than trying to push through it.
- Rotate shoes if you run high mileage.
- Take recovery seriously: sleep, nutrition, and easy days matter.

For a consultant's schedule specifically:
- Protect the long run as a non-negotiable calendar commitment.
- Use shorter weekday runs efficiently rather than trying to maximize every session.
- Plan travel weeks in advance and identify running routes, hotel treadmills, or alternative workouts.
- Consistency over months is more valuable than occasional heroic training weeks.
