My girlfriend is buying a house. I plan to marry her in a year or so. I don't know if it's fair for me to pay for the house or not. What's the best solution?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn't one universally "fair" answer. The best solution depends on what you're trying to accomplish: reducing her costs, building a life together, protecting both of you legally, or some combination of those.

A few key facts matter:
- Will only her name be on the deed and mortgage?
- Will you live there full-time?
- How different are your incomes and assets?
- Are you expecting to marry within a year, or is the timing uncertain?
- Are there other major financial obligations either of you have?

In general, there are a few common approaches:

- Tenant-style arrangement
  - She buys the house in her name.
  - You pay a mutually agreed amount toward living expenses (similar to rent).
  - She retains the house equity because she is taking the ownership risk and providing the capital.
  - This is often the cleanest arrangement before marriage.

- Shared-expense arrangement
  - She pays mortgage, taxes, insurance, and major capital expenses.
  - You split utilities, groceries, maintenance, and other household costs.
  - This avoids ambiguity about whether your payments are creating an ownership interest.

- Equity-sharing arrangement
  - If you're contributing significantly to the down payment, mortgage, renovations, or carrying costs, you may want a written agreement specifying what ownership interest those contributions create.
  - This should be documented formally with a lawyer. Verbal understandings can become problematic later.

- Wait until marriage and then reassess
  - If marriage is expected relatively soon, some couples keep ownership separate until they're married and then decide whether to combine finances, refinance, or purchase a future home together.

From a practical and legal standpoint, the riskiest arrangement is often paying substantial amounts toward a house you do not own without a clear written agreement. That can create differing expectations later.

A reasonable middle-ground approach for many couples in your situation is:
- She buys the house in her name.
- You contribute an amount comparable to what you'd pay for housing elsewhere or split agreed household expenses.
- You do not contribute large sums toward down payment, principal reduction, or major renovations unless you've both agreed in writing how that affects ownership.

Before making a decision, I would have a detailed conversation about:
- What each of you considers fair.
- Whether either of you expects house appreciation to be shared.
- How finances will likely work after marriage.
- What happens if the relationship ends before marriage.

Given that you're considering marriage within a year, a brief consultation with a family-law attorney or real-estate attorney in your jurisdiction can be surprisingly valuable. A one-hour conversation can clarify how ownership, contributions, and marital property rules would apply where you live.
