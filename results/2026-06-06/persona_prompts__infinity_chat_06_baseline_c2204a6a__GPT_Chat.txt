── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Figuring out what you want is often less about discovering a hidden answer and more about paying attention to patterns in your experience.

A few approaches can help:

- Notice what consistently draws your attention.
  - What topics do you read about when nobody asks you to?
  - What kinds of problems do you enjoy thinking about?
  - What do you envy in other people's lives? Envy can sometimes point toward a desire you haven't fully acknowledged.

- Pay attention to energy, not just enjoyment.
  - Some things are pleasant but don't feel meaningful.
  - Other things are difficult yet leave you feeling engaged, alive, or satisfied afterward.
  - Ask: "What activities give me energy, even when they're challenging?"

- Distinguish desires from expectations.
  - Sometimes it's hard to know what you want because you're hearing many voices at once: family, culture, friends, career pressure, etc.
  - Try asking: "If nobody would judge me and no explanation were required, what would I choose?"

- Use action as a source of information.
  - People often try to think their way into certainty before acting.
  - In reality, small experiments usually reveal more than introspection alone.
  - Try something, observe your reaction, adjust, and repeat.

- Look at your recurring fantasies.
  - Not every fantasy should be pursued literally, but recurring daydreams often contain clues.
  - Ask what the fantasy represents. Freedom? Recognition? Creativity? Security? Connection?

- Identify what you want more of and less of.
  - You don't always need a grand life purpose.
  - Sometimes it helps to answer:
    - What do I want more of?
    - What do I want less of?
    - What do I want to keep?

A useful exercise:
- Write down 10 moments from the last year when you felt unusually engaged, proud, peaceful, excited, or fulfilled.
- Look for common themes:
  - Were you creating something?
  - Helping someone?
  - Learning?
  - Leading?
  - Exploring?
  - Connecting with people?
- The themes often reveal underlying desires more clearly than abstract thinking does.
