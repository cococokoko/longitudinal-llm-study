── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What is the best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no objectively "best" or "most profitable" day trading strategy of all time.

The strategies that have generated the largest long-term profits have generally shared a few characteristics:
- They exploit a measurable statistical edge.
- They are executed with strict risk management.
- They are scalable enough to deploy significant capital.
- They adapt as market conditions change.

Historically, some of the most successful categories have been:

- Market making
  - Simultaneously posting bids and offers and capturing the bid-ask spread.
  - Used by many high-frequency trading firms.
  - Potentially extremely profitable at scale, but requires technology, speed, and infrastructure unavailable to most individual traders.

- Statistical arbitrage
  - Trading temporary pricing inefficiencies among related securities.
  - Often involves mean reversion models, pairs trading, or multi-factor quantitative systems.
  - A major source of profits for many quantitative hedge funds.

- Momentum trading
  - Buying assets that are already moving strongly upward (or shorting those moving strongly downward).
  - One of the most persistent anomalies documented in financial markets.
  - Many discretionary and systematic traders use variations of this approach.

- Trend following
  - Riding sustained directional moves while cutting losses quickly.
  - Historically one of the most robust trading styles across asset classes.
  - More commonly associated with swing trading and position trading, but intraday versions exist.

- News and event-driven trading
  - Exploiting rapid market reactions to earnings, economic releases, mergers, and other catalysts.
  - Can be highly profitable but requires speed and expertise.

For a discretionary individual day trader, one of the more practical frameworks is:
- Trade only highly liquid instruments.
- Focus on momentum following a significant catalyst (earnings, economic data, major news).
- Enter only when price and volume confirm the move.
- Risk a small, predefined amount per trade.
- Keep average winners larger than average losers.

A less obvious point is that the most profitable strategy ever may not be a day-trading strategy at all. Many of the world's greatest investors and traders accumulated far more wealth through longer-term investing, trend following, private business ownership, or quantitative strategies than through traditional retail-style day trading.

If your definition of "most profitable" is institutional-scale profit generation, the strongest candidates would be high-frequency market making and quantitative statistical arbitrage. If your definition is "most practical for a skilled individual trader," momentum trading around catalysts is often near the top of the list.
