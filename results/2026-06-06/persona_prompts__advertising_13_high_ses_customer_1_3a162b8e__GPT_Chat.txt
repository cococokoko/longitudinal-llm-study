Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Mastering steak comes down to controlling three variables: the cut, the temperature, and the crust.

1. Choose the right cut
- Ribeye: rich, heavily marbled, forgiving.
- New York strip: beefy flavor, firmer texture.
- Filet mignon: very tender, milder flavor.
- Porterhouse/T-bone: combines strip and tenderloin.
- Thickness matters: 1.25–2 inches (3–5 cm) is ideal for precise cooking.

2. Start with a dry surface
- Pat the steak thoroughly dry with paper towels.
- Moisture inhibits browning and crust formation.
- For even better results, place the steak uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator for several hours or overnight.

3. Season properly
- Salt generously.
- Either:
  - Salt 40–60 minutes before cooking, or
  - Salt immediately before cooking.
- Freshly ground black pepper can be added before or after cooking.

4. Use high heat for the crust
- Cast-iron pans are excellent.
- Preheat the pan until very hot.
- Add a high-smoke-point oil (avocado, canola, grapeseed).
- Place the steak in the pan and avoid moving it initially.

5. Manage internal temperature
Target final temperatures:
- Rare: 120–125°F (49–52°C)
- Medium-rare: 130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: 140–145°F (60–63°C)
- Medium-well: 150–155°F (66–68°C)

Use an instant-read thermometer. This is the most reliable way to achieve consistency.

6. Baste for flavor
During the last minute or two:
- Add butter.
- Add crushed garlic and herbs (thyme or rosemary).
- Tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the steak repeatedly.

7. Rest before slicing
- Rest 5–10 minutes for smaller steaks.
- Rest 10–15 minutes for larger cuts.
- This helps retain juices when sliced.

8. Learn reverse searing
For thick steaks:
- Cook in a 225–275°F (107–135°C) oven until the center is about 10–15°F below your target.
- Finish with a very hot sear in a pan or on a grill.
- This produces a more uniform interior and excellent crust.

Common mistakes
- Cooking cold steak is less important than often claimed; surface dryness matters more.
- Crowding the pan lowers heat and reduces browning.
- Relying on cooking time instead of temperature.
- Cutting immediately after cooking.

A simple gold-standard method for a 1.5-inch ribeye:
- Dry and salt the steak.
- Reverse-sear in a 250°F (121°C) oven until 120°F (49°C) internal.
- Sear in a smoking-hot cast-iron pan 60–90 seconds per side.
- Butter-baste for 1 minute.
- Rest 5–10 minutes.
- Serve at approximately 130–135°F (54–57°C) medium-rare.

Once you can consistently control internal temperature and develop a deep brown crust, steak cookery becomes highly repeatable rather than guesswork.
