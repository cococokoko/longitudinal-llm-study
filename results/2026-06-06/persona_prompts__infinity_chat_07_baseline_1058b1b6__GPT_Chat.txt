Is it impossible to know everything?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Yes, for several different reasons, it appears impossible for any person—and perhaps any finite being—to know everything.

Some reasons come from practical limits:
- The universe contains an enormous amount of information. A human lifetime, memory, and attention span are finite.
- New facts are constantly emerging. Even if someone somehow knew all current facts, they would have to keep up with ongoing changes.

Some reasons come from physics:
- There are limits to how much information can be stored and processed by a physical system.
- Certain aspects of nature may be fundamentally inaccessible. For example, quantum mechanics places limits on what can be known simultaneously about a system.

Some reasons come from mathematics and logic:
- Gödel's incompleteness theorems show that in sufficiently powerful formal systems, there are true statements that cannot be proven within the system.
- Turing's work on computation shows that there are questions that no algorithm can decide in all cases.

There is also a philosophical angle:
- To "know everything" would require a clear definition of "everything." Does that include every fact about the past, present, future, every possible universe, every subjective experience, every mathematical truth? The concept itself becomes difficult to define precisely.

That said, impossibility depends on what kind of knower you're imagining:
- For a human being, knowing everything is overwhelmingly impossible.
- For a hypothetical omniscient being (such as in some religious traditions), omniscience is defined as knowing everything, though whether such a being can exist is a philosophical and theological question.

So the strongest conclusion is: given our current understanding of physics, mathematics, and human cognition, it is impossible for humans to know everything, and there are good reasons to think no finite physical being could know everything either.
