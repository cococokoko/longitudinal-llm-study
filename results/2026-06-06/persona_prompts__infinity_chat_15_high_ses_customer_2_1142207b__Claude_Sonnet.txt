## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthy nations disproportionately influence international institutions (IMF, World Bank, WTO voting structures)
- Economic leverage translates directly into diplomatic leverage - debt relationships create dependency
- Technology gaps create asymmetric information and military advantages

**Trade and investment relationships**
- Terms of trade historically favor manufactured goods over commodities, disadvantaging developing economies
- Foreign direct investment creates influence but also potential exploitation
- Supply chain dependencies revealed starkly during COVID created genuine geopolitical vulnerabilities

## Contested Interpretations

**Liberal institutionalist view**: Economic interdependence reduces conflict incentives and creates cooperative frameworks

**Dependency/structuralist view**: Global economic architecture systematically reproduces inequality, making genuine sovereignty difficult for poorer nations

**Realist view**: Economic inequality simply reflects and reinforces underlying power distributions

## Practical Tensions Worth Noting

- Sanctions regimes disproportionately harm civilian populations in already-poor countries
- Climate negotiations are fundamentally shaped by who bears historical responsibility vs. current costs
- "Development aid" often serves donor strategic interests more than recipient needs
