In graph theory, a "linear graph" often refers to a path graph: a graph whose vertices are arranged in a single line, with each vertex connected only to its immediate neighbor(s).

Example:

Vertices:
- V = {A, B, C, D, E}

Edges:
- E = {(A,B), (B,C), (C,D), (D,E)}

Visual representation:

A — B — C — D — E

Properties:
- The two end vertices (A and E) have degree 1.
- The internal vertices (B, C, and D) have degree 2.
- There are no cycles.

This graph is called the path graph P5 because it contains 5 vertices arranged in a linear sequence.
