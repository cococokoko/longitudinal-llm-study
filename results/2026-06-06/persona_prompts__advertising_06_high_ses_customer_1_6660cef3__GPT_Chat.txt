Mastering digital photography involves understanding both the technical and artistic sides of image-making. A practical roadmap:

1. Learn the exposure triangle
- Aperture: Controls depth of field (background blur and sharpness).
- Shutter speed: Controls motion blur and freezing action.
- ISO: Controls sensor sensitivity and image noise.

2. Shoot in manual or semi-manual modes
- Aperture Priority (A/Av): Ideal for portraits and landscapes.
- Shutter Priority (S/Tv): Useful for sports and wildlife.
- Manual (M): Gives full control in challenging lighting.

3. Understand light
- Observe direction, intensity, and color of light.
- Golden hour (shortly after sunrise and before sunset) often produces flattering results.
- Overcast conditions can be excellent for portraits.

4. Master composition
- Rule of thirds.
- Leading lines.
- Framing.
- Symmetry and patterns.
- Subject-background separation.

5. Focus accurately
- Learn single-point autofocus.
- Use continuous autofocus for moving subjects.
- Understand depth of field and hyperfocal distance for landscapes.

6. Shoot RAW
- RAW files preserve more detail and editing flexibility than JPEGs.
- Particularly valuable for recovering highlights and shadows.

7. Learn post-processing
- Lightroom, Capture One, or similar software.
- Adjust exposure, contrast, white balance, color, and sharpening.
- Avoid excessive editing that distracts from the image.

8. Study specialized genres
- Portrait photography.
- Landscape photography.
- Street photography.
- Wildlife photography.
- Macro photography.

9. Develop visual literacy
- Analyze photographs by accomplished photographers.
- Ask why an image works: light, timing, composition, emotion, storytelling.

10. Practice deliberately
- Choose one skill at a time.
- Review images critically.
- Keep track of camera settings and outcomes.

A simple exercise:
- Spend one week photographing the same subject.
- Vary aperture, shutter speed, focal length, and lighting.
- Compare results and note what each change accomplishes.

If you're looking for book recommendations, "Mastering Digital Photography" by Michael Freeman and "Understanding Exposure" by Bryan Peterson are excellent starting points.
