── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a tenured professor at a prestigious university.

Creating a successful YouTube channel.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a tenured professor, you have a significant advantage on YouTube: deep expertise, credibility, and access to ideas that many creators cannot easily replicate. The challenge is translating academic knowledge into content that a broad audience wants to watch.

A useful approach:

- Define a specific audience
  - Undergraduate students?
  - Professionals in your field?
  - Lifelong learners?
  - Policymakers or journalists?
  - The more specific the audience, the easier it is to grow.

- Focus on problems and questions, not lectures
  - Instead of: "Lecture 7: Economic Development"
  - Try: "Why Some Countries Stay Poor for Centuries"
  - People click on questions, mysteries, and practical relevance.

- Develop 3–5 recurring content formats
  - Explainers: "The science behind..."
  - Myth-busting: "What most people get wrong about..."
  - Research breakdowns: "A new study claims..."
  - Historical case studies
  - Q&A responses to audience questions

- Prioritize storytelling
  - Start with a surprising fact, conflict, puzzle, or real-world example.
  - Introduce the theory afterward.
  - Most successful educational channels teach through narrative rather than lecture.

- Invest in production quality strategically
  - Clear audio is more important than expensive cameras.
  - Good lighting and concise editing matter.
  - Slides alone rarely perform well for general audiences.

- Publish consistently
  - One high-quality video per week is often better than several rushed uploads.
  - Consistency helps both audience expectations and platform recommendations.

- Use titles and thumbnails that create curiosity without misleading viewers
  - "The Experiment That Changed Psychology Forever"
  - "Why Experts Were Wrong About X"
  - Avoid academic-style titles.

- Leverage your academic advantages
  - Interview leading researchers.
  - Explain cutting-edge papers.
  - Provide context missing from news coverage.
  - Share insights from years of teaching and research.

- Study successful educational creators
  - Veritasium
  - Sabine Hossenfelder
  - Derek Muller
  - CGP Grey
  - Wendover Productions
  - Economics Explained
  - Examine structure, pacing, titles, and audience engagement.

- Build an ecosystem
  - Newsletter
  - LinkedIn
  - X/Twitter
  - University talks
  - Podcast appearances
  - These channels can help drive early viewers to new videos.

A common mistake professors make is optimizing for accuracy alone. Accuracy is essential, but attention comes first. The most successful educational channels maintain rigor while packaging ideas in ways that create curiosity and engagement.

What academic field are you in? The optimal channel strategy differs substantially between STEM, social sciences, humanities, law, medicine, and business.
