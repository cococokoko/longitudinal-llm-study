── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are signs that show you were not loved enough as a child by your mom?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There is no reliable checklist that can prove a mother "didn't love you enough." Love, attachment, emotional availability, mental health, stress, and parenting skill are all different things. A parent may love a child deeply and still fail to meet important emotional needs.

That said, adults who grew up with chronically inadequate maternal nurturing often report patterns such as:

- Difficulty believing that people genuinely care about them.
- Persistent fear of abandonment or rejection.
- Feeling that they must earn love through achievement, usefulness, or perfection.
- Strong discomfort receiving affection, praise, or support.
- A tendency to choose emotionally unavailable partners or friends.
- Chronic feelings of emptiness, loneliness, or being emotionally unseen.
- Excessive self-criticism and a harsh inner voice.
- Difficulty identifying or expressing emotions because those emotions were ignored or dismissed in childhood.
- Hyper-independence ("I can only rely on myself").
- People-pleasing and difficulty setting boundaries.
- Feeling responsible for other people's feelings from an unusually young age.
- A deep sense that their needs are burdensome.
- Intense sensitivity to criticism or disapproval.

Looking back at childhood, experiences that are commonly associated with emotional deprivation include:

- Rarely being comforted when distressed.
- Having achievements valued more than personal feelings.
- Being criticized, shamed, compared to others, or emotionally invalidated on a regular basis.
- Receiving affection only when behaving a certain way.
- A parent who was consistently emotionally absent, preoccupied, unpredictable, or cold.
- Being expected to care for the parent's emotional needs rather than the reverse.
- Feeling invisible in the family despite having physical needs met.

It is important to distinguish between:
- A mother who did not love her child.
- A mother who loved her child but was emotionally unavailable, immature, depressed, addicted, traumatized, overwhelmed, or otherwise unable to provide consistent nurturing.

The impact on the child can sometimes look similar even when the underlying reason is very different.

One useful question is not "Did my mother love me?" but "When I was hurt, scared, sad, or needed comfort, was there someone who reliably noticed, cared, and responded?" The answer to that question often reveals more about childhood emotional experience than the question of love alone.
