# Review: *The Half-Life of Dr. Voss* (2024)

★★★★½

Director Mara Okonkwo's quietly devastating film follows a computational biologist who discovers a fundamental error in her most-cited paper — twelve years after it shaped an entire field of treatment.

What could have been a procedural thriller instead becomes something rarer: a meditation on **scientific ego, institutional loyalty, and the courage required to be wrong publicly**. The lab scenes feel genuinely lived-in, avoiding the usual Hollywood shorthand of dramatic pipetting and glowing screens.

The central performance is extraordinary. We watch the protagonist rehearse the retraction conversation seventeen different ways before making the call. No explosions. No villains. Just the specific, suffocating weight of accountability.

The film's best scene — a grant committee meeting that doubles as a kind of secular confession — will be uncomfortable viewing for anyone who has ever oversold preliminary data in a funding application.

**Where it stumbles:** the third act introduces a pharmaceutical subplot that feels imported from a different, lesser movie.

**Where it soars:** it understands that science is not a collection of discoveries but an ongoing argument, and that the most important thing a researcher can do is sometimes *stop defending themselves.*

Recommended viewing. Bring colleagues. Argue afterward.
