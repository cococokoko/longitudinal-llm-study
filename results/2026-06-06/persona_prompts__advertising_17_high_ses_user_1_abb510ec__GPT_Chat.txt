── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a neurosurgeon at a major teaching hospital.

How can I make my home more energy efficient?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving home energy efficiency is usually most effective when you prioritize the biggest sources of energy loss first. A systematic approach often delivers better results than replacing individual appliances at random.

Start with an energy assessment:
- Arrange a professional home energy audit, or use a utility-sponsored program if available.
- Consider a blower-door test and, if possible, thermal imaging to identify air leaks and insulation gaps.
- Review 12–24 months of utility bills to understand seasonal patterns.

Focus on the building envelope:
- Air-seal leaks around doors, windows, attic hatches, plumbing penetrations, electrical outlets, and duct penetrations.
- Improve attic insulation if it is below recommended levels for your climate.
- Insulate and air-seal crawl spaces, basements, and rim joists where appropriate.
- Seal and insulate ductwork, especially if ducts run through unconditioned spaces.

Optimize heating and cooling:
- Replace older HVAC equipment with high-efficiency systems when replacement is due.
- Heat pumps can be highly efficient for both heating and cooling in many climates.
- Install a smart or programmable thermostat.
- Change filters regularly and maintain equipment according to manufacturer recommendations.
- Use ceiling fans appropriately to improve comfort and potentially reduce HVAC demand.

Upgrade water heating:
- Consider a heat-pump water heater if suitable for your home.
- Insulate hot-water pipes where practical.
- Lower water-heater temperature if currently set unnecessarily high.
- Install low-flow showerheads and fixtures to reduce hot-water use.

Improve windows and shading:
- If windows are relatively modern, weatherstripping and sealing may provide better value than replacement.
- For older, inefficient windows, replacement may be worthwhile during renovations.
- Use exterior shading, awnings, blinds, curtains, or strategically placed trees to reduce summer heat gain.

Use efficient lighting and appliances:
- Replace remaining incandescent or halogen bulbs with LEDs.
- When appliances reach end of life, choose high-efficiency models.
- Look for ENERGY STAR or equivalent certifications where available.

Reduce standby power:
- Use advanced power strips for entertainment and office equipment.
- Unplug infrequently used chargers and devices.
- Enable energy-saving settings on computers and electronics.

Consider renewable energy:
- If your roof and local regulations are favorable, rooftop solar can reduce long-term electricity costs.
- Pairing solar with battery storage may improve resilience, though economics vary by location.

Monitor results:
- Use a whole-home energy monitor or utility usage data to track changes.
- Compare energy consumption before and after upgrades to identify which measures are most effective.
