There is no single "best and most profitable day trading strategy of all time." If such a strategy existed and remained reliable, market participants would eventually arbitrage away much of its edge.

A few important realities:

- Most retail day traders underperform the market over long periods after costs, taxes, and slippage.
- Profitability depends on market conditions, execution quality, risk management, capital size, and trader discipline.
- A strategy that works well in trending markets may fail in ranging or highly volatile markets.

Among professional traders, some of the most durable approaches have been:

- Trend-following momentum
  - Buy strength in stocks, futures, or currencies that are breaking out with volume.
  - Cut losses quickly and let winners run.
  - This has a long history of success across many markets.

- Mean reversion
  - Buy short-term weakness in strong assets or sell short-term strength in weak assets.
  - Often works in range-bound markets.
  - Requires strict risk controls because trends can overwhelm mean-reversion setups.

- Opening range breakout
  - Trade a break above or below the high/low established shortly after the market opens.
  - Popular among day traders because volume and volatility are often highest early in the session.

- Market making and statistical arbitrage
  - Historically among the most profitable quantitative approaches.
  - Usually requires sophisticated technology, data, and infrastructure beyond what most individuals have.

If your goal is maximizing the probability of long-term wealth creation while supporting family and managing many responsibilities, the evidence generally favors:
- Broad index investing for core wealth building.
- Using only a small portion of capital for active trading, if desired.
- Focusing heavily on risk management rather than searching for a perfect strategy.

A practical day-trading framework many traders use is:
- Trade only highly liquid markets.
- Wait for a clear trend and volume confirmation.
- Risk no more than 0.5%–1% of account equity per trade.
- Maintain a reward-to-risk ratio of at least 2:1.
- Track every trade and evaluate results over hundreds of trades, not a handful.
