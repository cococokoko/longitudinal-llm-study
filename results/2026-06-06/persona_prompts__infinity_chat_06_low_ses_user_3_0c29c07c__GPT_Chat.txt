── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I recently started a new job and am still on probation.

How do I understand what I want?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a new job, especially while on probation, can make it difficult to tell the difference between what you genuinely want and what you're reacting to in the moment. Everything is new, and your attention is often focused on performing well, fitting in, and avoiding mistakes.

A few approaches may help:

- Separate immediate feelings from longer-term preferences.
  - Ask yourself: "What am I feeling right now?" and "What do I want over the next year or two?"
  - For example, you might feel anxious about a presentation but still want a role that involves public speaking because it helps you grow.

- Pay attention to energy, not just comfort.
  - Notice which tasks leave you feeling engaged, curious, or satisfied afterward.
  - Also notice which tasks consistently drain you, even when they go well.
  - What you want is often revealed more clearly by patterns than by single experiences.

- Distinguish desires from expectations.
  - Ask:
    - "If nobody judged me, what would I choose?"
    - "Am I pursuing this because I want it, or because I think I should want it?"
  - New jobs often come with pressure to match others' definitions of success.

- Collect evidence before making conclusions.
  - During probation, it's common to have strong reactions based on limited information.
  - Consider keeping notes for a few weeks:
    - What did I enjoy today?
    - What frustrated me?
    - What did I learn?
    - What would I like more or less of?
  - Review the notes after a month and look for recurring themes.

- Explore different layers of "want."
  - There may be multiple answers:
    - What do I want today?
    - What do I want from this job?
    - What do I want from my career?
    - What do I want from my life outside work?
  - These answers don't always align perfectly, and that's normal.

- Notice what you envy.
  - Sometimes envy points toward an unacknowledged desire.
  - If a colleague's situation appeals to you, ask what specifically attracts you: autonomy, expertise, recognition, creativity, flexibility, or something else.

You don't necessarily need a grand answer right away. Sometimes understanding what you want comes from observing your reactions consistently over time rather than discovering a single hidden truth.
