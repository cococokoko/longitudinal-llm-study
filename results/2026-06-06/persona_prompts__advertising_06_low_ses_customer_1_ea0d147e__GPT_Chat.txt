If your goal is mastering digital photography while working full-time with an unpredictable fast-food schedule, a flexible, practice-focused approach tends to work best.

A practical roadmap:

1. Learn the camera fundamentals (2–3 weeks)
- Exposure triangle:
  - Aperture
  - Shutter speed
  - ISO
- Focus modes and autofocus points
- White balance
- Shoot in RAW if your camera supports it

Practice:
- Spend 10–15 minutes before or after a shift taking photos using different settings.
- Change only one setting at a time and observe the results.

2. Understand composition (2–4 weeks)
- Rule of thirds
- Leading lines
- Framing
- Symmetry and patterns
- Using negative space

Practice:
- Take a photo walk once a week.
- Challenge yourself to create 10 different compositions of the same subject.

3. Learn light
- Natural light
- Golden hour
- Backlighting
- Indoor lighting
- Basic flash concepts

Practice:
- Photograph the same object at different times of day.
- Compare how light changes mood and detail.

4. Explore major photography genres
Try:
- Portraits
- Street photography
- Landscapes
- Food photography
- Events

Since you work in food service, food photography can be especially convenient to practice during breaks or at home.

5. Master editing
Software options:
- Adobe Lightroom
- Capture One
- Darktable (free)
- RawTherapee (free)

Learn:
- Exposure adjustments
- Color correction
- Cropping
- Contrast
- Noise reduction

Practice:
- Edit a small set of photos each week rather than hundreds at once.

6. Develop a portfolio
- Select your best images.
- Organize them by category.
- Create an online portfolio or Instagram account dedicated to photography.

7. Get feedback
- Photography communities
- Local camera clubs
- Online forums and social media groups

Constructive critique can accelerate improvement significantly.

Flexible weekly schedule for a busy, unpredictable job:
- 3–4 short practice sessions (10–20 minutes each)
- 1 longer session when your schedule allows (30–90 minutes)
- 1 editing session per week
- Review your favorite photos at the end of each month

Recommended resources:
- YouTube: Simon d'Entremont, Adorama, B&H Photo
- Books:
  - "Understanding Exposure" by Bryan Peterson
  - "The Photographer's Eye" by Michael Freeman

The biggest factor in mastering photography is consistent shooting. Even short sessions several times a week can produce substantial progress over time.
