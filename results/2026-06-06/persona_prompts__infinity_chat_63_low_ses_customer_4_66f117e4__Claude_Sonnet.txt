# Partial Derivatives: A Numerical Example

## Setup

Suppose a **hospital patient care aide's** fatigue level is modeled by:

$$F(h, p) = 3h^2 + 2hp + p^2$$

Where:
- **h** = hours worked in a shift
- **p** = number of patients assisted
- **F** = fatigue score (arbitrary units)

---

## Computing the Partial Derivatives

### ∂F/∂h (rate of fatigue change with respect to hours)

Treat **p as a constant**, differentiate with respect to h:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial h} = 6h + 2p$$

### ∂F/∂p (rate of fatigue change with respect to patients)

Treat **h as a constant**, differentiate with respect to p:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial p} = 2h + 2p$$

---

## Numerical Evaluation

Let **h = 8 hours** and **p = 10 patients**:

| Quantity | Calculation | Result |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| F(8, 10) | 3(64) + 2(80) + 100 | **452** |
| ∂F/∂h at (8,10) | 6(8) + 2(10) | **68** |
| ∂F/∂p at (8,10) | 2(8) + 2(10) | **36** |

---

## Interpretation

- **∂F/∂h = 68** → Working **one additional hour** (holding patients fixed at 10) increases fatigue by approximately **68 units**
- **∂F/∂p = 36** → Assisting **one additional patient** (holding hours fixed at 8) increases fatigue by approximately **36 units**

> **Key idea:** A partial derivative measures the rate of change of a function with respect to **one variable at a time**, while all other variables are held constant.
