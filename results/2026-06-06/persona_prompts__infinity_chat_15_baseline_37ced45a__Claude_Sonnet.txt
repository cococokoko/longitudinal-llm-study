── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
How do global economic inequalities shape international relations?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely complex question worth thinking through carefully rather than just listing factors.

## Core Mechanisms

**Structural power dynamics**
- Wealthier nations set terms for international institutions (IMF, WTO, World Bank)
- Dollar dominance gives the US leverage most countries lack
- Technology gaps create dependency relationships

**Bargaining asymmetries**
- Poor countries often accept unfavorable trade terms due to limited alternatives
- Debt creates political leverage for creditors
- This can look like "voluntary" agreements while being structurally coerced

## Genuine Tensions in the Analysis

**Competing interpretations matter here:**
- Dependency theorists argue inequality is *reproduced* by international relations
- Liberal institutionalists argue institutions reduce inequality's worst effects
- Realists focus on power regardless of economic framing
- Each captures something real

**Complicating factors**
- China's rise challenges simple rich/poor narratives
- Some poor countries exercise significant regional influence
- Economic interdependence sometimes constrains powerful nations too
- Inequality within countries affects foreign policy differently than between-country inequality

## What Evidence Suggests
- Economic inequality correlates with reduced negotiating leverage
- But military, geographic, and demographic factors complicate this
- Institutions matter - rules can partially offset raw economic power

## Honest Uncertainties
- How much inequality *causes* versus *reflects* power dynamics is genuinely debated
- Whether current trends are increasing or decreasing inequality's effects is contested
