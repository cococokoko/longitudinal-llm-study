── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

How can I learn to cook healthy meals quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
As a physician-scientist, you already have experience learning complex systems efficiently. Cooking can be approached the same way: focus on a small number of high-yield techniques and build repeatable workflows rather than collecting recipes.

A practical strategy:

1. Learn 5–7 core meals, not 50 recipes

Start with meals that:
- Take 15–30 minutes
- Use overlapping ingredients
- Are nutritionally balanced
- Scale well for leftovers

Examples:
- Sheet-pan chicken/fish + vegetables
- Stir-fry with protein and mixed vegetables
- Grain bowl (rice, quinoa, or farro + protein + vegetables + sauce)
- Omelet or frittata with vegetables
- Lentil or bean soup
- Greek-style salad with protein
- Salmon, vegetables, and potatoes

Once these become automatic, expand gradually.

2. Master a few techniques

A surprisingly small skill set covers much of healthy cooking:
- Roasting vegetables
- Sautéing
- Pan-searing protein
- Cooking grains
- Making simple dressings/sauces
- Knife skills

One hour spent improving knife skills can save many hours over time.

3. Use a meal template

Instead of deciding from scratch each night:

- Protein:
  - Fish
  - Chicken
  - Tofu
  - Beans/lentils
  - Eggs

- Vegetables:
  - 2–3 servings

- Healthy carbohydrate:
  - Potatoes
  - Rice
  - Whole grains
  - Beans

- Flavor:
  - Olive oil
  - Herbs
  - Lemon
  - Vinegar
  - Yogurt-based sauce

This reduces decision fatigue and simplifies shopping.

4. Optimize your environment

Equipment with high return on investment:
- Sharp chef's knife
- Large cutting board
- Sheet pans
- Nonstick skillet
- Instant-read thermometer
- Rice cooker or multicooker (optional but useful)

Many people spend extra time compensating for poor tools.

5. Batch-prep strategically

Rather than full meal prep:
- Cook a large batch of grains once or twice weekly.
- Roast multiple trays of vegetables.
- Prepare proteins for 2–3 days.
- Make one versatile sauce.

Then assemble meals in minutes.

6. Develop a "20-minute default menu"

Maintain ingredients that allow immediate meal creation:
- Eggs
- Frozen vegetables
- Canned beans
- Greek yogurt
- Frozen fish fillets
- Prewashed greens
- Rice or microwavable grains

This prevents reliance on takeout during busy weeks.

7. Learn from a few reliable sources

For healthy, efficient cooking:
- NYT Cooking
- Serious Eats (technique-focused)
- America's Test Kitchen
- Mediterranean-diet-oriented cookbooks
- Budget Bytes (efficient and practical)

Avoid trying recipes from dozens of sources simultaneously.

8. Track what actually works

For a month, record:
- Preparation time
- Taste/satisfaction
- Nutritional quality
- Whether you'd make it again

Create your own "lab notebook" of successful meals. Most people repeatedly eat a relatively small set of dishes.

A sample week requiring relatively little effort:
- Monday: Sheet-pan salmon, broccoli, potatoes
- Tuesday: Chicken and vegetable stir-fry with rice
- Wednesday: Lentil soup and salad
- Thursday: Omelet with vegetables and whole-grain toast
- Friday: Grain bowl with leftover vegetables and protein
- Weekend: One new recipe to expand skills

If your goal is specifically minimizing time while maximizing nutritional quality, the highest-yield habit is usually learning 5–10 repeatable meals and batch-prepping ingredients rather than preparing fully distinct meals every day.
