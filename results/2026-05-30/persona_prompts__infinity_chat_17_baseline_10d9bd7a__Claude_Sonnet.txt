# The Hollow Bell

## A Mystery of Millhaven

---

The bell above Carver's Hardware had not rung in eleven years.

Everyone in Millhaven knew this. The bell had gone silent the same morning that Douglas Carver was found dead at the bottom of his own basement stairs, and his widow, Ruth, had never wound the mechanism again. She said the silence was her way of mourning. The town accepted this the way small towns accept most things — quietly, and without too many questions.

So when Deputy Nora Fitch heard the bell ring at 6:47 on a Tuesday morning in October, she stopped walking and stood very still on the empty sidewalk, her coffee going cold in her hand.

She counted the rings.

Three.

Then silence again.

---

Nora had come back to Millhaven two years ago after a decade working homicide in the city. People assumed she had returned for her mother, who was getting older and lived alone on the east side of town. That was part of it. The other part she kept to herself — a case that had gone wrong, a decision she had made in a parking garage at two in the morning that she could not entirely justify, even now.

Millhaven was supposed to be quiet. It was supposed to be the kind of place where the most serious crime was a fender bender outside the diner or a teenager shoplifting energy drinks from the gas station on Route 9.

She had been mostly right about that.

Until this morning.

She crossed the street toward the hardware store. The front window was dark, the *Closed* sign hanging crooked in the glass. Ruth Carver didn't open until eight. Nora tried the door anyway.

It swung open.

---

The store smelled the way it always had — oil and sawdust and something faintly metallic, like old pennies. The overhead lights were off, but gray morning light pushed through the windows, laying pale rectangles across the wooden floor.

"Ruth?" Nora called.

Nothing.

She moved through the aisles slowly, her hand resting on the radio clipped to her belt. Bins of screws and bolts lined the shelves. Coils of rope hung from iron hooks. Everything was exactly where it should be, which somehow made the open door feel worse.

She found Ruth Carver in the back office.

The old woman was sitting upright in her desk chair, her hands folded in her lap, her eyes closed. For one terrible second, Nora thought she was dead. Then Ruth's chest rose and fell, and Nora let out a breath she hadn't realized she was holding.

"Ruth." She touched the woman's shoulder.

Ruth's eyes opened. They were clear and sharp — not the eyes of someone who had been sleeping.

"I was wondering when you'd come," Ruth said.

---

Nora called Sheriff Dale Pruitt from the parking lot while Ruth sat inside with a cup of tea that Nora had made from the small kettle on the filing cabinet. Dale arrived in twelve minutes, which was fast for him. He was a large, slow-moving man who had been sheriff for twenty-two years and who approached most situations the way a person approaches a sleeping dog — carefully, and with the hope that it would stay asleep.

"She say anything?" he asked, standing beside Nora in the cold air.

"She said she found the office door open when she came downstairs this morning. She said someone had been through the files."

"Anything missing?"

"She doesn't know yet. She seemed — " Nora paused, choosing the word carefully. "Unsurprised."

Dale looked at her. "What does that mean?"

"It means she wasn't scared. She was waiting."

He rubbed the back of his neck. "Nora, Ruth Carver is seventy-three years old and she lives alone above a hardware store. Somebody broke into her office. That's a B and E, maybe. Let's not — "

"Dale." Nora kept her voice even. "Someone rang the bell."

He was quiet for a moment.

"The bell hasn't worked in eleven years," he said.

"I know."

---
