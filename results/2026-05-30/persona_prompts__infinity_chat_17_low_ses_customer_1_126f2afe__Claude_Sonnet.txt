# The Last Light at Harrow's Mill

The town of Crestfall had exactly one stoplight, and it had been blinking yellow for eleven years.

Nobody bothered to fix it. Nobody bothered to fix much of anything in Crestfall anymore, not since the mill closed and took half the population with it. The people who stayed did so out of stubbornness, or poverty, or love — sometimes all three at once.

Deputy Nora Vance was all three.

---

She found the first note on a Tuesday morning, tucked beneath the windshield wiper of her patrol truck like a parking ticket from God.

*She didn't fall. Check the water.*

Nora stood in the gravel lot outside the sheriff's office and read it three times. The handwriting was careful, deliberate — someone who had thought hard about every letter. The paper was ordinary. The kind you'd find in any kitchen drawer in any house in any small town in America.

She knew immediately who *she* referred to.

Margaret Hollis. Seventy-three years old. Found at the bottom of her basement stairs four days ago. The county medical examiner had ruled it an accidental fall within forty-eight hours, which Nora had thought was fast, but Sheriff Dale Pruitt had signed off without a question, and Dale Pruitt had been signing off on things without questions for twenty-two years.

Nora folded the note and put it in her breast pocket.

---

Margaret Hollis had lived alone in the blue house on Fenwick Road since her husband Raymond passed in 2019. She had a daughter in Phoenix who sent Christmas cards and called on birthdays. She had a garden that produced more tomatoes than one person could ever eat, so she left bags of them on neighbors' porches every August.

She had, according to everyone Nora asked, no enemies.

*Everyone has enemies,* Nora's father used to say. *Most people just don't know it yet.*

Her father had been the deputy before her. He'd died of a heart attack at his desk at fifty-four, which Nora sometimes thought was the most honest way a man like him could have gone — working until the very end, in the only place he'd ever wanted to be.

She drove out to the Hollis property on her lunch break.

The house was locked, but Margaret had kept a spare key under a ceramic frog by the back door — Nora knew because Margaret had told her, two summers ago, when Nora had helped her carry groceries inside during a thunderstorm. Small towns ran on that kind of knowledge. Everyone knew where everyone kept their spare key, who drank too much on weekends, whose marriage was held together with silence and habit.

The basement smelled like old wood and mineral water.

Nora stood at the bottom of the stairs and looked at the concrete floor where Margaret had been found. There was a dark stain that the daughter hadn't yet come to clean. Nora crouched and studied the stairs themselves — twelve steps, wooden, with a simple railing on the right side.

The railing was loose.

Not broken. Not splintered. *Loose.* As if the screws had been carefully, methodically removed and then replaced just enough to look intact.

Nora took photographs with her phone and said nothing to anyone.

Crestfall's water came from Harrow's Reservoir, a dark, cold lake two miles north of town, fed by underground springs and managed by a private utility company called Meridian Water Solutions. Meridian had bought the water rights from the county eight years ago for a number that the county commissioners had called *generous* and that Nora's father had called *suspicious* before his heart gave out.

Meridian was owned, at several removes through a chain of holding companies, by a man named Gerald Fitch.

Gerald Fitch had grown up in Crestfall. He'd left at eighteen and come back at fifty-two with money and plans. He'd bought the old mill property, the water rights, and three commercial lots on Main Street. He sat on the town council and sponsored the Little League and smiled at everyone with teeth that were too white for a man his age.

Nora had never trusted him, but distrust wasn't evidence.
