At your level (busy, travel-heavy, limited patience for fiddly logging), the “best” apps are the ones that balance accuracy with low friction. The stack I see work best is usually one fitness tracker + one nutrition app + (optionally) a wearable.

Here are the strongest options right now:

Fitness tracking (activity, recovery, performance)

- Whoop  
  Best for: recovery, sleep, strain management  
  Why it stands out: it’s passive and very good at telling you when to push vs back off. Great if you don’t want to think, just follow guidance.  
  Tradeoff: subscription, no screen, less useful for detailed workout tracking like lifting programs.

- Apple Fitness + Apple Watch  
  Best for: all-around ecosystem, simplicity  
  Why: seamless, good enough at everything, integrates with tons of apps.  
  Tradeoff: recovery insights aren’t as deep as Whoop.

- Garmin (Forerunner / Fenix + Garmin Connect)  
  Best for: data depth, endurance training  
  Why: extremely detailed metrics, great for running, cycling, VO2 max tracking.  
  Tradeoff: UI can feel clunky; overkill unless you like data.

- Strava  
  Best for: motivation + social accountability  
  Why: turns workouts into something social/competitive.  
  Tradeoff: not a full health platform on its own.

Nutrition tracking

- MacroFactor  
  Best overall right now  
  Why: uses your weight + intake to dynamically adjust calorie targets; very low mental load once set up. No “guilt” nudging.  
  Tradeoff: paid, but worth it.

- MyFitnessPal  
  Best for: largest food database, simplicity  
  Why: easiest to log almost anything, especially when traveling.  
  Tradeoff: more manual, less intelligent guidance.

- Carbon Diet Coach  
  Best for: structured coaching feel  
  Why: gives macro targets and adjusts weekly based on progress.  
  Tradeoff: slightly more rigid than MacroFactor.

- Lose It!  
  Best for: lightweight, fast logging  
  Why: quicker and simpler than MyFitnessPal.  
  Tradeoff: fewer advanced features.

All-in-one / behavior-focused

- Noom  
  Best for: behavior change, psychology  
  Why: good if consistency is your main issue.  
  Tradeoff: less precise on performance optimization.

- Levels (if you’re into CGMs)  
  Best for: glucose tracking  
  Why: useful for understanding how you personally respond to food.  
  Tradeoff: expensive, arguably overkill unless you’re very data-driven.
