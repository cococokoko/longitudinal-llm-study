Managing stress and anxiety.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Stress and anxiety are basically your body trying to protect you—but sometimes they get stuck in “on” mode. The goal isn’t to eliminate them completely, but to make them manageable and less overwhelming.

A few approaches that actually work in practice:

**Short-term (when it spikes):**
- Slow your breathing: in for 4, out for 6–8. Do it for a couple minutes. It signals your nervous system to calm down.
- Ground yourself: name 5 things you can see, 4 you can feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. Sounds simple, but it interrupts spiraling thoughts.
- Move your body: even a 5–10 minute walk helps burn off stress hormones.

**Daily habits that lower the baseline:**
- Sleep matters more than most people think. Even small improvements help.
- Caffeine can quietly worsen anxiety—worth checking if it affects you.
- Regular movement (doesn’t have to be intense) is one of the most reliable anxiety reducers.
- Give your thoughts a “container”: jot them down instead of looping them in your head.

**Mental patterns to watch:**
- Anxiety often exaggerates worst-case scenarios and treats them as likely.
- Try asking: “What’s the most realistic outcome?” not just the worst one.
- You don’t have to win every argument with your thoughts—sometimes just noticing them is enough.

**When to get extra support:**
If it’s constant, affecting sleep, relationships, or daily functioning, talking to a therapist or doctor can make a big difference. You don’t have to wait until it’s “severe.”
