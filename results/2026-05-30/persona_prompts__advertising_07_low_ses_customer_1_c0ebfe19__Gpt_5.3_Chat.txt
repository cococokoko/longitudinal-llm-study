If your schedule’s all over the place, the best energy-saving changes are the ones that don’t rely on you remembering anything. Focus on “set it and forget it” stuff first, then layer in a few easy habits.

Start with the biggest wins:
- Switch bulbs to LEDs. It’s boring but huge—uses ~75–80% less energy and you won’t be constantly replacing them.
- Install a programmable or smart thermostat. Set it to ease off heating/cooling when you’re usually out or asleep. Even a simple schedule can cut a lot without you thinking about it.
- Seal drafts. Cheap weatherstripping around doors/windows and outlet gaskets stop heat/AC from leaking out.
- Wash clothes cold and air-dry when you can. Heating water and running dryers are energy hogs.

Appliances and daily use:
- Unplug “always-on” electronics (TVs, consoles, chargers) or use a power strip you can flip off in one go.
- Run dishwasher and laundry only when full. Use eco modes—they’re slower but use less energy and water.
- Keep your fridge efficient: don’t leave it open, set it to about 37–40°F (3–4°C), freezer at 0°F (-18°C), and clean the coils if you can access them.
- Cook smarter: use a microwave, air fryer, or slow cooker instead of the oven when possible—they use less energy and don’t heat up your place.

Heating/cooling tricks:
- Use fans to make higher temps feel cooler (so you can raise the thermostat a bit in summer).
- In winter, let sunlight in during the day; at night, close curtains to keep heat in. Reverse that in summer.
- Close off rooms you’re not using if your system allows it.

Low-effort upgrades if you can:
- Smart plugs for things you forget to turn off (set schedules or auto-off).
- Low-flow showerheads: less hot water used without much comfort loss.
- Insulated curtains if your place runs hot/cold.

Quick habit tweaks that don’t require a routine:
- If you leave for work, do a 10-second “lights + power strip” check on your way out.
- Charge devices during off-peak hours if your utility has time-of-use rates (often late evening/night).
