── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a partner at a major consulting firm.

What are the best ways to save for retirement?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, retirement saving stops being about “where do I put money?” and becomes “how do I optimize across taxes, liquidity, and optionality?” The basics still matter, but the real gains come from structuring.

Here’s how most partners in your position think about it:

First, fully exploit tax-advantaged space  
Max out every available tax shelter every year, no exceptions:
- 401(k) / profit-sharing plan (including after-tax + mega backdoor Roth if your plan allows it)  
- Backdoor Roth IRA  
- HSA (treat it as a stealth retirement account, not a spending account)  
- Deferred compensation plans (common in consulting firms—these can be very powerful if used carefully)

The key is consistency and using these as “tax location” tools, not just savings buckets.

Second, use deferred compensation strategically  
This is often underused or misused. You’re likely in peak earning years, so:
- Defer income when you’re in the highest marginal bracket  
- Schedule distributions for lower-income years (early retirement, sabbaticals, post-partnership step-down)

But be careful: this is unsecured exposure to your firm. Don’t overload it—think of it as one component, not the core.

Third, build a large, tax-efficient brokerage portfolio  
Once you’ve filled tax-advantaged space, your brokerage account becomes the main engine:
- Favor low-turnover, tax-efficient investments (index funds, ETFs, direct indexing)  
- Harvest losses systematically  
- Be intentional about asset location (e.g., bonds in tax-deferred, equities in taxable)

This is what gives you flexibility before traditional retirement age.

Fourth, think in terms of “after-tax, after-fee, after-time” returns  
At your income level, taxes dominate:
- Municipal bonds may make sense depending on your state  
- Private investments (PE, VC, private credit) can help, but only if you understand fees, illiquidity, and concentration risk  
- Avoid overcomplicating—many partners underperform simple portfolios because of over-allocation to alternatives

Fifth, manage concentration risk (this is a big one for partners)  
You may already have:
- Economic exposure to your firm (equity, profit share, deferred comp)  
- Industry concentration (consulting, corporate cycle sensitivity)

So your portfolio outside the firm should deliberately diversify away from that.

Sixth, plan for liquidity and “optionality windows”  
Retirement isn’t always a clean stop:
- Build a taxable portfolio large enough to fund 5–10+ years of living expenses  
- This lets you step back early, switch careers, or wait out markets before drawing down tax-deferred accounts

Seventh, use real estate selectively  
Not mandatory, but can be useful:
- Primary residence as a long-term, tax-advantaged asset  
- A small allocation to income-generating property or RE funds if you want diversification and inflation hedging  
- Avoid turning this into a second career unless you actually want that

Eighth, don’t ignore estate and tax planning  
At your income level, this is part of retirement strategy:
- Backdoor Roths today = tax-free assets later  
- Consider trusts if your estate will exceed thresholds  
- Coordinate with a tax advisor on multi-year planning, not just annual filing

Finally, define what “retirement” actually means for you  
Most consulting partners don’t go from 100 to 0. The real goal is:
- Financial independence (work becomes optional)  
- Flexibility to scale down, not necessarily stop
