If you’re aiming to really *understand* Design and Analysis of Algorithms (not just pass a course), a mix of theory + problem-solving is the sweet spot. Here are the best picks, depending on how deep you want to go:

**1. Core textbook (most recommended)**
- *Introduction to Algorithms* — Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein (CLRS)  
  The classic. Very comprehensive: covers everything from basics to advanced topics. It’s not the easiest read, but it’s the gold standard and widely used in universities.

**2. More approachable + intuitive**
- *Algorithm Design* — Kleinberg & Tardos  
  Much better explanations than CLRS for many topics. Focuses on the “why” behind algorithms and patterns (greedy, divide & conquer, DP). Great if CLRS feels dense.

**3. For coding + interview-style thinking**
- *Grokking Algorithms* — Aditya Bhargava  
  Very beginner-friendly, visual, and intuitive. Not deep, but great if you’re just starting or want quick clarity.

- *Elements of Programming Interviews* — Aziz, Lee, Prakash  
  Strong for practicing algorithmic thinking with real coding problems.

**4. For deeper theory / serious CS study**
- *The Algorithm Design Manual* — Steven Skiena  
  Practical + engaging. Includes “war stories” and real-world applications.

- *Algorithms* — Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani  
  Concise and mathematically clean. Often underrated, but excellent for understanding proofs.

**5. If you prefer online + interactive**
- MIT OpenCourseWare (6.006 / 6.046)  
- Coursera “Algorithms” by Princeton (Sedgewick & Wayne)

---

**Simple path (if you want a plan):**
- Start with *Grokking Algorithms* (quick foundation)
- Move to *Kleinberg & Tardos* (core understanding)
- Use *CLRS* as a reference + for deeper dives
- Practice problems alongside (very important)
