Mastering the art of cooking steak.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Cooking a great steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control: heat, timing, and seasoning. Once you understand those, you can make restaurant-level steak at home pretty consistently.

Start with the meat itself. Go for thicker cuts (about 1–1.5 inches) like ribeye, strip, or filet. Thinner steaks are much harder to cook well because they overcook before you get a good crust.

Salt matters more than most people think. Salt the steak generously—like more than feels reasonable—and do it at least 40 minutes before cooking, or right before it hits the pan. That window avoids the awkward phase where salt pulls moisture to the surface and ruins your sear.

Bring the steak to room temp for about 20–30 minutes before cooking so it cooks more evenly.

Now the pan: use something heavy (cast iron is ideal). Get it really hot—borderline smoking. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like avocado or canola), then lay the steak down away from you.

Don’t touch it for a couple minutes. Moving it too early kills the crust. Flip once you’ve got a deep brown sear, then cook the other side.

If the steak is thick, finish it with butter, garlic, and maybe thyme. Tilt the pan and spoon the melted butter over the steak for a minute or two—this adds flavor and helps it cook evenly.

Use a thermometer if you can. It takes the guesswork out:
- Rare: ~125°F (52°C)
- Medium rare: ~130–135°F (54–57°C)
- Medium: ~140–145°F (60–63°C)

Pull it a few degrees early because it keeps cooking after you take it off.

Rest it for 5–10 minutes before slicing. This keeps the juices from running all over the plate.

A couple of common mistakes to avoid:
- Not drying the steak before cooking (moisture = no crust)
- Cooking on medium heat instead of high
- Flipping constantly
- Skipping the rest period

Once you’ve nailed the basics, you can play with extras like compound butter, pan sauces, or reverse searing. But honestly, salt, heat, and timing will get you 90% of the way there.
