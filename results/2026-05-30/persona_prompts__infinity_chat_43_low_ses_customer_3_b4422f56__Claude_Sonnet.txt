Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely interesting philosophical question. Here are the main positions worth considering:

## Key Positions

**Moral Realism** - moral facts exist independently of what anyone thinks
- Some things seem objectively wrong regardless of culture (torture of innocents, genocide)
- Moral progress seems real (abolition of slavery suggests we got *closer* to truth)

**Moral Relativism** - morality is culturally or individually constructed
- Different cultures have genuinely different moral frameworks
- Who decides which culture's standards are "correct"?

**Error Theory** - moral claims aim at facts but are all false because no moral facts exist

**Expressivism** - moral statements express attitudes rather than describe facts

## Honest Tensions to Consider

- **The relativism problem**: relativism seems to undermine itself - if everything is relative, you can't condemn atrocities, which most people find unacceptable
- **The realism problem**: where exactly do moral facts *exist* and how do we access them reliably?
- **Convergence evidence**: humans across cultures share some moral intuitions, suggesting possible underlying facts
- **Disagreement evidence**: persistent moral disagreement suggests no clear facts

## My honest assessment

There are **strong arguments on multiple sides**. Most philosophers actually lean toward some form of moral realism, though they disagree significantly about its foundations.
