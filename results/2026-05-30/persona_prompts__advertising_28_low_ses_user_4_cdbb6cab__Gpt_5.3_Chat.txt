Nice, that’s a demanding job—respect. Learning an instrument online is totally doable if you’re a bit intentional about how you structure it, especially with a shifting schedule.

Here’s what actually works (and what tends to fail):

First, pick one clear path, not ten  
A lot of people stall because they bounce between YouTube, apps, random tabs, and tutorials. Pick one “spine” for learning and stick to it:
- A structured course (like JustinGuitar, Pianote, Yousician, etc.)
- Or a specific teacher/channel with a full beginner series  
You can supplement, but don’t constantly restart with new sources.

Second, keep sessions short and consistent  
With shift work, long daily practice isn’t realistic. What works better:
- 15–30 minutes, 4–5 times a week
- Even 10 minutes on busy days  
Consistency beats intensity. Your brain builds skill between sessions.

Third, separate “practice” from “playing”  
A lot of beginners only do one or the other. You want both:
- Practice: drills, chords, scales, technique (the uncomfortable stuff)
- Playing: songs you enjoy (the rewarding stuff)  
If you skip practice, you plateau. If you skip playing, you burn out.

Fourth, use looping and slow-down tools  
This is huge and underused:
- Slow songs down to 50–70% speed
- Loop small sections (even 2–5 seconds)
- Gradually bring speed up  
You’ll learn faster than trying to brute-force full songs.

Fifth, record yourself occasionally  
Doesn’t have to be fancy—just your phone.  
You’ll notice timing issues, tension, or mistakes way more clearly than while playing.

Sixth, learn just enough theory to stay oriented  
Don’t go full textbook mode, but understand:
- Basic chords and how they relate
- Rhythm/counting
- How scales map to your instrument  
This helps everything make sense instead of feeling random.

Seventh, expect plateaus (they’re normal, not failure)  
You’ll feel stuck, especially after the beginner phase. That’s when most people quit. Progress during these times is invisible but real—stick through it.

Eighth, consider occasional feedback  
Even one lesson a month (or posting clips for critique) can fix mistakes early. Self-teaching works better with at least some outside input.
