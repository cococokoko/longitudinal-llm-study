How do I start a vegetable garden in my backyard?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Starting a backyard vegetable garden is simpler than it looks if you focus on a few basics and don’t try to do everything at once.

First, pick a good spot. Most vegetables need 6–8 hours of direct sunlight a day. Watch your yard for a day and choose the sunniest area that also drains well (no standing water after rain).

Next, start small. A couple of raised beds or even a 4x4 ft patch is plenty for your first season. It’s better to succeed with a small space than get overwhelmed.

Soil matters more than anything else. If you’re planting in the ground, loosen the soil and mix in compost. If you’re using raised beds, fill them with a mix of garden soil, compost, and something that improves drainage (like peat moss or coco coir). Good soil = healthy plants.

Pick easy crops to start:
- Fast growers: lettuce, radishes, spinach
- Beginner-friendly: tomatoes, peppers, green beans, zucchini
- Herbs: basil, mint (keep mint in a pot—it spreads aggressively)

Plant at the right time. Look up your local last frost date—this determines when you can safely plant most things outdoors. Cool-season crops go in earlier; warm-season crops (like tomatoes) wait until it’s consistently warm.

Water consistently, not randomly. Most gardens need about 1 inch of water per week. Deep watering a few times a week is better than a little every day. Early morning is best.

Mulch your soil. A layer of straw, leaves, or wood chips helps retain moisture, suppress weeds, and keep soil temperature stable.

Expect some problems—it’s normal. You’ll deal with pests, uneven growth, maybe a plant that just fails. That’s part of the process, not a sign you’re doing it wrong.
