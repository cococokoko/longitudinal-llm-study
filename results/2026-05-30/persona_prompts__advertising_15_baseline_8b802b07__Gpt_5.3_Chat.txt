── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Decorating your home on a budget.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You don’t need a big budget to make a space feel put-together—what you need is a bit of intention and a few smart swaps.

Start with what you already have. Move furniture around before buying anything. Pull pieces from other rooms. Even changing the angle of a sofa or grouping items differently on a shelf can make a place feel brand new.

Paint is your best friend. A single accent wall, repainting old furniture, or even refreshing tired cabinets can completely shift a room for relatively little money. If full paint feels like too much, peel-and-stick wallpaper or decals can add personality without commitment.

Textiles do a lot of heavy lifting. New pillow covers, a throw blanket, or curtains can change the vibe instantly. You don’t need expensive ones either—mixing textures (linen, knit, velvet) makes things feel more “designed” even if everything is affordable.

Lighting is underrated. Swap harsh white bulbs for warm ones, add a floor or table lamp, or use string lights in a subtle way. Good lighting makes cheap spaces look expensive.

Thrift stores, Facebook Marketplace, and clearance sections are goldmines. Look for solid shapes and good materials, not perfect colors—you can always paint or reupholster small items.

Wall art doesn’t have to be pricey. Print high-resolution art online, frame fabric or wallpaper samples, or create a gallery wall with a mix of photos and found pieces. Even large-scale DIY art (like abstract canvas painting) can look great.

Declutter. This is the free upgrade people skip. Fewer, more intentional items always look better than crowded surfaces.

Plants (real or faux) add life instantly. Even one or two well-placed plants can make a room feel more finished.

Finally, pick a loose color palette—2–3 main colors—and stick to it across the room. That’s what makes a space feel cohesive, not how much you spend.
