You don’t need expensive “superfoods” or complicated prep to eat well—what drives both cost and health is consistency, not novelty. A simple, repeatable system beats constantly searching for new recipes.

Here’s a practical way to approach it:

Start with a weekly template rather than individual meals. Think in building blocks:
- Protein: eggs, chicken thighs, canned tuna/salmon, beans, lentils, Greek yogurt
- Carbs: rice, oats, potatoes, whole-grain pasta, tortillas
- Vegetables: frozen mixes, carrots, cabbage, onions, spinach
- Fats/flavor: olive oil, peanut butter, basic spices, soy sauce, garlic

If you keep those stocked, you can assemble meals quickly without waste.

Batch cooking is where the savings really show up. Cook 2–3 base items twice a week:
- A pot of rice or quinoa  
- A protein (e.g., roasted chicken thighs or a pot of lentils)  
- A tray of roasted or sautéed vegetables  

Then mix and match instead of cooking from scratch every night.

A simple 5-day example:
- Breakfast: oats with fruit or eggs + toast  
- Lunch: rice bowl with protein + vegetables  
- Dinner: same components, different seasoning (e.g., stir-fry one night, taco bowl the next)  

This keeps variety without increasing cost or effort.

Cost control tips that actually move the needle:
- Buy store brands and larger sizes for staples  
- Lean on frozen vegetables—they’re cheaper, last longer, and are nutritionally solid  
- Use meat as a component, not the centerpiece (e.g., stretch chicken across multiple meals)  
- Plan around what’s on sale, not fixed recipes  
- Avoid single-use ingredients that sit in your fridge and die
