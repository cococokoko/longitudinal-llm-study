That’s a big question, and there isn’t a single agreed “biggest” controversy—but one issue that sits right at the center of moral philosophy is this:

Are moral values objectively real, or are they just human-made?

In other words:
- Moral realism says some things are truly right or wrong, independent of what anyone thinks (for example, “torturing innocent people is wrong” would be true even if a society approved of it).
- Moral relativism or subjectivism says morality depends on culture, individuals, or circumstances—so there’s no universal right or wrong, just perspectives.

This debate matters because it shapes how we judge everything else. If morality is objective, then we can say some actions or systems are truly unjust, no matter where or when they happen. If it’s subjective, then moral disagreements become more like differences in taste or tradition, and it’s harder to claim any universal standard.

A close second major controversy is how we should decide what’s right:
- Consequences (utilitarianism: maximize happiness, minimize suffering)
- Duties/rules (deontology: some actions are wrong no matter the outcome)
- Character (virtue ethics: focus on being a good person rather than rules or outcomes)

These two layers—“is morality real?” and “how do we decide right and wrong?”—drive most of the biggest moral disagreements you’ll see in real life, from workplace fairness to global politics.
