── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You’ve got a lot going on, so the goal here is simple, high-impact changes that don’t add much effort day to day.

First, the biggest win for most homes is better filtration. If you have a central HVAC system, use a good-quality filter (look for MERV 11–13 if your system can handle it) and change it regularly—every 2–3 months, or more often if you have pets. If you don’t have central air, a portable HEPA air purifier in the main living area and your child’s bedroom makes a noticeable difference, especially for dust, allergens, and smoke.

Second, control the main pollution sources:
- Cooking: use your range hood every time you cook, especially with gas stoves. If there’s no vent hood, even cracking a window helps.
- Cleaning products: switch to simpler, low-fragrance or fragrance-free options. Strong scents often mean more airborne chemicals.
- Smoking or vaping indoors: if this applies, moving it fully outside is one of the highest-impact changes you can make.

Third, manage moisture. Mold and dust mites thrive in humidity.
- Aim for indoor humidity around 30–50%.
- Use a bathroom fan during and after showers.
- If your place tends to feel damp, a small dehumidifier can help.

Fourth, keep dust under control without overcomplicating your routine:
- Vacuum weekly if you can (a vacuum with a HEPA filter is ideal).
- Wash bedding regularly in hot water.
- A quick damp wipe of surfaces beats dry dusting, which just kicks particles back into the air.

Fifth, bring in fresh air when outdoor air quality is decent. Even 5–10 minutes of cross-ventilation (windows on opposite sides) can refresh indoor air. If you live somewhere with pollution or wildfire smoke, check an air quality app before opening windows and rely more on filtration on bad days.

A couple of smaller but helpful tweaks:
- Take shoes off at the door to avoid tracking in pollutants.
- Avoid candles and incense as regular habits—they add fine particles to the air.
- If you’re using air fresheners, consider skipping them; they often add more than they help.

If you want a “minimal effort” setup: one good HEPA purifier in the main room, a solid HVAC filter changed on schedule, always using the bathroom fan and kitchen vent, and a simple weekly vacuum—that alone covers most of the benefit without adding much stress to your routine.
