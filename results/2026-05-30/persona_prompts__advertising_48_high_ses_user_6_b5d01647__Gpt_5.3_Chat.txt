If you want organic gardening that actually performs (good yields, low headaches), it comes down to managing systems rather than reacting to problems.

Start with soil as your primary asset. Healthy soil replaces most inputs. Prioritize:
- Regular addition of organic matter: compost, well-rotted manure, leaf mold. Aim for consistency more than volume spikes.
- Minimal disturbance: avoid frequent tilling, which breaks soil structure and microbial networks.
- Mulching: keeps moisture stable, suppresses weeds, and feeds soil as it breaks down.

Think in terms of closed loops where possible. Reuse garden waste (compost), capture rainwater, and rotate crops so you’re not depleting the same nutrients repeatedly.

Crop planning matters more than most people expect:
- Rotate plant families (e.g., don’t follow tomatoes with peppers; both are nightshades and share pests/diseases).
- Mix crops instead of monocropping—interplanting reduces pest spread and improves resilience.
- Time your planting to avoid peak pest cycles where possible.

For fertility, organic doesn’t mean “no inputs,” it means slower, biology-driven inputs:
- Compost is the baseline.
- Supplement with things like bone meal (phosphorus), kelp meal (micronutrients), or blood meal (nitrogen) when needed.
- Legumes (beans, peas, clover) fix nitrogen naturally—use them strategically.

Pest management is where people either overreact or do nothing. The middle path works:
- Focus on prevention: healthy soil and diverse planting reduce outbreaks.
- Encourage beneficial insects (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps) with flowering plants like dill, alyssum, fennel.
- Use physical barriers early (row covers, netting).
- If intervention is needed, start with least disruptive options: neem oil, insecticidal soap, Bt—used sparingly and targeted.

Watering is often inefficient:
- Deep, infrequent watering beats frequent shallow watering.
- Morning watering reduces disease risk.
- Drip irrigation is ideal if you want consistency and efficiency.

Weeds are a signal, not just a nuisance:
- Bare soil invites them—mulch heavily.
- Don’t let them seed; that’s 90% of the battle.
- Some “weeds” improve soil—learn which ones are doing useful work.

Finally, treat it like a portfolio:
- Expect some crops to fail each season.
- Diversify what you grow.
- Track what works (simple notes beat memory).
