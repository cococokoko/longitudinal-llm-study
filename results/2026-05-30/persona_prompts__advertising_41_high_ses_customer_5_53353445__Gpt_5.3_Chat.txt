If you need to move your credit score quickly, you’re really trying to influence a few specific inputs the scoring models care about most. The fastest gains usually come from fixing utilization and eliminating any reporting errors.

Start with utilization—this is the biggest lever you can pull in weeks, not months. If your credit cards are reporting high balances, even if you pay in full each month, your score can look worse than it should. Aim to get reported balances below 30% of each card’s limit, and ideally under 10%. A quick tactic: make a payment *before* the statement closes, not just by the due date. That way the lower balance is what gets reported.

Next, check for errors on your credit reports (all three bureaus). If there’s anything inaccurate—late payments, accounts that aren’t yours, wrong balances—dispute them. Legitimate removals can cause a fast bump.

If you have any missed payments, get current immediately. A single late payment hurts, but being currently delinquent hurts more. If the late payment is recent, you can try a goodwill request with the lender—this isn’t guaranteed, but it occasionally works, especially if your history is otherwise clean.

If you’re near your limits and can’t pay balances down quickly, another lever is increasing your credit limits. Requesting a limit increase (preferably a soft pull) can lower your utilization instantly if approved.

Avoid opening new accounts right now unless you have a thin file. New inquiries and reduced average account age can drag your score in the short term.

One less obvious move: if you have access to a well-managed, old credit card (for example, via a partner or family member), being added as an authorized user can sometimes boost your score quickly—*if* that account has low utilization and perfect payment history.

Finally, timing matters. Scores update when lenders report, typically monthly. So if you pay down balances now, you may see changes within 30–45 days.
