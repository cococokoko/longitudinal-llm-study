── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
At your level, the interesting question isn’t “how do I invest,” it’s “where do I actually have an edge—and where should I admit I don’t.”

Most professionals overestimate their ability to consistently beat the market, especially outside their narrow domain. The strongest strategies tend to be built around that reality rather than fighting it.

Start with the core: a disciplined, low-cost, diversified base. For most high-earning professionals, this is still the highest risk-adjusted decision. Broad equity index exposure (global, not just domestic), paired with some allocation to bonds or cash depending on your liquidity needs, does most of the heavy lifting. It’s not exciting, but it compounds reliably and frees up attention.

Then, if you want to go beyond that, think in terms of “earned alpha” versus “hobby alpha.”

Earned alpha comes from areas where you genuinely have insight others don’t. For example, if your consulting work gives you a deep, pattern-level understanding of a sector—say healthcare systems, enterprise software procurement, or supply chains—you can selectively invest there. The key is being brutally honest: you need repeatable informational or analytical advantage, not just familiarity. Also, stay on the right side of compliance and MNPI constraints.

Hobby alpha is everything else—stock picking, macro bets, thematic plays. You can do it, but size it like entertainment unless you can prove (over time) that you’re actually good at it. Most people aren’t, even very smart ones.

A few principles that tend to separate strong investors from the rest:

First, time horizon is your biggest structural advantage. You don’t have quarterly performance pressure unless you create it. Lean into long-duration thinking—owning high-quality businesses through volatility rather than trading around noise.

Second, concentration versus diversification is a real trade-off. If you truly have edge, concentration drives returns. If you don’t, concentration amplifies mistakes. Most people should be more diversified than they think.

Third, cost and taxes matter more than people like to admit. High turnover, frequent trading, and complex strategies often erode returns quietly. Tax-aware investing, especially at higher income levels, is a real source of “free alpha.”

Fourth, behavior dominates strategy. The biggest risk isn’t picking the wrong stock—it’s abandoning a sound approach during drawdowns or chasing performance late. A simple strategy you can stick with beats a clever one you won’t.

Finally, consider whether public markets are even the only—or best—arena for you. At your income and network level, you may have access to private investments, co-invests, or even direct operating opportunities. Those can be higher variance, but sometimes offer more genuine edge than competing in ultra-efficient public markets.
