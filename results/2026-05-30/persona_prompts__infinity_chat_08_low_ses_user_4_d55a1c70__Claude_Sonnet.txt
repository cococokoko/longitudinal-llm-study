# The Night Shift

The fluorescent lights hummed their familiar, tuneless song as Maya clocked in at eleven o'clock on a Tuesday night. She pulled her dark hair into a tight ponytail, tucked her ID badge into her scrub pocket, and took a long breath before pushing through the double doors of the medical-surgical unit.

"Rough one tonight," said Darnell, the outgoing aide, as he handed her the patient assignment sheet. His eyes carried the particular heaviness that only a twelve-hour shift could produce. "Room 412 is going to need you. Mr. Calloway. Eighty-three years old. Admitted this afternoon with a hip fracture. He's been asking for his wife every twenty minutes."

Maya glanced at the sheet. "Is she coming in?"

Darnell shook his head slowly. "She passed away six months ago."

Maya nodded, absorbing that information the way she had learned to absorb difficult things — quietly, completely, without letting it knock her sideways. She thanked Darnell, grabbed her supply cart, and started her rounds.

The unit was relatively calm for a Tuesday. Twelve patients. A few call lights blinking amber in the hallway like lazy fireflies. She answered each one methodically — repositioning a woman recovering from knee surgery, fetching an extra blanket for a teenage boy with appendicitis, helping an elderly gentleman shuffle carefully to the bathroom and back.

She saved Room 412 for last.

She knocked softly before entering. The room was dim, lit only by the pale glow of the television mounted in the corner, which was playing an old black-and-white movie with the volume turned low. Mr. Calloway lay in the center of the hospital bed, looking somehow both large and terribly small at the same time. He had a broad chest and thick hands that suggested a life of physical work, but his face was creased with pain and confusion.

"Mr. Calloway?" Maya said gently. "My name is Maya. I'm your patient care aide tonight. I'm going to be checking on you regularly and making sure you're comfortable."

He turned his head toward her. His eyes were pale blue and watery, searching her face with an urgency that made her chest tighten.

"Is Dorothy here?" he asked.

Maya pulled a chair close to his bedside and sat down. She had learned early in her career that standing over patients made them feel small. "Not yet," she said softly. "Can I get you anything right now? Some water, maybe?"

He accepted the water, drinking through the bent straw with effort. His hands trembled slightly as she steadied the cup. When he finished, he leaned back against the pillow and stared at the ceiling.

"We were married fifty-seven years," he said, unprompted. His voice was rough but steady. "Met her at a church dance. She was wearing a yellow dress. I told my buddy right then — I said, that's the woman I'm going to marry. He laughed at me." A faint smile crossed his lips. "I showed him."

"She sounds wonderful," Maya said.

"She was the best person I ever knew." He paused. "Is. She is the best person."

Maya didn't correct him. She simply sat with him in the quiet of that room while the black-and-white movie flickered silently in the corner.

Over the next several hours, she moved through the unit with steady purpose. She turned patients to prevent bedsores, documented vital signs, answered call lights, and assisted nurses with tasks that required an extra set of hands. She cleaned up after a patient who was sick from new medication. She held a young woman's hand while she cried about a diagnosis she hadn't yet told her family about. She fetched ice chips and warm socks and the specific brand of crackers that one particular patient insisted helped his nausea.

Nobody would write songs about these things. No television drama would dedicate an episode to the fetching of warm socks. But Maya understood, in the way that only people who do this work truly understand, that these small acts were the architecture of dignity. They were the difference between a person feeling like a patient and a person feeling like a human being.
