# The Cloud Who Couldn't Rain

High above the rolling green hills, there lived a small cloud named Pip.

All the other clouds in the sky were very good at their job. Big clouds, fluffy clouds, dark clouds — they all knew exactly how to gather up their water and let it fall as rain. Farmers smiled when they came. Flowers stretched up their petals. Frogs leaped into puddles with happy splashes.

But Pip couldn't do it.

Every morning, Pip would puff up as big as possible, scrunch up tight, and try with all his might. Nothing came out. Not a single drop.

"Don't worry," said an old gray cloud named Mabel, drifting slowly past. "It'll happen when it's ready to happen."

But Pip did worry. He watched the other clouds rain and felt very left out.

One afternoon, Pip floated over a small garden at the edge of town. A little girl named Rosa was kneeling in the dirt, carefully watering her sunflowers with a tiny red can. She filled it from a barrel, walked to each flower, and poured. Then she walked back and did it all over again.

It took a very long time.

Pip watched her work. The sun was hot. Rosa wiped her forehead and kept going.

"She really loves those flowers," Pip thought.

Something strange happened then. Pip felt a warm, full feeling spreading through him — not like puffing up on purpose, but something softer. He thought about how tired Rosa looked. He thought about how much she cared. He thought about the flowers reaching toward the sun, thirsty and hopeful.

And then, very quietly, it began.

*Drip.*

Pip looked down in amazement.

*Drip. Drip. Drip.*

Rosa looked up. She held out her hand and laughed — a bright, surprised laugh that floated all the way up to the sky.

"Rain!" she shouted. "Real rain!"

She spun around with her arms out while Pip rained down on the sunflowers, the garden path, the little red watering can, and the tip of Rosa's nose.

It wasn't a big storm. It wasn't a dramatic downpour. It was a gentle, quiet rain — just enough.

When it was over, Rosa looked up at the sky.

"Thank you, cloud," she said.

Pip felt so light he thought he might float all the way to the moon.

Old Mabel drifted over, wearing what looked very much like a smile.

"See?" she said. "I told you."

"But how did it finally happen?" Pip asked.

Mabel thought for a moment. "You stopped thinking about yourself," she said, "and started thinking about someone else."

Pip looked down at the garden one more time. The sunflowers stood tall and bright, their yellow faces glowing in the afternoon light.

From that day on, Pip rained often — not because he was trying to, but because he was paying attention.

And that made all the difference.
