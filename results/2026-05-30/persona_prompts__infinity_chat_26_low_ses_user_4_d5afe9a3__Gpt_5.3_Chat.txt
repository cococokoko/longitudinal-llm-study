Working in a hospital, you probably already see how stress, isolation, and information overload affect people—those same pressures make social media a really efficient environment for extremism to grow. It’s not just about “bad people posting bad ideas”; it’s about how the systems are set up.

A few big mechanisms:

- Recommendation algorithms push people toward more intense content  
If someone watches or engages with mildly controversial material, platforms often suggest slightly more provocative versions to keep attention. Over time, that can move people from curiosity → skepticism → more rigid, extreme beliefs without them realizing the shift.

- Echo chambers and filtering  
People tend to follow others who agree with them. Platforms reinforce that by showing similar content, so users rarely encounter strong, good-faith disagreement. Without opposing views, beliefs can become more absolute and less reality-checked.

- Emotional amplification  
Content that triggers anger, fear, or outrage spreads faster. Extremist messaging often uses those emotions very deliberately, so it gets boosted more than calm, nuanced discussion.

- Identity and belonging  
Extremist communities often offer a strong sense of identity (“we’re the ones who see the truth”) and belonging, especially to people who feel overlooked or disconnected. That social reinforcement makes beliefs stick harder.

- Gradual normalization  
Ideas that would seem extreme at first get introduced in softer, coded, or “just asking questions” ways. Repeated exposure makes them feel less shocking over time.

- Misinformation ecosystems  
False or misleading claims can circulate quickly, especially when they’re simple, emotionally charged, or confirm what people already suspect. Corrections rarely spread as far or as fast.

- Anonymity and reduced accountability  
People may say or endorse more extreme things online than they would in person, which shifts what feels “normal” within those spaces.

- Crisis and uncertainty  
During events like pandemics (something you’ve likely experienced up close), people look for explanations and control. Extremist narratives often provide simple, emotionally satisfying answers, even if they’re inaccurate.

If you’re thinking about this from a practical standpoint—like dealing with patients, coworkers, or even family—it can help to focus less on “debating facts” and more on maintaining trust and rapport. People are more likely to reconsider beliefs when they don’t feel attacked or dismissed.
