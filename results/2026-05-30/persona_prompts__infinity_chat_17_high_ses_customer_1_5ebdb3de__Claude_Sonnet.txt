# The Millhaven Cipher

## A Mystery

---

The body was found on a Tuesday, which Deputy Carla Reyes always thought was the worst day for anything to happen. Not dramatic enough for a Monday, not close enough to the weekend to feel like an accident.

Gerald Fitch lay face-down in the shallow end of Millhaven Creek, his good church shoes still on his feet.

---

## Chapter One: Still Water

Millhaven had 2,300 residents, one stoplight, and the kind of quiet that newcomers mistook for peace. Carla had grown up here. She knew better. Quiet in a small town wasn't the absence of noise. It was the sound of people deciding what not to say.

She crouched beside the body while Sheriff Dale Pruitt stood back, thumbs hooked in his belt, looking at the tree line like it might offer an explanation.

"Heart attack," Dale said. "Man was seventy-two."

"Seventy-two-year-old men don't usually walk into creeks at three in the morning," Carla said.

"Maybe he sleepwalked."

"His shoes are tied in double knots, Dale."

The sheriff had no answer for that.

Gerald Fitch had been the town's only pharmacist for forty years. He'd filled prescriptions for every family in Millhaven, watched children grow into adults, attended every funeral, every graduation. He was the kind of man people described as *fixtures* — as though he were part of the architecture rather than a person.

Carla photographed everything before the county coroner arrived. She photographed the creek bank, the disturbed mud, the strange absence of a struggle. She photographed the small leather notebook tucked into Gerald's breast pocket, its pages swollen with creek water but still legible.

Numbers. Pages and pages of numbers.

---

## Chapter Two: The Pharmacist's Ledger

The county medical examiner was a compact, precise woman named Dr. Anita Solis who drove two hours from the city and arrived looking like she'd slept perfectly, which Carla found irritating at six in the morning.

"Blunt force trauma to the posterior cranium," Dr. Solis said, not looking up from her examination. "Significant. Someone struck him before he entered the water. The drowning was secondary."

"So it's a homicide."

"It's a homicide."

Dale Pruitt went pale. Millhaven hadn't had a murder in thirty years, and that one had been straightforward — a domestic dispute that resolved itself in a terrible way. This was different. This had *intention* written all over it.

Carla spent the afternoon at Gerald's pharmacy, a narrow building wedged between the hardware store and the diner on Main Street. His assistant, a nervous young man named Tommy Briggs, let her in with shaking hands.

"He was here yesterday until six," Tommy said. "Normal day. He seemed... I don't know. Distracted, maybe."

"Did he have any arguments recently? Anyone angry with him?"

Tommy hesitated. That hesitation lasted exactly one second too long.

"No," he said. "Everyone loved Mr. Fitch."

Carla looked at the prescription records, the inventory logs, the ordinary machinery of a small-town pharmacy. Everything was meticulous. Gerald Fitch had been a careful man.

Which made the notebook stranger still.

She'd had the pages carefully dried and photographed. The numbers appeared in groups — three digits, a dash, four digits. Hundreds of entries spanning what looked like several years. No names. No dates. No context.

She showed them to her cousin Marcus, who taught mathematics at the regional high school and had the useful habit of finding puzzles irresistible.

"It's not a standard cipher," he said, turning the photographs in his hands. "But look — these groupings repeat. See this sequence? 247-3891. It appears eleven times. Whatever it represents, it's significant."

"Could it be prescription numbers?"

Marcus looked at her. "You're the detective."

She drove back to the pharmacy and pulled the prescription records. The numbering system didn't match. But when she cross-referenced the pharmacy's *supplier invoices* — the records of what medications were ordered and received — the numbers aligned perfectly.

Gerald Fitch had been tracking something coming in and going out of his pharmacy. Something that didn't match the official records.
