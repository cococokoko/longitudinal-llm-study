── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely contested philosophical question. Here's an honest breakdown:

## Strong Cases for Objectivity
- Some moral claims seem clearly true regardless of opinion (torturing children for fun is wrong)
- Moral progress appears real - we didn't just *change*, we seemingly *improved*
- Mathematical and logical truths are objective without being physical
- Moral disagreement implies people are arguing about something real

## Strong Cases for Subjectivity
- Significant cross-cultural moral variation exists
- Moral intuitions correlate with upbringing, culture, neurology
- No clear mechanism for how moral facts would exist independently
- Evolutionary psychology explains moral feelings without requiring objective facts

## Key Distinctions Worth Making
- **Relative vs. subjective** - something can vary by culture without being merely personal preference
- **Epistemology vs. metaphysics** - whether moral facts exist vs. whether we can know them are separate questions
- **Moral realism has varieties** - naturalist, non-naturalist, constructivist approaches

## My honest assessment
The question is genuinely hard. Most serious philosophers take moral realism seriously rather than dismissing it. The strongest position is probably that **some moral claims are more defensible than others**, even if complete objectivity is difficult to establish.
