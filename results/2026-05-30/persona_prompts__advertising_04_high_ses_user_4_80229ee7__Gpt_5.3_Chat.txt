Nice—those two worlds can actually complement each other really well if you’re intentional about it.

The biggest constraint you’re going to face isn’t ideas, it’s bandwidth and conflict-of-interest risk. So the “right” kind of online business for you is one that is:
- Asynchronous (doesn’t require you to be constantly present)
- Leverages your existing expertise/reputation
- Ethically clean relative to your clinical/research role
- Scalable without needing a large team right away

A few directions that tend to work well for physician-scientists:

1. High-trust knowledge products  
Things like short courses, paid newsletters, or practical guides aimed at a very specific audience (e.g., early-career researchers, clinicians transitioning to research, grant writing, study design, statistical intuition).  
These are slow to build but extremely aligned with your background and can become durable assets.

2. Niche consulting (productized)  
Instead of open-ended consulting, package it:
- “Grant Specific Aims Review – $X”
- “Study design audit for startups”
- “Medical/scientific due diligence for investors”  
You can cap volume and keep control of your time.

3. Digital tools/templates  
If you’ve built internal tools (protocol templates, data pipelines, literature triage systems), turning those into sellable resources or lightweight SaaS can work. This is more effort upfront but scalable.

4. Curated intelligence  
A sharply focused newsletter or briefing (e.g., “what actually matters in X field this month”) aimed at clinicians, VCs, or industry. Your edge is filtering signal from noise.

5. Education for a non-academic audience  
Translating complex science for:
- founders
- policy folks
- patients (careful with claims/compliance here)  
This can pair with speaking, courses, or memberships.

A few things to be very deliberate about early:

- Conflict of interest: check your institution’s policies before you even pick a niche. Some topics or client types may be restricted.
- Scope creep: if it depends on your real-time availability, it’ll collide with your lab quickly.
- Positioning: don’t go broad (“medicine + science”). Go oddly specific. That’s what converts online.
- Distribution: your network is your unfair advantage—colleagues, trainees, collaborators. Start there rather than trying to “build an audience from scratch.”
