If you’re teaching or revisiting a first course in Design and Analysis of Algorithms, the “best” books really depend on how you want to balance rigor, intuition, and breadth. A strong mix typically includes one primary text and one or two complementary perspectives.

Here are the most consistently valuable choices (still very current in 2023–2025 curricula):

**1. CLRS — *Introduction to Algorithms* (Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein)**  
Still the canonical reference. It’s encyclopedic, mathematically clean, and covers essentially the full core: asymptotics, divide-and-conquer, greedy, dynamic programming, graph algorithms, NP-completeness, etc.  
- Strength: breadth + rigor; great as a long-term reference  
- Weakness: can feel dry or dense for first exposure  

**2. Kleinberg & Tardos — *Algorithm Design***  
Arguably the best “first serious algorithms” book pedagogically. It emphasizes design paradigms and intuition, with well-chosen problems and narrative explanations.  
- Strength: clarity, structure, and problem-driven teaching  
- Weakness: less comprehensive than CLRS  

**3. Dasgupta, Papadimitriou, Vazirani — *Algorithms***  
A more modern, elegant, and slightly lighter alternative to CLRS. It introduces ideas with a cleaner conceptual flow and fewer technical overheads early on.  
- Strength: conceptual clarity; good for theory-minded students  
- Weakness: fewer exercises and less depth in some areas  

**4. Skiena — *The Algorithm Design Manual***  
Excellent as a companion text. It focuses on intuition, real-world problem modeling, and includes a very practical “catalog” of algorithmic techniques.  
- Strength: practical insight and “how to think” about problems  
- Weakness: less formal analysis  

**5. Erickson — *Algorithms* (free online book)**  
Widely used in top programs (UIUC). It’s rigorous but unusually engaging, with carefully crafted explanations and exercises.  
- Strength: depth + personality; freely available  
- Weakness: idiosyncratic ordering; not as standardized as CLRS  

---

**How I’d combine them (for a course like “Algorithms I”):**  
- Primary text: Kleinberg & Tardos *or* Dasgupta et al.  
- Reference: CLRS  
- Supplement: Erickson (for deeper understanding) or Skiena (for applications)

If you’re leaning more theoretical (proof-heavy, asymptotics, reductions), CLRS + Dasgupta is a strong pairing. If you want a cleaner pedagogical arc for students, Kleinberg & Tardos is hard to beat.
