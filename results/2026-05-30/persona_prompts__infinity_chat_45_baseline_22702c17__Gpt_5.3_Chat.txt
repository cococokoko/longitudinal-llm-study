── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Is moral objective or subjective?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: people disagree because there are good arguments on both sides, and neither side fully settles the issue.

If you mean “are moral truths true independent of what anyone thinks?” that’s moral objectivism. It says things like “torturing someone for fun is wrong” would be true even if a whole society approved of it. The appeal is that it explains why we criticize injustice across cultures and why moral progress makes sense.

Subjectivism (or relativism) says moral judgments depend on human attitudes—individual or cultural. On this view, “wrong” really means something like “disapproved of (by me/us).” It explains why moral codes vary so much and why disagreement persists.

There are also middle positions. Some argue morality is constructed from shared human needs and rational agreement (so not purely objective, but not arbitrary either). Others think there are objective facts about well-being that ground morality, even if we discover them imperfectly.

Where this leaves things in practice: most people talk and act as if at least some moral claims are objective (e.g., condemning slavery or genocide), while also recognizing that many norms are culturally shaped (like dress codes or etiquette).

If you want a clean takeaway: morality isn’t obviously objective in the way physics is, but treating core moral claims as more than mere opinion seems necessary to make sense of criticism, rights, and justice.
