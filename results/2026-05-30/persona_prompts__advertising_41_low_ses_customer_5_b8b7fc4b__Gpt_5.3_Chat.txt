How can I improve my credit score quickly?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You can get a noticeable boost fairly quickly, but it depends on what’s currently dragging your score down. Here are the fastest-impact moves, especially if you’re juggling a lot of household expenses:

First, focus on credit utilization (this is a big one).  
If your cards are carrying balances, try to get each one below about 30% of its limit—under 10% is even better. If you can’t pay everything off, spreading payments across cards or making a mid-cycle payment before the statement closes can help your reported balance drop faster.

Second, don’t miss a single payment going forward.  
Payment history matters more than anything else. Even one recent late payment hurts. If you’ve missed any recently, getting current and staying current is critical. Setting up autopay for at least the minimum can protect you.

Third, ask for a credit limit increase.  
If your income supports it, a higher limit lowers your utilization instantly without needing to pay anything down. Just make sure it’s a soft inquiry (many issuers do this automatically).

Fourth, check your credit report for errors.  
You can pull your reports for free and look for incorrect late payments, duplicate accounts, or wrong balances. Disputing errors can sometimes give a relatively quick bump once corrected.

Fifth, consider becoming an authorized user.  
If a trusted family member has a long-standing card with low balances and perfect payment history, being added can help your score fairly quickly.

A couple things that don’t help fast (but people often try anyway):  
Opening new accounts (can drop your score short-term), closing old cards (can hurt utilization and history), or carrying a small balance “for scoring” (not necessary).
