Getting really good at steak isn’t about fancy tricks—it’s about control: heat, timing, and knowing what you’re looking for.

Start with the meat itself. Thicker cuts (about 1–1.5 inches) are much easier to cook well than thin ones. Ribeye, strip, or sirloin are solid choices. If you can, take it out of the fridge 20–40 minutes before cooking so it’s not ice-cold in the center.

Salt matters more than anything else. Season generously with salt at least 30 minutes before cooking (or right before it hits the pan if you’re short on time). Pepper is fine, but salt is doing the heavy lifting.

Use a heavy pan—cast iron if you’ve got it—and get it properly hot. Like, just shy of smoking. Add a high-smoke-point oil (canola, avocado, etc.), then lay the steak down away from you.

Now the part people mess up: don’t keep flipping or poking it constantly. Let it sit long enough to build a crust—usually 2–4 minutes depending on thickness—then flip. After flipping, you can add butter, garlic, and herbs and baste if you want to level it up.

For doneness:
- Rare: very soft, cool red center  
- Medium rare: warm red center (this is the sweet spot for most cuts)  
- Medium: pink center, firmer  
- Well done: no pink, but easy to overcook  

If you can, use a thermometer:
- 130°F (54°C): medium rare  
- 140°F (60°C): medium  

Pull it off about 5 degrees early—it keeps cooking while resting.

Rest the steak for 5–10 minutes before cutting. This keeps the juices from running out.
