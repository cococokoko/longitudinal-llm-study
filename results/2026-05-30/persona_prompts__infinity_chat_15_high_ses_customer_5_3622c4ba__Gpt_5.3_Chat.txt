There isn’t a single “best” trading strategy—there are a few that consistently work over time, and the right one depends on your time horizon, risk tolerance, and how much attention you want to devote.

For most people (including very sophisticated ones), the highest-performing approach over time is surprisingly simple:

Long-term, low-cost investing  
Buy diversified assets (like broad index funds or ETFs), hold them for years, and avoid frequent trading. This works because markets tend to rise over time, and costs + mistakes from active trading compound against you.

If you’re specifically asking about *trading* (shorter-term, more active), here are the main archetypes and what actually matters:

1. Trend following  
You buy assets that are already going up and sell those going down. This works because markets exhibit momentum. It requires discipline and risk management, not prediction.

2. Mean reversion  
You bet that extreme moves will snap back. This can work in stable markets but gets crushed during strong trends.

3. Event-driven  
Trading around earnings, macro events, or news. Edge here comes from speed, information processing, or differentiated insight—not easy to sustain.

4. Quant/systematic  
Rule-based strategies using data and automation. This is where most professional “edge” lives now, but it requires infrastructure and constant refinement.

Across all of these, the things that actually separate success from failure are pretty consistent:

- Risk management matters more than entry timing  
- Position sizing is critical (don’t let one trade kill you)  
- Costs (fees, spreads, taxes) quietly erode returns  
- Emotional discipline beats cleverness  
- Most short-term trading strategies degrade once widely adopted  

If you’re thinking at an executive level, the real question is less “what strategy” and more:

Where do I have a durable edge, if any?

If the honest answer is “none,” then active trading becomes more like entertainment than a reliable return engine—and you’re usually better off allocating most capital to long-term compounding and treating trading as a small, controlled-risk sleeve.
