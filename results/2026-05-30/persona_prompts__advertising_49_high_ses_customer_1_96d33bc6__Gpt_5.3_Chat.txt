How to improve home WiFi?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You can usually get a big improvement without doing anything exotic—just fix the common weak points.

First, placement matters more than people think. Put the router roughly central in the home, elevated (a shelf, not the floor), and out in the open. Avoid closets, metal racks, and corners of the house. Wi‑Fi behaves more like light than sound—walls, plumbing, and appliances block it.

Second, check whether your issue is coverage or raw speed. If you get great speed standing next to the router but poor performance in other rooms, you don’t need a faster plan—you need better coverage. That’s where a mesh system (e.g., Eero, Orbi, Google Nest) is far more reliable than a single router or old-style extenders. For most homes, 2–3 nodes placed strategically will eliminate dead zones.

Third, make sure your hardware isn’t the bottleneck. If your router is more than ~4–5 years old, it’s likely worth upgrading to Wi‑Fi 6 or 6E. You don’t need the most expensive model, but very cheap ones often struggle under multiple devices.

Fourth, use the right band.  
- 5 GHz (or 6 GHz if you have it): faster, shorter range → use this for most devices  
- 2.4 GHz: slower, longer range → good for far rooms or smart devices  

Fifth, reduce interference. Nearby networks (especially in apartments) can overlap channels. Most modern routers auto-select channels, but if performance is inconsistent, manually choosing a less congested channel can help.

Sixth, wire what you can. Anything stationary and bandwidth-heavy (TVs, gaming consoles, desktop computers) should be on Ethernet if possible. Every device you take off Wi‑Fi improves performance for everything else.

Finally, sanity check your internet plan vs reality. Run a speed test directly next to the router. If that’s already slow, your issue is your ISP or plan—not your Wi‑Fi.
