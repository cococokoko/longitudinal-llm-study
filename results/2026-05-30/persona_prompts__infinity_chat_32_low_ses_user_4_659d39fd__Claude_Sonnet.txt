# The Orderly's Apprentice

Marcus had worked the night shift at Riverside General for eleven years. He knew every creak of the linoleum floors, every rhythm of the ventilators, every face that passed through the double doors at 2 AM.

He was good at his job. Quiet, steady, reliable.

Nobody expected what happened on a Tuesday.

---

It started with the old man in Room 14.

He had been admitted with no identification, no emergency contact, and no explanation for why his vital signs read perfectly normal despite the fact that he appeared to be approximately one hundred and forty years old. The attending physician had ordered every test imaginable and received results that made her sit down heavily in her chair and stare at the wall for a long moment.

Marcus was assigned to check on him every hour.

The first visit, the old man was sleeping.

The second visit, he was watching Marcus with eyes the color of deep water.

"You have careful hands," the old man said. His voice sounded like wind through old library stacks.

"Thank you, sir." Marcus adjusted the blanket. Checked the IV line. Noted the time on his clipboard. "Can I get you anything? Water? Are you comfortable?"

"I am dying," the old man said pleasantly.

Marcus paused. He had heard those words before, in many different tones. Frightened. Angry. Resigned. Peaceful. This was the first time he had heard them delivered like a simple weather report.

"I'm sorry to hear that," Marcus said carefully.

"Don't be. I have had a very long run." The old man tilted his head. "Sit down, young man."

"I have other patients to—"

"Sit down."

Marcus sat.

---

The old man's name, he explained, was Aldric. He had been born in a village that no longer existed, in a country that had changed its name four times since his birth. He had spent the better part of a century learning things that most people had decided were not real.

"Magic," Marcus said flatly.

"That word will do," Aldric agreed.

"Sir, it's three in the morning and I have—"

"You have a gift," Aldric interrupted. "You have always had it. You simply never had a name for it." He studied Marcus with those deep water eyes. "You know when a patient is frightened before they say a word. You know when pain is being hidden. You walked into Room 7 tonight before the monitor alarmed because something told you to. Yes?"

Marcus went still.

That had happened. Mrs. Okafor in Room 7 had been in the early stages of respiratory distress. He had caught it twelve seconds before the alarm. He had told himself it was experience. Intuition. Pattern recognition.

"That's just nursing instinct," Marcus said. "Any good aide develops—"

"It is the beginning of something considerably larger," Aldric said. "And I am running out of time to pass it along."

---

Marcus did not believe him.

He believed him a little more on the third visit, when Aldric described the exact contents of Marcus's apartment without ever having been there. The stack of paperback novels on the nightstand. The photograph of his mother on the refrigerator. The broken window latch he kept meaning to fix.

He believed him considerably more on the fourth visit, when Aldric pressed something into his palm. It felt like a smooth river stone, but when Marcus looked down, his hand was empty.

"It's inside you now," Aldric said. "Don't panic."

"What is—"

"Knowledge. Just knowledge. It will take a few days to settle." The old man leaned back against his pillow. "Think of it like a software update. Uncomfortable. Occasionally disorienting. But necessary."

"You know what software is?"

"I am old, not ignorant." Aldric almost smiled. "I have been updating myself for a very long time."

---

The disorientation started on the drive home.

Marcus sat at a red light and suddenly knew, with absolute certainty, that the woman in the car beside him had a slow leak in her rear left tire and would have a blowout on the highway if she didn't stop. He rolled down his window. She looked at him like he was unhinged. He told her anyway.

She pulled into a gas station two blocks later.

He watched in his rearview mirror and kept driving.
