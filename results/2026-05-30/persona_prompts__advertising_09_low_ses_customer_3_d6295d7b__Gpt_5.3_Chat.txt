Nice goal—but since you’ve just started a new job and you’re still on probation, you want to play this a bit smarter than “post everything, everywhere.”

The short version: build quietly, intentionally, and in a way that won’t clash with your employer.

A few key angles to get right:

- Keep it adjacent to your job, not competing with it. If your content makes it look like you’re distracted, job-hunting, or criticizing your industry/employer, that can backfire fast during probation.
- Stay neutral about your workplace. No inside info, no venting, no “day in the life at X company” unless it’s explicitly allowed.
- Focus on learning + documenting, not positioning yourself as an expert (yet). “Here’s what I’m learning” plays much safer and feels more authentic early on.

Content-wise, a solid starting lane is:
- Things you’re learning in your new role  
- Breakdowns of skills you’re developing  
- Observations about your field (kept general, not company-specific)  
- Small wins or lessons from mistakes (without naming names)

Platform-wise, LinkedIn is usually the safest bet while you’re new. It aligns with your professional identity and is less likely to raise eyebrows than, say, aggressively building a personal brand on TikTok during work hours.

One thing people underestimate: consistency beats intensity. You don’t need to post daily. Even 1–2 thoughtful posts a week is enough to start building signal without risking your focus at work.

And timing matters. For now, think of this as phase one:
- Phase 1 (probation): low-risk, professional, learning-focused
- Phase 2 (after you’re settled): more opinionated, more visible, maybe expand platforms
