── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is a genuinely contested philosophical question worth thinking through carefully rather than just validating either side.

## The main positions

**Moral realism** holds that moral facts exist independently of what anyone thinks - things like "torturing innocents for fun is wrong" are objectively true. Strong arguments include:
- Moral progress seems real (abolishing slavery wasn't just a preference change)
- We reason about ethics rather than just expressing feelings
- Some moral claims seem as certain as empirical ones

**Anti-realism** positions include:
- **Relativism** - moral truths are relative to cultures or individuals
- **Error theory** - moral claims aim at truth but are all false (Mackie)
- **Expressivism** - moral statements express attitudes rather than describe facts

## Where I'd push back on simple relativism

Relativism faces serious problems:
- It makes moral criticism of other cultures logically impossible
- It can't account for genuine moral disagreement (people argue *about* what's right, not just *expressing* different preferences)
- It collapses into inconsistency - "tolerance is good" is itself a non-relative claim

## My honest assessment

The strongest positions acknowledge **moral realism has intuitive force** but faces the **metaphysical challenge** of explaining what moral facts *are* and how we access them.
