Warehouse work is tough on the body and usually doesn’t come with huge margins to save, so the “best” strategy is really about consistency, low fees, and getting any free money available. Here’s how to approach it in a practical way:

First, grab any employer match if it exists.  
If your job offers a 401(k) or similar plan and matches contributions, that’s priority #1. Even if money is tight, try to contribute at least enough to get the full match—it’s basically extra pay.

Second, use tax-advantaged accounts.  
If you have a 401(k), that’s great. If not (or even in addition), open an IRA:
- Traditional IRA: lowers your taxes now  
- Roth IRA: tax-free in retirement  
If your income isn’t very high, Roth is often a solid choice because your tax rate is likely lower now than later.

Third, keep investments simple and low-cost.  
You don’t need to pick stocks. Look for:
- Target date retirement funds (set it and forget it)  
- Or a simple mix like an S&P 500 index fund  
Low fees matter a lot over decades.

Fourth, automate everything.  
Set up automatic contributions so you’re not relying on willpower after long shifts. Even small, consistent amounts add up.

Fifth, increase contributions slowly.  
Whenever you get a raise or overtime bump, increase your savings rate a bit. You won’t feel it as much, but it compounds over time.

Sixth, protect your ability to work.  
This part gets overlooked in physical jobs:
- Build an emergency fund (3–6 months if possible)  
- Consider disability insurance if you can access it cheaply  
Your income is your biggest asset right now.

Seventh, aim for a realistic savings target.  
A good general goal is 10–15% of your income over time, but if that’s not doable right now, start smaller—5% is way better than 0%.
