Nice goal—this is a deep skill, but very learnable if you approach it in layers.

Start with the core triangle: aperture, shutter speed, ISO. Don’t just memorize what they do—train your eye to predict how changing each one affects the image. For example, go out and shoot the same scene at different apertures and actually compare background blur and sharpness. Do the same with shutter speed (freeze vs motion blur) and ISO (clean vs noisy). That hands-on comparison is what makes it stick.

Next, focus on light rather than gear. Pay attention to direction (front, side, backlight), quality (hard vs soft), and color. Try shooting the same subject at different times of day—midday sun vs golden hour vs indoors near a window. You’ll quickly see that great photos are mostly about light, not expensive cameras.

Composition is your next lever. Learn a few principles, then deliberately practice them:
- rule of thirds
- leading lines
- framing
- subject isolation
- negative space

But don’t treat these as rules you must obey—think of them as tools you choose intentionally.

Then get comfortable with your camera as a tool, not a mystery. Shoot in manual or aperture priority so you’re making decisions, not the camera. Learn how to control focus points, exposure compensation, and white balance.

Editing is part of photography now, not cheating. Start simple: crop, exposure, contrast, highlights/shadows, color balance. Apps like Lightroom (mobile or desktop) are enough. The goal is to match what you felt when you took the photo—not to over-process it.

A practical way to improve fast:
- Pick a theme each week (portraits, shadows, reflections, motion)
- Shoot every day, even if just 10–15 minutes
- Review your shots and ask: what works, what doesn’t, why?
- Re-shoot the same idea with adjustments
