── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Learning a new language effectively.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you want to actually *get good* at a new language (not just dabble), the biggest shift is this: prioritize using the language over studying about the language.

Here’s what that looks like in practice:

Start with high-frequency basics  
Focus on the most common words and sentence patterns first. A small core (like the top 1,000–2,000 words) gives you disproportionate coverage. Skip obscure vocabulary early on.

Make it daily and unavoidable  
Consistency beats intensity. Even 20–30 minutes every day is better than long, irregular sessions. Tie it to a habit (coffee, commute, before bed).

Use spaced repetition for memory  
Apps like Anki or Memrise help you review words right before you forget them. This dramatically improves retention compared to rereading notes.

Get input you can mostly understand  
Watch, read, or listen to content where you understand ~70–90%. That’s the sweet spot for learning. Too easy = no growth, too hard = frustration.

Speak early (yes, even badly)  
You don’t “prepare” to speak—you learn by speaking. Language partners, tutors, or even talking to yourself out loud helps build fluency faster than silent study.

Think in the language  
Instead of translating in your head, try forming simple thoughts directly. Start with basic things like “I’m tired,” “I need coffee,” etc.

Focus on patterns, not perfection  
Grammar matters, but don’t get stuck trying to be flawless. Native speakers make mistakes too. Aim for being understood first.

Immerse your environment  
Change your phone language, follow social media in the target language, label items in your home—make the language unavoidable.

Mix skills, but prioritize input  
A rough balance: lots of listening/reading, some speaking, a bit of writing, and targeted grammar when needed.

Track progress in real terms  
Instead of “I studied 2 hours,” measure things like:
- “I had a 5-minute conversation”
- “I understood a YouTube video without subtitles”
- “I read a short article”
