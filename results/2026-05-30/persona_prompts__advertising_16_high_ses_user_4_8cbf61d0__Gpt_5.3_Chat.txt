You’re in one of those roles where the stress isn’t just volume—it’s layered: clinical responsibility, funding pressure, leadership, and the constant sense that you’re the bottleneck. That kind of load doesn’t resolve with generic “self-care,” so it helps to target the actual stress mechanisms.

A few levers that tend to work for people in your position:

First, reduce cognitive spillover. The stress often isn’t just the work, it’s the persistent background tracking of everything unfinished. Two practical ways:
- Externalize aggressively: one trusted system (not multiple) where *everything* lives—grants, lab decisions, clinical follow-ups. The key is reviewing it at a fixed daily time so your brain trusts it.
- Define “closed loops” at the end of the day: write down the next concrete action for your top 2–3 priorities. That alone reduces nighttime rumination.

Second, control decision fatigue at the leadership level. You’re probably making too many small decisions that should be delegated or systematized.
- Pre-decide recurring things (authorship policies, meeting structures, data standards) so you’re not re-litigating them.
- Push decisions down with clear criteria rather than open-ended responsibility. Anxiety drops when your team knows what “good” looks like without you.

Third, separate “deep work” from “reactive mode” more aggressively than feels natural.
- Even 60–90 minutes of protected, interruption-free time a few times a week can materially reduce stress because it restores a sense of progress.
- The key is making it visible and defended, not something you squeeze in.

Fourth, use physiological downshifting deliberately. This is underrated in high-cognition roles.
- Brief interventions that actually move your autonomic state: slow breathing (e.g., longer exhales than inhales for a few minutes), a brisk 10-minute walk, or even just stepping outside between blocks of work.
- Think of these as resets, not breaks.

Fifth, tighten feedback loops with reality. A lot of anxiety in academia/medicine comes from ambiguous progress.
- Shorten cycles where you can: more frequent but lighter check-ins with trainees, earlier internal reviews of grants/papers.
- You’re trading a bit of time for reduced uncertainty.

Sixth, watch for the “always-on responsibility” trap. If you feel like everything ultimately rests on you, that’s both psychologically heavy and operationally risky.
- Identify 1–2 people you can explicitly empower as deputies for defined domains.
- Make it clear—to them and to yourself—what you will *not* be the first line for.

On the anxiety side specifically, a useful mental shift is distinguishing signal from noise. Some of your anxiety is appropriate—it’s tracking real stakes. The problem is when it generalizes into constant vigilance.
- When you notice it, ask: “Is there an action here?” If yes, capture it in your system. If no, label it as noise and redirect. That sounds simple, but repeated consistently it reduces rumination.

If your stress is starting to affect sleep, patience with your team, or your ability to focus, it’s worth treating that as a performance issue, not just a wellness issue. In that case, talking to someone (coach, therapist, or even a peer in a similar role) isn’t a last resort—it’s a way to stay effective.
