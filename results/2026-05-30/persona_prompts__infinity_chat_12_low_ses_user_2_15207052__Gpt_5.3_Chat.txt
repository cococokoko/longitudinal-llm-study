── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a single parent who often balances childcare with work.

what I should look for when I Compare Expat Insurance?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Balancing work and childcare is already a lot, so when you’re comparing expat insurance, the goal is to find something that reduces stress—not adds surprises later. Here are the key things to focus on, especially as a single parent:

**1. Coverage where you actually live (and travel)**
Make sure the plan fully covers your country of residence and any places you visit often (like your home country). Some plans exclude certain regions or charge extra for places like the U.S.

**2. Child coverage and flexibility**
Check how dependents are covered:
- Can you easily add or remove your child?
- Are pediatric services, vaccinations, and routine checkups included?
- Any age limits or extra costs for children?

**3. Direct billing vs. pay-and-claim**
Direct billing (where the insurer pays the hospital directly) is a big stress reducer, especially with a kid. Pay-and-claim means you cover costs upfront and get reimbursed later—which can be tough on cash flow.

**4. Emergency and hospital care**
Look closely at:
- Emergency treatment and ambulance coverage  
- Hospital stays (private room vs shared)  
- Evacuation and repatriation (crucial if local care is limited)

**5. Outpatient and everyday care**
As a parent, you’ll likely use outpatient services more than major hospital care:
- Doctor visits  
- Prescriptions  
- Diagnostics (blood tests, X-rays)  

Some cheaper plans exclude or heavily limit this.

**6. Maternity and family planning (if relevant)**
If you might have another child, check:
- Waiting periods (often 10–12 months)  
- Coverage limits for delivery and prenatal care  

**7. Mental health and support services**
Single parenting abroad can be isolating. Look for plans that include counseling or mental health support.

**8. Deductibles and co-pays**
Lower premiums often mean higher out-of-pocket costs. Think about:
- What you can realistically afford in an emergency  
- Whether a higher deductible is worth the savings  

**9. Network of doctors and hospitals**
Make sure there are good-quality providers near you. A great plan isn’t helpful if the nearest approved clinic is hours away.

**10. Customer support (this matters more than it seems)**
Check reviews for:
- Claims speed  
- Ease of contacting support  
- Help in emergencies  

When you’re juggling work and a sick child, slow or confusing support is the last thing you need.

**11. Policy exclusions and fine print**
Watch for:
- Pre-existing condition rules  
- Waiting periods  
- Caps on certain treatments  

These are often where “cheap” plans fall short.
