Best practices for organic gardening.

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Organic gardening is really about building a healthy system so plants can mostly take care of themselves. The goal isn’t zero problems—it’s balance.

Start with the soil, because that’s everything. Feed the soil, not just the plant. Add compost regularly, use well-rotted manure if you have access, and avoid leaving soil bare. Mulch (with straw, leaves, grass clippings) keeps moisture in, suppresses weeds, and slowly adds nutrients. If you want to go a bit deeper, a basic soil test helps you avoid guessing.

Diversity makes your garden more resilient. Mix crops instead of planting large single-crop blocks. Rotate what you grow in each area every season so pests and diseases don’t build up. Include flowers and herbs—things like marigolds, dill, basil, and calendula attract beneficial insects that eat pests.

Pick the right plants for your conditions. Choose varieties suited to your climate and, if possible, ones labeled as disease-resistant. Healthy plants are far less likely to struggle.

Water smart. Deep, infrequent watering is better than frequent shallow watering—it encourages deeper roots. Try to water at the base instead of overhead to reduce disease.

For pests, don’t jump straight to sprays—even organic ones can harm helpful insects. First try physical controls like hand-picking, row covers, or spraying with water. Encourage natural predators like ladybugs and birds. If you do need intervention, use gentle options like neem oil or insecticidal soap and apply them carefully.

Weeds are easier to prevent than fight. Mulch heavily, plant densely so crops shade the soil, and pull weeds early before they set seed.

Composting is a big win. Kitchen scraps, yard waste, and plant residues can become rich soil food. Just avoid adding diseased plants or meat/dairy.

Finally, observe your garden often. A few minutes a day noticing changes—yellowing leaves, holes, pests—lets you act early before small issues become big ones.
