Running a marathon well is mostly about restraint, consistency, and logistics—not heroics. A few things that actually move the needle:

**1) Don’t “wing” the build-up**
You want 12–20 weeks of structured training with gradual mileage increases (no more than ~10% per week). Anchor your plan around:
- 1 long run per week (peaking around 18–22 miles)
- 1 quality session (tempo or intervals)

**2) Learn your pace early—and respect it**
Most first marathons are blown in the first 10 miles. Use recent race data (10K/half) or training paces to set a realistic goal. If in doubt, start 10–15 seconds per mile slower than your “hope” pace and negative split.

**3) Fueling is not optional**
You will run out of glycogen otherwise. Practice this in training:
- 30–60g of carbs per hour (gels, chews, drink mix)
- Start early (around 30 minutes in), then every 20–30 minutes
- Hydrate consistently; include electrolytes if it’s warm

**4) Long runs are dress rehearsals**
Use them to dial in shoes, socks, anti-chafe strategy, and fueling. Nothing new on race day—especially not shoes.

**5) Strength and durability matter**
2 short sessions per week (glutes, calves, hamstrings, core) reduce injury risk and help late-race form. Think simple: squats, lunges, deadlifts, calf raises, planks.

**6) Taper properly**
Reduce volume over the last 2–3 weeks but keep a bit of intensity. You should feel slightly undertrained and antsy by race week—that’s a good sign.

**7) Race-day execution**
- Start conservative; the race begins at mile 20
- Stick to your fueling schedule even if you don’t feel like it
- Break the race into segments (e.g., 10 miles easy, 10 steady, 10K compete)

Almost everyone hits one. It’s usually fuel, pace, or heat. Adjust pace, take fluids, keep moving. It passes.
