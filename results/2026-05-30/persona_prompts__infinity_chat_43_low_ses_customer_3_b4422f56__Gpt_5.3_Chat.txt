Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: there’s no universal agreement, but it’s not a free-for-all either.

There are three big ways people answer this:

Moral realism: there really are moral facts, independent of what anyone thinks (like “causing unnecessary suffering is wrong”). Philosophers in this camp argue that some values are so fundamental to human life—like well-being, fairness, harm—that they’re not just opinions. Disagreement doesn’t disprove them any more than disagreement about science disproves physics.

Relativism: morality depends on culture, society, or individual perspective. What’s “right” is what a group accepts. This explains why norms vary a lot across time and place. The problem is it can make it hard to criticize clearly harmful practices if a culture endorses them.

Constructivist / middle views: morality isn’t “out there” like gravity, but it’s not arbitrary either. Humans construct moral systems based on shared needs—cooperation, trust, reducing harm. Under this view, some moral claims are better justified than others because they better support those needs.

My honest take: pure relativism doesn’t hold up well—if everything were equally valid, you couldn’t meaningfully say anything is unjust. But strict “objective morality” in a cosmic sense is hard to prove. The most defensible position is that moral norms emerge from human nature and social life, and some are more justified than others because they promote flourishing, reduce suffering, and can be defended with reasons others can recognize.

So not absolute in a mystical sense—but also not “anything goes.”
