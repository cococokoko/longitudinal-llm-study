# The Cloud Who Couldn't Rain

High above the green hills and the winding river, there lived a small cloud named Pip.

All the other clouds in the sky were very good at their job. Big Bertha could pour down buckets of rain in minutes. The twins, Fluff and Puff, could drizzle softly over flower gardens. Even old Grandpa Nimbus could rumble with thunder and flash with lightning.

But Pip? Pip could not make a single drop of rain.

Every morning, Pip would puff up as big as possible, scrunch up tight, and try with all his might.

Nothing.

Not a drizzle. Not a sprinkle. Not even a tiny drip.

"Don't worry," said Big Bertha kindly. "Keep trying, little one."

But Pip did worry. Down below, Pip could see a small farm with a garden full of drooping sunflowers, thirsty carrots, and sad, wilting tomatoes. A little girl named Rosa visited the garden every day, watering the plants with her small green can. But her can was almost empty, and the well had run dry.

Pip watched Rosa look up at the sky with hopeful eyes.

"Please," she whispered. "We really need rain."

Pip felt something strange happen then. Something warm and tight grew in the middle of his fluffy chest. He thought about Rosa's tired arms carrying the heavy watering can. He thought about the sunflowers drooping their yellow heads.

Pip didn't think about trying to rain. He just thought about Rosa.

And then — *drip.*

Pip looked down in shock.

*Drip. Drip. Drip.*

"I'm doing it!" Pip cried.

The drops fell faster now, pattering down onto the dry garden soil. The sunflowers lifted their heads. The carrots seemed to stand up straighter. The tomatoes turned a little more red.

Rosa laughed and spun around with her arms wide open, letting the rain fall on her face.

"Thank you, sky!" she shouted happily.

Big Bertha floated over and smiled warmly. "You see, Pip? You just needed the right reason."

"But I wasn't even trying," said Pip, confused.

"Exactly," said Bertha with a wink. "You stopped thinking about yourself and started thinking about someone else. That's when the magic happened."

Pip thought about that for a long moment.

From that day on, Pip never had trouble raining again. Whenever the job felt too hard, Pip would simply look down at the world below — at the gardens and the rivers, the children playing and the animals drinking — and remember that the rain wasn't really about Pip at all.

It was about everyone else.

And somehow, that made everything easy.

Rosa planted extra sunflowers that year. They grew tall and bright and golden, reaching all the way up toward the sky.

Pip liked to think they were reaching up to say thank you.

And maybe, just maybe, they were.
