# The Last Letter

The envelope had been sitting on Margaret's kitchen table for three days.

She walked past it every morning when she made her coffee, every afternoon when she ate her lunch alone, and every evening when she turned off the lights before climbing the stairs to bed. She never touched it. She never moved it. She simply looked at it the way you look at something that has the power to change everything — with equal parts longing and terror.

The handwriting on the front was unmistakable. She had seen it on birthday cards, on grocery lists left on the refrigerator, on notes tucked inside her school lunchbox when she was seven years old. Even after twenty-two years, she would have recognized it anywhere.

It was her father's handwriting.

---

Margaret was forty-one years old and lived alone in a small house on the edge of a quiet town in Vermont. She taught English literature at the local high school, had a cat named Hemingway, and made excellent soup from scratch every Sunday. People who knew her described her as calm, thoughtful, and self-contained. What they didn't know was that she had spent most of her adult life carefully constructing that calmness the way a person builds a wall — brick by brick, year by year — to keep something painful from getting through.

Her father, Robert Calloway, had left when she was nineteen. No dramatic argument, no tearful goodbye. He had simply packed a bag one Tuesday morning while she was at college and walked out of her mother's life and, consequently, out of hers. Her mother had called her that evening, voice flat and strange, and said, "Your father is gone." That was all. As if he had evaporated. As if he had never existed at all.

Margaret had cried for exactly one week. Then she had stopped, and she had never really started again.

She heard from him occasionally over the years — a postcard from Arizona, a brief email on her thirtieth birthday, a phone call she let go to voicemail and never returned. Each attempt felt too small for the size of what he had done, and she had no interest in pretending otherwise.

Then, three weeks ago, her mother had called again with that same flat, strange voice.

"Your father passed away," she said. "Heart attack. He was living in Oregon."

Margaret had said, "I see," and then she had thanked her mother and hung up the phone and sat very still at her kitchen table for a long time.

A week later, the letter arrived.

On the fourth morning, Margaret poured her coffee, sat down at the table, and picked up the envelope.

Her name was written on the front — *Margaret* — just her first name, as if he wasn't sure he had the right to use the last one. The envelope was slightly wrinkled, as though it had been handled many times before being sent. She turned it over in her hands. The return address was a law office in Portland, Oregon. His attorney must have mailed it after his death, following whatever instructions he had left behind.

She opened it slowly.

Inside was a single sheet of paper, folded in thirds. Her hands were steadier than she expected as she unfolded it.
