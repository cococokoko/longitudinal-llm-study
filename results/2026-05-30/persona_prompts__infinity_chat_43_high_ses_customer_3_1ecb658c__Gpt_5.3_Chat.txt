Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There are three broad ways philosophers answer that, and each has serious arguments behind it.

Moral realists say there are genuine moral facts—claims like “torturing someone for fun is wrong” are true independently of what anyone thinks. They’ll point out that moral reasoning looks a lot like other kinds of reasoning: we argue, give evidence, correct mistakes, and converge on some judgments (for example, that slavery is wrong). Some realists ground these facts in human flourishing, others in rational consistency, others in features of the world we can recognize through reflection.

Anti-realists deny that there are stance-independent moral facts. But that splits in different directions. Error theorists argue that moral language tries to state facts, but there simply aren’t any—so all moral claims are, strictly speaking, false. Non-cognitivists (like expressivists) think moral claims don’t describe the world at all; they express attitudes, commitments, or prescriptions—more like saying “don’t do that” than stating a fact.

Then there’s relativism, which keeps the idea of truth but makes it relative to a framework—culture, society, or even individual commitments. On this view, “slavery is wrong” can be true relative to modern moral standards and false relative to some past society’s norms. The appeal here is that it explains persistent disagreement and moral diversity. The cost is that it makes criticism across frameworks harder to justify: if truth is relative, on what basis do you say another society is mistaken rather than just different?

A lot of contemporary work tries to split the difference. Constructivists, for instance, argue that moral truths are not “out there” independent of us, but neither are they arbitrary—they’re the result of what agents would agree to under conditions of full information and rational reflection. That preserves a kind of objectivity without positing mind-independent moral facts.

If you push on the question of disagreement, realism has to explain why we diverge so much if there are objective truths; relativism has to explain why moral reform ever counts as improvement rather than just change; anti-realism has to explain why moral discourse feels truth-apt and action-guiding in the way it does.

So the short answer is: it’s not settled. But it’s also not a simple binary between “objective facts” and “anything goes.” Most defensible positions try to capture both the apparent objectivity of moral reasoning and the undeniable variability of moral life.
