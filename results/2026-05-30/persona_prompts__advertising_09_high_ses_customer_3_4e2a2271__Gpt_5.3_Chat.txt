If you’re thinking about “personal brand,” it helps to reframe it slightly: for someone in your position, it’s less about self-promotion and more about shaping an intellectual presence that travels beyond your institution. Done well, it amplifies your work, attracts strong collaborators and students, and lets you influence how your field is understood in public.

A few principles tend to matter more than tactics:

First, clarity of voice. You likely already have a body of work, but online audiences won’t parse a CV. They respond to a through-line: what problems you care about, what you consistently question or challenge, and what perspective you bring that’s recognizably yours. If someone reads five of your posts, they should start to anticipate how you think.

Second, translation without dilution. The advantage you have is depth; the challenge is accessibility. The strongest academic voices online don’t “dumb things down,” they re-express them. A good heuristic: explain the core idea as if to an excellent undergraduate in another discipline. If you can’t compress it, the issue is usually conceptual, not stylistic.

Third, choose your venues deliberately. You don’t need to be everywhere:
- X (Twitter) is still the fastest way to engage in real-time disciplinary and public debates.
- LinkedIn has become surprisingly important for longer-form, policy-adjacent visibility.
- A newsletter (Substack, university-hosted, or personal site) gives you control and depth.
- Occasional media (podcasts, op-eds) extends reach beyond academic audiences.

It’s better to do one or two of these consistently than to scatter your attention.

Fourth, cadence and format. Consistency matters more than volume. For many professors, something like:
- short, frequent observations or paper threads
- periodic longer essays that synthesize a line of thought
- occasional commentary on current events where you have genuine expertise  
works well. Over time, this builds an archive of thinking, not just isolated posts.

Fifth, intellectual generosity. Signal-boosting others’ work, offering concise readings of new papers, or publicly thinking through ideas-in-progress tends to attract serious audiences. It positions you less as a broadcaster and more as a node in a network.

Sixth, boundaries. The risks are real: reputational spillover, misinterpretation, time drain. Decide in advance what you won’t engage with (e.g., certain political topics, bad-faith debates, student-related matters). A light but firm boundary practice preserves both your time and your authority.

Finally, think in terms of “compounding.” A well-crafted thread can become a talk, which becomes a paper, which becomes a public essay. The brand isn’t something separate from your scholarship—it’s a visible layer of it.
