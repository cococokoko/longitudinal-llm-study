Nice contrast—warehouse grind + growing your own stuff is a solid combo.

For organic gardening, the goal is to build a healthy system so you’re not constantly fighting problems. Here are the practices that give you the most return without demanding tons of time:

Start with your soil  
Healthy plants come from healthy soil. Add compost regularly (even store-bought is fine). If you can, mulch everything—straw, leaves, or wood chips—this keeps moisture in and reduces weeds, which saves you time after long shifts.

Pick low-maintenance crops  
Go for things that don’t need constant babysitting:  
- Leafy greens (lettuce, kale, spinach)  
- Herbs (mint, basil, chives)  
- Zucchini and tomatoes (once established, they’re pretty forgiving)  
- Beans (fast, productive, low effort)

Water smart, not often  
Deep watering a few times a week is better than shallow daily watering. Early morning is best. If you can set up a simple drip hose or soaker hose, that’s a huge time saver.

Use natural pest control  
Don’t aim for zero bugs—that’s unrealistic. Instead:  
- Encourage good insects (ladybugs, bees) by planting flowers  
- Use neem oil or insecticidal soap if things get out of hand  
- Hand-pick pests when you see them (annoying, but effective)

Companion planting helps  
Some plants protect each other:  
- Basil near tomatoes (can help with pests)  
- Marigolds around beds (general pest deterrent)  
- Garlic/onions to discourage certain insects

Feed plants naturally  
Compost is your main fertilizer. You can also use things like fish emulsion or seaweed extract occasionally if plants look weak.

Keep it manageable  
Since you’re working long hours, don’t go too big too fast. A few raised beds or containers done well will beat a large garden you can’t keep up with.

Rotate crops if you can  
Don’t plant the same thing in the same spot every season—it helps prevent soil depletion and disease buildup.
