── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best resources for starting a plant-based diet?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Great question—especially coming from a physician-scientist, you’re probably looking for resources that are both evidence-based and practical.

I’ll group the best ones by type so you can quickly find what fits your style.

First, high-quality, evidence-based overviews  
These are good starting points if you want the science and clinical framing:

- Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health (Nutrition Source)  
  Clear, well-balanced summaries on plant-based diets, protein, fats, and micronutrients. Not ideological, very usable clinically.

- Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics position paper (Vegetarian Diets)  
  This is one of the most cited clinical references. It covers adequacy across life stages, key nutrients (B12, iron, omega-3s), and practical considerations.

- British Dietetic Association (BDA) plant-based resources  
  Concise and clinically grounded guidance, including meal composition and risk mitigation.

- “Plant-Based Diets: A Physician’s Guide” (various CME-style reviews, e.g., in JAMA, Circulation, or Permanente Journal)  
  These tend to summarize cardiometabolic evidence and adherence considerations.

Second, clinically oriented lifestyle medicine resources  
These are useful if you’re thinking about patients or translating into interventions:

- American College of Lifestyle Medicine (ACLM)  
  Offers toolkits, CME, and practical dietary frameworks (often whole-food plant-based leaning).

- Physicians Committee for Responsible Medicine (PCRM)  
  Strong on protocols (e.g., diabetes, hyperlipidemia), meal plans, and patient handouts. Slightly advocacy-forward, but still evidence-referenced.

Third, practical “how-to” guides (for daily life)  
These help bridge the gap between theory and implementation:

- “How Not to Die” / “How Not to Diet” (Michael Greger)  
  Very comprehensive literature synthesis. Heavy on citations, though somewhat selective in emphasis—best read with a critical eye.

- “The Plant-Based Diet for Beginners” (Gabriel Miller) or similar structured guides  
  Simple meal plans and shopping lists—good for onboarding.

- Forks Over Knives (website + app)  
  Recipes, meal planners, and transition strategies. Again, somewhat advocacy-driven but very practical.

Fourth, nutrient-specific guidance (important for clinicians)  
These are key to doing this well rather than just “more vegetables”:

- NIH Office of Dietary Supplements  
  Excellent fact sheets on B12, iron, iodine, calcium, vitamin D, omega-3s.

- VeganHealth.org (Jack Norris, RD)  
  Surprisingly rigorous, detail-oriented breakdowns of micronutrient risks and how to cover them.

- Linus Pauling Institute Micronutrient Information Center  
  Deep dives into nutrient metabolism and requirements.

Fifth, quick practical framework (useful for you or patients)  
A simple, evidence-aligned way to structure a plant-based diet:

- Base: legumes, whole grains, vegetables, fruits  
- Protein: beans, lentils, tofu/tempeh, soy milk, optionally seitan  
- Fats: nuts, seeds, olive oil  
- Critical nutrients to plan deliberately:  
  - B12 (supplement is essentially mandatory)  
  - Vitamin D (depending on exposure)  
  - Omega-3 (ALA sources + consider algae DHA/EPA)  
  - Iron, zinc (absorption-aware planning)  
  - Iodine (iodized salt or seaweed with caution)

One important nuance, especially from a research perspective:  
“Plant-based” spans a spectrum—from whole-food plant-based to highly processed vegan diets. The health signal in the literature is largely driven by minimally processed patterns (high fiber, low refined carbs, low ultra-processed foods), not simply the exclusion of animal products.
