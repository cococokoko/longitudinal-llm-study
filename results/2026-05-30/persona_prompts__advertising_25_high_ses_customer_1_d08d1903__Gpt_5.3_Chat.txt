How can I decorate my apartment on a minimalist budget?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You don’t need money to get a clean, intentional space—what you need is restraint and a bit of curation. Most “budget decorating” fails because people add too much low-quality stuff instead of shaping what’s already there.

Start by stripping things back. Clear surfaces until each area has only one or two purposeful objects. Negative space reads as “expensive” because it signals control. Then build back selectively.

Focus on three anchors:
- Lighting: Swap harsh bulbs for warm ones (2700–3000K). One well-placed floor or table lamp can change the entire feel of a room more than new furniture.
- Textiles: A neutral throw, simple curtains, or a rug (even a flatweave or secondhand one) adds softness without clutter.
- One statement per zone: A large piece of art, a plant, or even a striking book stack. Bigger, fewer items look more deliberate than lots of small ones.

Use what you already have, but edit it. Books can be stacked horizontally, kitchen items can be decanted into simple containers, and mismatched colors can be unified by grouping (all whites together, all wood tones together).

For walls, avoid the temptation to fill every inch. One oversized print (you can print high-res public domain art cheaply) will look far more refined than a gallery of small frames. If you do a gallery, keep frames consistent—black, white, or natural wood.

Secondhand is your friend, but be picky. Look for solid materials—wood, metal, glass—and ignore trends. A slightly worn wooden chair will age better than a brand-new particleboard one.

Finally, hide visual noise. Use boxes, baskets, or even a dedicated drawer to keep cables, papers, and daily clutter out of sight. Minimalism isn’t about owning nothing—it’s about controlling what’s visible.
