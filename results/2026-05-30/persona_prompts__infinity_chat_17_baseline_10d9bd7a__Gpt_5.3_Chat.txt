The town of Black Hollow had a way of holding onto things—fog in the mornings, heat in the summer, and secrets year-round.

Nobody locked their doors there. Not because they trusted each other, but because everyone already knew what there was to find.

So when Evelyn Carter’s house was found wide open at dawn, the screen door gently knocking against its frame like a slow heartbeat, people noticed.

Evelyn wasn’t the sort to forget things. She taught third grade for twenty-seven years, kept her hedges trimmed with a ruler’s precision, and once returned a library book three minutes before its due time—with an apology note.

But that morning, her curtains fluttered like she’d left in a hurry. Or been taken in one.

Sheriff Daniel Ruiz was first on the scene, coffee still warm in his hand. He stood in the doorway longer than necessary, listening. The house creaked softly, the way old houses do when they’re remembering something.

“Ms. Carter?” he called.

No answer.

Inside, nothing looked disturbed. A mug sat on the table, tea gone cold. Her reading glasses rested neatly atop an open book—page 143. A bookmark lay unused beside it.

Daniel frowned. Evelyn never left a book unfinished without marking her place.

He moved carefully through the house. Kitchen, living room, bedroom—everything in order. Too in order. Like a photograph instead of a moment.

Then he noticed the clock.

It had stopped at 2:17.

The power was fine. The rest of the house hummed quietly. Only the clock had given up.

By noon, the whole town knew.

At Millie’s Diner, conversations dropped to whispers when Daniel walked in. Coffee poured slower. Eyes followed him.

“You think she wandered?” Millie asked, wiping a spotless counter.

Daniel shook his head. “Evelyn doesn’t wander.”

Millie nodded, but her eyes flicked toward the window—the road leading out of town. No one ever really left Black Hollow. Not for long.

Daniel returned to the house that evening.

The fog had rolled in early, swallowing the street in soft gray. The porch light cast a weak halo, barely reaching the steps.

Inside, the air felt different now. Thicker. As if the house had exhaled something during the day and was waiting to see who noticed.

He went back to the clock.

2:17.

He pulled it off the wall. Behind it, the wallpaper was slightly discolored—a faint square marking where the sun hadn’t touched for years.

And in that square, carved into the wall itself, were numbers.

2:17.

Below it, barely visible, more numbers layered over each other. Dates. Dozens of them.

Daniel stepped back.

Every one of them corresponded to something. A missing dog. A broken window. A fire that started and stopped without reason. Small things. Forgettable things.

Except they weren’t.

They were all reported at exactly 2:17 a.m.

Opened.

His father, who had died fifteen years ago in that same house.

It collected them.
