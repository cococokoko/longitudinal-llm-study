Alright, I’m going to be blunt: most lawyer YouTube channels fail because they’re either too stiff, too jargon-heavy, or too vague to be useful. Your advantage is credibility—but if you present like a conference panel, people will scroll past you.

If you want this to actually work, you need to translate your expertise into clarity, relevance, and watchability.

Start with positioning. “Senior attorney” is not a niche. You need a lane people can immediately understand and search for. For example:
- “Explaining high-profile legal cases in plain English”
- “Business law for founders who can’t afford a GC”
- “What actually happens inside lawsuits (from a senior attorney)”
- “How contracts screw you—and how to spot it”

Pick one primary angle. You can expand later, but early growth comes from being known for something specific.

Next is content style. Think less like a memo, more like a sharp conversation. The best-performing legal content tends to fall into a few buckets:
- Breaking down newsworthy cases or lawsuits
- “You’re doing this wrong” style risk warnings (contracts, employment, IP)
- Real-world scenarios: “What happens if…”
- Myth-busting (“No, you can’t just…”)
- Quick explainers on things people are afraid to ask a lawyer

Your tone matters more than your credentials. Speak like you would to a smart client who isn’t a lawyer. Short sentences. Concrete examples. No hedging every statement like you’re writing a brief.

On format: start simple.
- Talking head, good lighting, clear audio
- 5–10 minute videos for depth
- Cut those into short-form clips (15–60 seconds) for reach

Consistency beats production value early on.

A sample structure that works:
Hook (first 5–10 seconds): “If you sign this clause, you could lose your entire business—and most people don’t even read it.”
Context: what this is and why it matters
Breakdown: explain clearly, step-by-step
Example or story
Takeaway: what the viewer should actually do

Also, you should lean into your experience without sounding like a résumé. Instead of saying “I’ve been practicing for 20 years,” say “I’ve seen this clause blow up deals more times than I can count.”

On growth: don’t rely on YouTube alone.
- Post clips on LinkedIn (huge for your profile)
- Engage in comments with actual answers
- Collaborate with creators in adjacent spaces (finance, startups, real estate)

One more thing: be careful but not paralyzed about ethics. Add a simple disclaimer in your description, but don’t turn every sentence into “this is not legal advice.” That kills watchability.
