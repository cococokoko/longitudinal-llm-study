# The Last Apprentice

The rain had been falling for three days straight when Marcus Hale found the book.

He hadn't been looking for anything extraordinary. He was forty-two years old, recently divorced, recently fired, and recently moved into a apartment so small that his elbows touched both walls when he stretched his arms out. He had been cleaning out the storage unit he could no longer afford, hauling boxes of his former life into a dumpster, when his hand closed around something that felt warm despite the cold October air.

It was a book. Leather-bound, dark as charcoal, with no title on the spine. The cover was smooth except for a single symbol pressed into the center — a circle with a line through it, like a crossed-out zero, or perhaps a door.

Marcus almost threw it away.

Almost.

---

He brought it home and set it on his kitchen counter next to an empty coffee mug and a stack of unpaid bills. He stared at it while he ate cereal for dinner. The book stared back.

"You're a book," he said out loud. "You can't stare."

He opened it anyway.

The pages were blank.

All of them. Every single one. He flipped through from front to back, holding pages up to the light, looking for invisible ink or watermarks or anything at all. Nothing. Just cream-colored pages that felt slightly warmer than paper had any right to feel.

He set it down and went to bed.

At three in the morning, he woke up because his hand was burning.

Not painfully — not exactly. It was more like the feeling of holding your palm over a candle flame from a safe distance. A deep, insistent warmth that radiated up through his wrist and into his forearm. He sat up in the dark and looked at his right hand.

His palm was glowing.

A faint silver light, like moonlight trapped under skin, pulsed in a slow rhythm that matched his heartbeat. He watched it for a long time, waiting to feel afraid. The fear didn't come. Instead, something else settled into his chest — something he hadn't felt in years.

Curiosity.

He walked to the kitchen. The book was open on the counter. The pages were no longer blank.

---

The writing was small and precise, filling the pages in dark ink that seemed to shift slightly when he looked at it from the corner of his eye. He read for hours, standing at the counter in his bare feet, forgetting entirely about the cold floor or the late hour or the fact that none of this was possible.

The book explained things in a calm, almost academic tone, as though it were a manual for assembling furniture rather than a guide to the fundamental forces of reality.

*You have been chosen*, it began, *not because you are special, but because you are ready. Readiness is rarer than talent. The world is full of talented people who are not ready. You have lost enough to understand what matters. You have been humbled enough to listen. You have been quiet enough, in your grief, to finally hear.*

Marcus read on.

The book described a force that existed beneath the surface of the physical world — not magic in the fairy-tale sense, not spells and sparkles and pointed hats. Something older and more serious. It called this force *the Current*, and it described it the way a physics textbook might describe electricity: a real thing, a measurable thing, a thing that followed rules even when those rules were strange.

*The Current flows through everything*, the book explained. *Through stone and water and living things and the spaces between living things. Most people are like rocks in a river — the Current flows around them and they never know it. A wizard is someone who learns to be like water instead. To move with the Current. To redirect it. To become, in small ways, part of its flow.*

By the time the sun came up, Marcus had read two hundred pages and his hand had stopped glowing. He felt different. Not powerful — not yet. But different in the way a person feels different after a long conversation that changes how they see something they thought they already understood.

He made coffee. He sat down. He looked at his hands.

"Alright," he said quietly. "Show me what to do."

The book turned its own page.
