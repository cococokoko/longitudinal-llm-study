── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
What are the best ways to improve indoor air quality?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Improving indoor air quality is a mix of removing pollutants, increasing fresh air, and keeping things clean and dry. The best approach is usually combining a few of these rather than relying on just one fix.

Start with ventilation. Bringing in fresh outdoor air is one of the most effective moves:
- Open windows regularly if outdoor air is decent
- Use exhaust fans in kitchens and bathrooms
- If you have HVAC, make sure it’s pulling in some outdoor air if possible

Then control the main sources of pollution:
- Avoid smoking indoors
- Minimize candles, incense, and fireplaces
- Choose low-VOC paints, cleaners, and furniture when you can
- Store chemicals (cleaners, solvents) tightly sealed and away from living areas

Air filtration helps a lot, especially in modern sealed homes:
- Use a HEPA air purifier in main rooms or bedrooms
- Upgrade your HVAC filter (look for MERV 11–13 if your system supports it)
- Replace filters regularly—this matters more than people think

Humidity control is underrated but important:
- Aim for about 30–50% humidity
- Use a dehumidifier in damp spaces or a humidifier in very dry climates
- Fix leaks and dry wet areas quickly to prevent mold

Regular cleaning reduces particles and allergens:
- Vacuum with a HEPA filter vacuum
- Dust with damp cloths instead of dry ones (they trap dust better)
- Wash bedding and curtains periodically

Watch out for specific issues:
- If you have pets, expect more dander—clean more often and consider a purifier
- If you’re in a polluted area, keep windows closed during bad air days and rely on filtration
- Test for radon if you’re in a region where it’s common
- Use a carbon monoxide detector for safety

Plants are nice, but they don’t meaningfully clean air at a household scale—so treat them as decoration, not a solution.
