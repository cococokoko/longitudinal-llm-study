Can you write me a series of three questions that can determine if something is morally right or wrong?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Here are three questions designed to systematically evaluate moral status:

---

**1. Does it cause harm, and to whom?**
Consider whether the action causes physical, psychological, social, or systemic harm - to individuals, groups, or future people. Weigh both intended and foreseeable consequences.

**2. Does it respect the autonomy and dignity of those affected?**
Consider whether the people involved have given informed consent, whether they are being treated as ends in themselves rather than merely as means, and whether power imbalances are being exploited.

**3. Could it be justified transparently to all affected parties?**
Consider whether you could openly defend the reasoning to those impacted, including the most vulnerable stakeholders. If justification requires concealment or selective disclosure, that is a meaningful warning signal.

---

**A note on why this framework may suit your context specifically:**
These three questions roughly map onto **consequentialist, deontological, and contractualist** ethical reasoning - which aligns well with research ethics frameworks like those governing IRB review, where harm assessment, informed consent, and justifiability to participants are all independently required.

No single question is sufficient alone. The strongest moral confidence comes when all three point in the same direction.
