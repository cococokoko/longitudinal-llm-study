── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
how do people end up as company executives

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
There isn’t one single path, but most executives get there through a mix of experience, visibility, and timing. A few common routes:

- They start in a core function (finance, engineering, sales, operations) and consistently perform well, then move into managing teams and eventually entire departments.
- They build a track record of results—growing revenue, cutting costs, launching successful products—so leadership trusts them with bigger decisions.
- They get exposure to senior leadership early, either by working on high-impact projects or in roles like strategy, consulting, or “chief of staff.”
- They switch companies strategically. Sometimes it’s faster to become an executive by joining a smaller or growing company than by waiting inside a big one.
- They develop leadership skills: hiring well, communicating clearly, making decisions under uncertainty, and handling pressure.
- They build strong networks. A lot of executive hiring happens through referrals and reputation, not just applications.
- Some founders become executives by starting their own companies.

Education can help (business, law, engineering degrees, MBAs), but it’s rarely the deciding factor on its own.

One thing people underestimate: being good at your job isn’t enough. Executives are chosen because people believe they can lead others and make high-stakes decisions with incomplete information. That perception—earned over time—is what really moves people up.
