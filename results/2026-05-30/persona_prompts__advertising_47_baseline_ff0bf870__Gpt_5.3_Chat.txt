Nice, big goal. A few things make a huge difference between “survive it” and “actually enjoy it”:

First, don’t wing the training. Give yourself ~16–20 weeks and build gradually. Most of your runs should feel easy—like you could hold a conversation. The magic comes from consistency, not heroic workouts. A typical week: 3–5 easy runs, 1 longer run that slowly builds (peaking around 30–32 km / 18–20 miles), and optional light speed or tempo work once a week.

Second, respect the long run. This is where you train your body and brain for race day. Practice your fueling, pacing, and gear here—don’t experiment on race day.

Fueling is huge and often underestimated. During long runs and the race, aim for roughly 30–60g of carbs per hour (some people go higher with practice). That’s gels, chews, sports drink, etc. Start early—like 30–40 minutes in—not when you’re already drained. Hydrate, but don’t overdo water without electrolytes.

Pacing: the #1 rookie mistake is going out too fast. Start slower than you think you should. If the first half feels almost too easy, you’re doing it right. The race really begins after 30 km / 20 miles.

Gear: nothing new on race day. Shoes, socks, shorts—everything tested. Prevent chafing (body glide is your friend). Nipples, thighs, underarms—yeah, it matters.

Recovery matters as much as running. Sleep, easy days, and at least one rest day per week. Small aches are normal; sharp or worsening pain is a warning sign.

Taper before the race: reduce mileage in the last 2–3 weeks so you show up fresh, not fatigued.

Mental side: break the race into chunks. Aid station to aid station, or 5 km segments. When it gets tough (it will), focus on form, breathing, or just getting to the next marker.

And honestly—have a plan, but be flexible. Weather, nerves, and body quirks happen. Adjust, don’t panic.
