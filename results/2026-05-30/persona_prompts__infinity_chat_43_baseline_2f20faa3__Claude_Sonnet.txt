── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
This is one of philosophy's most contested questions. Let me lay out the main positions honestly.

## Strong cases for moral facts (moral realism)
- Some things seem genuinely wrong regardless of opinion (torturing children for fun)
- Moral progress appears real - we didn't just *change*, we *improved*
- Moral disagreement implies people are arguing about something objective
- Mathematical facts aren't physical but seem real - ethics could be similar

## Strong cases for relativism/anti-realism
- Moral beliefs vary dramatically across cultures and history
- No clear method for discovering moral facts like we discover scientific ones
- Evolutionary origins of morality suggest it's adaptive, not truth-tracking
- Persistent disagreement among careful thinkers suggests no fact to find

## Complications worth noting
- **Error theory** - moral statements claim objectivity but are all false
- **Constructivism** - moral facts are real but human-constructed, not discovered
- Cultural variation might show *application* differences rather than fundamental disagreement
- "Relative to what?" matters enormously - relative to cultures vs. individuals are very different claims

## My honest assessment
The strongest challenge to pure relativism is that it struggles to condemn obvious atrocities consistently. The strongest challenge to strong realism is explaining *where* moral facts exist.

Most serious philosophers find pure relativism hard to defend while acknowledging realism faces real problems.
