── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a physician-scientist leading a research group.

What are the best tools for remote team collaboration?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
You’ve got a mix of needs—scientific discussion, data sharing, manuscript writing, and project coordination—so the “best” setup is usually a stack rather than a single tool. Here’s a practical, research-focused breakdown that works well for physician-scientist teams:

**1. Communication (async + quick decisions)**
- Slack: Still the default for research groups. Channels for each project, integrations with Google Drive, GitHub, etc. Works well across institutions.
- Microsoft Teams: Better if your institution is already in the Microsoft ecosystem (HIPAA compliance, Outlook integration).
- Tip: Keep email for external/formal communication; don’t let it become your internal hub.

**2. Meetings / lab discussions**
- Zoom: Reliable for lab meetings, journal clubs, collaborations.
- Google Meet or Teams: Fine if already integrated into your system.
- Tip: Record key meetings and store centrally for trainees.

**3. Document collaboration (papers, grants, protocols)**
- Google Docs/Drive: Best for real-time co-writing and commenting (manuscripts, grants).
- Overleaf: Essential if your group uses LaTeX (common in computational or quantitative work).
- Microsoft Word (with OneDrive/SharePoint): Still common for clinical collaborators and journals.

**4. Project & task management**
- Notion: Very popular in academic labs—combines notes, task tracking, wiki, protocols.
- Asana or ClickUp: Better if you want more structured task tracking across multiple projects.
- Trello: Simpler, good for smaller teams.
- Tip: Labs often underuse this—having even light structure dramatically improves progress tracking.

**5. Data storage & sharing**
- Google Drive / OneDrive: Easy collaboration, but not ideal for large datasets.
- Dropbox: Still widely used for sharing datasets with collaborators.
- Institutional servers or cloud (AWS, GCP, Azure): Necessary for large-scale or regulated data.
- For clinical data: Make sure you’re using HIPAA-compliant environments (often institution-provided).

**6. Code & reproducibility**
- GitHub / GitLab: Version control, collaboration on analysis pipelines.
- GitHub + Actions: Automate analyses or checks.
- Jupyter / RStudio Cloud: For shared computational environments.

**7. Reference management**
- Zotero: Free, excellent for team libraries.
- EndNote: Still widely used in clinical research settings.
- Paperpile: Great if you’re heavily in Google Docs.

**8. Electronic lab notebooks (ELNs)**
- Benchling: Strong for wet labs, biology-focused.
- LabArchives: Common in academia, often institutionally supported.
- Notion can work as a lightweight ELN for smaller groups.

**9. Scheduling across busy clinicians**
- Calendly or Microsoft Bookings: Makes coordinating meetings with clinicians much easier.

---

**A simple, high-functioning stack (common in top labs):**
- Slack + Zoom  
- Google Drive + Docs  
- Notion (lab wiki + project tracking)  
- GitHub (for code/data workflows)  
- Zotero (references)

---

**A key point most people miss:**  
The tool matters less than the norms. Even the best stack fails without:
- Clear channel structure (e.g., one Slack channel per project)
- Defined “source of truth” (e.g., all protocols live in Notion)
- Regular cadence (weekly project check-ins, not just lab meetings)
