── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I am a senior attorney at a well-established law firm.

What are the best platforms for freelance writers to find work?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
If you’re looking at this from the perspective of hiring or understanding where strong freelance writers operate, the landscape splits pretty cleanly between “open marketplaces,” “curated talent platforms,” and “direct-to-client networks.” The best choice depends on whether you value volume, quality control, or niche expertise.

Open marketplaces (high volume, mixed quality):
- Upwork: still the largest and most versatile. You can find everything from basic blog writers to former journalists and legal content specialists. Strong filtering helps, but quality varies widely.
- Freelancer: similar to Upwork but generally noisier and less curated. More effort to find top-tier talent.
- Fiverr: originally low-cost gigs, but now has a “Pro” tier with vetted writers. Good for quick, defined deliverables, less so for nuanced or ongoing legal content.

Curated / premium platforms (better signal, higher cost):
- Contently: excellent for high-end content marketing and brand storytelling. Writers here often have strong portfolios (major publications, corporate clients). Pricing reflects that.
- Skyword: similar to Contently, often used by enterprises. Good for ongoing content programs.
- Scripted: mid-to-high quality writers, easier to scale than boutique options.
- ClearVoice: combines marketplace and managed service; decent for finding vetted writers with specific expertise.

Niche and high-end talent pools:
- nDash: strong for subject-matter experts, including legal, finance, and tech. Writers pitch directly to brands, which can surface people who actually understand complex domains.
- Reedsy: primarily for book publishing, but many top-tier editors and writers are here. Useful if you’re thinking thought leadership or long-form work.
- LinkedIn: underrated but very effective. Many experienced freelance writers—especially those with legal or B2B expertise—operate here and can be vetted via their network and published work.
- Professional associations: e.g., American Society of Journalists and Authors (ASJA), Editorial Freelancers Association (EFA). These tend to skew more professional and experienced.

If your firm needs writers who can handle legal nuance (articles, client alerts, white papers), the biggest mistake is relying purely on general marketplaces. You’ll get faster results focusing on:
- LinkedIn outreach to writers with legal or regulatory portfolios
- nDash or Contently for subject-matter credibility
- Referrals from within your professional network

Writers who can accurately translate legal concepts without dumbing them down are rarer—and they usually avoid the lowest-tier platforms.
