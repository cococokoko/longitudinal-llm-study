Nice goal—marathons are equal parts fitness, pacing, and logistics. Since you’ve got a lot on your plate day-to-day, the trick is training smart, not just hard.

Start with a simple structure you can stick to. Aim for 3–4 runs per week:
- One long run (build gradually, adding about 1–2 miles per week, with occasional cutback weeks)
- One shorter, easy run
- One run with some quality (like intervals or tempo, but keep it light if you’re newer)
- Optional: a very easy recovery jog

Consistency beats intensity. If life gets busy, protect the long run and one other run—that’s enough to keep progress moving.

Pacing is where most first-time marathoners go wrong. You should be able to hold a conversation on most runs. On race day, start slower than you think you need to. The real race begins around mile 18–20.

Fueling matters more than people expect:
- Practice eating during long runs (gels, chews, even simple carbs like bananas)
- Take in carbs every 30–45 minutes on runs over ~90 minutes
- Hydrate regularly, not just when you feel thirsty

Don’t ignore recovery:
- Sleep is huge (I know, easier said than done with family demands)
- Rotate shoes if you can
- Light strength work (core, hips, glutes) 1–2 times a week helps prevent injuries

Plan your logistics early so they don’t become stress later—route, childcare, time of day, and even laying out gear the night before runs can make a big difference.

And mentally, break the race into chunks. Thinking “just get to the next mile marker” is a lot easier than “26.2 miles.”
