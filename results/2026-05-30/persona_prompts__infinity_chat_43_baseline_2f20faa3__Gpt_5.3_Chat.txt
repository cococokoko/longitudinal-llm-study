── Prompt ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Are there moral facts or is it all relative?

── Response ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Short answer: there’s a real debate, and both sides have serious arguments. You don’t have to pick “everything is objective” or “everything is relative” to make sense of morality.

**Moral realism (there are moral facts):**  
This view says some things are actually right or wrong independent of what anyone thinks. For example, “torturing someone for fun is wrong” would be true even if a whole society approved of it. Realists argue that:
- We talk and argue about morality as if there are correct answers.
- Some moral claims seem as solid as basic facts about harm or fairness.
- Moral progress (like abolishing slavery) makes more sense if we’re getting closer to truth, not just changing preferences.

**Moral anti-realism / relativism (no objective moral facts):**  
This side says morality depends on human attitudes, cultures, or frameworks. There’s no stance-independent “fact of the matter.” Arguments here include:
- Different cultures disagree deeply about values.
- Moral judgments seem tied to emotions, upbringing, and social needs.
- It’s hard to explain what kind of “thing” a moral fact would even be.

**Middle-ground views:**  
A lot of philosophers try to split the difference:
- **Constructivism:** moral truths are “built” from shared rational principles (like fairness), not discovered like physics but not arbitrary either.
- **Relativism with limits:** morality varies, but there are common constraints (like avoiding unnecessary suffering) rooted in human nature.
- **Error theory:** we talk as if there are moral facts, but strictly speaking there aren’t—yet the system is still useful.

**Where that leaves you:**  
Even if morality isn’t objective in a cosmic sense, it doesn’t collapse into “anything goes.” Humans share needs—avoiding pain, needing trust, cooperation—and stable societies depend on certain norms. That gives morality real weight in practice.

If you’re asking what’s most plausible: many people find a kind of “practical objectivity” convincing—morality grounded in facts about human well-being and rational cooperation, even if it’s not written into the fabric of the universe like gravity.
